# What is Reparenting?

Source: https://www.goodinside.com/blog/reparenting-your-inner-child/

---

Why does reparenting prove especially valuable when raising children? Consider common parenting frustrations—whining, defiance, tantrums—and pose the central question: "What does this situation bring up for me?"

Parents must examine their own reactions before addressing children's behavior. Good Inside's mission centers on equipping parents with knowledge and tools to raise resilient, confident children, recognizing that parental struggles often trace back to childhood experiences.

## The Concept of Reparenting

**Definition:** "Reparenting yourself is the practice of exploring your unmet needs in your childhood and giving yourself what you missed."

This practice helps individuals understand their triggers and challenges to enable personal growth.

**Common reasons to pursue reparenting:**

- Difficulty setting boundaries and declining requests
- Guilt surrounding self-care
- Harsh inner criticism and self-blame
- Reducing parental yelling and reactive responses
- Breaking cycles of harmful parenting inherited from one's family

Reparenting doesn't require blaming caregivers; rather, both truths coexist—parents did their best with available resources AND children may have experienced unmet needs.

### What is Your Inner Child?

An "inner child" represents younger aspects of ourselves—memories, experiences, beliefs—influencing adult behavior. Drawing from attachment theory, this concept explains how early caregiver bonds shape later relationships. Children instinctively develop traits bringing them closer to caregivers (safety) while suppressing those creating distance (perceived danger). These patterns persist into adulthood, affecting relationships, emotional expression, conflict navigation, and security definitions.

## How Reparenting Benefits Parents

Reparenting transcends past healing; it enables breaking harmful intergenerational cycles while building healthier family patterns.

Most parents unconsciously replicate their own upbringing. Despite adult independence, our nervous systems remain conditioned to follow childhood attachment patterns. Strong reactions to children's behavior typically stem from personal childhood triggers.

### How Inner Child Impacts Parenting

Three examples of intergenerational pattern repetition:

**1. Emotional expression:** Parents told to suppress tears during childhood often react intensely to their children's tantrums, perpetuating emotional suppression cycles.

**2. Academic performance:** Those receiving validation primarily for academic achievement may respond with frustration when their own children struggle academically.

**3. Boundaries:** Parents who grew up neglected or parentified often struggle modeling healthy boundaries, feeling guilty during self-care or unable to decline requests.

**Encouraging note:** Brain neuroplasticity allows genuine change; reparenting rewires emotional responses and relational patterns.

## How to Reparent Your Inner Child

Reparenting functions as a gradual process requiring behavioral reflection, childhood experience revisitation for neural rewiring, and acknowledging inner child narratives while constructing new behavioral patterns.

An analogy: knowing intellectually that cars no longer pose danger doesn't prevent automatic protective responses—changing ingrained survival patterns requires consistent practice.

Readers engaging with this material represent "cycle-breakers" demonstrating courage and strength.

## Three Key Takeaways

1. Parental triggers don't indicate personal failure; children's behaviors activate our own childhood experiences.

2. Addressing children's conduct requires first examining parental reactions through: "What does this parenting situation bring up for me?"

3. Reparenting healing encompasses past and future generations, dismantling harmful cycles while establishing beneficial patterns.

Dedicating time to parenting reflection demonstrates remarkable personal commitment.
