# What Is a Deeply Feeling Kid?: A Guide for Parents

Source: https://www.goodinside.com/blog/understanding-your-deeply-feeling-kid/

---

Is your child highly sensitive? Do they struggle to calm down, or hate discussing their emotions? You might be parenting a Deeply Feeling Kid.

If this resonates, understand that nothing is wrong with your child or your parenting abilities. Child-rearing isn't one-size-fits-all, and some kids—particularly highly sensitive ones—require different approaches. Deeply Feeling Kids face distinctive challenges, and conventional parenting strategies often prove inadequate or counterproductive.

Good Inside aims to equip parents with knowledge and tools for raising confident, resilient children, especially those with deeper emotional sensitivity. Their approach helps transform behavior while strengthening parent-child bonds.

## What is a Deeply Feeling Kid?

Deeply Feeling Kids demonstrate heightened sensitivity to their surroundings: they experience emotions more intensely, emotions persist longer, and they're more likely to resist assistance.

These children represent "the most misunderstood group of kids in the world." While parenting them presents challenges, they offer profound lessons. They perceive the world deeply, know their preferences, and resist external pressure. With appropriate frameworks and tools, parents can finally understand their child, intervene confidently, and develop hope for meaningful change.

## Common Signs of a Deeply Feeling Kid

Several indicators suggest you're parenting a Deeply Feeling Kid:

- Overreact to minor events, earning labels like "dramatic" or "difficult"
- Display aggressive behaviors: screaming, crying, yelling, hitting, or kicking
- Struggle to calm down after becoming upset (potentially hours)
- Push parents away when needing them most ("Leave me alone!")
- Resist conversations about emotions and feelings
- Assign blame unfairly ("You're the reason I failed that test!")
- Display hypervigilance regarding details
- Express strong opinions and struggle with change

Every child differs; some exhibit only several traits. "Deeply Feeling Kid" is an understanding framework, not a clinical diagnosis.

If your child has diagnoses of ADHD, oppositional defiant disorder, pathological demand avoidance, or sensory issues, Good Inside's approach can still help.

## Understanding Deeply Feeling Kids' Behaviors

A fundamental principle: parents must understand before intervening—especially with highly sensitive children.

Two essential concepts shape Deeply Feeling Kids' behaviors: emotional regulation and attachment. Grasping these will transform your perspective on your child's actions.

### What is emotional regulation?

Emotional regulation means managing feelings constructively—identifying emotions, communicating feelings, and coping with distress.

The difficult truth: kids arrive with all their emotions but "none of the skills to manage those feelings!" When children act uncontrollably, they feel uncontrollable—not because they're "bad," "rude," "dramatic," or "defiant." Your parental role involves teaching missing skills, fostering confident, resilient, compassionate adults.

Think of yourself as your child's emotional regulation coach. Your Deeply Feeling Kid experiences more intense emotions than peers, naturally needing additional time and support—similar to children requiring extra help learning reading or swimming. With proper strategies, "they _can_ and _will_ learn."

### What is attachment?

Attachment represents the emotional connection between two people. In parenting, attachment constitutes a child's primary evolutionary drive—how kids develop safety feelings. Young children depend on proximity to caregivers for basic needs (food, shelter, water) and survival.

Children constantly monitor their environment, noticing what strengthens and weakens their connection—protecting attachment against threats. For early humans, these threats involved predators. For Deeply Feeling Kids, "threats" are intense emotions.

Two truths coexist: emotions aren't genuinely life-threatening for your child AND they feel profoundly threatening physically. They fear being overwhelmed by the feeling while simultaneously fearing their emotion could overwhelm you—their primary attachment figure.

This explains why Deeply Feeling Kids push parents away during outbursts. They're essentially expressing: "My feeling is DANGEROUS! I need help… but what if you cannot handle this feeling either? I am too much for anyone, I am bad!"

## How to Support Deeply Feeling Kids

### Stay close… but not too close

During dysregulation, your calm, loving presence represents your most powerful parenting tool. Whether sitting directly beside them or positioned outside their bedroom door, you communicate: your overwhelming, painful, threatening feelings don't frighten me.

Your child might protest ("Go away! I hate you!")—this reflects their fear, not their true wish. Trust that difficulty will pass. Your emotional stability demonstrates they can develop regulation skills too.

## 3 Key Takeaways on Deeply Feeling Kids

- Deeply Feeling Kids aren't "bad" kids—they're good kids experiencing emotions more intensely who need different support frameworks
- Emotional regulation skills form the foundation for behavioral change
- During difficult moments, your calm presence represents your most powerful parenting asset

Remember: dedicating time to understanding your child's needs demonstrates amazing parenting.
