# Should Parents Tell the Truth About the Tooth Fairy, Easter Bunny and Other Myths?

Question
My very sensitive 8-year-old asked me today if the Tooth Fairy really exists. He wanted to know if I was the one putting the money under his pillow, which I denied. I asked him the reason for his question and he told me that most of the kids in his classroom have told him that the Tooth Fairy doesn't exist. His response to his friends was that his mother (me) is not a liar, so his friends told him "Well, we think your mother is lying." 
After a minute I understood that my son was at a disadvantage and could probably be teased in the future, so I told him the truth, to which he responded with tears of disappointment. He couldn't believe that all this time there was no Tooth Fairy. Or he was probably crying because I made him believe in something that doesn't exist. In my attempt to calm him down I kind of fell into my own trap, I told him that I put the money under his pillow a couple of times, but I don't know who did the other times. 
He also believes in Santa; even though I told him, when he was two, that Santa Claus existed but he died and whoever he sees as Santa is a man wearing a costume. He was ok with that, but many Christmas went by and he heard from friends and family about what was Santa bringing them, and naturally he started believing. I went with the flow, mostly because I was concerned about him telling other children that Santa was a myth. Could you please give me some advice about the right age and how to explain these things? 
Answer
I can understand why you got rattled and "fell into your own trap." Most parents wonder what to do in the situation you describe. And most parents, when their kids get upset, are tempted to back off and say whatever they can to make things better.  
But children need to know they can trust their parents to tell them the truth, no matter what -- even about things like this. In other words, when your kids ask if Santa, the Tooth Fairy and the Easter Bunny are real, you should tell them the truth. 
First, doublecheck that your child really wants to know. You might say _"I wonder why you're asking?"_ or _"Do YOU think the Tooth Fairy is real?"_
If they persist in asking, doublecheck again:_"It sounds like you really want to know for sure. I wonder how you would feel if it turned out that the Tooth Fairy wasn't real? Would you be ready for that?"_
Finally, if your child is still asking, go ahead and explain that you love the idea of the Tooth Fairy and wanted them to experience it, so you appointed yourself as their Tooth Fairy (or as the designated Easter Bunny, or whatever.) 
That's not always easy, of course. We may feel that we are crushing a belief that our child needs. When my daughter was five years old, she asked me if the Tooth Fairy was real. When I told her no, she became very angry at me, not because I had lead her to believe that a make-believe character was real, but because she wanted so much for the Tooth Fairy to be real. I desperately wanted to hedge, so I know how you felt with your son. Somehow I resisted the temptation, and let her cry and rage, not just at the unfair world, but at me. Over and over, I reflected back to her how disappointed she was, and how much she wished the Tooth Fairy could be real, and how angry she felt at that moment at the world and at me to discover this truth. 
When my daughter was twelve, she still remembered this incident clearly. She told me that she thought that I did the right thing, and that she would have been even angrier at me if I had lied in response to her direct question. Even though she was disappointed that the Tooth Fairy wasn't real, it was better for her to know that I would always tell her the truth when she asked.
As far as the right age goes, most kids seem to start asking whether the Tooth Fairy is real between ages 4 and 7, when they lose a lot of teeth and thus have the opportunity to experience the whole Tooth Fairy fantasy. It's my hypothesis that the kids who don't ask us may be the ones who so love the fantasy that they don't really want to know the truth. 
Unfortunately, since children are so often determined to prove their "grown-up-ness" by denying and ridiculing their previous dependencies, many youngsters delight in telling their classmates that the tooth fairy isn't real. The result is that most kids seem to hear the "truth" about the tooth fairy from peers by age 7 if not before.  
Your son will probably ask about Santa now that he knows about the Tooth Fairy, and it's my opinion that you should tell him the truth. But with Santa you can also explain about the real Santa, who is no longer alive in body, but whose spirit is manifest whenever people are generous with each other. And of course, you can affirm your son's wish that Santa and the Tooth Fairy would be real, and you can acknowledge and empathize with his disappointment. 
I want to add that kids do get angry and disappointed, as we all do, when the world does not live up to their expectations. This disappointment is not a terrible thing to be prevented, but an opportunity for us to strengthen our relationship with our child by understanding how they feel. After all, children don't need everything they want. What they do need is a parent who understands and accepts them as they are, big feelings and all. 
As painful as it is for us to see our children suffer, it is a gift when we can accept those challenging feelings rather than talking our kids out of them. It gives our child the message that they are acceptable, messy feelings and all, that they can tolerate disappointment and survive, and that we are always there to comfort them -- not by denying the feelings, but by loving the child through them.
