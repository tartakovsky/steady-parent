# Should You Make Your Child Apologize?

Source: Peaceful Parent, Happy Kids (Dr. Laura Markham)
URL: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/should-you-make-your-child-apologize

---

## Core Insight: Forced Apologies Backfire

Most parents automatically insist that children apologize after conflicts because it seems like the right way to teach relationship repair. However, **forced apologies do not repair relationships and may actually damage them**.

Research by **John Gottman** (described as the pre-eminent couples researcher in the US) on adult couples shows that when an adult feels forced to apologize before they're ready, it creates resentment and the relationship gets worse rather than better. While direct research on forcing kids to apologize doesn't exist yet, children's own accounts reveal the harmful lessons they absorb from the practice.

### What Children Actually Learn from Forced Apologies

Children's reported experiences reveal several problematic outcomes:

1. **It teaches lying**: "It's lying to apologize when you don't mean it." Children recognize the inauthenticity and learn that saying things you don't mean is acceptable or expected.

2. **It creates a winner/loser dynamic**: "When my parents make my brother apologize, I feel like I won. That feels good, but it doesn't make me like him any better." The receiving sibling experiences triumph rather than reconciliation, which does nothing to improve the actual relationship.

3. **It intensifies anger**: "When I'm mad, I hate apologizing. It just makes me madder at my sister." Forcing the apology while emotions are still high amplifies rather than resolves negative feelings.

4. **Inauthentic apologies make the recipient angrier**: "I don't like it when my brother apologizes to me when my parents make him do it, because he acts like he doesn't even mean it. It makes me mad all over again." Both children end up worse off than before the forced apology.

5. **Natural timing matters**: "Later I always like my sister again. I could apologize then. But not when I'm mad." Children intuitively understand that genuine repair happens after emotions have settled, not during peak anger.

---

## The Alternative Framework: Empowering Repair

Instead of extracting apologies, the goal is to **empower children to see themselves as capable of making things better**. This reframe shifts from punishment/compliance to agency/generosity.

### Step 1: Wait Until Anger Has Subsided

If the child is still angry, they cannot genuinely engage in repair. A child who is still angry needs to feel heard before they can listen to their sibling's perspective. Attempting repair while emotions run high will fail because the child lacks the emotional capacity to take the other person's viewpoint.

### Step 2: Empower the Child to Repair (Don't Extract an Apology)

Once anger has passed, humans often look at the wreckage caused by their anger and naturally wish they could make things better. Parents should help children get into this habit early.

**The key reframe**: Instead of an apology that makes your child feel like they "lost," help them feel like a hero - someone who makes things better.

**Example language**: "Your brother loves you and looks up to you. When you yelled at him, it looked like it really hurt his feelings. I wonder what you could do to make things better with him."

This approach:
- Reminds the child of the positive relationship foundation
- Names the impact of their behavior on the sibling
- Invites the child to generate their own solution rather than comply with a prescribed one

### Step 3: If the Child Suggests Apologizing, Check for Authenticity

If the child suggests an apology but sounds sullen or angry, acknowledge that a heartfelt apology isn't possible yet. Ask whether they think that kind of apology would actually make their sibling feel better.

**Example language**: "Apologizing is a wonderful way to make things better, Sweetheart, but I don't want you to apologize until you mean it. I'm not asking you to say something that's untrue; I don't think that ever makes anyone feel better."

This teaches that:
- Apologies are valuable tools when genuine
- Authenticity matters more than ritual compliance
- Saying something untrue doesn't serve the relationship

### Step 4: Offer Ideas for Repair (If Needed)

If the child draws a blank and wants suggestions, offer concrete options:

- Help rebuild the tower that the fight was about
- Build a tower for the sibling to knock down (creative reversal)
- Repair or replace damage done (such as a broken toy)
- Draw a picture or make a card listing three things you love about your sibling
- Give a big hug
- Play a game that the sibling wants to play
- Help the sibling with a chore
- Make an agreement and sign it, promising not to repeat the infraction and describing how the situation will be handled in the future

**Critical distinction**: This is NOT assigning a consequence to "pay off a debt." The purpose is empowering the child to see themselves as a generous person who can make things better when they've done something hurtful.

**The child must choose** - you can give ideas, but then step back: "I know you'll figure out the perfect thing to do...I can't wait to see what it is!" Then hug them and leave the room. This expresses confidence in the child's capacity while giving them space and ownership.

### Step 5: Handle Resistance ("I Don't Want to Repair Things with Her!")

If the child refuses to engage in repair, acknowledge that they're still very angry and validate why. If possible, help them process the anger.

Then set the expectation that repair is expected when they feel better - this isn't optional, but the timing is flexible.

**Example language**: "I guess you're still so mad that you don't want to make things better with her right now...and I know that even though you and your sister fight, you also love each other, and it makes things better if you can do something to repair things...Maybe you need some time before you're ready to repair things with your sister. It can take some time for the anger to melt away...I can't help you with your feelings any more right now, but we can talk again later...When you're ready, I know that you will know just the right thing to do to make things better."

This language:
- Validates the current emotional state without approving of inaction forever
- Affirms the underlying love between siblings
- Acknowledges that processing anger takes time
- Maintains the expectation of eventual repair
- Expresses confidence in the child's ability to figure it out
- Removes pressure while keeping the door open

### Step 6: Be a Role Model

Children learn how to repair relationship ruptures by watching their parents. When you have a relationship rupture with your child, demonstrate apology and find ways to reconnect. Modeling genuine repair teaches children what authentic reconciliation looks like.

### Step 7: Focus on Communication Skills Over Apology Rituals

The deeper work involves helping children:
- Express their wants and needs clearly
- Listen to each other genuinely
- Restate what they heard their sibling say

When children develop these communication skills, conflicts heal at a deeper level. Apologies then become easier, more spontaneous, and almost superfluous - the same pattern seen in healthy adult relationships. The ritual becomes unnecessary when the underlying communication and understanding are strong.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **Forcing apologies while the child is still angry** - This intensifies anger rather than resolving it

2. **Treating apology as a required ritual** - This teaches inauthenticity and compliance over genuine repair

3. **Creating winner/loser dynamics** - Forced apologies position one child as victor and the other as vanquished

4. **Assigning repair as punishment** - Repair activities should feel empowering, not like consequences to pay off debt

5. **Taking over the repair process** - The child must choose their own repair action to develop agency and see themselves as capable

6. **Expecting immediate resolution** - Anger needs time to subside; pushing for quick repair backfires

---

## Key Principles Summary

**Timing matters**: Wait for anger to subside before attempting any repair work.

**Agency over compliance**: Children should choose their own repair actions rather than follow prescribed apologies.

**Identity framing**: Help children see themselves as generous people who make things better, not as wrongdoers paying debts.

**Authenticity requirement**: Never ask children to say something untrue; insincere apologies help no one.

**Communication foundation**: The real goal is teaching children to express needs, listen to siblings, and understand each other - not to perform apology rituals.

**Parental modeling**: Children learn repair skills primarily from watching their parents handle relationship ruptures.
