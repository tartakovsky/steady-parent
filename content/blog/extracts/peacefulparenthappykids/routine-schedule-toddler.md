# Routines That Toddlers Can Understand

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids

---

## Why Toddlers Need Predictable Routines

**Core insight**: Toddlers and preschoolers feel small in the world because most things happen TO them rather than being chosen by them. This creates a fundamental psychological need for predictability.

**Predictability provides a sense of control**. When children know what will happen next, they gain some agency in a world where they otherwise have very little. This sense of control is not about actually controlling events, but about being able to anticipate them.

**Predictable routines create safety**. When children feel safe, they can relax. When they can relax, they develop a sense of mastery in handling their lives. This mastery builds confidence and emotional security.

**The connection between routine and cooperation**: Toddlers are famously oppositional, but much of this opposition stems from feeling "pushed around by what seems like arbitrary circumstance." When children understand the routine and what comes next, they are more likely to cooperate because they don't feel blindsided or controlled by random adult decisions.

**Brain development benefit**: Routines develop the prefrontal cortex, which handles planning and executive function. Children learn that things happen in a certain order during the day, which builds their capacity for sequential thinking and future planning.

---

## Why Routines Help Parents Too

Adults caring for children also benefit from having a plan for the day. While many parents love the freedom of deciding spontaneously what comes next (and this can be the basis of creativity), there's an important distinction: spontaneity works best when WE decide what rules to break.

Without a routine, life with children can overwhelm and derail us, leaving us feeling "run over by life, rather than confident and in charge." The routine provides a foundation that makes intentional flexibility possible, rather than reactive chaos.

## How to Talk About the Routine with Toddlers

### Preview What Comes Next

Talk about what comes next throughout the day. This verbal narration helps children build an internal map of their day.

**Morning example**: After "good morning" cuddles, you might say: "Let's get dressed so we can have breakfast...Then we can go on an adventure!"

**Reframing mundane tasks**: A trip to the grocery store or drugstore can be framed as "an adventure." If you treat it as an adventure and a learning experience, that's how your child will perceive it. The framing matters.

### Review What Happened

On the way home from activities, review the morning with your child: "What a great morning we had...we had cuddles when we woke up...then we had oatmeal for breakfast...then we brushed teeth and got shoes on like we do every morning...then we went on an adventure to the grocery store. What was your favorite thing?"

This review reinforces the routine pattern and helps children understand the structure of their day. Asking "what was your favorite thing?" also invites the child into reflection and connection.

### Preview Again

After reviewing, talk about what comes next: "When we get home, I will put these groceries away. Do you want to help me? Then we will make lunch, and have a story, and then it will be nap time. This afternoon, do you want to go to the park or set up the sprinkler in the back yard to run through?"

This combines predictability with choice, which is the ideal balance for toddler autonomy.

## Flexibility Within Structure

### The Balance Between Routine and Freedom

A routine should not be rigid. The article explicitly addresses this concern: routines should not "force" kids into something that isn't working, nor should they prevent children from stopping to examine a bug on the sidewalk.

**Why flexibility matters for development**: A child's routine needs plenty of ease in it to allow them to accomplish some of their most important life tasks: exploration and experimentation. These are not interruptions to the schedule; they are essential developmental activities.

**Joy requires spontaneity**: If you want a child who is able to feel joy, you need to let her seize those opportunities to smell the roses and enjoy watching the worm inch through the garden. Joy comes from presence and discovery, not from adhering to a schedule.

### Decision-Making Within Windows

Children need plenty of opportunity to make their own decisions and choices about how to use their own time within certain windows of their schedule. The structure provides the container; the child fills it.

**What knowing the routine enables**: Knowing what to expect allows toddlers to relax in the moment, rather than frantically casting about for the next entertainment. Without predictability, children may become anxious about what comes next, which prevents them from fully engaging with the present moment.

## Creating a Routine Chart

### What It Is

A **Routine Chart** is a poster with photos of your child doing their usual activities. It serves as a visual representation of the daily routine that the child can reference independently.

These charts can be purchased commercially, but can also be easily made at home, modeled after the ones used by preschools but personalized with photos of your child.

### How to Make One

**Step 1**: Take photos (or find pictures) depicting your child's daily routine. Examples include: wake up, potty, dress, breakfast, errands, snack, play, lunch, nap, etc.

**Step 2**: Write the words next to each photo.

**Step 3**: Arrange photos on a poster board in the order they usually happen.

### Layout Considerations

Some people like to arrange the photos in a circle like a clock, but toddlers think of time as more linear, so the clock arrangement may not help your toddler as much as a simple timeline.

### Making It Adaptable

Use tape or velcro instead of glue to allow for schedule changes. This flexibility lets you exchange "errands" for "Visit Grandma" when needed.

**Advanced option**: Buy a magnetized bulletin board and glue photos to magnets, so you can easily move them around as the schedule changes.

---

## Evolving the Chart as Children Grow

### Separate Morning and Evening Charts

As your child gets older, you'll probably want two separate charts for morning and evening routines. This separation allows your child to begin to take charge of her own schedule for specific parts of the day.

### Child-Arranged Task Order

Keep the "tasks" of the morning in an envelope under the chart, so your child can arrange the tasks in the order that she wants to tackle them. This gives the child agency over the sequence while maintaining the overall structure.

## How Routine Charts Reduce Power Struggles

### The Mechanism

This kind of chart empowers your child to take charge of her own routine, which reduces power struggles and the need for you to be involved in every move your three year old makes in the morning.

**Why it works**: Instead of the parent directing every step (which feels controlling to the child), the chart becomes the authority. The child follows the chart, not the parent's moment-to-moment commands.

### Changing the Parent's Role

You will still want to connect at the beginning of the routine, and throughout the routine as necessary to keep your child on track. However, your intervention can take the form of hugs and checking in, rather than barking orders.

**Example of supportive check-in**: "I see you're dressed already. Wow! You got that shirt on all by yourself! What's next on your chart?"

This approach acknowledges accomplishment, reinforces autonomy, and redirects to the chart rather than to parental direction.

---

## Why Strong-Willed Children Especially Benefit

**Strong-willed kids especially love routine charts** because they get to be in charge of themselves and avoid the power struggles. The chart satisfies their need for autonomy without requiring the parent to give up structure.

But all children benefit from feeling less controlled and having opportunities to master the demands of their lives.

---

## The Connection Between Autonomy and Compliance

**Key principle**: Autonomy is a basic human need. Children who are given more independence where they can handle it become more willing to comply with parental direction in general.

This seems paradoxical but makes psychological sense: when children feel respected and given appropriate control, they don't need to fight for it. They have less reason to resist parental guidance because they don't feel their autonomy is under constant threat.

---

## Long-Term Benefits of Early Routine Mastery

A child who takes charge of their routine early learns responsibility and becomes a **self-starter**. This has cascading benefits:

- Less nagging required from parents
- More enjoyment of the child (because interactions aren't dominated by conflict)
- Strong foundation established before the child even gets to preschool
- Preparation for the independence demands of school and beyond

The early investment in routine charts and child-led scheduling pays dividends in reduced parental effort and increased child competence over time.
