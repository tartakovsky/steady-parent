# Handling Intrusive Remarks from Strangers About a Child's Speech or Size

Source: Dr. Laura Markham, Peaceful Parent Happy Kids

## The Core Problem

When a child is visibly different from expectations (such as being very tall for their age) and also has a developmental variation (such as a speech delay), strangers often make incorrect assumptions and verbalize them in front of the child. This creates a dual problem: the remarks are inappropriate, and the child hears characterizations of themselves that aren't accurate (being called anxious when they're not, being assigned disabilities they don't have).

## When to Intervene vs. Let Children Handle It Themselves

**The general principle** is to avoid rushing in to rescue or speak for your child. The instinct to let children navigate social situations themselves is valuable because it builds their social competence and independence.

**However, this principle has important exceptions:**

1. **Age matters** - A two-year-old cannot possibly know how to handle inappropriate remarks from adults. They lack the social experience, verbal skills, and cognitive development to respond effectively.

2. **The nature of the remarks matters** - When comments are inappropriate and potentially harmful to a child's self-concept, passively standing by fails the child.

**What happens when we don't intervene with a child who is "floundering" socially:**
- The child feels lost
- The child feels unprotected
- The child feels inadequate
- The child feels without guidance
- The child interprets the parent's silence as either helplessness or judgment of them

This is why the common advice to "let kids figure it out" needs to be applied with nuance. Standing there while a very young child struggles with something beyond their capability isn't teaching independence - it's abandonment.

## The Recommended Approach: Model, Discuss, Practice

**Step 1: Model how to respond in the moment**

For any age child who is "floundering socially" (meaning they're visibly struggling and don't have the tools to respond), the parent should demonstrate how to handle the situation. This gives the child a template they can eventually use themselves.

**Step 2: Discuss the interaction afterward**

After the situation has passed, ask your child what they thought about the interaction. This serves multiple purposes:
- It gives them space to express any feelings about what happened
- It validates that the situation was noteworthy and their reactions matter
- It opens a conversation about how such situations can be handled

**Step 3: Practice for the future (if the child is interested)**

If the child wants to, practice the interaction with them through role-play. The goal is for them to start feeling confident about answering for themselves in future similar situations. This is optional and should be child-led - "if they want to at that point."

## Specific Verbal Techniques

### Technique 1: Answer and Reframe Simultaneously

When a stranger asks a question and your child doesn't answer, you answer for them while naturally weaving in information that corrects their assumptions.

**Example:**
- Stranger (to child): "Do you like snow?"
- Parent (warmly, to both): "We sure do! Doesn't every two-year-old like snow?"

**Why this works:**
- It answers the social question, removing the awkward silence
- It mentions the child's actual age casually, so the stranger can recalibrate their expectations
- The warm tone models graciousness rather than defensiveness
- The direction "to both of them" means you're not excluding either party

**The likely response** from strangers: "You're only two? What a big boy you are!" - This is a pivot that most people make naturally once they have accurate information. They may still comment on size, but the inappropriate pressure on the child to perform beyond their age level stops.

### Technique 2: Reframe Body Comments

When strangers comment on your child's body (size), you can redirect to values rather than physical attributes.

**Example:**
- After someone says "What a big boy!": "Strong and healthy, that's my boy... Let's go, Sweetie."

**Why this works:**
- "Strong and healthy" shifts from size (which the child can't control and which has been causing problems) to positive attributes
- "Let's go, Sweetie" provides an exit - you're not obligated to continue an interaction that's been uncomfortable
- The brevity prevents getting drawn into a longer discussion about your child's body

**The author's aside:** She notes it would be inappropriate to comment back on the stranger's body, implying that adults wouldn't appreciate the same scrutiny they apply to children. This reframe technique is the socially acceptable alternative.

### Technique 3: Deflect Inappropriate Personal Remarks

When strangers make assumptions about why your child isn't speaking (anxiety, disability), you need to reframe specifically for your child's benefit, not just to correct the stranger.

**Example:**
- Stranger (to child): "What's the matter, cat got your tongue? Feeling a bit nervous today?"
- Parent (warmly, to both): "I guess two-year-olds have as much right as the rest of us to decide when they want to talk... He talks when he's ready."

**Why this works:**
- It asserts a principle that applies universally (everyone has the right to decide when to speak)
- It frames the child's silence as a valid choice rather than a deficit
- "Two-year-olds" again provides the crucial age context
- "He talks when he's ready" normalizes waiting to speak without suggesting there's something wrong

### Technique 4: Direct Counter-Messaging to the Child

After deflecting to the stranger, speak directly to your child to explicitly counter the false narrative they just heard.

**Example:**
- Directly to your son, with eye contact and playful warmth: "Nervous? I don't think so... I think you can't wait for class to start so you can..."

**Why this works:**
- Eye contact and playful warmth signal that you're not worried
- Explicitly contradicting the stranger's assessment ("I don't think so") gives the child permission to reject that label for themselves
- Shifting to something positive and forward-looking (excitement about class) replaces the negative narrative with a positive one
- The ellipsis suggests you'd continue with something specific your child is looking forward to, making it personal and real

**Key insight:** The child understands what is being said about them. This means false characterizations can become internalized if not countered. The parent's role is to provide the correct counter-narrative so the child doesn't "believe what these people say about him."

## Underlying Philosophy

**Children need protection AND modeling, not just one or the other.** The goal is not to shield them forever, but to show them how to handle situations while they're still too young to figure it out alone, so they can eventually do it themselves.

**Warmth is the tone throughout.** Every suggested response includes maintaining warmth - toward both the stranger and the child. This isn't about putting strangers in their place or being defensive. It's about smoothly redirecting while keeping everyone's dignity intact.

**The child's self-concept is the priority.** The core concern isn't the stranger's rudeness - it's what the child hears about themselves. Every intervention technique centers on what the child will take away from the interaction.

**"Polite AND effective" are both required.** The parent asking the question wanted both, and the advice delivers both. Being effective doesn't require being rude, and being polite doesn't mean being ineffective. The techniques demonstrate how to accomplish both simultaneously.
