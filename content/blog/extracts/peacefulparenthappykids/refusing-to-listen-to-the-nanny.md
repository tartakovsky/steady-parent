# When Your Child Refuses to Listen to the Nanny

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/refusing-to-listen-to-the-nanny
Expert: Dr. Laura Markham, PhD, Clinical Psychologist

---

## Core Principle: Cooperation Stems from Relationship

**Children only cooperate with us because of their relationship with us.** This is the foundational insight that explains why a child may listen to parents but not to a nanny. The willingness to follow directions, accept limits, and cooperate is not about authority or control - it is about connection and the quality of the relationship.

This principle applies universally: children cooperate with teachers, nannies, grandparents, and other caregivers to the degree that a meaningful relationship exists between them.

## What the Nanny Needs: Three Pillars of Relationship

For a child to cooperate with a nanny, the nanny needs to develop a relationship with the child that contains three essential elements:

1. **Trust** - The child must feel safe and secure with the nanny
2. **Fun** - The relationship must include positive, enjoyable interactions
3. **Firmness** - The nanny must be comfortable setting limits

When a nanny sets a limit, the child needs to understand there is actually a reason to cooperate - and that reason comes from the relationship itself. The child wants to please the nanny because **the nanny matters** to the child.

## The Teacher Analogy

Consider how children relate to teachers in school: children get a new teacher every year, yet master teachers are able to bond with children quickly enough that children want to please them. This happens because skilled teachers understand that bonding comes first, and cooperation follows.

Nannies need the same outcome but typically lack the formal training that teachers receive. This means **parents need to actively help nannies develop these skills** rather than assuming the nanny will figure it out independently.

## The Parent-Nanny Relationship: Openness and Disclosure

A critical but often overlooked factor is the relationship between the parent and the nanny. For the nanny to succeed with the child, **the parent-nanny relationship must be characterized by openness and disclosure**.

The nanny must feel comfortable:
- Telling the parent when the child does something "out of line"
- Sharing struggles she is having with the child
- Asking for help without fear of judgment or job loss

**Warning:** If the nanny is afraid to tell the parent about problems because she fears losing her job, she will never be able to effectively manage the child's behavior. Fear of disclosure creates a cycle where problems compound because the nanny cannot get the support she needs.

## Parents Must Actively Share Their Strategies

Parents who have been with their child since birth have developed a repertoire of strategies for handling difficult situations. These hard-won insights need to be explicitly shared with the nanny.

**Example: Getting the Toddler in the Car Seat**

If parents have a toddler who resists the car seat (a common challenge), they have likely developed multiple approaches that work:
- "Please buckle up your seat belt for take off!"
- Starting the music as an incentive
- Other playful or routine-based techniques

The nanny does not automatically know these strategies. Parents must proactively share this information so the nanny can be as successful as the parent has been. This transfer of knowledge is essential for consistency and for building the nanny's confidence.

## Summary of Action Steps for Parents

1. **Help the nanny bond with your child** - Create opportunities for positive interactions and give them time together without hovering
2. **Create an open relationship with your nanny** - Make it clear that disclosure about challenges is welcome and expected, not punished
3. **Share your successful strategies** - Actively teach the nanny what works for your specific child in specific situations
4. **Support the nanny in setting limits** - Back up the nanny's authority and help her become comfortable with firmness
5. **Recognize that relationship comes first** - Cooperation will follow once the nanny-child bond is established

## Why This Matters

Without these foundations in place, children will continue to refuse to listen to the nanny. The problem is not the child being "bad" or the nanny being "ineffective" - the problem is that the conditions for cooperation have not been established. Addressing the relationship dynamics solves the behavioral issue at its root.
