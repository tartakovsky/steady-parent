# Helping an Angry Child When a Parent Has Anger Management Issues

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/angry-10-year-old-has-dad-with-anger-management-issues

---

## The Presenting Situation

A 10-year-old girl who was previously happy-go-lucky, positive, adventurous, and social has become increasingly irritable and angry. She no longer finds joy in usual activities, prefers to withdraw with a book, gets thrown off by small things, and has frequent anger outbursts. She says she is not happy, that "things are unfair," and that everyone is unfair to her - including her dad, her mom, her best friend, and a "mean" friend at school.

**Key background factors:**
- Father has anger management issues and has historically blamed the daughter for small and big things
- Mother admits she hasn't always been empathetic because she gets caught up in the stress
- Father is currently trying to control his issues but is losing patience with the daughter's behavior, creating risk of a vicious cycle
- The daughter is also dealing with social stressors: a best friend going through tough times and being angry, plus another friend who can be very mean

The mother has tried showing empathy, which works to a certain degree, but has also been telling the daughter she needs to "not let some things get to her." The mother recognizes that while she says the family can work through things, she ends up "dumping all the responsibility" on the daughter. The daughter says she feels blamed for everything.

---

## Why the Child's Anger is Actually a Positive Sign

**The daughter expressing her upset to her mother indicates trust and a desire to stay connected.** This is significantly better than the alternative - she could be acting out in ways that would be much more problematic. The fact that she is communicating her distress to her mother, even through anger, shows the relationship has a foundation to build on.

The mother should not try to talk the daughter out of her "strong reactions" because the reactions are valid responses to a genuinely difficult situation. The child's concerns deserve to be taken seriously, not minimized.

---

## How Children Learn Anger Management (or Don't)

**Children learn coping skills by observing others.** If a parent has anger management issues, the child is likely to develop them as well - not because there's something wrong with the child, but because that's the coping model they've been exposed to.

**Children learn both sides of any relationship they're in.** This means a child who experiences being on the receiving end of someone's anger may:
1. Develop similar anger patterns themselves
2. End up in relationships where they are on the receiving end of someone else's anger

The daughter's relationship with her "mean" friend may be reminiscent of her relationship with her father - she is recreating a familiar dynamic where she tolerates mistreatment.

---

## The Trap of Putting Responsibility on the Child

When parents tell a child to "take charge" of their life or "not let things get to them," this effectively dumps all the responsibility onto the child while the adults who created the environment escape accountability.

**The daughter cannot actually take charge of her life without assistance from her mother and father.** A 10-year-old does not have the power to change family dynamics, address a parent's anger issues, or transform her home environment on her own. Telling her to do so is setting her up for failure and making her feel blamed.

The daughter already says she "feels blamed for everything" - and she is correct in perceiving this. The father has historically blamed her for things, and the mother, despite good intentions, ends up in the same pattern when trying to help her "take control."

---

## Developmental Context: The Tenth Year

**Girls in their tenth year typically become more volatile as hormones begin to kick in.** However, this developmental reality has two important implications:

1. It does not make the child's concerns any less valid - the hormonal changes may amplify emotional responses, but they don't invalidate the underlying issues
2. It increases parental responsibility to practice calmness in the face of the child's upset, so the child can learn to stay calm themselves

The parent's job during this developmental stage is to model emotional regulation, not to match the child's volatility or dismiss it as "just hormones."

---

## Why Family Therapy is the Right Approach (Not Individual Therapy for the Child)

**This is fundamentally an issue of family dynamics and communication, not the child's adjustment.** Six specific reasons support recommending family therapy:

1. **The problem is systemic.** Family dynamics and communication patterns are at the root of this situation, not a defect in the child that needs fixing.

2. **Individual therapy would send the wrong message.** Taking the daughter to a therapist to help her "take charge" of her life would communicate that there is something wrong with her. This would make her more angry, not less.

3. **The child needs adult support to change anything.** A 10-year-old cannot take charge of her life without help from her parents. Individual therapy cannot provide what she actually needs.

4. **The father needs to hear the impact of his behavior.** Family therapy creates a structured setting where he can hear how his anger management issues are affecting his wife and child. This feedback is essential for change.

5. **Going to therapy together demonstrates commitment.** Even if the father refuses to participate, the mother attending therapy with her daughter shows the daughter that her mother is committed to her and to improving their relationship.

6. **Therapy will strengthen the mother-daughter bond before puberty.** Going to therapy together will solidify their bond and bring them closer right before the daughter enters the more turbulent teenage years. This strong relationship is described as "the best insurance policy she can have."

---

## Finding a Therapist - Practical Suggestions

Three avenues for finding an experienced family therapist:
- Church counselors (if applicable)
- Referrals from the daughter's school
- Referrals through health insurance plans that cover family therapy

The therapist should be experienced specifically in family therapy, not just any licensed counselor.

---

## Critical Safety Note

**If the father hits the daughter or the mother, the advice changes entirely: leave immediately.** The nuanced guidance about family therapy and working through issues only applies when there is no physical violence. Physical abuse requires immediate exit, not family therapy to improve communication.

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **Don't try to talk the child out of strong reactions.** The reactions are valid responses to a difficult situation.

2. **Don't tell the child to "not let things get to her."** This minimizes her experience and puts responsibility on her for managing an environment she didn't create and can't control.

3. **Don't take the child to individual therapy to help her "take charge."** This pathologizes the child and makes her feel something is wrong with her.

4. **Don't dump responsibility on the child while saying "we can work through things."** This is a contradiction - if it requires the child to do all the work, it's not actually "we."

5. **Don't match the child's volatility.** When a 10-year-old becomes more volatile due to developmental changes, the parent's responsibility is to practice calmness, modeling the regulation the child needs to learn.

---

## The Mother's Unique Position

**The mother may be the key to changing the entire family dynamic.** Her willingness to act now could change her daughter's life path. She has several advantages:
- Her daughter still trusts her and wants to stay connected (evidenced by the daughter expressing herself)
- She is questioning her own contribution to the problem (showing self-awareness)
- She has recognized that her attempts to help have backfired (creating opportunity for new approaches)
- She can take action even if her husband doesn't participate (going to therapy together with her daughter)

The mother's commitment and willingness to examine her own role - even when her husband has more obvious "anger management issues" - positions her to be the catalyst for change.
