# Shared Custody Visitation Schedules: Expert Analysis

Source: Dr. Laura Markham, Peaceful Parent Happy Kids

## The Fundamental Trade-off in Joint Custody

Joint custody creates an inherent tension between two competing sources of stress for children:

**Stress Factor #1: Separation from a beloved parent.** The child experiences this 100% of the time since they are always away from one parent. This stress can be lessened by more frequent transitions, keeping the child connected to both parents regularly.

**Stress Factor #2: The stress of constantly transitioning between households.** This stress can be lessened by fewer transitions.

These two factors directly conflict with each other. More frequent transitions reduce separation anxiety but increase transition stress. Less frequent transitions reduce transition stress but increase separation anxiety. This is the central dilemma parents must navigate.

## Why Joint Custody Exists Despite Being Stressful

Joint custody is better for kids than having one parent vanish from their lives. It is rare for a parent to stay meaningfully close to a child who does not live with them at least some of the time. Therefore, joint custody serves as a mechanism to keep both parents in meaningful relationship with the child.

However, joint custody is not an ideal situation. It is explicitly described as a **severe stressor and risk factor** for the child. Even when both parents are highly invested in making the arrangement work, the stress remains inherent to the situation.

## How the Balance Should Shift with Age

**For toddlers and young children:** More frequent transitions are typically chosen, even though transitions stress the child. The reasoning is that young children cannot handle long separations without it damaging the parent-child relationship. The relationship preservation takes priority.

**As children enter school age:** The stress from transitions often becomes more apparent. Signs include:
- Difficulty in school
- Problems remembering things from house to house
- Children becoming directly vocal about disliking transitions
- Even when children don't verbalize it, the stress is usually observable

**The developmental progression:** As kids get older and school becomes their primary focus, the cost-benefit analysis shifts toward longer stays with each parent. Teenagers often prefer two-week blocks with each parent.

**Critical age threshold:** Separations of more than 5-7 days are never recommended for a seven-year-old child. This sets an upper limit on how long transitions can be spaced for younger school-age children.

## The Split Week Arrangement

The split week arrangement is the most common arrangement once children reach school age. A typical structure: one parent has the children Sunday through Tuesday, the other Wednesday through Saturday.

**Requirements for this arrangement:**
- Parents must live close to each other within the same school district
- Best suited for cooperative former spouses
- Not recommended if there is significant parental conflict

**Potential benefits:** Children may feel secure because they truly have two parents and two homes.

**Potential drawbacks:** Going back and forth between two homes might be difficult for children and parents.

**Age recommendation:** This arrangement is better suited for children seven years of age and older. It doesn't usually work for young children.

**Individual assessment required:** The specific temperament, age, and needs of the child must be considered. Children must have the flexibility and resiliency to make frequent transitions.

**Warning about overprescription:** Studies show that this arrangement has been overprescribed and many couples have been pressured to adopt it. It is important to think about how well parents handle their differences and the actual needs of the children.

## Joan B. Kelly's Framework

**Joan B. Kelly** is recognized as an expert in custody arrangements. Her essential guidance: if your kid can't handle the transitions, do them less frequently. The key assessment is whether the child is having difficulty, though this is a matter of perception between parents.

## Additional Stress Factors Beyond the Core Trade-off

Beyond the separation and transition stress, several other factors can cause severe stress:

- **Loss of home feeling:** The child may feel they have no real home but are a visitor in both places
- **Continuity problems:** Difficulties keeping up with school, social activities, and friends
- **Inconsistent rules:** Different bedtimes, expectations, and routines at each home (example given: bedtime is 8:30 at Mom's but 10:00 at Dad's)
- **Homework management issues:** Assignments given while at one home but due when at the other can be inadvertently overlooked
- **Social fragmentation:** Friends are different at each home and harder to maintain
- **Caregiving inconsistency:** Different babysitters and routines

These factors can be lessened through parental communication, using mediation if necessary.

## Research on Children's Experience of Shared Custody

### Dr. Jennifer McIntosh's Research (Family Transitions)

Studied children's development in 130 high-conflict families. Findings:
- Children in shared care are more troubled, distressed, and anxious than children with more flexible arrangements
- These children had higher rates of hyperactivity compared to children with a stable home base with one parent

**Important caveat:** Most research is compromised by parental conflict being present. While studies show children having difficulty with transitions, it is always complicated by conflict between the parents, making it hard to isolate the effect of transitions alone.

### Carol Smart's Research (University of Leeds, CAVA Programme)

Longitudinal study asking children what it feels like to be shared. Key findings:

- Even where children had good relationships with both parents and felt shared residence was "a good thing," there were costs for them
- Children looked forward to a time when they could stop living like nomads
- Problems tended to increase as children got older and wanted to make their own social arrangements

### Liz Trinder's Research (University of East Anglia)

Findings:
- Even in the most harmonious post-divorce families, children tended to refer to one place as "home" and speak of visiting the other parent
- Children are happiest where it is clear that their needs, rather than the needs of their parents, take priority
- **Critical insight:** If children realized that each parent wanted 50% of them because they could not tolerate the idea that the other parent had more, they did not feel loved so much as like a possession to be fought over

## Guidelines for Elementary School Age Children

For younger children in this age group, frequent visitation (at least once per week) with their noncustodial parent is desirable. As a child matures, longer visitation with fewer transitions may be preferred.

**Recommendations for parents:**
- Establish and follow a predictable visitation routine
- Gradually change the frequency and increase the duration of visitation over time

## The Case Against Joint Physical Custody

A more critical view acknowledges that joint physical custody is not the ideal solution it was once thought to be. The child may be shuttled back and forth and have no real feeling of a "home."

**Specific challenges:**
- Consistency is difficult to achieve
- Rules differ at each parent's home
- Schoolwork can suffer due to logistics
- Friends differ at each home and are harder to maintain
- **Children who have difficulty adapting to change may find joint physical custody too chaotic**

**What makes it work (when it does):**
- Joint physical custody seldom reduces hostility between parents and may even increase it
- Requires two parents who maintain a commitment over time to put the needs of the child first
- Parents must create a conflict-free zone for their child
- Requires open and frequent communication between parents
- Both parents must be committed to being co-parents

## Practical Communication Safeguards

Children should ALWAYS be able to phone and email each parent at all times. This honors the connection to both parents and helps mitigate separation stress.

## Assessing Whether a Child Is Struggling

The key question is whether a particular child can handle the arrangement imposed by the parents. Every child is different and they all show stress differently. However:

- We know there is ALWAYS stress in joint custody situations
- We know that transitions exacerbate that stress
- Children becoming directly vocal about disliking transitions is an indicator
- Academic difficulties, forgetfulness between homes, and observable distress are signs

## When Parents Disagree About the Arrangement

When one parent wants to change the schedule and the other resists, a therapy or mediation session is recommended to work this out. The goal is to assess what arrangement truly serves the child's needs rather than the parents' preferences or fears.

## The Specific Case Discussed

The mother in this case had a 5-2-2 arrangement (5 days with dad, 5 days with mom, 2 days with dad, 2 days with mom) for a child who started at age 4 and was now 7.5 years old. She wanted to switch to week-on/week-off to reduce transitions from four per cycle to two.

**The father's objections:**
1. Not sure it would help the continuity problem
2. Doesn't want to be away from the child for that long

**The mother's proposed solution:** Allow the non-custodial parent to see the child during the other's week (for dinner, etc.) to address the separation concern.

**Dr. Laura's assessment:** The mother is on the right track. While there is no definitive "proof," two transitions per cycle instead of four would likely be helpful. The recommendation is to pursue therapy or mediation to work through the disagreement with the ex.
