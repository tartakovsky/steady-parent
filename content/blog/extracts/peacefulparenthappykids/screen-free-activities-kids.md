# Screen-Free Activities for Kids: Age-by-Age Guide

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/screen-free-activities-kids

---

## Core Concept

This guide provides developmentally appropriate activities that children can do with **minimal supervision**, organized by age group. The underlying principle is that children at different developmental stages have different capabilities, interests, and safety considerations, so activities must be matched appropriately.

A key design feature: **activities build cumulatively across age groups**. Each age group can enjoy activities from younger age groups, plus new activities appropriate to their developmental stage. This means older children have access to a much larger activity pool.

---

## Toddler Activities (Youngest Age Group)

Toddler activities emphasize **sensory exploration, fine motor development, and simple cause-and-effect experiences**. Many involve water play, pouring, threading, and basic art materials.

### Sensory and Water Play
- Explore ice cubes and pouring toys with water in a baby bathtub on the bathroom or kitchen floor
- Wash toys in a baby bathtub and dry them on a bath mat
- Pour rice from a plastic bottle or measuring cup into smaller cups in a baby bathtub
- Float toys in a baby bathtub and ladle them out onto a towel with a large slotted spoon
- Play with bubbles in the sink while standing in a safety tower
- Play with rainbow spaghetti (dyed cooked spaghetti for sensory exploration)

### Fine Motor Skill Development
- Poke pom-poms through an opening in a small bottle
- Thread cheerios or pasta shapes onto a piece of uncooked spaghetti stuck upright into clay
- Poke q-tips or colorful pipe cleaners into an egg carton with pre-poked holes
- Arrange clothespins on cardboard
- Drop balls through a hole in the top of a box
- String a necklace out of pasta

### Art and Creative Activities
- Use painters tape to completely cover a large toy, then unmask it after a parent admires it
- Line up animals in a parade on painter's tape
- Decorate a rock and make a house to keep it as a pet
- Draw Grandpa a picture
- Make a paper chain
- Cut magazines with toddler scissors
- Trace pictures on tracing paper
- Play with playdoh or clay
- Sit in a box and decorate the inside with washable markers
- Throw or stick small pieces of colored paper to a contact paper shape posted on the wall

### Practical Life Activities
- Match pairs of socks
- Use a sponge and bucket of soapy water to clean kitchen cabinets and floor
- Dig a hole in the backyard while parent is outside watching

### Rescue and Problem-Solving Activities
- "Rescue" plastic animals that parent has taped to cardboard

## Preschooler Activities

Preschoolers are ready for **more complex construction, social play with siblings, pretend play, and activities requiring more steps**. They can handle more independence than toddlers.

### Construction and Building
- Use old cardboard tubes and boxes to build a marble maze
- Make a curving line of dominoes and knock the first one to watch them fall
- Glue popsicle sticks together to make picture frames, then decorate
- Build a fort with blankets and pillows
- Use masking tape to make a race track for cars all over the living room

### Physical Play and Games
- Make an obstacle course in the hall with yarn and tape
- Blindfold your sibling and take them on a tour of house and yard, then trade places
- Bowl in the hallway with soda bottles or toilet paper tubes
- Put on music and dance
- Have an indoor "snowball" fight with sibling using socks
- Play Simon Says or Mother May I with sibling
- Play Tag or Freeze Tag
- Blow bubbles
- On a hot day, have a wet sponge toss in the backyard with clean sponges and two buckets of water
- Make a spiderweb in a doorway with painters tape and throw scrunched up newspaper balls at it to see how many stick

### Art and Craft Projects
- Cut out a crown, tape into a circle to fit head, and decorate
- Make a drawing of your family
- Use pipe cleaners to make animals
- Make a birthday card for the next person you know having a birthday
- Make paper bag puppets or sock puppets
- Cut out pictures from magazines and make a collage
- Make placemats (parents can laminate at local copy shop)
- Make your room into a rainforest by drawing plants and animals
- Paint sea shells or rocks
- Sit in a big box with markers and color the inside

### Pretend Play
- Make a zoo for stuffed animals
- Set up a shop and be the shopkeeper with play money you make

### Food Activities
- Make a peanut butter and banana sandwich
- Make a sculpture from pretzels and peanut butter

### Messy Play and Science
- Play at a sand table or with sand in a baby bathtub
- Mix ivory soap, kleenex, and water to make clean clouds on a cookie sheet
- Mix liquid hand soap, cornstarch, and food coloring into paint and paint the inside of the bathtub

### Character Building
- Do something kind for someone, in secret

### Self-Directed Planning
- Make a list of fun things you can do without a grownup

## Elementary Schooler Activities

Elementary schoolers are ready for **more complex projects, independent cooking, writing, and activities requiring sustained attention and multiple steps**. They can work on longer-term projects and handle more sophisticated materials.

### Complex Construction and Science
- Make a scene in a cardboard box of the ocean, or a jungle
- Make a boat using a plastic soda bottle base and popsicle sticks (use duct tape) for the top, then float it at the pond
- Make homemade slime or playdoh
- Cut a guitar out of cardboard and add rubber band strings
- Use a large container that fits in freezer to make a giant ice cube filled with plastic animals; use tools to excavate the ice and rescue them
- Use boxes to build a castle
- Use an eye dropper to drop vinegar tinted with food coloring onto a pie pan filled with baking soda
- Make snow globes or calming jars with glycerin and glitter
- Make a potion lab or pouring station outside with food coloring and containers (wear an apron)
- Make rock candy
- Plant a terrarium
- Use the hose, PVC pipe, and soda bottles to construct waterways in yard
- Use pipe cleaners to make an indoor ring toss game
- Use a basket and string to rig an elevator to hoist stuffed animals up stairwell
- Make and fill a bird feeder
- Use the hose and a tarp to make a slip and slide on lawn

### Writing and Literacy
- Make a book of jokes
- Write Grandma a letter
- Write down ten things you love about each person in your family to surprise them
- Learn a tongue twister
- Start a journal
- Write a story
- Create a family newspaper/newsletter
- Write the story of your life
- Write some limericks or haiku
- Memorize a poem to recite for parents at dinner

### Art and Creativity
- Draw a picture of a desert island with all the things you would want on it
- Cut out paper dolls and costumes for them
- Draw a tree
- Make a dollhouse out of cardboard
- Cut paper snowflakes
- Make paper airplanes and fly them
- Draw a cartoon
- Paint a picture
- Draw your own puzzle on poster board, color it and cut out the pieces
- Make homemade wrapping paper
- Use plain white paper and envelopes to decorate your own personalized stationery
- Cut up old holiday cards and make holiday stickers for next year by coating the back with gelatin glue, let dry (dissolve 2 tsp gelatin in 5 tsp boiling water)
- Make and decorate a calendar of the summer with important dates marked
- Hang a clothesline in your room and clip photos to it to make an art display
- Make "funky junk" art out of old jewelry
- Make puppets with old socks, buttons, and markers
- String beads to make friendship bracelets
- Decorate an old teeshirt with cool buttons and fabric pens

### Food and Cooking
- Make homemade ice cream in a baggie
- Surprise your mom by making lunch
- Put juice and cut-up fruit into ice cube trays to make ice cubes
- Make dessert
- Make popsicles

### Physical Activities
- Ride your bike
- See how many times you can dribble the basketball
- Play dodgeball with a soft ball
- Practice kicking a soccer ball
- Play hopscotch
- Play jumprope
- Have a water balloon fight (outside)
- Draw on the sidewalk with chalk

### Science and Discovery
- Get a magnet and make a list of everything in your house that is magnetized
- Use a ruler to measure things in your house, recording their length
- Find shapes in the clouds
- Plant some seeds

### Pretend Play and Performance
- Create a play with costumes
- Set up a restaurant and serve pretend meals
- Create a circus performance
- Create your own board game

### Pet and Nature Care
- Brush the dog
- Give the dog a bath
- Make a daisy chain
- Weed the garden
- Make a fairy house for your garden
- Make a windsock
- Make bean bags and play a toss game

### Organization and Planning
- Organize your room
- Plan a treasure hunt, with clues, to do later with your parent
- Start a collection (leaves, rocks, buttons) and make a museum display

### Games
- Learn a new card game
- Read a book
- Play a board game

### Household Help
- Wash the car
- Wrap your sibling up in toilet paper like a mummy

### Unique Art Challenges
- See if you can draw a picture with your foot
- Paint your toenails

### Self-Directed Planning
- Make a list of fun things you can do without a grown-up

## Middle Schooler (Preteen) Activities

Middle schoolers are ready for **more sophisticated projects, learning real-world skills, longer-term commitments, and activities that could become hobbies or even small businesses**. They can handle more responsibility and independence.

### Skill Development
- Learn to cook your favorite food
- Learn to play an instrument
- Learn a language
- Learn a new board game

### Creative Projects
- Write a song
- Make a giraffe, a spaceship, or something else out of papier mache
- Put on a play
- Make jewelry
- Make a video
- Build something from wood
- Record a "letter" to send to someone
- Draw a comic book
- Write a poem
- Tie dye a teeshirt

### Science and Nature
- Plant seeds in a window box or start a garden outside
- Put together a terrarium or aquarium
- Start a Science Journal where you write questions and then record observations and discoveries
- Get glow-in-the-dark stars and make constellations on your ceiling

### Responsibility and Service
- Make dinner for your family
- Clean out your closet
- Every day for a month, do a random act of kindness
- Wash the car
- Hire yourself out to your neighbor as a mother's helper once a week
- Organize a bike and vehicle wash for neighborhood kids (can social distance)

### Recreation and Adventure
- Play ping pong (no table? play against the wall)
- Build a fort or treehouse
- Camp out in your yard
- Go roller-blading

### Extended Reading and Learning
- Read every book by an author you like
- Read a book

### Self-Directed Planning
- Make a list of fun things you can do without a grown-up

## Key Patterns and Principles

### Cumulative Skill Building
The guide explicitly states that older children "will love many of the activities" from younger age groups. This means:
- Preschoolers can do toddler activities plus preschool activities
- Elementary schoolers can do preschool activities plus their own
- Middle schoolers can do elementary school activities plus their own

This gives older children access to a much wider variety of activities and acknowledges that simpler activities remain enjoyable at older ages.

### Recurring Activity Types Across Ages
Certain activity categories appear at every age level, with increasing complexity:

**Water and sensory play** progresses from supervised bath tub play (toddler) to elaborate outdoor water systems (elementary).

**Art projects** progress from tracing and coloring (toddler) to making personalized stationery and comic books (elementary/middle school).

**Pretend play** progresses from simple animal parades (toddler) to elaborate plays with costumes (elementary).

**Self-directed planning** - every age group ends with "make a list of fun things you can do without a grown-up," teaching children to generate their own ideas.

### Materials Commonly Used
The guide relies heavily on common household materials:
- Painter's tape and masking tape
- Cardboard boxes and tubes
- Baby bathtubs (used as contained water/sensory play stations)
- Food items for crafts (pasta, cheerios, spaghetti, peanut butter)
- Basic art supplies (markers, paper, scissors, pipe cleaners)
- Household items for repurposing (socks, magazines, old jewelry)

### Safety Considerations
Toddler activities explicitly mention parental proximity ("while your parent is on the phone but outside watching"). Older age groups have activities that assume less supervision but still avoid inherently dangerous materials or locations.

### Social Development Integration
Many activities involve siblings, from blindfolded house tours (preschool) to sock snowball fights (preschool) to wrapping siblings in toilet paper (elementary). This acknowledges that screen-free time often involves multiple children.

### Character and Service Emphasis
Multiple age groups include acts of kindness:
- Preschool: "Do something kind for someone, in secret"
- Elementary: "Write down ten things you love about each person in your family"
- Middle school: "Every day for a month, do a random act of kindness"

This integrates character development into activity planning.

### Entrepreneurship and Real-World Skills (Middle School)
Middle school activities include real-world skill development:
- "Hire yourself out to your neighbor as a mother's helper once a week"
- "Organize a bike and vehicle wash for neighborhood kids"

This acknowledges that preteens are ready to start learning about work and service in their communities.
