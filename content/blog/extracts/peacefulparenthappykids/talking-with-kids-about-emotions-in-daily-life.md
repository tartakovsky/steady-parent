# Teaching Kids About Emotions in Daily Life

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/talking-with-kids-about-emotions-in-daily-life

---

## Core Principle: Children Learn Emotions Through Contextual Exposure

The article opens with an illustrative comparison: Inuit children reportedly learn 50+ words for different types of snow simply by hearing these words used in context during daily life. Whether or not this specific claim is accurate, the underlying principle holds true for all children: **children learn to correctly use the words they hear in their culture and family**.

This means parents who talk about a wide range of emotions give their children the vocabulary and framework to understand both their own emotions and those of other people. This understanding and acceptance of emotions is the foundational first step in learning to regulate them. You cannot regulate what you cannot identify and name.

## The Feelings Wheel: A Framework for Emotional Vocabulary

**Dr. Gloria Willcox** created the **Feelings Wheel**, which visually illustrates the wealth of emotions available to humans. This tool can be helpful for parents who want to expand beyond basic emotion words.

However, if the idea of dozens of emotions feels overwhelming, parents can simplify by starting with just four basic emotions and going deeper as the child is ready.

## The Four Basic Emotions Framework

### Happiness
- Includes: **love**, **joy**, and **peace**
- This is described as our natural state, the feeling when we're in flow

### Fear
- A reaction to threat
- Includes: **terror**, **anxiety** (fear of an unspecified threat), **worry** (fear of a specific threat), and the feeling of being powerless or defenseless
- **Important pattern**: When mammals feel fear, they often shift into **anger** as a defense mechanism. This means what looks like anger may actually be fear underneath.

### Sadness
- A reaction to loss and disappointment
- Includes: **grief**, **depression**, and **loneliness**
- **Important pattern**: Many people defend against disappointment and sadness by becoming **angry**. Again, anger can be a mask for deeper sadness.

### Anger
- A reaction to threat from within or without
- Includes: **irritation**, **frustration**, and **rage**
- **Important pattern**: When anger is not heard or acknowledged, the person may turn it inward, which can become **depression** or **numbness**

### Key Insight About Anger
Anger appears in two different patterns as a secondary emotion:
1. Anger as a defense against fear (mammals shift to anger when threatened)
2. Anger as a defense against sadness (people become angry to avoid feeling disappointed or sad)

This means when you see anger in a child, it's worth considering what might be underneath - fear or sadness that the child is defending against.

## The Teaching Method: Observation and Nonjudgmental Commentary

The core method for teaching children about emotions is simple: observe what your child and others are feeling, and comment on it in a **nonjudgmental, accepting way**. This teaches children to identify emotions in themselves and others.

### Example Phrases for Acknowledging Feelings
- "You look frustrated."
- "You're jumping up and down! You must be excited!"
- "I understand. You feel safer when you know exactly what's going to happen. Me, too."
- "I hear you! You really don't like spinach and you wish you could never see it again!"

Notice that these examples:
- Name the emotion directly
- Connect observable behavior to internal states ("jumping up and down" = "excited")
- Validate the feeling without judgment
- Show empathy by relating to the child's experience ("Me, too")
- Reflect back the child's perspective without criticism, even for preferences the parent might not share (the spinach example)

## Anti-Pattern: Avoid Lecturing

When talking with children about emotions, **resist the urge to lecture**. Lecturing is less effective than helping children learn through their own reflection.

## Better Approach: Ask Questions to Prompt Reflection

Instead of telling children what to do with their emotions, ask questions that help them think it through themselves.

### Questions About Handling Anger in Different Contexts
- "If you felt angry at a friend, what could you do?"
- "If you felt angry at me, what could you do?"
- "If you felt angry that your block tower fell down, what could you do?"

Notice how these questions present the same emotion (anger) but in different contexts - with a friend, with a parent, with an inanimate situation. This helps children understand that different contexts may call for different responses.

### Questions About Self-Awareness and Self-Regulation
- "Do you make a better decision when you feel angry, or when you feel calm?"
- "What helps you calm down when you're angry?"

These questions help children develop metacognition - thinking about their own thinking and emotional states. They guide children toward self-awareness about how emotions affect decision-making and what strategies work for them personally.

## Building Empathy Through Observation of Others

When you and your child observe another child who is upset (for example, crying), use questions to develop empathy:
- "That child looks so unhappy. I wonder why he's upset?"
- "What do you think he wants/needs?"
- "Is there anything we can do to help?"

### Research-Backed Application: Sibling Relationships
When parents wonder aloud to their young child about what their baby sibling thinks, feels, and wants, two positive outcomes occur:
1. The older child develops more empathy for their sibling
2. The relationship between the two siblings becomes more positive

This shows that the questioning technique has measurable effects on children's empathic development.

## Using Books to Teach Emotional Literacy

Reading books about children feeling and expressing emotions is another effective method for teaching emotional literacy.

### Research Finding
When adults read books and talk to toddlers and preschoolers about how other children feel:
- Their prosocial (positive) actions toward their peers increase
- Their aggression toward their peers decreases

This suggests that simply exposing children to stories about emotions and discussing them has a real impact on behavior, not just understanding.

## The Naming-to-Taming Connection

The article concludes with a key distinction: while naming snow doesn't help you control or tame it, **naming emotions does help children learn to manage their behavior**.

When a child begins talking about their emotions, they're taking the first step in learning to manage their behavior. The causal chain is:
1. Parents talk about emotions in positive, accepting ways
2. Children learn to recognize emotions
3. Children learn to articulate (name) emotions
4. Naming emotions enables children to manage their behavior

This is described as happening even with young children when parents consider emotion "part of a rich human life" and discuss emotions in positive ways.
