# Intervening in Sibling Fights: A Conflict Resolution Framework

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - "A Script To Intervene In a Sibling Fight"

---

## The Core Principle: Parents as a "Holding Environment"

Research shows that one of the most important things parents can do to help children learn to manage their emotions is to **stay calm themselves**. Children need to experience their parents as a "holding environment" - a safe harbor in the storm of their turbulent feelings. When parents can stay calm and soothe their children, the children eventually learn to stay calm themselves. This is the first step in children learning to manage their feelings.

The parent's emotional state is not incidental - it is the foundation upon which children build their own emotional regulation capacity. The mechanism works through modeling and co-regulation: children borrow their parent's calm nervous system until they develop the neural circuitry to regulate themselves.

---

## Decision Point: When to Intervene

Before intervening, the parent should assess the situation. The key considerations are:

1. **Severity**: Does it sound explosive or manageable?
2. **Capacity**: Are the children likely to work it out themselves this time?
3. **Teaching opportunity**: Is this a good moment to teach better conflict resolution skills?

Not every sibling conflict requires intervention. Sometimes children work things out on their own. However, when the situation sounds explosive, or when it presents a valuable teaching opportunity, intervention is warranted.

Preparation before entering: Take a deep breath and consciously remind yourself to stay calm. This is not passive - it is active emotional preparation for what you are about to enter.

---

## Critical Anti-Pattern: Do Not Play Detective

**Never try to figure out who started it, who provoked whom, or who hit whom first.** This approach has two problems:

1. **It never works** - Children's accounts of "who started it" are always self-serving and contradictory. You will never reach a satisfying conclusion.

2. **It creates victims and bullies** - When you determine who is "at fault," you assign one child the role of victim and the other the role of bully. This crystallizes identity roles that children then internalize and act out.

**Instead: Always treat both children the same when you intervene.** This means each child gets equal airtime, equal empathy, and equal accountability. Neither child is positioned as the wronged party or the wrongdoer.

## The Intervention Sequence

### Step 1: Enter with Observation, Not Judgment

Begin by naming what you observe without assigning blame or taking sides:

> "I hear some loud, angry voices. What's going on?"

This opening accomplishes several things:
- Signals that you are present and aware
- Does not prejudge the situation
- Invites both children to share their perspectives

### Step 2: Empathize with Both Children Simultaneously

After hearing initial complaints from both sides, acknowledge both children's emotional states:

> "You two sound really upset!"

This validates that both children have feelings that matter, before any attempt to problem-solve. The validation must come first because children cannot engage their thinking brains while their emotional brains are overwhelmed.

### Step 3: State the Family Rule and Create Structure

When emotions escalate further (for example, children saying "I hate you"), the parent takes a deep breath to maintain calm and sets a limit by invoking the family standard:

> "The rule in our house is that we treat each other with kindness and respect. I hear screaming and hurtful language."

Then immediately create physical and procedural structure:

> "Let's all sit down. Come on Charley, sit down right here next to me. Jane, right here on my other side."

**The seating arrangement matters**: Both children sit next to the parent, not facing each other as adversaries. The parent is literally in the middle, serving as a buffer and mediator.

### Step 4: Calm Down Together with Breathing

Lead the children through a calming exercise:

> "Now, let's everyone take three deep breaths so we can calm down and listen to each other... one... two... three."

This serves multiple purposes:
- Activates the parasympathetic nervous system to reduce fight-or-flight arousal
- Models emotional regulation in action
- Creates a shared ritual that signals "now we are transitioning to problem-solving mode"
- Gives both children's nervous systems a chance to settle before thinking is required

### Step 5: Structured Turn-Taking for Perspectives

Announce the structure clearly before beginning:

> "I want to hear what's upsetting each of you so much. One at a time. Last time, Charley went first. This time, Jane goes first."

**Key structural elements**:
- Alternating who goes first prevents perceived favoritism
- Explicit turn-taking rules prevent interrupting and talking over each other
- The parent enforces the structure consistently: "One at a time. Jane, it's Charley's turn to talk now. You'll get your turn again in a moment."

### Step 6: Active Listening and Reflection Back

As each child speaks, the parent reflects back what they heard, adding empathic acknowledgment:

> "Charley knocked down your zoo and you're really mad, huh? I see all the blocks and animals all over..."

Then probe for completeness: "Jane, anything else happen?"

This does several things:
- Confirms to the child that they have been heard accurately
- Slows down the pace, giving emotions time to settle
- Models what good listening looks like
- Draws out the full story rather than just the headline grievance

### Step 7: Summarize Each Perspective Back to the Child

After hearing a child's full account, summarize it back to confirm understanding:

> "Let me see if I got this right. Charley, you were very angry that Jane went into your room and played with your lego ships. Then she called them stupid and hurt your feelings. Then she told you to get out of her room. Is that right?"

This step is crucial because:
- The child confirms or corrects the parent's understanding
- It demonstrates to the child that their perspective has been heard and understood
- It slows everything down further, allowing more emotional processing
- It models careful listening that the children can eventually do for each other

## Why This Process Works: The Four Outcomes

Going through this structured listening process accomplishes four important developmental goals:

### 1. Each child feels heard
When children feel unheard, they escalate. When they feel heard, they can begin to calm down and think. The listening process itself is therapeutic.

### 2. Reflection enables good judgment
Good judgment comes from experience, but **only when combined with reflection**. By narrating back what happened, the parent helps each child see how acting on their anger got them into this situation. Without reflection, children just experience conflict after conflict without learning anything.

### 3. Hearing the other side builds empathy and social intelligence
Children develop understanding of others' motivations by literally hearing the other person's perspective explained. This builds the social intelligence to anticipate how others might react.

### 4. Processing emotions verbally builds impulse control
Both children are furious, but they are sitting, breathing through their rage, and putting it into words. **This helps their brains process the emotions rather than just lashing out, and builds the neural circuits to regulate their anger in the future.** The verbal processing is literally building new brain wiring.

## Phase 2: Teaching Empathy and Reflection

After both perspectives have been heard, the parent moves from listening into teaching. This has two components:

### Component A: Naming the Emotional Pattern

Help children see the common pattern in their experience:

> "I hear how angry you both are... You're both hurt that your things got messed up, right? Often when we're hurt, we get mad, right? So you both got pretty mad and blamed each other, right?"

This names a universal human pattern: hurt leads to anger leads to blaming. By naming it, the parent helps children recognize this pattern in themselves and others.

### Component B: Perspective-Taking Exercise

Ask each child to imagine what the other was feeling:

> "Now I want each of you to imagine what the other one was feeling during the fight. Jane, what do you think Charley was feeling when he came in your room?"

Then follow up with a causal question:

> "Yes... and when he talked to you, did that make him feel better?"

This helps children see the cause-and-effect chain of the interaction - how their words and actions affected the other person's emotional state.

Continue tracing the escalation:

> "Jane, what do you think happened inside Charley when he came into your room?"

The child then narrates: "I called his legos stupid... and he got madder... and I told him to leave... and he got madder..."

This makes visible the escalation ladder that the child was climbing without awareness in the heat of the moment.

---

## Phase 3: Identifying Personal Agency

After understanding the other person's experience, shift to personal responsibility with this key question:

> "Do you think there was anything different you could have done?"

If the child deflects ("But he plays with my things too"), redirect gently but firmly:

> "Right now we're talking about what just happened. Is there anything you could have done to make things go differently?"

If the child deflects again ("He's the one who knocked down my zoo!"), persist:

> "Yes, he did. But right now I'm wondering if you see anything you had the power to do that would have made things unfold differently."

**The key phrase is "had the power to do."** This reframes the question from "who was wrong" to "what agency did you have." It is not about blame - it is about recognizing one's own influence over outcomes.

Eventually the child identifies their contribution: "I could have been nicer and apologized. I didn't have to call his legos stupid."

### Validation Through the Other Child

Confirm the insight by checking with the other child:

> "So you think that if you had apologized when he first came in, he might not have gotten so mad?... Charley, if Jane had done that, would that have changed how you felt?"

The other child confirms: "I would still have been mad that she messed up my legos. But I wouldn't have knocked down her zoo."

This is powerful because:
- The insight is validated by the other party, not just the parent
- It shows that de-escalation was possible - the outcome was not inevitable
- It demonstrates that listening and "making things better" helps the other person control themselves

The parent names this explicitly:

> "He was mad you played with his legos, but if he thought you were listening and making things better, he would have had an easier time controlling himself."

This teaches an important social truth: when people feel heard, they regulate better. Your behavior affects others' capacity for self-control.

---

## Phase 4: Repair

The next step (not fully detailed in the source) is to ask each child:

> "Is there anything you can do to repair things with the other child?"

The goal is for the children to move from understanding to action, potentially working together to fix what was broken - both physically (rebuilding the zoo) and relationally.

---

## Contrast with Punishment-Based Intervention

The source explicitly contrasts this approach with "the more usual parental intervention" where the offending child (or both children) would be yelled at and given a "consequence" to punish them, with the hope that punishment would motivate them to be nicer to each other.

**The rhetorical question posed**: Would sibling resentment and fighting be likely to increase or decrease after punishment?

The implied answer: Punishment increases resentment. It does not teach skills. It does not build empathy. It does not help children understand what happened or what they could do differently. It just adds another layer of negative emotion to an already negative situation.

## Realistic Expectations and Long-Term Outcomes

**You cannot do this every time.** This process takes significant time and energy. In the example, dinner is delayed. The parent had to stop cooking and give full attention to the conflict.

**But you do not have to do it every time.** If you intervene this way consistently for a few months, children begin to learn the skills being taught:
- Calming down before talking
- Taking turns speaking
- Listening to the other person's perspective
- Identifying their own contribution to the problem
- Finding ways to repair

Eventually, children begin to work things out themselves, sometimes with minimal prompting from the parent. The investment in teaching pays off as the skills become internalized.

**The broader family benefit**: Everyone in the family becomes calmer and a better listener once this habit takes hold - including the parent.

---

## Summary of Key Practices

1. **Prepare yourself emotionally** before entering the conflict - take a breath, remind yourself to stay calm
2. **Enter with observation, not judgment** - "I hear loud voices" not "What did you do?"
3. **Never try to determine who started it** - this creates victim/bully dynamics
4. **Treat both children the same** when intervening
5. **Empathize with both children** before problem-solving
6. **Create physical structure** - sit down together, parent in middle
7. **Use breathing to transition** to problem-solving mode
8. **Enforce turn-taking strictly** - one person talks at a time
9. **Reflect back what you hear** to confirm understanding
10. **Help children see the escalation pattern** - what happened inside each person
11. **Ask what each could have done differently** - focus on personal agency, not blame
12. **Validate insights through the other child** - let them confirm that a different approach would have changed things
13. **Move toward repair** - what can be done now to make things better
14. **Be consistent over months** - skills take time to internalize but eventually children can self-manage
