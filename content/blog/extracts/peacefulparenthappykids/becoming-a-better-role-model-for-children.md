# How To Heal Yourself To Be a Better Role Model for Your Children

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/becoming-a-better-role-model-for-children

---

## The Central Insight: Emotional Regulation Is the Root Issue

The core premise is that most parenting struggles - control issues, grudge-holding, negative attitudes - stem from a single underlying problem: the inability to regulate one's own emotions. These surface behaviors are symptoms, not root causes.

**Fear causes control issues.** When parents feel afraid (of losing control, of things going wrong, of their child's future), they grip tighter and struggle to say "yes" to their children's requests.

**Hurt, fear, and sadness make positive attitudes impossible.** You cannot fake positivity when you're drowning in painful emotions. The feelings are so unbearable that lashing out becomes the escape valve.

**Grudge-holding is deflected emotion.** When we cannot tolerate our own painful feelings, we externalize them by blaming others. Holding onto negative thoughts about someone else ("it's all their fault") is a defense mechanism against feeling our own hurt. A grudge is essentially frozen anger that protects us from vulnerable emotions underneath.

This means that fixing the surface behaviors (trying harder to say yes, trying to stop holding grudges, trying to be more positive) won't work. You must heal the underlying emotional patterns.

---

## The Healing Process: Feel Without Acting

The method for healing is deceptively simple in concept but difficult in practice: **hold yourself with compassion while feeling painful emotions, without taking action.**

### Step-by-Step Process

**1. Drop the storyline.** When you're upset, your mind wants to rehearse WHY you're upset - what they did, why it was wrong, how unfair it is. This mental narrative takes you into your head and AWAY from healing. The storyline is a trap because it feels productive but actually prevents emotional processing.

**2. Notice the sensation in your body.** Shift attention from thoughts to physical experience. Where is the emotion living in your body? Common locations:
- Tightness in the throat
- Heaviness or numbness in the heart
- Tension in the belly

**3. Breathe into it.** Don't try to change the sensation. Just breathe and stay present with it.

**4. Love yourself through it.** Don't add self-blame on top of the painful emotion. Instead, offer yourself compassion. Hug yourself. Remind yourself this will pass and you're safe.

**5. Allow physical discharge.** As you process, the body may release emotion through:
- Crying
- Sweating
- Trembling
- Yawning
- Feeling like you might throw up

These are healthy signs of emotional discharge, not something to suppress.

**6. Notice the shift.** If you stay with the process, the sensations begin to change. The throat loosens. The heart feels lighter. This is the emotion "delivering its message" and fading.

### Why This Works

Emotions are messengers. They carry information stored in the body. When you actually feel an emotion - really let yourself experience it without running away or attacking - it delivers its message and naturally dissipates. Emotions that are pushed down or acted out never complete this cycle, so they stay stuck in the body, waiting for another trigger.

Another framing: this process is like shining a light into the dark basement of your psyche. You have memories and emotional patterns stored in the shadows. Simply bringing loving awareness to them - witnessing them without judgment - allows them to dissolve.

## The Observer Self: Your Tool for Healing

A key concept is the **observer self** - the part of you that can watch your emotional experience without being consumed by it. When you're flooded with anger or fear, there's still a part of you that can notice "I'm feeling angry right now."

This observer self:
- Has access to your deeper equilibrium and wisdom
- Acts as a "compassionate witness" to your pain
- Is the part "holding the light"
- Creates the separation needed to process rather than react

The noticing itself is therapeutic. The act of observation loosens the tight grip of the emotion. You create space between stimulus and response.

---

## The Three Defense Mechanisms to Resist

When painful emotions arise, the body urgently wants to avoid them through three patterns:

**Flight or Freeze:** Suddenly, urgently needing to find a screen or food. The body is trying to numb out or escape the discomfort.

**Fight:** Starting to think about how this is someone else's fault. Blame is a form of action - it redirects the emotional energy outward so you don't have to feel it.

Even mental blaming (holding a grudge in your thoughts) counts as taking action. It's a subtle way of deflecting the raw emotional experience.

The goal is to resist ALL these escape routes - not act, not numb, not blame - and instead stay present with the sensation.

---

## The Cumulative Effect of Practice

Each time you don't act when triggered, the triggers lose power. This is gradual but real. Over time:
- You feel more free, less reactive
- Grudges become harder to hold because you're not accumulating frozen anger
- Saying "yes" becomes easier because fear isn't driving your responses
- Your sense of humor returns because you're not weighed down by unprocessed emotion
- You think more positively (this happens naturally, not through force)
- You get triggered less often because fewer things activate old wounds
- You become better at helping your child process their emotions because you understand the process from the inside

## Practical Support Strategies

**Daily inspiration.** Find something to read or listen to daily that keeps you on track. Parenting transformation isn't a one-time insight - it requires ongoing reinforcement. Podcasts, emails, books that you return to regularly can serve this function.

**Meditation.** Research shows meditation physically changes the brain and reduces reactivity. This isn't just about feeling calmer in the moment - it's structural change that makes regulation easier over time.

**Professional help.** This work is very hard. Many people find it too difficult to do alone and benefit from working with a therapist or coach. There's no shame in needing support for what amounts to rewiring your nervous system.

**Reading in small doses.** Books that keep you positive can be read "here and there" rather than cover to cover - dipping in when you need a reset.

---

## Timeline and Expectations

This process takes years, not weeks. You will discover triggers gradually, one by one, and deactivate them through repeated practice. But you can start immediately - the very next time you get triggered, you can choose to breathe through it instead of reacting.

The work is ongoing but becomes easier. As you heal more, you have fewer triggers. Life gets lighter.

---

## The Philosophical Frame: Love Versus Fear

Every moment presents a choice between acting from love or acting from fear. The practice described above is essentially the practice of choosing love:
- When fear tells you to control, choosing love means allowing
- When hurt tells you to hold a grudge, choosing love means releasing
- When pain tells you to lash out, choosing love means staying present

The claim is that each time we resist acting from fear/hurt/anger and instead feel those feelings and heal them, we allow more love to enter. This isn't just personal healing - it's described as ushering more love into the world.

The final distillation: **Choose love.** Every minute, make that choice again.

---

## Warnings and Anti-patterns

**Don't try to fix surface behaviors directly.** Working on "being more positive" or "saying yes more" without addressing the underlying emotional patterns is putting the cart before the horse. You cannot will yourself into different reactions when unprocessed emotions are driving the bus.

**Don't engage with the storyline.** Analyzing why you're upset feels productive but prevents healing. The mind's explanations are a defense mechanism, not a path forward.

**Don't blame yourself.** Adding self-criticism to painful emotions makes them harder to process. Compassion is required - you cannot shame yourself into healing.

**Don't expect quick results.** The urgency to "fix" yourself quickly is itself a form of not accepting where you are. This work unfolds over years.

**Don't go it alone if you're struggling.** Seeking professional help isn't failure - it's recognizing that this is genuinely difficult work that most people cannot do in isolation.
