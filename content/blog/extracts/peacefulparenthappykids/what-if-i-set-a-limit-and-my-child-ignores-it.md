# What if you set a limit and your child ignores it?

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/what-if-i-set-a-limit-and-my-child-ignores-it

## Key Concepts

### The Foundation of Effective Limit-Setting
- Children need limits and expectations throughout the day
- Children also need support to meet those expectations
- When children feel understood, they are more willing to cooperate even with requests they dislike
- Empathy is the key to setting limits effectively

### Three-Step Limit-Setting Process
1. **Set the limit** - State the boundary clearly
2. **Offer empathy** - Show understanding of why the child is doing the behavior
3. **Redirect** - Tell the child what they CAN do instead

## Eight Strategies When Children Ignore Limits

### 1. Evaluate Whether the Limit Is Reasonable
- Listen to your child to learn important information
- Consider whether adjustments could meet the child's need for autonomy
- Example: Child ready to hold onto your bag instead of holding your hand when crossing the street

### 2. Be Firm on Essential Limits
- Children naturally test limits to understand where boundaries truly are
- Inconsistency (waffling) invites continued pushing
- Clarity gives children freedom to grieve the limit and eventually accept it

### 3. Connect Before Correcting or Redirecting
- Move physically close to your child
- Use touch and make a connecting comment
- Avoid giving instructions from across the room
- Connect first, then set your limit

### 4. Say It Once
- Repeating yourself trains children to ignore you until you raise your voice
- If the child doesn't respond, you haven't truly connected and gotten their attention
- Return to connection (step 3) and make eye contact
- Children want to connect with parents who reach out warmly (not from a hostile approach)

### 5. Maintain Your Position
- Show through action that you are serious about the limit
- If you're willing to be flexible, state that explicitly with parameters
- Giving in after setting a limit trains children to ignore future requests
- Approach with friendly determination rather than hostility

### 6. Empathize with Their Perspective
- Acknowledge what the child wishes they could do
- Allow children to have their feelings about the limit (crying, rage)
- Children must comply with the limit but are allowed emotional responses
- Sometimes children know you're right but need you to understand their sacrifice

### 7. Manage Your Own Emotions
- Stay calm and kind while maintaining the limit
- Avoid any punitive approach
- Angry insistence creates resistance
- Children accept expectations when parents regulate their own emotions
- Find ways to support the child in working with you
- Offer flexibility where possible to help them cope

### 8. Maintain the Emotional Bond
- Children need to experience you as looking out for their best interests
- If children perceive limits as arbitrary or unfair, they reject both empathy and limits
- When children trust your intentions, they accept limits and eventually internalize them

## Core Principles

1. **Empathy enables compliance** - Understanding the child's perspective increases cooperation
2. **Connection requires proximity** - Physical closeness and eye contact establish true connection
3. **Consistency teaches boundaries** - Firm limits teach children what boundaries really are
4. **Self-regulation is contagious** - Children adopt parents' emotional management through modeling
5. **Relationship is the foundation** - A strong bond makes children want to cooperate

## On Punishment vs. Repair

- No transgression deserves punishment
- Larger transgressions indicate greater disconnection requiring more parental support
- There may be need for relationship repair or replacement of damaged items
- Help the child find solutions themselves once calm (this empowers rather than punishes)

## The Three Big Ideas of Peaceful Parenting

1. **Regulate your own emotions**
2. **Connect with your child**
3. **Coach instead of threatening and punishing** - Set limits firmly with understanding while helping children process their emotional responses

## Outcomes of This Approach

- Children learn to regulate their own emotions because parents model regulation
- Children take expectations seriously because parents do
- Children feel connected even while being guided, creating intrinsic motivation to cooperate
- Children cooperate with limits, making parenting less stressful
