# Nine Options To Transform "Bad" Behavior Through Empathic Limits

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

---

## Core Insight: There Are No "Brats," Only Struggling Children

The foundational principle underlying everything in this approach is that **there is no such thing as a brat, only a child who is struggling**. This reframe is essential because it shifts how parents interpret challenging behavior.

When a child acts "bratty" or provocatively - deliberately doing what they know they shouldn't - this behavior is actually a signal. The child is communicating one of three things:

1. **They need a stronger connection with the parent** - The relationship bond has weakened and needs repair
2. **They have big feelings they need help processing** - Emotions are building up and driving the behavior
3. **They need tailored support to meet expectations** - The expectation is reasonable but the child lacks the capacity to meet it without help

This matters because children fundamentally want to protect their relationship with their parents above almost anything else - *as long as it doesn't compromise their own integrity*. When a child repeatedly defies you, they're not being malicious; they're signaling distress in the only way they know how.

## The Nine Options Framework

The article presents nine potential responses to challenging behavior, evaluating each for effectiveness. These range from counterproductive to highly effective.

### Option 1: Ignore the Behavior

**When it works**: Ignoring is appropriate for temporary issues that have a clear, addressable cause. For example, if a child is acting out because they're extremely hungry, you can acknowledge the behavior non-judgmentally ("You are so hungry, you're getting very impatient... Let's calm down and get you some food!") while addressing the underlying need. The child returns to their normal self once the need is met.

**When it fails**: Ignoring does not work when a child is repeatedly testing limits. If your child keeps jumping on the couch with shoes on despite knowing the rule, ignoring this behavior doesn't help because *she's asking you to intervene to help her*. The repetitive limit-testing is itself a communication - the child needs engagement, not avoidance.

### Option 2: Scream and Shout (The Parental Tantrum)

**Why it fails**: Screaming is never an effective long-term tactic for enforcing expectations. Its only "effectiveness" is that it scares the child into immediate compliance through fear.

**The relationship cost**: In adult relationships, when someone indulges in a tantrum, it erodes the relationship. The same is true with children. This erosion actually causes children to act out *more* over time, not less - the opposite of what the parent intended.

**What it signals**: Screaming is a symptom that the parent has "slipped onto the low road of parenting" - into fight-or-flight mode where the child is perceived as the enemy. But children are never the enemy, no matter how ugly their behavior. They are young humans with immature brains who signal their need for help in the only ways they can.

### Option 3: Timeout

**Short-term effect**: Timeout can stop the specific "bratty" behavior immediately.

**Why it "works" in the moment**: Timeout works because it is a **symbolic abandonment**. The child needs the parent's presence to survive - this is a deep biological reality. Putting the child in timeout is essentially a threat: "At any time I might decide to withdraw my love and presence, leaving you unprotected." The child complies out of fear of abandonment.

**The hidden message**: Timeout tells the child that the parent is not there to help with the upsetting feelings driving the behavior. The child is left alone with their distress.

**Long-term problems**:
- **Power struggles**: Most children aren't compliant enough to go willingly to timeout, creating power struggles that degrade the overall relationship
- **Decreasing effectiveness**: Timeout stops working as children get older
- **Resentment over cooperation**: Instead of a child who WANTS to cooperate, you get a resentful child in rebellion
- **Behavior displacement**: Timeout doesn't actually stop bad behavior overall. It might stop the specific behavior being interrupted, but because the child ends up feeling angry, they start acting out in other ways

**The future cost**: The teen years "won't be pretty" for parents who rely on timeout, because they've built a relationship based on compliance through fear rather than genuine connection and cooperation.

### Option 4: High Expectations for Behavior

**Yes, this works** - but with critical conditions.

High expectations are effective when they are:
- **Age-appropriate** - The expectation must be something the child is developmentally capable of meeting
- **Constantly enforced** - The expectation must be consistent
- **Cheerfully enforced** - The enforcement happens without anger or resentment
- **Empathically enforced** - The enforcement includes understanding of the child's perspective

**When high expectations aren't enough**: If the child knows the age-appropriate expectation and still doesn't meet it, this signals one of two things:
1. The child needs help with tangled-up feelings that are keeping them from cooperating
2. The child needs a better connection with the parent so they WANT to cooperate

High expectations alone are insufficient without the relational and emotional foundations.

### Option 5: Tailored Support So the Child Can Meet Expectations

This option addresses what children fundamentally need to feel: **connected, valued, and in control**.

**Specific support strategies** (using the example of a child who runs to jump on the couch with shoes on):

1. **A warm warning about the transition** - Given before entering the house, this helps the child mentally prepare for what's expected
2. **A game that gets the child giggling about power and obedience** - This defuses the tension about "feeling pushed around" that drives defiance
3. **A special job for the child when entering the house** - This gives the child a sense of power and makes them feel their contribution is valued
4. **Special warm bonding time before coming into the house** - This strengthens connection so the child wants to follow the parent's lead
5. **An alternative place to jump before coming inside** - This channels the child's energy in an acceptable direction
6. **A ritual of taking shoes off before crossing the threshold** - This creates a predictable routine that removes the decision point

The common thread: each strategy addresses the child's emotional needs (connection, value, control) rather than just demanding compliance.

### Option 6: Setting Empathic Limits

**Why kids test limits**: Children don't share adult priorities - and why should they? They have their own priorities (jumping on the couch is fun!) and no understanding of the adult worldview (couches cost money). The parent's job is to guide them continuously.

**The formula for effective limits**:
- **Firmness + Consistency** -> The more firm and consistent you are, the more your child can accept your limit, grieve about it, and move on
- **Empathy** -> The more empathic you are, the more your child will accept your limits without needing to rebel against them

A limit-setting statement example: "Shoes get the couch dirty... no shoes on the couch."

**Redirection is key**: Redirection is the best way to stop behavior because it channels the energy rather than just blocking it. Example: "I see that's so much fun! And you know the couch is not for jumping. Come, off the couch. Let's go jump on the old mattress in the basement."

**Limit-testing is normal**: All kids naturally test limits to see if they're firm. This means you may need to stay with the child and physically help them meet the expectation (e.g., staying with her as she enters the house and helping her get shoes off every time before she heads for the couch). Eventually this becomes automatic habit for both parent and child.

**When the child darts away anyway**: If the child runs to defy the limit despite your support, she's signaling that something is getting in the way of cooperation. That something is *emotions*. Children store up their feelings, waiting for a safe chance to release them with a compassionate witness - that's the parent.

**The deeper solution**: Getting to the bottom of these tangled emotions stops "bad" behavior before it starts.

**The compliance trap**: You can make the child "stuff" those feelings through screaming or punishment. She'll comply eventually. But:
- She'll probably act out in other ways
- You'll erode the close relationship you want
- She'll be less cooperative in general
- The teen years will be difficult
- You're purposely damaging your relationship with someone you brought into the world

**The alternative path**: Help her with those feelings. This:
- Helps her cooperate with your agenda
- Teaches emotional intelligence
- Makes her more able to meet expectations as she gets older
- Brings you closer together

**The two-part approach**: Connect, and help her with the emotions driving the behavior. In other words: **Play when you can. Cry when you have to.**

### Option 7: Help With Feelings Through Play ("Play When You Can")

**The mindset shift**: "It is not an emergency. We can play with this."

**Practical example** (for the couch-jumping-with-shoes scenario):

Keep your tone light and playful to get the child giggling:
- "Excuse me?! Are you on that couch with your shoes on?! We'll see about that! I'm the couch protector, and I always get my girl!"
- Scoop her up laughing and toss her over your shoulder
- Run around the house with her, taking her shoes off and tossing them toward where they belong
- Sing a silly chant about how much you love her and you'll never let her go
- Keep dropping her on the couch and scooping her up again
- Finally collapse together on the couch for a good snuggle

**Building on the game**: The next time you enter the house, tell her before going in the door that you want to play the game again, but first she has to take her shoes off with you right inside the door. This **transforms the game from one of defiance into one of reconnection and celebration**.

**Why giggling works**: Giggling releases upset feelings almost as well as crying does. It also creates oxytocin (the bonding hormone), so when parent and child laugh together, they're bonding. Use this approach any time you need to interrupt "bad" behavior.

### Option 8: Help With Feelings Through Crying ("Cry When You Have to")

**When play doesn't work**: If the child doesn't giggle, won't let you take her shoes off, gets angry and defiant - she's beyond play. Her provocative behavior is signaling that she needs to cry and express all the emotions she's been stuffing.

**What the child can't say**: The child would benefit from being able to articulate something like: "Mom, I feel like someone is always telling me what to do... I get so tired of it!" But she can't TELL you how she feels, so instead she SHOWS you with her defiance.

**The approach**:
1. Summon up all your compassion
2. Look her in the eye
3. Set your limit clearly and kindly: "Sweetie, you know shoes get the couch dirty. I won't let you wear shoes on the couch."

**The critical moment**: If you're able to stay compassionate, she may burst into tears. However, if you take this personally and get mad, there's no way you'll get to tears - you'll just have a fight on your hands.

**During the emotional release**:
- Hold her while she cries, if she'll let you
- Don't talk much
- Just tell her she's safe
- If she lashes out, remember that the tears are right behind the anger - she just needs to feel a little safer to let them out

**Building safety for emotional release**:
- **In the moment**: Through your calm compassion
- **Throughout all interactions**: Through connection and empathy, play, and physical connection

### Option 9: Hugs

**Hugs are not the only option** for reconnection and cooperation, but they are probably the most useful.

**What hugs do physiologically**: Hugs release oxytocin, which clears stress hormones in the body and helps the child feel more connected to the parent.

**What hugs accomplish**:
- Heal the disconnection that drives so much misbehavior
- Help children WANT to cooperate
- Melt away crankiness and anger
- Help the child feel safe enough to cry and release upsets driving the acting out
- Benefit the parent too by reminding them that their child, even when acting "bratty," is their beloved
- "Rescue the child from the low road they've stumbled onto and pull them back up onto the high road with us"

**Hugs are necessary but not sufficient**: Hugs don't substitute for 24/7 empathy and daily one-on-one reconnection time with your child.

**Specific recommendation**: **Nine hugs a day are essential** for a child to thrive and want to cooperate.

## The Disconnection Principle

**Quote from Dr. Lawrence Cohen**: "Disconnection is at the heart of many behavior problems. We often respond to 'bad behavior' with isolation, time outs, humiliation, hitting, slapping, threats, yelling or withdrawal of love. These responses create even more disconnection, which is why they don't work very well."

This encapsulates the entire framework: misbehavior stems from disconnection, and punitive responses create more disconnection, which creates more misbehavior. The solution is to restore connection.

## Key Distinctions and Nuances

**Compliance vs. Cooperation**: Children can be made to comply through fear and force, but this is different from cooperation. Compliance is externally driven and breeds resentment; cooperation comes from within because the child wants to maintain the relationship and feels capable of meeting expectations.

**Stopping behavior vs. transforming behavior**: Punishment may stop a specific behavior in the moment, but it often displaces the behavior elsewhere. Addressing underlying emotions and connection actually transforms the pattern.

**The child's integrity**: Children want to protect the relationship with parents *as long as it doesn't compromise their own integrity*. This explains why children will defy even at great cost - they're protecting their sense of self. Empathic limit-setting respects this integrity while still maintaining boundaries.

**Repetitive limit-testing as communication**: When a child repeatedly tests the same limit, they're not being stubborn or defiant for its own sake - they're asking for help. The parent who recognizes this can respond to the need rather than just the behavior.

**The role of modeling**: Support and modeling are how we teach acceptable behavior, because that is what helps children learn and what motivates them to cooperate. Punishment teaches fear; modeling and support teach skills and build motivation.

## Summary of Effective vs. Ineffective Approaches

**Ineffective (or situationally limited)**:
- Ignoring behavior (only works for temporary, addressable issues)
- Screaming/shouting (erodes relationship, increases acting out over time)
- Timeout (symbolic abandonment, creates resentment, displaces behavior)

**Effective when conditions are met**:
- High expectations (require age-appropriateness plus connection plus emotional support)

**Highly effective**:
- Tailored support addressing connection, value, and control
- Empathic limits with firmness, consistency, and empathy
- Play to release feelings through giggling and build connection
- Supporting crying to release stored emotions
- Hugs to restore connection and release oxytocin

The underlying principle connecting all effective approaches: **restore and maintain connection while addressing the emotional needs driving the behavior**.
