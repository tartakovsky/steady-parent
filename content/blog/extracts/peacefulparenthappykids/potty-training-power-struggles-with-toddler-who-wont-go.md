# Potty Training Power Struggles: Understanding and Resolving Resistance

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids (Dr. Laura Markham)

---

## Core Statistics on Potty Training (from Elizabeth Pantley's "No Cry Toilet Training Solution")

**More than 80% of children experience setbacks in toilet training.** This reframes what parents typically call "setbacks" as simply the normal path to mastery. Daily accidents are not unusual and should not be treated as failure or regression.

**The timeline for daytime toilet independence is 3-12 months from the start of training.** This extended window means parents who expect quick results are setting themselves up for frustration and potentially creating counterproductive pressure.

**The emotional temperature test:** If your child has temper tantrums or cries over potty training, or if you find yourself getting angry, stop training entirely. Review your approach, then try again with modifications in one to two months. The emotional state of both parent and child is a reliable signal that the current approach is not working.

---

## Understanding Readiness: Physical vs. Psychological

Children can show physical readiness without psychological readiness, and the distinction matters greatly for how you respond.

**Physical readiness indicators:**
- Staying dry during naps
- Staying dry overnight/in the morning
- Noticing when they are urinating (even if they don't stop)

**Psychological readiness requirements:**
- Internal motivation (wanting to use the potty for themselves, not to please parents)
- Ability to interrupt play without excessive frustration
- Self-discipline to stop an engaging activity to use the toilet
- Not being in a developmental phase where asserting autonomy is paramount

**The underwear fascination problem:** Children often become enamored with underwear before they are actually ready to train. The desire to wear "big kid underwear" is not equivalent to readiness for the responsibility that comes with it.

---

## The Power Struggle Dynamic

**Core principle:** Fights with your child about their body are fights you will never win. The child controls their own bodily functions, and if you turn toileting into a battle of wills, the child will always have the final say.

**Why power struggles develop:** Two-year-olds are in a developmental phase focused on establishing a sense of agency. Potty training presents an opportunity to assert control, especially when:
- Parents show strong emotional investment in the outcome
- There is pressure or repeated asking
- The child senses parental frustration or anxiety

**The pressure paradox:** The child needs to WANT to use the potty. When pressure feels too great, they won't even try. The more parents push, the less likely success becomes.

**How to identify a power struggle:** When the child refuses to use the potty when asked, then immediately has an accident afterward, this pattern indicates resistance to parental requests rather than lack of readiness. The behavior is about autonomy, not inability.

---

## The Role of Parental Attitude and Emotional Climate

**Research finding:** Negativity when children have accidents always results in more accidents. This creates a vicious cycle where parental frustration leads to more accidents, which leads to more frustration.

**Parental self-doubt is contagious:** Children pick up on parental anxiety, frustration, and self-doubt. One parent in this case noted "My self-doubts were getting to be too much too, which I'll bet [my daughter] picked up on." The emotional state of the parent directly affects the child's performance.

**Both parents must be aligned:** Different reactions from different caregivers (one parent handling accidents negatively while another stays calm) creates inconsistency that the child notices and that can worsen the situation.

---

## Environmental Factors: Home vs. School

**Diagnostic question:** Does the child have accidents at school as well as at home?

**If no accidents at school but accidents at home:** The child IS ready. The problem is either:
1. Parental attitude (emotional reactions, pressure, negativity)
2. Differences in routine between home and school

**If accidents happen at both home and school:** Either:
1. The child simply isn't ready yet
2. Something happened that triggered backsliding

**Potential backsliding triggers:**
- A mishandled accident at school where caregivers responded negatively
- A negative response to an accident at home
- Something so small the parent wouldn't have noticed it

## Strategic Approaches to Defuse Power Struggles

### The Pull-Up Transition Strategy

**When to use:** When a child has already been in underwear and doing somewhat well, but has stalled and begun refusing. Going back to diapers entirely would send the message that the child has failed.

**The key distinction:** The child hasn't failed, they have stalled. Parents can help them through by decreasing the pressure of their own reactions.

**Why pull-ups work in this context:**
- They allow the parent to truly stay low-key about accidents (because cleanup is minimal)
- They signal a step back without feeling like complete regression
- They remove the high-stakes feeling from every bathroom visit
- Most kids won't fully train in pull-ups because they feel too much like diapers, but they serve as a bridge

**Implementation script:** "Oops, we don't have any clean underwear, we have to do the wash. But that's ok, you can wear pull-ups today." This frames it as a practical matter, not a judgment about the child's progress.

### The "Choice to Return to Diapers" Strategy

**Powerful psychological mechanism:** Offering the child the choice to go back to diapers can paradoxically motivate them toward the potty. The choice gives them agency, and they often choose to continue trying.

**How it was used in this case:** The parent suggested to the child that maybe she would rather go back to diapers for a little while. This was done "in a good, positive way" - not as a threat or punishment, but as a genuine option.

**Result:** "Within the next week, she's been going like a champ, no more accidents, or rare ones since then. The suggestion to go back to diapers seemed to make her think, and push her toward the potty."

**Why this works:** It removes the power struggle entirely. The child is no longer resisting parental pressure; they are making their own choice. The resistance dissolves because there's nothing to resist against.

### The Clear Escalation Path

If the child refuses to use the potty repeatedly despite low-pressure approaches:
1. Ask directly: "Would you rather wear diapers or use the potty?"
2. If they choose diapers, accept that choice without negativity
3. Return to diapers completely
4. Wait two months before trying again

## Avoiding the Shame Trap

**The danger of backing off:** If you completely back off potty training, especially when the child did well for a while, you risk giving them the message that they failed.

**The nuance:** Backing off is sometimes necessary, but HOW you back off matters enormously. It must be framed as a choice or a practical matter, never as a consequence of the child's inability or accidents.

---

## Routine and Structure

**The importance of having an actual plan:** Rather than ad-hoc requests to use the potty, parents need a structured routine with predictable toilet visits.

**Example of routine-based language:** "Ok let's use the potty now before we get in the car." This connects potty use to natural transition points rather than random interruptions.

**Modeling behavior:** If the child refuses, the parent can use the toilet themselves and say: "We always try before we get in the car. Some day you'll be ready, too." This normalizes the behavior without making it a demand.

**The naked bottom approach:** If you don't have rugs, moving to a naked bottom at home and focusing completely on positive toilet training during warm months can accelerate the process by increasing awareness.

---

## What Not to Do

- **Don't fight about it.** If the child won't use the potty when asked, don't escalate. Certainly don't physically force or threaten.

- **Don't react negatively to accidents.** Research confirms this always makes things worse.

- **Don't let frustration show.** Children pick up on parental anxiety and it increases their resistance.

- **Don't treat setbacks as failures.** They are the normal path to mastery for over 80% of children.

- **Don't expect fast results.** The 3-12 month timeline is normal.

- **Don't continue when emotions run high.** Tantrums, tears, or anger are signals to stop and reset.

---

## The Two-Intervention Approach (What Worked in This Case)

The parent made two changes simultaneously:

1. **Spoke to husband about his responses to accidents** - addressing the negativity issue
2. **Suggested to the child that maybe she would rather go back to diapers** - removing the power struggle dynamic

The parent notes that "one, or both seemed to do the trick." Both interventions target the same underlying issue: removing pressure and negativity from the situation so the child can choose to use the potty without it being about compliance or resistance.

---

## Key Takeaways

The fundamental insight is that potty training succeeds when children are internally motivated and not experiencing it as a battleground for autonomy. Every parental behavior should be evaluated against the question: "Does this increase or decrease my child's sense of pressure and my emotional investment in the outcome?"

When power struggles develop, the solution is not to push harder but to genuinely back off - either by reducing emotional stakes (pull-ups, calm reactions) or by explicitly offering the child the choice to delay training entirely. Paradoxically, giving the child permission to stop often motivates them to continue.
