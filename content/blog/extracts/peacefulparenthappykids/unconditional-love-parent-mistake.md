# Healing Your Relationship After Parenting Mistakes

Source: Peaceful Parent, Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

---

## Core Principle: It Is Never Too Late to Heal

The fundamental message is that **it is never too late to heal your relationship with your child**, regardless of past mistakes. However, the difficulty of healing increases with the child's age because older children have developed **emotional armor** - they lash out to keep parents from getting too close as a protective mechanism. Despite this defensive behavior, children secretly and desperately want their parent's love.

This means parents should not interpret resistant or hostile behavior as evidence that their child doesn't want connection. The opposite is true: the armor exists precisely because the desire for connection is so strong and the fear of being hurt again is real.

---

## Why Children Don't Need Perfect Parents

Children do not need perfect parents. In fact, **having a "perfect" parent would actually harm the child** because if the child sees their parent as perfect, the child will feel worse about themselves since they know they are not perfect.

What children actually need is:
- **A model of how to be a gracious human** - someone who demonstrates the messy, real process of being human
- A parent who admits when they've been wrong
- A parent who is willing to grow
- A parent who gives themselves support to do better
- A parent who works hard to regulate their own emotions instead of acting out, no matter what the child does

This reframes parenting from achieving perfection to modeling authentic human growth and self-compassion.

---

## The Self-Love Prerequisite

**Loving your child unconditionally starts with loving yourself unconditionally.** This is not optional or secondary - it is the foundation.

The reasoning: You cannot simultaneously feel bad about what you've done AND feel good enough to do better. This psychological state applies equally to both parents and children. The shame and self-criticism that comes from past mistakes creates a state incompatible with positive change.

**Reframing mistakes as learning experiences:** If you use your past mistakes to guide you toward a better way in the future, that is called a learning experience, not a mistake. This isn't just positive thinking - it reflects the reality that everyone is doing the best they can with whatever they know at the time.

---

## Children's Resilience and the Healing Process

Children are resilient. The child may need to do some crying about times when they felt alone in the face of the parent's anger. This emotional release is part of the healing process, not a sign of permanent damage.

The healing happens when the child is able to show the parent all those hurt feelings from the past. If the child is in a place that hurts emotionally, they will be "acting ugly" - their difficult behavior is an expression of pain, not defiance or bad character.

## Five Steps to Transform the Relationship

### Step 1: Lighten Up

**Most parental upsets stem from fear**, not the actual situation. Common fear-based thoughts include:
- "What if he NEVER learns?"
- "What if she gets in big trouble and ruins her life?"
- "What if I've damaged him forever?"

**Fear never helps parents make good decisions.** You can always choose a new course now and start moving in a better direction - the past does not determine the future.

**Most issues with kids are developmental** - as the brain matures, the child matures. This means many behaviors that seem alarming are temporary developmental phases, not permanent character traits.

**Practical self-talk:** "He's getting better parenting than I did, and I came out ok." This provides perspective and reduces catastrophic thinking while acknowledging imperfection as normal.

### Step 2: Make Self-Regulation Your Highest Priority

If you can stay centered and pay attention, you can respond with love and creativity to whatever's going on with your child. Self-regulation is what enables all other positive parenting responses.

**When you find yourself off-track** (which is inevitable):
1. Take a deep breath
2. Apologize
3. Suggest a "Do Over"
4. Try again

This process of recovering from mistakes is itself fantastic modeling for your child. The child learns not that mistakes are unacceptable, but that mistakes can be acknowledged and repaired.

### Step 3: Focus on Connecting - Start Where the Child Is

The healing comes when the child is able to show the parent all the hurt feelings from the past. This requires:
- **Patience** - the child may resist initially
- **Courage** - it is emotionally difficult to witness your child's pain, especially pain you caused
- **Going in after them** - actively pursuing connection rather than waiting for the child to come around

If the parent can keep themselves calm and compassionate, the child will start to soften. The hurt will surface beneath the anger or resistance.

**What to say when the hurt surfaces:**
- "I am so sorry that happened"
- "I didn't understand how much that hurt your feelings"
- "That must have made you feel alone and scared"
- "I never want you to feel that way"
- "I am always here for you and I love you so much"

**Critical warnings during this process:**
- Don't take it personally - the child's expressions of hurt are about their healing, not about attacking you
- Don't try to teach any lessons until the child feels better - this is not a teaching moment, it's a healing moment
- Don't get lost in the dark with them - you're the one who is supposed to have the candle

The parent's role is to be a steady, compassionate presence who can witness the pain without being destabilized by it. The process won't go on forever, even though it may feel endless in the moment.

**The outcome:** Bring your child back into connection with you, and they'll be a whole different kid. Connection transforms behavior.

### Step 4: Focus on Repair, Not Blame

It wouldn't be good for your child to have perfect parents because then the child would never see the parent role-modeling self-forgiveness and repair. The inevitable little rifts of life are opportunities to get closer and make things better, not failures to avoid.

**The STOP, DROP, and BREATHE framework:**
- **STOP** - what you're doing immediately
- **DROP** - your agenda (just until you calm down)
- **BREATHE** - regulate yourself before continuing

**How to ask for a Do-Over:**
"I'm sorry I was starting to yell. Let's try a do-over. I'm worried that we're running out of time here, and I need you to do [specific task] now. How can we all work together to make this easy?"

This language:
- Acknowledges the parent's mistake without excessive self-flagellation
- Names the underlying concern (time pressure)
- States the need clearly
- Invites collaboration rather than demanding compliance

Repair requires being willing to summon up humility and humor and apologize, sometimes often. The frequency of apologies is not a sign of failure but of awareness and commitment to the relationship.

### Step 5: Enjoy Your Child

Parents often become so busy managing their children that they forget what kids really need. **Children need us to enjoy them** - this is how they learn they're of value. If a child doesn't feel enjoyed by their parent, they internalize that they are not valuable.

This is also how children learn what joy is and what makes life worth living. Enjoyment isn't just nice - it's formative.

**The lure of screens:** Resist the temptation to sit on the couch with screens whenever possible. Instead, focus on what the author calls the "invisible tasks of parenting" - the connection activities that don't look productive but are actually what heals the relationship.

**Examples of invisible parenting tasks at different ages:**
- Baby: Tummy time
- Toddler: Floor time
- Elementary schooler: Bedtime snuggles
- Tween: Couch time
- Teen: Walks

**Connection activities for all ages:**
- Empathizing
- Giggling together
- Healing hurt feelings
- Family game night
- Helping children think about the world and explore emerging passions
- Supporting kids to solve their own problems
- Pillow fights
- Star-gazing
- Listening
- Lighting candles
- Connecting

## The Fundamental Stance: Choosing Love Over Fear

**You don't have to have all the answers.** You don't have to fix your child or the situation. All you have to do is stay present and try to choose love instead of fear.

**What the child really needs:** Your child doesn't even need the red cup, or whatever they're crying for. The child needs your loving acceptance of them, complete with all their tangled up feelings.

**All emotions are acceptable:** The child's disappointment, rage, and grief are all ok. They are part of a rich emotional life, and they will all pass without you doing a thing. The parent's job is just to love the child - and themselves - through it. Unconditionally.

## Dan Siegel Quote on Embracing Imperfection

"Understanding alone cannot prevent disrupted connections from occurring. Some will inevitably happen. The challenge we all share is to embrace our humanity with humor and patience so that we can in turn relate to our children with openness and kindness. To continually chastise ourselves for our 'errors' with our children keeps us involved in our own emotional issues and out of relationship with our children."

This quote emphasizes that:
- Disrupted connections are inevitable, not preventable
- Self-criticism keeps the parent focused on themselves rather than the child
- The goal is not error-free parenting but humorous, patient acceptance of human imperfection
- This self-acceptance is what enables openness and kindness toward the child
