# Healing Your Heart to Enable Unconditional Love

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/Healing-forgiveness-unconditional-love

## The Core Problem: Why We Cannot Let Go of Past Wounds

The fundamental insight is that we cannot simply decide to "move on" from resentment or rage because **we need our rage as a defense mechanism**. The anger serves a protective function - it shields us from feeling the underlying agony.

When we approach the pain from our past, the wounded child inside us instinctively wants to run away. To avoid feeling that pain directly, we retreat into "the story" - the narrative about what happened and who was at fault. This storytelling is itself a defense mechanism that prevents us from accessing the actual emotional pain that remains walled off in a corner of our heart.

This defense mechanism made complete sense during childhood. Children cannot process overwhelming pain, so blocking it off with anger was adaptive - it prevented the pain from "swamping" the developing child and allowed growth to continue. The problem is that this same mechanism persists into adulthood, where it creates a new problem: **you cannot love wholeheartedly when half your heart is unavailable** because it is occupied with maintaining defensive anger.

## The Paradox of Healing: Giving Up Hope for a Better Past

There is a saying: "It's never too late to have a happy childhood." This does not mean rewriting history. It means we can embrace and nurture the sad or angry child who still exists within us. However, doing this requires first confronting a painful truth.

**The paradox**: To heal, we must first accept the pain of not having received the happy childhood we deserved. More pointedly, we must give up all hope of ever having a better past. This feels profoundly unfair - we did not deserve the pain, and now we must fully accept its reality rather than fighting it.

But this acceptance is precisely what unlocks healing. Acceptance enables grief, and **grief functions as a washing away of pain from the past**. Grief is what frees us to move forward. Once we have grieved, we can finally give ourselves the love we were missing.

## The Re-Parenting Process: A Six-Step Framework

This process is described as "not easy, but you can do hard things." The essential requirements are time and courage. For those with trauma backgrounds or who find this frightening, doing the work with a counselor is recommended.

### Step 1: Find a Childhood Photograph

Locate a photograph of yourself as a child. This provides a visual anchor for connecting with your younger self.

### Step 2: Create a Safe Container

Light a candle and sit quietly. Breathe deeply. Imagine a place where you feel safe and happy. Wrap yourself in love. This establishes the emotional safety needed before approaching painful material.

### Step 3: Reach Out to the Wounded Child

Look at your photograph. Embrace the small child within you who is still suffering from your childhood experiences. Let that child tell you the whole painful story. Throughout this, breathe and hold yourself in love. **Be the parent you needed then** - this is the essence of re-parenting.

### Step 4: Resist Trivializing the Pain

A critical warning: Do not compare your pain to anyone else's pain. If it hurt, it hurt - full stop. Accept that pain without minimizing it. Let yourself feel it fully. Do not suppress tears; let them flush out the pain. Stay in your body and your heart, not your head. The mental space is where we get caught in defensive storytelling.

### Step 5: Focus on Body Sensations Rather Than the Story

This is presented as a crucial distinction in technique. When we focus on feelings using words, we get caught in the story and before we know it, we are tangled up in the past again. Alternatively, we may feel overwhelmed and re-traumatized. Neither outcome is healing.

**The better approach**: Instead of replaying the story, summon compassion and embrace yourself. Then focus purely on what is happening in the body. The reasoning is that **the body is the unconscious, and the past is stored there**.

Specific instructions:
- Notice sensations without judging or trying to fix them
- Tenderly notice the lump in your throat, the heaviness around your heart, the tightness in your belly
- Breathe into these sensations
- If you feel an urgent need to jump up and take action, resist that urge
- Just breathe and watch as the pain loosens, shifts, and begins to evaporate

The mechanism: Every time you allow yourself to feel what is happening in your body and love yourself through it, you melt away some of the pain from the past. This is a gradual, cumulative process.

### Step 6: Integrate the Inner Child Into the Present

Take that hurting child into the present, where you can offer daily nurturance and healing. Reassure your inner child that they are completely lovable and loved, and deserved better than they received. Make this explicit promise: "From now on, I will make sure you get what you need."

This is ongoing work - not a one-time exercise but a continuing relationship with yourself.

## What This Healing Accomplishes

### Regarding Triggers

The process is described as "a powerful healer for those triggers your child always seems to find." Once you work through the pain you have been ignoring, you will not need to hang on to anger.

This does not mean pain and anger will never arise again - you are still human. The difference is that you will be able to notice these feelings and let them go rather than acting on them. And because you are dealing with your own pain appropriately, you will not inflict it on your child.

### Regarding Unconditional Love

**Compassion for yourself is the key to healing any place inside you that hurts.** This self-compassion opens your heart to the unconditional love you have always deserved. The implication is that loving your child unconditionally requires first developing this capacity for self-love and self-compassion.

## The Question of Ongoing Harmful Relationships

The article opens with a reader question: "What can you do if the people in your life, in particular family, continue to inflict wounds although you keep forgiving? Isn't it better to keep them at arms length?"

While the article does not directly answer this question, the framework suggests that healing the internal wounds is the priority. The work is focused on your relationship with yourself and your inner child, not on managing external relationships. This implies that whether to maintain boundaries with harmful people is a separate question from whether to do the internal healing work - you can and should do the healing work regardless of what you decide about external relationships.

## Key Quote

"Healing comes when we meet our wounded places with compassion." - Stephen Levine

This encapsulates the entire approach: the agent of healing is compassion directed inward toward the places where we are hurt.
