# Understanding and Helping Intense, Demanding Toddlers with Attachment Challenges

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/3-year-old-demanding-tantrums-rages

## The Core Problem: When Intensity Meets Attachment Disruption

This article addresses a specific pattern: a highly intense, sensitive child who exhibits massive tantrums, social difficulties, and extreme preference for one parent (usually the mother) over the other. The key insight is that these behaviors typically stem from the combination of two factors: **innate temperamental intensity** and **disrupted attachment** (usually with the father due to extended absences).

The child described is nearly 3.5 years old, extremely bright and verbally advanced, but struggles socially and has massive meltdowns when the father attempts caregiving tasks. These issues existed before a new sibling arrived but intensified afterward.

## Diagnostic Framework: Three Possible Causes

Dr. Laura presents a diagnostic hierarchy for evaluating severe behavioral challenges:

### 1. Asperger's/Neurological Issues

**Signs that might suggest Asperger's:**
- Significant impairment in social behavior
- Lack of interest in peers
- Normal language and learning development alongside social delays

**Signs that argue against Asperger's:**
- The child is highly related to the primary caregiver
- The child CAN calm down and verbalize feelings when with the trusted parent
- Social difficulties appear to be about overwhelm rather than lack of interest

**Action:** If the child shows additional signs like repetitive motion, seek evaluation from a neurologist. Otherwise, move to considering relational causes.

### 2. Relational/Attachment Issues (Most Likely Cause)

This is typically the primary driver when:
- The child functions well with one parent but not the other
- One parent has been consistently absent or unavailable
- The child shows signs of anxiety about the trusted parent disappearing

**Key insight:** Children form conclusions about parental reliability by approximately 12 months of age. If a parent has been absent frequently during this period, the child may have concluded that parent cannot be trusted to meet their needs.

### 3. Other Factors (Diet, Unknown Issues)

Dr. Laura mentions the **Feinberg diet** as something that has helped some "out of control" children who cannot handle salicylates. If temperament and attachment interventions don't work, this represents another avenue to explore.

## Understanding Intense Temperament

### What Intense Temperament Means

**Intense children feel all emotions bigger.** This is not a choice or learned behavior - it is inborn, likely inherited. The same intensity that makes tantrums overwhelming also creates capacity for deep joy, passionate interests, and strong convictions.

**Core characteristics:**
- They feel deeply and don't give up
- All feelings are "bigger" to them
- They tend to get stuck in emotions
- They find social situations overwhelming, over-stimulating, and threatening
- They need to stay connected to their trusted person in social situations to keep their bearings

**Critical reframe:** This is not something the child is creating - it is something they were born with. Just as unusual height would be both blessing and challenge throughout life, so is intense temperament.

### How Intensity Affects Social Situations

Intense children often struggle socially not because they lack interest in other children, but because:
- Social situations feel overwhelming and threatening
- They cannot regulate their anxiety without their trusted attachment figure
- Sharing triggers deep anxiety about loss and scarcity

**The possessiveness paradox:** When an intense child screams about other children touching their toys, they are not being "spoiled" - they are experiencing genuine distress about their sense of safety and ownership being threatened. Standard interventions (putting away special toys, using timers) often fail because they don't address the underlying anxiety.

## Understanding Attachment Disruption

### How Attachment Gets Disrupted

In the case discussed, the father worked out of town 3 weeks per month. The child experienced a repeating pattern: just as he would begin to reconnect with his father after being home for a week, the father would disappear again.

**The double conclusion:** The child likely concluded (1) Dad is not reliable, and (2) Maybe Mom might disappear too, since Dad keeps disappearing. This explains why the child needs 100% of Mom's attention and becomes aggressive when her attention is elsewhere - he is terrified she might vanish.

### How Attachment Disruption Manifests

**With the unreliable parent:**
- Massive tantrums when that parent tries to care for the child
- Inability to relax or enjoy activities when alone with that parent
- Constant drive to return to the trusted parent
- Resistance to that parent doing things "wrong" (not the way the trusted parent does them)

**The "stick in the mud" pattern:** When a child consistently refuses activities with one parent and wants to go home, this is not defiance or lack of interest - it is an activated attachment drive. The child does not feel safe, so they cannot relax enough to participate. They need to return to the trusted parent to feel okay inside.

### The New Sibling Complication

When a new baby arrives, the standard approach is for Dad to take over more care of the older child. This usually works because Dad has been doing these things all along. But when Dad has been mostly absent:
- Every time Dad steps in, the older child is reminded that Mom no longer cares about them
- The child interprets the new baby as "proof" that Mom was disappointed with them and got a replacement
- Normal sibling jealousy gets magnified through the lens of attachment insecurity

**Important note:** All older siblings have feelings of rejection and jealousy when a new baby arrives. In children with attachment disruption and intense temperament, these feelings are dramatically amplified.

## The Caregiver's Perspective

### Why Parents Burn Out

Dr. Laura explicitly validates the exhaustion and resentment parents feel:
- Three years of having an exceptionally demanding child
- Lack of partner support (in this case, due to travel)
- Social isolation because playdates are embarrassing disasters
- Fear of having created a "spoiled monster"
- Physical depletion from tandem nursing and night wakings

### The Dangerous Shift in Perception

When parents become burned out, a critical shift happens: the child stops feeling like "your beloved child" and starts feeling like "this child" - a burden, a problem, a source of resentment.

**Why this matters enormously:** Intense children are exquisitely sensitive to their parents' emotional states. The child perceives the shift from beloved to burden. This confirms their worst fears (that they are unlovable, that Mom is pulling away, that they might be abandoned) and intensifies their desperate behaviors.

**The vicious cycle:** Child's difficult behavior -> Parent's resentment -> Child senses rejection -> Child's behavior intensifies -> More resentment -> More desperate behavior.

### Reframing the Child's Behavior

Dr. Laura offers this reframe: The child is not choosing to be a bad kid. They are not trying to make the parent's life difficult. They are doing the best they can given the difficult hand of cards they have been dealt.

**Critical point:** The parents chose to have another child. While this choice was made with good intentions and logical plans, it was the parents' choice, not the child's. The child is reacting to circumstances they did not create.

## The Three-Part Repair Strategy

### Part 1: Father Repairs Relationship with Son

**The core principle:** The father needs to go overboard to meet the child's needs, even to the point of babying him.

**Why this works:** The child needs to be convinced that Dad really cares about his perspective and truly wants to meet his needs. This is what will rebuild trust. The father must prove through consistent action that he will not abandon the child again.

**What this looks like:**
- Accept and validate the child's perspective even when it seems unreasonable
- Do not insist the child "toughen up" or "get over it"
- Let the child set the pace on accepting Dad's care
- Soothe your own wounded pride when the child rejects you - this is not about you
- Love the child through tantrums rather than trying to stop tantrums

**The counterintuitive truth:** The child needs to "revert to babyhood" with Dad in order to rebuild trust. This means more dependency and babying, not more pushing toward independence.

**What the father needs to overcome internally:**
- Feeling rejected by his own child
- Wondering if the child is "abnormal"
- Wondering if he or his wife "ruined" the child
- Natural inclination to set limits and require compliance

**Resource mentioned:** Heather Forbes, who specializes in children with attachment issues.

### Part 2: Help the Child Understand Themselves

**Making the implicit explicit:** The child's feelings about Dad's comings and goings are preverbal - formed before the child had language. The child needs help bringing these feelings to consciousness.

**Example language:** "Dad used to go away and then come back, all the time. That was hard for you. You always worried that someone would leave. I wonder if you thought I might leave?"

**Why this helps:** The more the child can access scary feelings and bring them to the surface, the less those feelings will control them. Unnamed fears drive behavior; named fears can be processed.

**Teaching self-soothing:** Intense children get anxious easily. They need:
- Ways to notice when they are getting anxious
- Strategies to calm themselves down
- An empathic parent to model and teach these skills through co-regulation

**How to teach self-soothing:** Empathize with the child and soothe them whenever they express emotions, including during tantrums. This is "emotion coaching" - not trying to stop feelings, but helping the child understand and navigate them.

**The power of predictable schedules:** Create a routine where some things Mom always does and some things Dad always does. This reduces the number of battles because not everything is negotiable every time.

**Implementation detail:** Start with something small that Dad will ALWAYS do (example: feeding breakfast). If the child rages, Dad takes an empathic approach and reassures, but does not back down. Mom does not intervene, no matter what. Over time, Dad expands his domain.

**Critical exception:** Don't start with high-stakes items like nighttime waking. Begin with lower-stakes routines and build from there.

### Part 3: Mother Repairs Relationship with Son

**The dual need:** The mother needs both rest/recovery AND active repair of the emotional connection with her son.

**Why the mother's healing matters:** She is the center of the child's universe. The child worries constantly about losing her. He needs to learn that she will always be there, will always come back, that relationships can have rifts but be repaired.

**What the mother needs:**
- To vent frustration (get it out rather than suppress it)
- More sleep and rest
- Time completely alone, without anyone hanging on her
- Unstructured, peaceful time snuggling with the older child
- Help processing her own feelings about the situation

**The division of labor shift:** Instead of the default pattern (Mom with baby, Dad with older child), aim for more even division. Sometimes Mom is with the older child while Dad has the baby. Sometimes Mom gets complete breaks.

**Internal work for the mother:**

Dr. Laura suggests a visualization practice:
1. Picture yourself hugging your son, with him beaming back at you
2. Let the infinite tenderness of your love for him wash over you
3. Ask for help (from whatever source feels right - higher power, deepest wisdom) to let go of anything blocking closeness
4. Ask for guidance to be the best mother you can be
5. Notice any pictures or words that come up (this is how the subconscious communicates)
6. Do this practice frequently

**Why visualization works:** It reconnects the parent with their love for the child, which has been buried under exhaustion and resentment. Multiple parents have reported this practice transformed their feelings.

## What NOT to Do (Anti-Patterns)

### Don't Expect the Child to "Get Over It"

The father's natural inclination may be that the child should toughen up, accept Dad's way of doing things, and stop being spoiled. This approach will fail because it does not address the underlying lack of trust. You cannot demand trust; you must earn it through consistent caregiving.

### Don't Give Up After 20 Minutes

In the described case, the mother waits out tantrums for 20 minutes, then intervenes to do the task herself. This teaches the child that 20 minutes of screaming works. The child is not learning to accept Dad; they are learning exactly how long they need to hold out.

### Don't Intervene as the Trusted Parent

When Dad is handling a tantrum, Mom must not intervene "no matter what." Every intervention teaches the child that Mom will rescue them from Dad, which prevents the child from building trust with Dad.

### Don't Frame Nursing Refusal as Manipulation

Tantrums about nursing are not manipulation or spoiled behavior. For an intense child with attachment fears, nursing represents safety, connection, and reassurance that Mom is still there. Weaning should happen gradually and with alternatives for connection, not by simply refusing and weathering tantrums.

### Don't Ignore Social Difficulties

While an intense child may grow into better social skills over time, significant social struggles warrant attention. The child needs to learn ways to manage their own anxiety and needs sufficient downtime to avoid overwhelm. Simply forcing more social exposure without support will not help.

## Prognosis and Expectations

**The good news:** With effort, attachment can be repaired. The father can "gradually win his way back into the son's heart."

**The timeline:** This will not be quick. Attachment took years to disrupt and will take consistent effort over months or years to repair.

**The threshold for seeking more help:** If following all this advice does not produce improvement, that signals something else may be going on. Options include professional counseling sessions or local therapeutic support, or exploring other factors like diet.

**The ultimate message:** Things can get better. The healing needs to begin inside the parents - in their attitudes, their patience, their willingness to do the hard work of repair.
