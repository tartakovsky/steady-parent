# Helping Kids with Grief, Loss and Bereavement

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/helping-kids-with-grief-loss-and-bereavement

## Summary

This article provides guidance for parents on supporting children through grief and loss. It emphasizes that grief is a natural part of life and that parents serve as crucial role models in how they handle loss. The key message is that allowing children to fully experience grief - rather than suppressing it - leads to healthier emotional development and greater capacity for joy.

## Key Concepts

### The Parent's Role in Grief

- Parents provide a "holding environment" for children experiencing grief - both physically and emotionally
- Children need permission and support to grieve, not protection from difficult feelings
- How parents handle loss models grief processing for children
- Accepting loss as normal is important for optimal mental health

### Core Principle on Grief and Joy

The article emphasizes a fundamental connection between grieving and emotional wellbeing: the more we allow ourselves to grieve when necessary, the more joy we can feel. Blocking grief doesn't make it disappear - it takes up residence as a shadow in our psyches, making us fragile and prone to depression.

### Talking to Children About Death

**What to say:**
- Be simple and direct: "Grandma's body stopped working and she died"
- Acknowledge the sadness and that you will miss them
- Emphasize that "people die, but love doesn't die"
- Explain that most people live a very long time before they die
- Note that the body usually heals itself; death only happens with serious injury or illness

**What to avoid:**
- Don't equate death with sleep (creates bedtime fears)
- Don't say the person "just went away" (creates separation anxiety)
- Be careful with religious messages like "God wanted her close" (can confuse children)

### The Five Stages of Grief

1. **Denial** - Resisting the reality of the loss
2. **Anger** - Lashing out to avoid feeling pain
3. **Bargaining** - Trying to gain control when feeling powerless
4. **Depression** - Feeling deep sadness as reality sinks in
5. **Acceptance** - Allowing grief leads to diminishment; able to remember with appreciation rather than only pain

Note: These stages don't always occur in order; people move back and forth between them.

### The Nature of Grief Over Time

Grief is compared to a slice of pie: at first it pervades the entire circle of life, but gradually the shadowed slice becomes smaller. Grief never fully disappears, but becomes manageable.

## Practical Strategies

### Supporting Grieving Children

- Let children choose whether to attend funerals; describe what it will be like beforehand
- Expect and be patient with acting out - children often blame themselves for death and may test if you'll stop loving them
- Give ongoing opportunities to ask questions and talk about the loss
- If children report "talking" with a deceased loved one, don't correct them - this may help healing
- Read books about death together
- Create rituals of remembrance, both large and small

### Ongoing Support

- Mention the lost loved one's name naturally in conversation over time
- Don't insist children grieve when they're trying to be happy
- Don't act as if the loss didn't happen
- Children need rituals with safe space for grieving AND a defined endpoint so they can play without guilt

### What Helps Children Heal

Children who successfully navigate loss:
- Find ways to feel connected to the person they've lost
- Are able to go on with their lives
- Have "safe spaces" (like school) where they can forget for a time
- Know parents are available when they want to talk
- Receive permission to go on with their lives

## Key Quotes

> "Those who can't weep with their whole heart don't know how to laugh either."

> "If we are so uncomfortable with loss that we cannot allow our child to mourn, we give a destructive message that is far reaching."

> "People die, but love doesn't die."

> "Grief is just love with no place to go." -- Jamie Anderson

## Main Takeaways

1. **Grief is natural and necessary** - Allowing children to grieve fully leads to healthier emotional development
2. **Parents model grief processing** - Your comfort with loss teaches children how to handle their own
3. **Be direct but gentle** - Use simple, honest language when explaining death
4. **Provide both space and support** - Children need safe places to grieve AND permission to continue living
5. **Unprocessed grief is harmful** - Blocked grief becomes stuck and compromises emotional resilience
6. **Connection continues** - Help children maintain a sense of connection with those they've lost while moving forward
