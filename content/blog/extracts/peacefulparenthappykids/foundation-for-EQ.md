# How to Raise an Emotionally Intelligent Child

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/foundation-for-EQ

---

## Core Concept

Emotional Intelligence (EQ) is the ability to manage emotions in healthy ways, and it predicts life success and happiness more fundamentally than IQ. EQ encompasses:
- Emotional self-knowledge and self-acceptance
- Sensitivity to others' cues
- Empathy (seeing and feeling from another's perspective)
- Regulating anxiety and anger to handle emotionally charged issues constructively

A child's EQ begins with their relationship with their primary caregiver.

---

## Key Principles

### 1. Trust Forms the Foundation
- Respond quickly to infant cries and hold them when they want connection
- Security and trust develop from earliest caregiver interactions
- This creates the base upon which all emotional intelligence builds

### 2. Your Calm Regulates Their Calm
- Infants pick up anxiety from parents (confirmed by research dating back to Harry Stack Sullivan)
- Parents' touch, voices, and movements either soothe or stimulate anxiety
- Use mindfulness practices to manage your own state
- Your calm helps your child build a calmer brain and nervous system

### 3. All Emotions Are Acceptable (Not All Behaviors)
- Limit actions as necessary while empathizing with feelings
- Children can't choose their emotions any more than they can choose their limbs
- They can and must choose what to do with those feelings
- Empathizing while setting limits works best

### 4. Empathy Enables Processing
- Your empathy helps children accept their emotions
- Acceptance allows feelings to resolve and dissipate
- Children learn their emotional life is not dangerous, shameful, but universal and manageable
- They learn they are wholly acceptable, including the less pleasant parts

### 5. Repression Backfires
- Disapproving of fear or anger doesn't stop the feelings - it forces repression
- Repressed feelings don't fade; they are trapped and looking for a way out
- Without conscious control, they emerge unmodulated (hitting siblings, nightmares, nervous tics)
- Freely expressed feelings naturally dissipate

### 6. Self-Soothing Is Built Through Being Soothed
- Neural wiring for self-soothing develops by first being soothed by someone else
- Babies' brains and nervous systems don't develop adequately without being held and soothed
- Unmet infant needs feel life-threatening; emotions become overwhelming
- Without early soothing, children struggle with self-regulation because every feeling triggers anxiety

## Practical Strategies

### Emotion Coaching
Rage doesn't dissipate until it feels heard. Accepting and reflecting feelings doesn't mean agreeing with them.

**Formula: Listen - Acknowledge - Reflect**

Example phrases:
- "You sure are angry at your brother. Tell me about it."
- "You must be really disappointed."
- "This day just isn't going the way you wanted, is it?"

### Problem-Solving Support
- When emotions are understood and accepted, feelings lose their charge
- This creates an opening for problem-solving
- Sometimes kids can solve problems themselves; sometimes they need help brainstorming
- Resist handling problems for them unless asked - doing so signals you don't have confidence in their ability

### Handling Anger Constructively
- Look under anger for the hurt or fear it defends against
- Avoid getting sucked into battles; stay calm to keep the situation safe
- Children will show you the hurt or fears driving bad behavior when they feel safe
- Use words, not force
- Don't let your own anger escalate

### Intervene Before You Escalate
- If you're yelling, you didn't intervene effectively before it was necessary
- Asking four times over twenty minutes isn't effective - a different, more involved approach is needed
- Pick up a tired dawdling toddler; firmly insist on help from teenagers - while still calm
- Maintain a peaceful tone and model emotional management
- Screaming teaches children nothing useful and much that is harmful

## Important Warnings

### Don't Undermine Emotional Self-Knowledge
- Respect children's feelings about others
- If uncomfortable with Uncle Herman's hug, teach them to shake hands instead
- Let them make decisions about who they play with
- Affirm their ability to trust their own feelings, including discomfort they can't identify
- Children need to make their own decisions about what feels safe

### Don't Punish, Distract, or Shame Emotions
- Don't say "A little scratch like that doesn't hurt" or "Big boys don't cry"
- Acknowledge, empathize, let them show you what happened
- Give time to process, then they'll be ready to move on

## Creating Space for Hard Conversations

Children may struggle with physical differences, absent parents, learning disabilities, adoption, divorce, alcoholism, school fears, bullying, or difficult coaches.

**Strategies:**
- Overcome your own discomfort with difficult topics first
- Create regular times when children can bring up concerns
- Quiet minutes at bedtime after lights out are powerful
- Ask: "What was great today? What was hard?"
- Darkness and impending bedtime often help reserved children open up

---

## The Modeling Principle

What children see you do is what they will do:
- Do you snap at people under stress?
- Have minor tantrums when things go wrong?
- Can you stay calm during emotionally charged discussions?
- Do you empathize when feelings are expressed?

Your child will learn to handle their emotions by watching you handle yours.

## Research Foundation

- A 50-year British longitudinal study found life success and happiness strongly correlated with emotional intelligence
- Psychologist Harry Stack Sullivan identified infant anxiety transmission from parents nearly 100 years ago
- John Gottman's research (5:1 ratio of positive to negative interactions predicting divorce) extends to parent-child relationships
