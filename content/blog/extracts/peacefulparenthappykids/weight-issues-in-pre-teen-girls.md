# Helping Preteen Girls Develop Healthy Body Image and Eating Habits

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

---

## The Core Challenge

The central question for parents of preteen girls around weight is: How do I help my emerging teen develop healthy habits that will keep her new womanly body fit, without harming her self-esteem, contributing to the formation of an eating disorder, or burdening her with my own longtime body/weight/food issues?

This framing matters because all three risks are interconnected. Parents who have struggled with weight themselves often inadvertently transmit their anxieties and unhealthy patterns to their daughters. The goal is to break this cycle while supporting healthy development.

---

## Focus on Fitness, Not Appearance

**Why this matters:** Telling a daughter to stay active so she looks attractive encourages **self-objectification**, which is a core psychological mechanism underlying eating disorders. When girls learn to view their bodies as objects to be evaluated by others, they become vulnerable to extreme measures to control that evaluation.

**The alternative approach:** Encourage daily physical activity framed around being fit and healthy, not around appearance. The concepts to introduce are building aerobic health, flexibility, and muscle strength.

**Making it work practically:** Participate with your daughter rather than just telling her to exercise. Concrete examples:
- Take a dance class together
- Do yoga together
- Take walks and bike rides together
- Buy pedometers or even a treadmill and compete (in a fun way) to see who logs the most miles in a week

The participation element is key because it transforms exercise from a prescription into a shared activity, and it models that physical activity is a normal part of adult life.

## Nutrition Education

**The gut microbiome connection:** The balance of bacteria in our gut microbiome depends on what we eat. Consuming processed foods and sugar disrupts this balance. This provides a scientific framework for understanding why food choices matter beyond calories.

**Empowering teens through media literacy:** Food companies aggressively target kids and adolescents to get them to buy junk food, even while they are well aware of the negative health impact. Many teenagers become less interested in fast food when they understand that food marketers are purposely trying to addict them.

This works because teenagers are developmentally primed to resist being manipulated and controlled. Reframing junk food consumption as "being manipulated by corporations" rather than "being unhealthy" taps into this adolescent psychology.

## Practical Healthy Eating at Home

**The protein principle:** If a child eats mostly carbs, she will get hungry sooner. Protein at every meal helps maintain stable blood sugar and reduces cravings.

**Developing a healthy snack repertoire:** Work with your daughter to develop a list of healthy protein snacks she actually likes:
- Yogurt
- Peanut butter on toast (make sure it's not hydrogenated)
- Deviled eggs
- Lentil soup
- Chicken noodle soup
- Nuts mixed with raisins
- Leftover chicken or salmon
- Cubed tofu dipped in soy sauce

**The self-check question:** Rather than controlling her food choices, teach her to ask herself: "Am I eating a well-rounded diet that includes 7 vegetables and fruits daily?" This builds self-monitoring skills rather than external control.

## Understanding Emotional Eating

**The psychology behind food cravings:** Most of us crave "comfort" food when we feel uncomfortable emotions inside, including boredom, sadness, and anxiety. The function of comfort food is to numb these feelings.

**Breaking the automatic pattern:** The more aware we are of those emotions, the more quickly they move through us, and the less they drive us to numb them by eating. Awareness is the intervention.

**The body check-in technique:** Teach your daughter to ask herself: "Am I actually physically hungry, or do fries just sound good?" This creates a pause between impulse and action.

**Testing real hunger:** If your daughter claims she is actually hungry when asking for junk food, offer her cheese sticks and an apple (healthy options you can carry with you). If she does not want them, her body is not really hungry. This is not a punishment; it is a diagnostic tool that helps her distinguish physical hunger from emotional craving.

**Regarding "forbidden foods":** Most kids think of fast food as a treat and will test parents to see if they will give them that treat. It is the parents' job to set limits on this, just like limits on everything else. You can either make these forbidden foods (eaten only a couple times a year) or "special occasion foods" rather than regular options. The key is that they should not be part of daily life.

## Rethinking Body Standards

**The puberty weight gain context:** Girls usually put weight on as they approach puberty, and this is not unhealthy. Most pediatricians warn parents about this at the annual checkup around age ten. A child in the 90th percentile for weight may not actually be overweight; she may simply be developing normally.

**Consult the pediatrician privately:** Have a conversation with her pediatrician, without your daughter present, about whether she is actually overweight. Ask specifically about her BMI (body-mass index). Do not assume that higher percentile equals unhealthy.

**The cultural distortion:** Start by consciously ditching the idea that your daughter should fit the overly thin model that dominates our culture. Fashion models are about 25% thinner than the majority of American women. The average woman in the U.S. is 5'4" and 143 lbs. Normal is more like a Botticelli Venus than a fashion model.

**The estrogen factor:** Since estrogen causes body fat to be stored in the hips, thighs, belly and buttocks, women naturally tend to be voluptuous. This is biology, not a problem to fix.

## Why Diets Are Harmful

**Do not put a preteen on a diet, even if the pediatrician confirms she is overweight.** The reasons:

1. **Metabolic damage:** Diets mess up the metabolism and make it very hard to lose weight long-term
2. **Yoyo pattern:** They set up a lifetime of yoyo weight and feeling deprived
3. **Emotional eating trigger:** The more your daughter feels like you do not love her the way she is, the more she will overeat to push away the feeling of being not good enough

**The alternative approach:** Instead of dieting, throw out all the unhealthy food in your house and just do not bring any more in. Cook healthfully and teach your daughter to eat healthfully. This gradually helps her stabilize at a healthier weight, and it benefits everyone in the family.

**For other family members:** If someone in your family who is in perfect health misses ice cream or other treats, they can have their cone out of the house when your daughter is not around. The home environment should support healthy eating for all.

## Parental Role Modeling

**The dieting-binging connection:** Kids are more likely to have severe weight issues if their parents constantly diet and binge. These behaviors go together because restriction creates deprivation which triggers binging.

**Working on your own issues:** Acknowledge your own issues around food and your body, and resolve to heal them. This is not optional if you want to help your daughter; your unresolved issues will leak through in subtle ways.

**Accepting your body type:** Make peace with your basic body type, which is not something you can change. Fighting against your natural body type models dissatisfaction to your daughter.

**Throw out the scale:** Scales lead to unhealthy body obsession. Weighing yourself regularly models that number-watching is important.

**Self-talk matters:** Never say anything mean about your own body. Resist commenting on other women's bodies. Your daughter is absorbing your attitudes even when you think she is not listening.

**Positive self-relationship:** Start lavishing love on yourself, along with healthy food and exercise habits. Model that caring for yourself is an act of love, not punishment.

---

## Rejecting Deprivation

**The deprivation-craving cycle:** Depriving oneself just sets up cravings. This is why strict diets backfire.

**The alternative philosophy:** Revel in all the pleasures of the senses: a bubble bath, a deep breath, a good stretch, a bird's song, the beauty of a garden, the warm sun, a delicious meal. Focus on the bountifulness of your life, and teach her to do so as well.

**The occasional indulgence principle:** Do not make food treats a part of your daily life (except fruit), but when one crosses your path, go ahead and indulge. If it is not a habit, it is fine. The key distinction is between occasional enjoyment and regular habit.

**Never skip meals:** Skipping meals causes hunger that induces us to eat whatever is available quickly, no matter how unnutritious. Regular meals prevent the desperate hunger that leads to poor choices.

## Educating About Eating Disorders

**Why this education matters:** We are barraged with images of overly thin women until we think those images are normal. They are not. Explicit education counters the implicit messaging.

**Key facts to share:**
- Most teen girls think they are overweight, even though they are not
- Weight gain is a normal part of developing as a woman
- Fitness is the goal, not some warped cultural ideal of over-thinness
- Fashion models represent an extreme, not a healthy standard

## Building Self-Esteem Through Positive Attention

**The principle:** What we pay attention to in our kids is what flourishes. Communicate that you adore her just the way she is.

**The practice:** Catch her doing things right, all day long, and acknowledge them specifically. Generic praise is less effective than specific observations.

**Example acknowledgments that build self-esteem:**
- "I noticed how nice you were to your sister when you helped her with her homework."
- "I love it when you practice the piano without being reminded."
- "I love how cheerful you are in the morning."
- "Thanks for taking out the trash with only one reminder!"
- "I know that you were mad about that, and I really appreciate you controlling your temper so we could talk about it reasonably. That shows real maturity and self control."
- "I love watching you play basketball. You're so fast, and such a good team player."
- "Wow, your work in math is really improving!"
- "Snuggling you like this makes me so happy, whether we talk or not."
- "I notice you came right home and started your homework without any reminders!"
- "I love our conversations in the car."
- "Thanks for telling me about your fight with your friend. I admire how you apologized to her."
- "One of the things I love about you is how you always give the dog lots of attention."

Notice these examples acknowledge character, effort, growth, and connection, not appearance.

## The Stress-Weight Connection

**The biological mechanism:** Stress and too little sleep increase levels of cortisol, which:
- Is a powerful appetite trigger
- Makes the body put on weight
- Makes us more irritable
- Makes it harder to fall asleep at night (creating a vicious cycle)

**Sleep requirements:** A 12-year-old needs about ten hours of sleep a night.

**Stress reduction strategies:**
- Brainstorm with your daughter about how she can get enough sleep and decrease stress
- Work with her so she feels like a competent problem solver (empowerment reduces stress)
- Simply being there to listen to her talk about her life on a regular basis, without judging, prescribing, or fixing, will reduce her stress level considerably
- Teach her to process her emotions in a healthy way

## Addressing Emotional Hunger

**The "hungry heart" concept:** The phrase "Everybody has a hungry heart" (Bruce Springsteen) captures the idea that sometimes we eat to fill emotional needs that food cannot actually satisfy.

**Common emotional hungers in 12-year-old girls:**
- Self-consciousness
- Anxiety about changing bodies
- Anxiety about peer dynamics
- Fear about losing connection to parents while stepping into new independence
- Starvation for physical contact (because parents become nervous about physical affection as their daughter starts looking more like a young woman than a little girl)

**The physical affection solution:** Many tween girls are starved for physical contact. Try offering more physical affection:
- Hug her as often as possible (when you wake her up in the morning, before she leaves for school, when you see each other again after school, etc.)
- Make sure you get some snuggle time while you chat with her daily, either at bedtime or on the couch
- Brush her hair for her
- Give her a shoulder or foot massage

**Beyond physical touch:** Sometimes being seen, accepted, and listened to fills our emotional hunger better than anything physical. Work to stay connected to your daughter as she begins developing appropriate independence. Make sure you spend intimate time with her and lavish affection on her every single day.

**The bottom line:** "Don't let her look for love in a bag of chips." When emotional needs are met through connection, the drive to meet them through food diminishes.

## Key Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **Focusing on appearance** rather than fitness and health
2. **Putting a preteen on a diet** even if she is overweight
3. **Modeling dieting and binging** yourself
4. **Saying negative things about your own body** or commenting on other women's bodies
5. **Using the scale** to measure success
6. **Making strict rules about forbidden foods** that set up deprivation and craving
7. **Skipping meals** which leads to desperate hunger and poor choices
8. **Lecturing or prescribing** when she talks about her life instead of just listening
9. **Withdrawing physical affection** as she enters puberty
10. **Assuming higher weight percentile equals unhealthy** without consulting with her pediatrician
