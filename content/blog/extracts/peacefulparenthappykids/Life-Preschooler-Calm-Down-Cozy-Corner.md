# Cozy Corners and Co-regulation to Calm Your Child

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/Life-Preschooler-Calm-Down-Cozy-Corner

## Summary

This article explains how to help children develop emotional self-regulation through co-regulation with a calm parent and the use of a designated "cozy corner" space. The key insight is that children cannot learn to self-regulate when isolated - they need a calm, supportive adult to help them feel safe first. Over time, with consistent practice, children will begin using the cozy corner independently.

## Key Concepts

### Co-Regulation

Co-regulation is when a child learns to self-regulate through interaction with a calm, self-regulated parent. The child's nervous system receives signals from the calm adult that the situation is not an emergency, which helps shift them out of fight-or-flight mode and into their thinking brain.

**How it works:**
- The parent stays calm and warm when the child is upset
- The child feels safe, seen, soothed, and understood
- This safety signal allows the child to shift from self-protection mode to their thinking brain
- From there, they can begin understanding their emotions and developing coping strategies

**Developmental timeline:**
- Begins in infancy
- Continues through preschool and grade school years
- Even teens benefit - those whose parents stay calm during their upset develop better vagal tone
- Adults in couples can co-regulate each other too

### The Science of Soothing

When a parent soothes an upset child, the child's body releases calming hormones and neurotransmitters, which strengthens self-soothing neural networks. This helps develop "vagal tone" - the vagus nerve becomes more effective at calming emotions when upset.

**Benefits:**
- Builds neural circuits for self-calming
- Develops resilience (ability to bounce back from adversity)
- These benefits last for life
- Also builds trust and strengthens the parent-child relationship, leading to better cooperation

### Time-In vs. Time-Out

Sending an upset child to their room alone sends the message that their emotions are shameful and not allowed. Instead, "time-in" means staying with the child and helping them calm down by loving them through their upset.

**Why time-out backfires:**
- Children learn their feelings are unacceptable
- They learn to repress emotions
- Repressed emotions don't stay stuffed - they bubble up and explode later
- This is why many adults still have "temper tantrums" and use food or screens to cope

### The Cozy Corner (Calm-Down Corner)

A designated comfortable space in the home where anyone can go to calm down.

**Critical rule: Never send a child there alone.** Sending a child to the cozy corner feels like banishment/rejection and will be received the same as a "naughty step." The parent must go WITH the child.

**How to establish it:**
1. Create the space with your child's input on soothing elements
2. Go there for quiet times even when not upset (build positive associations)
3. Always accompany your child there when they're upset
4. Model using it yourself when YOU get upset
5. Over time, the child will begin going there independently

**Recommended items for the cozy corner:**
- Comfortable seating (bean bag chair, fluffy rug, easy chair)
- Photo of family (research shows photos of trusted adults help calm children)
- Stuffed animals
- Emotions chart showing faces with different feelings
- Books about emotions
- Glitter jar to shake
- Bubbles (blowing shifts breathing to deeper, slower pace)
- Calming sensory activities (small figures, sensory bags/boxes)
- Audio of guided meditation for children
- Recording of parent's voice saying loving things or singing a special song

**For multiple children:** Have separate seating for each child and enforce a "soft voices only" rule.

## Practical Strategies

### When Child is Getting Dysregulated

1. **Early intervention:** Suggest "cozy time" - snuggle up and read a book together
2. The connection and shift to thinking brain often helps re-regulate
3. If too far gone for a book, use time-in approach

### During Meltdowns

1. Stay calm yourself (essential for co-regulation)
2. Offer understanding and calm presence
3. Give the message: "You're safe, I love you even when you're upset, I want to help you through this"
4. Take child to cozy corner WITH you
5. After they're calmer, help them solve the underlying problem

### Building Independence

1. Consistently practice going to cozy corner together
2. Visit during calm times to build positive associations
3. Model using it yourself when upset
4. Eventually child will initiate going there themselves
5. When busy with another child, you can say "I'll meet you in the cozy corner in a few minutes" and they'll go
