# Understanding and Addressing Toddler Aggression

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

---

## Core Principle: Toddler Aggression is Developmentally Normal

Most two-year-olds experiment with aggression because they are still learning to control themselves. This is completely normal behavior, not a sign that something has "gone wrong" with the child. However, parents are right to want to address it early before patterns become entrenched.

## What NOT to Do: Anti-Patterns That Backfire

### Hitting Back to "Teach a Lesson"

**Many studies have shown that hitting kids, even to show them that hitting hurts, only perpetuates more hitting.** The logic parents often use - "I'll hit them so they understand how it feels" - produces the opposite of the intended effect.

The mechanism: When we treat children with compassion and kindness, even when they hit others, they learn not to hit. When we hit them, they learn to hit. Children model what they experience, not what they're told.

### Timeouts Create Problems

Timeouts, while better than hitting, are not effective and actually encourage bad behavior. Dr. Laura has observed repeatedly that timeouts create power struggles and cause children to become more rebellious, sometimes to the point of hitting more.

The dynamic: Timeouts position parent and child as adversaries. The child feels rejected and punished, which increases their internal distress and acting out, rather than helping them learn self-regulation.

## The Correct Approach: A Two-Scenario Framework

### Scenario 1: When Your Child Hits Another Child

**Step 1: Attend to the victim first, ignore your child momentarily**

Immediately and kindly attend to the other child while ignoring your son for the moment. This serves two purposes:
- Your child sees that hitting does not get attention (removing the reward)
- Your child observes that their hitting has genuinely hurt another person (natural consequence visibility)

**Step 2: Remove your child from the situation**

Once the other child is recovered and being tended to by another adult, pick up your child and remove them from the situation. Be kind but firm.

Script to use: *"We never hit. When you hit, we can't play with the other children."*

**Step 3: End the play session**

Take your child home. If this happens in your own home, take them to their room. The key insight: stay with them while they calm down before rejoining others. Ideally, have the other children leave so the message is unmistakably clear - hitting ends play.

**Step 4: Empathize with your child's feelings while maintaining the limit**

Your child will likely be very angry. Acknowledge this:
- *"You wish you could play more. You don't want to leave."*
- *"I am really sorry, but when you hit, we can't play with the other kids."*

**Step 5: Give your child hope and protect their self-image**

Critical message: your child isn't bad, just little.

Script: *"Soon you will be a little older and you will remember not to hit. You will remember to ask me for help, or to use your words, instead."*

This gives them a vision of their future competent self and communicates your belief that they will develop this capacity.

### Scenario 2: When Your Child Hits You (the Parent)

**The relationship signal:** If your child is hitting you specifically, your relationship with them needs attention. This is diagnostic information, not just a behavior problem.

**Response framework:**

1. Set the limit clearly: *"We never hit."*
2. Acknowledge the emotion: *"I know you are angry."*
3. Redirect to acceptable expressions of anger:
   - *"When you are angry, you use your words and tell me."*
   - *"You can show me how angry you are by hitting the pillow."*
   - *"Or by yelling if we are outside."*
   - *"Or by showing me with your crayons on the paper how angry you are."*
4. Restate the limit: *"But we never hit."*

**The parent's internal work:** You must overcome your own anger at being hit and extend empathy to your son. This is essential. The combination of limit-setting plus empathy teaches children both that hitting is unacceptable AND positive ways to handle their feelings.

## Environmental Factors: The Marriage Connection

**Household tension manifests in children's behavior.** If parents are fighting, even if the child doesn't witness it directly, the child may act out the household tensions through aggression.

**Parental physical aggression is particularly damaging.** If a child observes any parental physical aggression, it is bad for them, and the parent should consider leaving the situation.

**Even "quiet" marital problems affect children.** Marital tension does not have to be loud or visible to impact the child. Divorce is hard on kids, but so is ongoing marital tension.

**Recommendation:** Consider counseling even if the problems seem contained. Even if one partner won't attend, individual counseling can help. The marriage may be salvageable with attention, but either way, working on oneself helps the child.

---

## The Deeper Insight: Parental Self-Work

**"Ultimately, the parents we are able to be always depends on who we are as people."**

If a parent has their own unhealed wounds, this affects their capacity to parent well. Working on healing oneself automatically reflects in one's child. This is not selfish - it is perhaps the most important parenting work there is.

**The fun factor:** A specialist noted that an aggressive child "needs more fun in his life." Dr. Laura extended this insight: the parent probably needs more fun too. She recommended creating a list of fun activities that don't have to cost anything, noting that part of the "fun quotient" depends on the parent's attitude.

## Summary of the Approach

The framework rests on three pillars:

1. **Clear, consistent limits** - "We never hit" is non-negotiable and stated calmly, not angrily
2. **Empathy for the child's emotions** - Their feelings are valid even when their behavior is not acceptable
3. **Teaching alternatives** - Children need to learn what TO do with big feelings, not just what not to do

The combination of kindness and firmness, empathy and limits, is what allows children to internalize self-control rather than simply fearing punishment. They learn that hitting doesn't work AND that they have other options for expressing their feelings.
