# The Reasons and Research Behind Peaceful Parenting

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/positive-discipline

---

## Core Definition: What Is a Peaceful Parent?

A peaceful parent is defined by three commitments that form the foundation of the approach:

1. **Regulating their own emotions** rather than taking them out on their child
2. **Strengthening and sweetening the relationship** so the child feels seen, secure, soothed, and safe
3. **Providing support** the child needs to meet expectations, rather than trying to control through threats, shame, or punishment

These three commitments can be summarized as the framework of **Calm, Connection, and Coaching**.

---

## Why Peaceful Parenting Works

Peaceful parenting uses love and connection as motivators, and these only get stronger over time. This is the key differentiator from fear-based approaches, which lose effectiveness as children grow.

Research shows that children are more open to parental guidance when they:
- Trust the parent
- Feel understood
- Feel connected

This openness to guidance is what helps children learn consideration and responsibility, resulting in happier children and parents.

**Comparative outcomes of different parenting styles:**

| Parenting Style | Child Outcome |
|-----------------|---------------|
| Strict/Punitive | Raises angry kids who lose interest in pleasing their parents |
| Permissive | Raises unhappy kids who test their parents |
| Peaceful | Raises emotionally resilient kids who WANT to do their best |

In both strict and permissive approaches, the child resists the parent's guidance and does not internalize self-discipline. Peaceful parenting is the only approach that produces children who genuinely want to cooperate.

## Why Punishment Does Not Help Kids Behave

### The Intuitive Appeal of Punishment

Fear seems like a time-honored and potent motivator. It appears to nip problem behavior in the bud quickly.

### What Research Actually Shows About Physical Punishment

Force teaches children all the wrong lessons. Children who are spanked learn:
- Might makes right
- Hitting is justified in some circumstances (specifically when you are bigger)
- People who supposedly love you may hurt you

**Long-term outcomes of physical discipline:**
- More aggressive toward other children
- More rebellious as teenagers
- More prone to depression as adults
- More prone to violent acting out as adults

### What Research Shows About Non-Physical Punishment

Even non-physical punishment does not help children behave better. Children who are regularly punished are:
- Less emotionally regulated
- Less self-disciplined
- Less resilient
- Less morally developed

All of these deficits are compared to other children their age.

### Why Punished Children Do Not "Learn Lessons"

Anyone who has ever been punished can attest to this dynamic: punished children become obsessed with fantasies of self-justification and revenge rather than considering how to control themselves to prevent future misbehavior.

**The motivation shift:** Instead of becoming motivated to change and avoid the misbehavior in the future, they become motivated to avoid more punishment. These are not the same thing at all.

**Behavioral outcomes:** Kids who are regularly punished are:
- More likely to repeat problem behavior than other children
- More likely to exhibit increasingly worse behavior
- More likely to engage in deception

## How Self-Discipline Actually Develops

### The Common Misconception

Most discipline approaches assume children learn to control themselves by developing more motivation and stronger "consciences."

### Why This Misconception Fails

"Doing the right thing" and overriding our "lesser" impulses does not result from:
- Admonishing ourselves to do better
- Making new and improved resolutions

If self-admonishment were sufficient, all adults would have perfectly balanced diets and fit bodies.

### The Actual Mechanism of Self-Discipline

**The secret of managing our impulses is becoming aware of and motivated by competing impulses.**

**Adult example:** "I'd like to go out drinking tonight, but I want to get a good night's sleep so I can do a good job at the big meeting tomorrow."

**Child example:** "I really want to skip my homework so I can play outside, but I don't want to face my teacher without it."

### The Progression to More Sophisticated Self-Regulation

**Initial stage (crimes of passion):**
- Adult: "This colleague is really attractive, but my marriage is too important to me."
- Child: "I really want to hit my sister over the head when she teases me like that, but Mom would be really mad."

**Mature stage:** The child moves from concern over losing parental love to awareness of what they want in their relationships:
- "I'm really annoyed at my sister right now, but I know that when she's not being obnoxious I do love her and I don't really want to hurt her."

This progression takes considerable maturity that children need parental help to develop. It takes practice. Children get this practice naturally as life deals them upsets and parents help them handle those upsets.

### The Key Enabling Condition

The key is providing children with the experience of relationships where compassion trumps anger.

**Why emotional state matters:** When the body is flushed with the hormones of "fight or flight," it is hard for anyone to:
- Make wise decisions
- Choose positively between competing priorities

### Why Punishment During Emotional Moments Fails

Helping children toward emotional insight and self-discipline does not happen in the heat of emotion, whether that emotion is:
- Related to the original transgression ("But she was teasing me!")
- Created by the punishing response ("I'll teach you to hit your sister! Take that!")

**What is needed instead:**
1. Reduce the amount of time the child spends in overcharged physical states of anger and fear
2. Give the child an opportunity to calm down and reflect
3. Once calm, work with them to strengthen positive motivation
4. Help them recognize and control their emotions so they can manage opposing impulses

## A Diagnostic Framework: Three Explanations for Misbehavior

When a child misbehaves, there are only three possible explanations:

### 1. She Does Not Know What Is Expected

**Solution:** Teaching is clearly in order.

**Examples of direct teaching:**
- "HOT! The stove is hot!"
- "We have to wait our turn for the slide."

**How most of this teaching happens:** It is modeled. Children learn desirable behavior from watching:
- Parents (thanking Aunt Jane for inviting you, waiting for the light to turn green)
- Classmates

### 2. She Does Know But Cannot Control Herself

**Solution:** Help her learn to manage herself through the competing impulse mechanism described above.

### 3. She Does Know But Does Not Care

**This is the most serious case.** When a child knows what is expected but does not care, the misbehavior is a symptom of a much greater problem.

**What is missing:** The competing impulse to control herself should come from her relationship with the parents. Children only learn to behave and manage themselves because:
- Parents want them to
- They want to please their parents

**What it means when this is absent:** If she does not care that she is upsetting her parents with her misbehavior, it means the relationship needs strengthening.

**The priority shift:** Children need guidance, but if the relationship is not strong enough to support that guidance, then the primary focus needs to be on repairing the relationship.

## The Role of Relationship as the Foundation of Behavior

### How Good Behavior Eventually Becomes Self-Sustaining

Eventually, children reap the rewards of good behavior:
- Good grades
- Self-esteem
- Approval from peers

At this point, good behavior begins to come naturally. It becomes part of their self-image, and they automatically act to preserve that self-image.

**Critical insight:** This positive way of being always starts with the child's desire to please the parent.

### Illustrative Example: The Sand Castle Child

A two-year-old on the beach was knocking down sand castles. He took such immense pleasure in this activity that it was infectious to watch. When his mother saw what he was doing and came running, he looked chagrined and allowed her to lead him reluctantly away.

**What this demonstrates:** His desire to be loved by her was already slightly stronger than his desire to knock down sand castles.

### Why Adults Do Not Knock Down Sand Castles

The answer to why adults do not run down the beach knocking down sand castles: We have discovered that it is more rewarding to be loved.

## Love as the Only Sustainable Leverage

**Core principle:** Love is the only leverage we have with our children.

**Why fear-based approaches fail long-term:**
- Fear and "Because I say so!" only last for as long as they can be physically enforced
- Every parent knows how fast children grow
- Fear works for a very short time, if it works at all

**Why love-based approaches succeed long-term:**
- Love becomes a more effective motivator over time
- It raises kids who WANT to behave

## Key Quotes

**Kathryn J. Kvols on the counterproductive nature of punishment:**
"Children misbehave when they feel discouraged or powerless. When you use discipline methods that overpower them or make them feel bad about themselves, you lower their self-esteem. It doesn't make sense to punish a child who is already feeling badly about herself and heap more discouragement on top of her."

---

## Etymology and Reframing

The word "discipline" has nothing to do with punishment. The root of "discipline" is "disciple," from the verb "to teach."

The real question becomes: What kind of discipline is most conducive to learning? And the ultimate goal of that learning is self-discipline, so the lesson does not have to be repeated.
