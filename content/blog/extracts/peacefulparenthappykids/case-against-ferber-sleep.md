# The Case Against Ferber Sleep Training: A Critical Analysis

## Source and Author Perspective

This analysis comes from a psychologist who follows research on infant development. The author acknowledges personal bias against Ferberizing but bases concerns on research about babies doing better when parents respond to crying, regardless of time of day.

**Key context about Richard Ferber himself**: Ferber is trained in physical health, not mental health. He readily admits he is not trained in infant psychology. Most notably, Ferber has said in interviews that he regrets some of his advice, feels badly that child health professionals encourage parents to leave very young babies to cry, and now says co-sleeping is acceptable.

---

## How the Ferber Method Works

### The Basic Protocol

**Minimum age requirement**: Never start with a baby younger than three months.

**The graduated waiting sequence**:
1. Let baby cry for 5 minutes, then go in to reassure verbally and by patting (do not pick up)
2. Leave, let baby cry for 10 minutes, return to reassure again
3. Leave, let baby cry for 15 minutes, return to reassure
4. Continue extending intervals with each cycle

**Vomiting protocol**: If the baby vomits, clean them up (preferably without picking up), but leave in crib and continue the process.

### Typical Outcomes and Timelines

- With very determined babies, crying can continue all night
- More commonly, baby becomes exhausted and falls asleep after a few hours
- When baby reawakens later in the night, the entire process is repeated
- Subsequent crying intervals are often shorter, either because baby has "given up on the parent staying" or because of exhaustion
- Sometimes intervals are longer because baby is re-energized or is "an extremely determined person, who will someday accomplish great things by virtue of his strong will"
- Crying usually diminishes on subsequent nights as baby learns not to expect parent to stay

### Why Babies Stop Crying

The author frames this carefully: babies "do eventually give up calling for their parents, and sleep." The phrasing "give up" rather than "learn to self-soothe" is significant. "Because they do not yet talk, and live so completely in the moment, we do not hear from them the next morning how they felt about the experience."

## Problems with the Method's Effectiveness

### Not Universal Success

**This approach does not work on all children, even when parents are consistent.** Some babies are still crying on the seventh night in a row.

### Medical Complications

It is not uncommon for babies to get an ear infection during the process from congestion caused by crying. When this happens, Ferberizing must be discontinued during the round of antibiotics, then re-initiated later.

### Requires Repeated Application

Any change in routine (brief illness, trip to grandma's) requires parents to respond to baby's cries, which then necessitates repeating the entire Ferberizing process again. This means the experience must be endured repeatedly by both baby and parents.

---

## Physiological Effects of Sustained Uncomforted Crying

### Documented Physical Responses

**Well-documented effects of sustained, uncomforted infant crying**:
- Increased heart rate and blood pressure
- Reduced oxygen levels
- Elevated cerebral blood pressure
- Depleted energy reserves and oxygen
- Cardiac stress
- Skyrocketing cortisol, adrenalin and other stress hormones
- Disrupted immune system
- Disrupted digestion

### The Cortisol Finding

The most recent claim that letting kids "cry it out" without reassurance may cause lasting damage: when a baby is left to cry alone, cortisol levels shoot up, indicating distress. This finding indicates the baby is experiencing genuine physiological stress, not just "fussing."

### Brain Development Implications

**The reasonable inference**: If this stress response is repeated over time, these babies would build a slightly different brain, more prone to "fight, flight or freeze" responses.

---

## The Trauma Analogy

### Adult Comparison

With adults, even one panic-inducing experience like a car accident or mugging that causes an extreme stress response can have ongoing stress effects for years.

### Application to Babies

Since babies' heart rates and blood pressure soar during Ferberizing, the author argues there can be no doubt that sleep training without parental comfort causes the experience to be indelibly etched on memory, similar to how any panic situation can evoke strong feelings years later.

**Why preverbal memory may be more powerful, not less**: The fact that the memory is sensory and preverbal gives it more power because it cannot be adequately processed. The baby has no cognitive framework to understand or contextualize what is happening.

---

## The Central Debate: Does Intermittent Reassurance Protect the Baby?

### The Pro-Ferber Position

Advocates claim that because the parent keeps returning to the child's room, this offers reassurance that the child has not been abandoned. They argue this keeps the experience from traumatizing the baby in the way that just letting them "cry it out" does.

### The Anti-Ferber Counter-Argument

Some anti-Ferber critics argue the opposite: the parent coming into the room and ignoring the baby's distress might actually increase the trauma by undermining the baby's trust in the parent. The parent is present but deliberately withholding comfort despite the baby's clear communication of need.

---

## What the Baby May Be Learning

### Lesson 1: Parent Cannot Be Depended On

The baby learns that the parent will regularly desert her when she needs them most.

### Lesson 2: Powerlessness

The baby learns she is powerless to have an impact on her world in the ways that most matter to her. Her communication (crying) does not produce the expected response.

### Lesson 3: Hostile World

The baby learns her world is a cold and lonely place.

### Lesson 4: Undermined Trust

The most important developmental work a baby is doing at this stage is learning how to trust. The author asks: "Why sabotage that?"

### Lesson 5: Not Lovable

When the parent comes back into the room and tells her to go to sleep (while she is clearly distressed), the baby concludes she is not, in the deepest dark of the night, really lovable. She may even conclude the parent is intentionally tormenting her.

### Long-Term Implications

These early lessons may underlie the child's sense of self and worldview for the rest of her life. The author notes insomnia is rampant in our culture, and some Ferber critics argue that adults who can't fall asleep without the TV on, or who wake at night and can't sleep, are "Ferber casualties."

---

## Nuanced Position and Acknowledgments

### When It May Be Acceptable

The author has heard of families where the baby learns to fall asleep with a few minutes of crying and never needs to be retrained. "In those cases, it seems to me a wonderful solution." The key difference: brief crying with successful outcome versus prolonged, repeated crying over multiple nights.

### Personal Observations

The author knows many kids who were Ferbered as babies (by friends who shall remain nameless). "These kids all seem fine to me."

### Hierarchy of Harms

**Ferbering is a risk factor, but not the worst thing you can do**:
- Regular yelling because you're exhausted would be worse
- Sleep deprivation definitely makes you a worse parent

### Why It Still Matters

Ferbering is "a risk factor, and an avoidable one" because gentler alternatives exist.

---

## The Key Distinction: Crying vs. Uncomforted Crying

**Critical insight**: It isn't crying that affects little ones negatively, or even disappointment, or sadness. What's bad for brain development is being left to cry uncomforted.

This distinction is central to the entire argument. Babies can experience negative emotions safely when a parent is present and responsive. The damage comes from distress without support.

---

## Guidelines for Older Babies and Toddlers

### Age Threshold

After one year, kids do fine learning to sleep without the parent IF the parent stays with the child while she's learning.

### Why Verbal Ability Matters

Once the baby becomes verbal, the risks are greatly decreased because she understands so much better what's happening. Language provides a framework for making sense of the experience.

### Non-Negotiable Rule

It's always essential for the parent to stay in the room with the baby while the baby learns to put herself back to sleep. Parents need to parent at night, just like they parent during the day.

---

## Alternative Approaches Mentioned

### Gentle Sleep Habits Starting at Three Months

You can begin encouraging gentle sleep habits that make it more likely your child will sleep at night as early as three months.

### Elizabeth Pantley's No Cry Sleep Solution

Referenced as a gentler alternative method for teaching babies to sleep.

---

## Research Limitations

The author acknowledges it's hard to evaluate research in this area because there are so many other factors (many of which are arguably more important) in how babies develop. The author's view is that susceptibility to lasting damage could be caused by many aspects of childhood in 21st century North America and would need to be triggered by later trauma to play out. However, "there is no question that leaving children to cry repeatedly could influence the developing nervous system."

---

## Summary of Core Arguments

1. **Ferber lacks relevant expertise** - trained in physical health, not infant psychology
2. **Even Ferber has expressed regrets** about how his advice has been applied
3. **Physiological stress is real and documented** - cortisol, heart rate, blood pressure all spike
4. **Preverbal trauma may be especially powerful** because it cannot be cognitively processed
5. **Intermittent reassurance may not protect and might worsen things** by demonstrating the parent's deliberate withholding of comfort
6. **Trust is the core developmental task** being potentially undermined
7. **Gentler alternatives exist** that achieve the same goal without the risks
8. **The key variable is uncomforted crying** - not crying itself, but crying without responsive presence
