# Raising a Child Who Loves to Read

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/child-loves-read

---

## The Core Argument: Why Reading Matters

**Reading to children and discussing the book as you read is the single best way to help your child love reading** - more effective than educational products like Baby Einstein. This isn't just about building reading comprehension skills. The deeper goal is nurturing a genuine love of reading, because when children love to read:

- They choose to read independently (without being forced)
- They become better readers through practice
- They score higher on achievement tests across all subject areas
- They develop greater content knowledge than non-readers
- School performance correlates more directly with reading scores than any other single indicator

## The Problem: Why Most Kids Stop Reading

Most parents start with good intentions - buying board books for babies and hoping their children will love reading. Yet by middle school, most kids stop reading books that aren't assigned in school. In the USA, by the end of middle school, two-thirds of all students do not attain proficiency in literacy and comprehension skills.

**What goes wrong:** The habit of reading never gets truly ingrained during childhood. Kids love leafing through books as toddlers, looking at pictures. They may enjoy reading as early elementary schoolers. But reading is hard work, and modern life offers many easier ways to be entertained. Early reading often feels more like work than play. These children never reach that "delicious place where reading a good book is more fun than almost anything."

The transition from picture books to chapter books is particularly vulnerable. A child might be able to read simple books, but their imagination craves more developed plots and characters. Those more sophisticated books are agonizing work with too many unfamiliar words. The labor distracts from the story, and enjoyment disappears.

## Strategy 1: Start Early and Create Positive Associations

### Read from the Earliest Age - Not Just at Bedtime

Board books and cloth books should be among your child's first toys. Carry them in the diaper bag alongside snacks. The goal is to make books a constant presence, not a special occasion.

### Create "Cozy Time" as a Connection Ritual

**Cozy time** is a ritual where both parent and child associate love, cuddling, and physical comfort with reading. Any time either of you needs a break, grab a book and read. This works:
- Post-tantrum (as calming transition)
- During lunch
- After school
- While you have your coffee
- Any moment that calls for connection

The key insight: you're pairing the emotional experience of being loved and comforted with the activity of reading. This creates a deep positive association that will sustain interest in reading even when it becomes more challenging later.

## Strategy 2: Remove All Pressure Around Learning to Read

### The Panic Theory of Reading Difficulty

Some experts argue that when kids struggle to learn to read, it's not because their brain isn't catching on. It's because they feel pressure to understand the "squiggles on the page," and they panic. When the brain is in a state of emergency, it cannot learn. This creates a negative feedback loop: pressure -> panic -> inability to learn -> more pressure.

Reference: "Is Dyslexia a School-Produced Disorder?" by Dr. Peter Gray explores this theory in depth.

### Critical Warning: Do Not Teach Your Child to Read

**Teaching your child to read will take all the fun out of reading and introduce pressure that will backfire.** The distinction here is between creating an environment where reading happens naturally versus applying explicit instruction and pressure.

If a child wants to teach themselves to read, don't stop them. But don't push it, and don't make early reading your child's "claim to fame."

### The Early Reader Myth

Some perfectly intelligent children don't learn to read until they're over seven years old. They quickly catch up with those who started at four or five.

**Illustrative example:** Two children - one reading at 3 years old, one at 6 years old. Both are now 9 and in fourth grade. Both read at about an eighth-grade reading level. The only difference: the early reader feels insecure about no longer being "special" and often acts obnoxiously superior to other kids.

This is comparable to walking - whether a child learns to walk at nine months or 16 months makes no lasting difference. Early reading is similar. The timeline matters far less than the relationship with reading.

## Strategy 3: Use Phonics as Support When Needed

### When Whole-Word Recognition Isn't Enough

Many children who grow up immersed in books learn to love them so much they're motivated to master reading. They often learn to recognize whole words on the page (whole language approach).

But this doesn't work for all children. Many children need to learn to sound out words letter by letter - the **Phonics approach**. This is particularly true for English speakers because spelling and pronunciation in English are famously illogical.

### Warning Signs That Extra Support Is Needed

Watch for a child who:
- Has a hard time recognizing letters
- Confuses letters
- Can't sound out words
- Can't recognize words she has seen many times before

If you notice these signs, discuss your concern with your child's school and ask to speak to their learning specialist, who should be experienced in diagnosis and early intervention.

## Strategy 4: Read During Meals (Pre-School Age)

Before children can really participate in meals, reading to them during lunch or early dinner entertains them enough to keep them sitting. They become much more likely to try foods with both parental company and the diversion of a book, compared to sitting alone while a parent cooks.

### Important Distinction: Reading vs. Screens During Meals

This is very different from putting kids in front of a screen while they eat:
- **Screen eating:** Children stare at the screen and unconsciously put things in their mouth
- **Being read to:** More like listening to the radio - children can look at their food and savor it as they listen, glancing back and forth at pictures being held up

The multisensory engagement is qualitatively different. Reading allows attention to food; screens capture attention entirely.

## Strategy 5: Make the Library a Regular Destination

### Start by Age Two

Begin visiting the library regularly by the time your child is two. If you do this consistently, they may well prefer reading to any other activity.

### Use Library Time for Reading, Not Just Borrowing

Use time in the library to read to your child as well as to select books. Don't just grab books and leave - make the library itself a reading environment.

### Story Time Is Optional

Some kids love library story times; others (like the author's children) won't sit still for them. This is fine either way.

### Practical Systems for Managing Library Books

The challenge: keeping track of borrowed books and returning them on time.

Solutions:
- Take photos or write down the names of books you check out (if library doesn't give printouts)
- Keep library books on a separate shelf in living room or kitchen - this prevents losing them and ensures you can always find something new to read
- Don't take library books out of the house if possible - eliminates losing them

### Building a Discovery System

Supervising a toddler while perusing bookshelves is challenging. Solutions:
- Develop a list of authors and books so you can find good ones easily
- Librarians usually have lists of favorite books for various ages
- Other parents and kids are good sources of suggestions
- Find series you like and share your child's excitement when you find another book by a beloved author

## Strategy 6: Never Stop Reading Aloud Once They Can Read

**This is identified as a critical tip.**

Read to your child every step of the way, for as long as they'll let you. Continuing to read to your child keeps them interested in reading to themselves and developing their skills.

### Why Kids Who Can Read Don't Want To

Parents often complain that their early readers CAN read but don't seem interested in doing so. Most kids go through this stage.

**The underlying problem:** A child can read simple books, but their imagination craves more developed plots and characters. Those advanced books require agonizing work with too many unfamiliar words. The labor distracts from the story.

**The solution:** Parents must keep reading to them to keep them fascinated with the secrets of books. That fascination is what motivates the hard work to become a proficient reader.

### Bonus: Conversations About Values

Reading aloud gives you fodder for great conversations about values, choices, hardships, and hope. This is a unique benefit that disappears if you stop reading to them too early.

## Strategy 7: Bridge the Gap with Appropriate Books

### During the Vulnerable Transition Stage

At this stage (when kids can read but advanced books are too hard), it's worth extra time to track down books the child can read and will find exciting.

**What works well:** Picture books with lots of words. The pictures help kids stay interested and figure out unfamiliar words.

### The Progression

Through school work and books picked up at home, reading skills will catch up with appetite for books. Within a few months, they'll handle simple chapter books.

**Key tactic:** Look for series books at this point. Series often lure kids on to the next book and the next, building reading stamina and habit through natural engagement.

## Strategy 8: Ritualize Daily Reading Time

### Set Up "Cozy Reading Time" Every Day

Possible timing options:
- After school (perfect chill-out time)
- After lunch in summer
- Wind-down time at end of evening

### The Bedtime Leverage Technique

**Kids are amazingly motivated to read if it allows them to stay up later.** One approach: negotiate a half-hour later bedtime (that first graders may be ready for anyway), as long as the time is spent in bed reading a book.

### When Evening Reading Doesn't Work

Some six-year-olds are simply too tired by the end of the day for reading to work. Reading is too much effort when exhausted.

Alternative: Set up cozy reading time while you make dinner, after homework is done.

**Downside:** You'll need to scrape out 15 minutes to start them off at what is probably your busiest time of day. But this investment gets them hooked so they continue independently.

## Strategy 9: Help Them Tackle the Next Level (Scaffolding Technique)

### The Method

1. Pick a book she can read, but that is a bit harder than she might choose on her own (example: a simple chapter book rather than a picture book)
2. Read together until you have to stop (answer the phone, start dinner) - but read a minimum of a quarter of the book so your child is hooked
3. Tell her it's time for her read-alone time
4. Give her the choice: keep reading the book you've just gotten her into, or read something else

**Expected result:** Most kids grab the book and finish it themselves.

**If she doesn't continue:** You may need to drop back a level to a slightly simpler book. The book was too challenging.

**Ongoing approach:** Keep choosing engrossing, slightly harder books. Gradually raise the level.

---

## Strategy 10: Alternating Pages During Read-Aloud Time

### The Technique

Alternate pages with your child during read-aloud time. This gives them practice while keeping the momentum of shared reading.

### Critical Rule: Supply Words When They Stumble

If he stumbles on a word, supply the word immediately. Do not make him stop and sound things out. The goal is to keep him excited about the book by moving forward with the story.

### Important Limitations

- Use this technique only for limited periods of time - it tires kids out
- Don't be rigid about enforcing participation (have them do every third page, or fourth page, rather than strict alternation)
- **If you take the fun out of reading with him, you've done more harm than good**

The principle: maintaining enjoyment is more important than maximizing practice.

## Strategy 11: Comics as an Entry Point for Reluctant Readers

Some kids get a terrific jump start from comics, which are less intimidating than chapter books. The visual support and shorter text chunks reduce the cognitive load while still building reading skills.

**Specific recommendation:** Bill Watterson's Calvin and Hobbes books are kid pleasers with sophisticated vocabulary and concepts. They're engaging while also being intellectually substantive.

## Strategy 12: Never Stop Reading to Them (Extended Version)

Even when they can read anything themselves, don't give up reading aloud until they fire you from the job.

### Why Continue?
- Important time to connect emotionally
- Chance to read books that trigger good discussions about values, choices, hardships, and hope

### It Works Even with Teenagers

**Personal example:** The author's fourteen-year-old could read physics books the author couldn't fathom, but he still let her read history or politics to him occasionally. The best part for both was talking about what they were reading.

The practice evolves from parent-as-reader to shared intellectual engagement.

---

## Strategy 13: Role Model by Reading Yourself

If children don't see you read, why should they read?

### Specific Tactics
- Discuss what you're all reading at the dinner table
- Institutionalize family reading time when a parent reads to the whole family
- As kids get older, they can take over the role of reader, or the book can be passed around the circle

Reading becomes a family activity and identity, not just an individual assignment.

---

## Strategy 14: Limit Screens

### The Fundamental Problem

**There is no way a book can compete with a screen.** Most kids, given the choice, won't choose the book often enough to make it a habit. Before you know it, they'll have developed other habits for relaxing, and reading will become "something other people do."

### Key Research Finding

**Research shows that the more TV kids watch before the age of eight, the less they read after the age of eight.**

Caveat: This is a correlation, so it doesn't definitively prove that one causes the other. But most researchers are convinced of the causal relationship.

### The Recommendation

**Limiting or even banning screen usage until reading is well-established may be the most important thing you can do to encourage reading.**

The logic: Don't let children get addicted to screens before they have become dedicated readers. The addiction to easy entertainment will crowd out the harder work of developing reading habits. Once reading is established and enjoyable, screens become less of a threat because reading can compete.

---

## Summary of Key Principles

1. **Association over instruction:** Create positive emotional associations with reading rather than teaching reading as a skill
2. **Remove pressure:** Anxiety about reading prevents learning to read
3. **Never abandon read-aloud:** Continue reading to children at every stage, even when they can read independently
4. **Bridge the gap:** Children's imagination outpaces their reading ability - parents must bridge this gap with continued read-aloud
5. **Ritualize:** Make reading a predictable daily habit built into the schedule
6. **Scaffold:** Help children tackle slightly harder books by getting them hooked, then letting them continue alone
7. **Protect the habit:** Limit competing activities (especially screens) until reading is well-established
8. **Model the behavior:** Read yourself and make reading a family activity
