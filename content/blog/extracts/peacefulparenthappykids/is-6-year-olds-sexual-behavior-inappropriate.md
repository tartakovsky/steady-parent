# Handling Sexual Behavior and Language in Young Children

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

---

## Understanding the Context: Why Children Display Sexual Interest

Children's sexual curiosity typically manifests as exploring their own bodies, masturbating, or "playing doctor" with peers. However, when a child displays heightened interest in sexual topics, uses provocative language repeatedly, or engages in touching behaviors toward others, this often indicates the child is responding to external stimulation rather than purely internal curiosity.

**Key insight**: We live in a sexualized culture, and protecting children from all external influences is nearly impossible. Other children share information, drawings, and provocative language that excites and confuses young children. When children bring this home, they are often testing how adults will react to these "naughty" topics.

The child picks up on the titillating tone of sexual conversations with peers, which signals that certain words or topics are forbidden or shocking. This creates a double bind: the topic is exciting precisely because it seems taboo, but the child also needs guidance on how to think about these body parts in a healthy way.

## Assessing Whether Behavior Is Normal or Concerning

When children have specific information about sexual activity at a young age that parents did not share with them, this can potentially indicate sexual abuse. However, in many cases, the information came from other children who were themselves exposed to inappropriate content.

**Distinguishing factors**: A child who repeatedly uses sexual words or engages in touching behavior is more likely responding to peer influence if the behavior seems oriented toward getting a reaction (testing boundaries, enjoying the shock value) rather than reenacting specific learned behaviors.

**The anxiety signal**: Unusual preoccupation with sexual topics in a young child (around age 6) often indicates underlying anxiety about these body parts or concepts. The child feels excited but also confused and unsettled. This anxiety drives the compulsive use of words and behaviors the child knows are "off limits."

## Three Core Parental Goals

**Goal 1: Help the child develop a positive view of sex and their own body.** The child needs to see their body as healthy and pleasurable, not shameful. Negative reactions from adults risk creating shame that persists into adulthood.

**Goal 2: Help the child learn socially appropriate behavior.** While feelings about bodies are natural and acceptable, certain behaviors and language have social boundaries. The child needs to understand these boundaries without internalizing shame.

**Goal 3: Instill respect for the bodies of others.** This includes family members, peers, and the parent themselves. The concept of bodily autonomy applies in all directions.

The general principle is to respond to children's sexual interest with **acceptance and appropriate limits** simultaneously. Acceptance without limits fails to teach social norms. Limits without acceptance creates shame.

## Seven-Step Framework for Addressing Sexual Behavior

### Step 1: Educate Yourself First

Before having conversations with the child, parents should become comfortable with the topic themselves. Recommended resource: *Everything You Never Wanted Your Kids to Know About Sex (But Were Afraid They'd Ask)* by Justin Richardson and Mark A. Schuster.

**Why this matters**: Parental discomfort transmits to children. A parent who has processed their own feelings about discussing sex will be calmer and more effective in guiding the child.

### Step 2: Provide Age-Appropriate Sex Education

If you have not already explained basic facts about sex to a 6-year-old, it is "high time" to do so. Even if you have discussed it before, a review session is appropriate as a preface to addressing the concerning behavior.

Recommended resource for use with the child: *It's Perfectly Normal* by Robie H. Harris and Michael Emberley (likely available at public libraries).

**The reasoning**: Children who receive accurate information from trusted adults are less likely to be confused by peer misinformation. Knowledge reduces the titillating mystery that drives compulsive exploration.

### Step 3: Introduce the Concept of the Body as Sacred

Frame private parts in terms of their important function: they play different roles for boys and girls in bringing babies into the world. We consider them "private" because they are **sacred** - part of the miracle of creating new life.

This framing accomplishes two things:
1. It gives the child a positive reason for privacy boundaries rather than shame-based reasons
2. It creates a sense of responsibility: children must protect their own bodies and respect others' bodies because these parts have significant future responsibilities

### Step 4: Teach and Reinforce Respect

Be direct with the child: acknowledge you have noticed their interest in your body (breasts, buttocks), but explain these are private and not to be touched by others. Frame it as mutual respect: "I treat you respectfully and don't touch you inappropriately. I need you to treat me the same way."

**Practical application**: At this age (around 6), the child should be handling their own hygiene for private areas rather than the parent doing it. This reinforces bodily autonomy.

**In-the-moment response**: When you notice inappropriate touching, immediately name it. Say directly that touching you there does not respect your body. Don't let it slide.

Connect past incidents: Help the child understand why the girl whose bottom he pinched was offended. It was not only because pinching hurts, but because touching private areas without permission is an invasion of privacy.

### Step 5: Process Your Own Discomfort

**Critical insight**: Children are highly attuned to parental discomfort and may deliberately provoke it to feel powerful. A child who uses a shocking word "incessantly" may be doing so precisely because it gets a strong reaction from the parent.

Young children often use sexual or "potty" words because shocking adults makes them feel powerful and even "grown up." If the parent remains calm and does not over-react, the word loses much of its appeal.

**Reframing for the parent**: "Nipple" is just a body part. It is actually "a wonderful, amazing, pleasurable body part." It is okay for the child to be excited about it. Feelings are never inappropriate - only behavior may be inappropriate.

This mental shift helps parents respond calmly rather than with visible distress that feeds the behavior cycle.

### Step 6: Help the Child Process Anxiety Through Laughter

**Key principle**: What the child needs is to laugh. Laughter vents anxiety.

The unusual level of interest (grabbing, asking to kiss nipples) signals anxiety about these body parts. We may not know what sparked these feelings, but we can help the child release the tension.

**Technique**: When the child begins using words you consider inappropriate, use it as an opportunity for a silly word game. Start shouting nonsense words in response. If you are silly, the child will laugh and be silly too. As long as the child is laughing, they are releasing anxiety.

**Why this works**: When anxiety is processed through laughter, the child loses the compulsive drive to engage in behaviors or vocabulary they know are off-limits. The emotional pressure behind the behavior dissipates.

### Step 7: Teach Cultural Standards While Preserving Outlets

Tell the child directly: words for certain body parts are not considered appropriate in public. You do not want them getting used to using impolite words in general conversation.

**The compromise**: The child is allowed to use any word they want during silly word games with you, and in the privacy of their own bedroom, but not in general household conversation.

**When the child breaks the rule**: Stay calm. Launch into a silly word game to get them giggling. When you are ready to stop, exit the game mode clearly: "Okay, sweetie, I'm not joking now. I'm done playing. That's a private word, so if you want to use it, you can use it in your own room in private."

This approach:
- Gives the child a legitimate outlet for the words
- Does not make the words completely forbidden (which would increase their allure)
- Establishes clear boundaries about context
- Models calm authority rather than reactive distress

## Why This Approach Works: The Three Protective Outcomes

**Outcome 1**: You help the child with the feelings driving the inappropriate behavior and language, so they don't need to engage in it. When the underlying anxiety is released through laughter and the curiosity is satisfied through education, the compulsive behavior diminishes.

**Outcome 2**: You protect the child's positive feelings about their body instead of making them feel ashamed. Shame about bodies and sexuality can persist into adulthood and cause significant harm to relationships and self-image.

**Outcome 3**: You keep the relationship safe enough that if there is more the child needs to tell you about what is spurring their interest, they will be able to do so. A child who feels shamed or fears punishment will not disclose if something concerning has happened to them.

## Key Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**Over-reacting to "shocking" words**: This gives the words power and encourages repetition. The child learns they can control your emotional state by using certain words.

**Making body parts shameful**: Negative reactions to curiosity about bodies teaches the child that these parts of themselves are bad or dirty.

**Punitive responses without processing**: Punishment addresses behavior but does not address the underlying anxiety or curiosity driving it. The pressure will find other outlets.

**Ignoring the behavior entirely**: While calm acceptance is important, the child also needs guidance about boundaries and respect. Acceptance does not mean no limits.

**Assuming the worst immediately**: While sexual abuse is a possibility to consider, most cases of this behavior stem from peer influence in our sexualized culture. Remaining calm allows for proper assessment.

## The Parental Stance

Throughout all interventions, maintain a stance that is **calm, kind, accepting, and firm**. Calm prevents feeding the shock-value cycle. Kindness keeps the relationship safe for disclosure. Acceptance protects the child's body image. Firmness teaches necessary boundaries.

The parent's job is to correct misinformation, help the child maintain positive feelings about their body, and process any feelings that arise when they receive unhealthy messages about sex from the culture around them.
