# Beyond Discipline for Teens

## Why Traditional Discipline Fails with Teenagers

The teen years parallel the toddler years in a specific way: teenagers often seem intent on doing exactly the opposite of what parents ask. This happens for developmental reasons, not defiance for its own sake. The teenager's developmental task is to find their footing as an independent person, to shape an identity, and to determine what matters to them personally. Their sense of integrity would be compromised by simply doing what parents ask just because parents ask it. They need to believe an action is the right thing for THEM before they will commit to it.

**The sledgehammer approach backfires**: If you come down hard on a teen, you can expect open rebellion. If you then crack down on the rebellion instead of listening to your child's reasons, you can expect your teen to become skilled at lying and sneaking behind your back. The escalation cycle produces the opposite of what parents want.

**The illusion of parental control**: Parents of compliant children sometimes believe they controlled their child until the teen years arrive. The reality is that no parent ever truly controlled their child. Either the child respected the parent and wanted to follow their lead (which means the parent was doing a great job staying connected, but the child was still making a choice to cooperate), or the child was intimidated into compliance out of fear. Fear-based compliance may work with naturally compliant children, but it does not work with strong-willed children, and it does not work with teenagers of any temperament.

**Strong-willed children teach this lesson early**: Parents of strong-willed children have already learned from their child's rebelliousness that they cannot control their child. They can only help the child WANT to cooperate and foster the emotional regulation skills that enable cooperation.

## What Makes Teens Actually Want to Cooperate

Three conditions predict easier teen years:

1. **A warm, affirming, open relationship** in which the teen feels respected and respects the parent
2. **Reliance on discussion** rather than commands to guide the child throughout their upbringing
3. **Empathy for the child's feelings** throughout development, which builds the child's ability to regulate their own emotions

When these conditions are present, the teen will honor family rules most of the time and will initiate negotiations about rules that do not work for them. This happens because:

- The parent remains at the center of the child's life
- The teen does not want to disappoint the parent
- The child has developed emotional regulation capacity from years of having their feelings acknowledged
- Children who are lovingly guided to make reparations and solve problems (rather than punished) develop internal discipline and a strong moral sense earlier

**The end result**: A teen with the ability to make hard choices to do what is right, regardless of peer pressure.

## The Shift Required for Punishment-Reliant Parents

If you have relied on punishment to control your child, expect a shock during the teen years. The problem is twofold:

1. **Resistance**: The teen will actively resist punishment-based control
2. **Missing self-discipline**: A parenting style that relies mostly on the threat of punishment does not give a child the self-discipline to manage themselves

Since parents cannot be present all the time, the solution is to shift toward a strong parent-child relationship that makes the child WANT to do the right thing rather than relying on external enforcement.

## Seven Strategies for Guiding Teens Without Punishment

### 1. Commit to a Respectful Tone for Everyone in the Household

If you are yelling, stop. Your child yells and is disrespectful because you yell. The modeling effect is direct and causal.

**Key insight**: It is not necessary to yell to get your child's attention or cooperation IF you have a good relationship. Yelling is bullying. Consider how you feel when someone yells at you, then understand that your teen feels the same way.

### 2. Focus on Strengthening the Relationship

The relationship is what makes limits and expectations actually work. When you set a limit like "Homework before screen time!" or express an expectation like "We speak civilly in this house," your child needs to want to cooperate for these to function.

**Practical implementation**:
- Have one-on-one time with each child every day
- During this time, mostly listen rather than talk or instruct
- If your teen is "too busy" to connect, give foot massages as a way to create contact
- The fundamental principle: You cannot hope to have any influence if your kid does not enjoy being with you

### 3. Stop Punishing

Replace punishment with:

**Non-negotiable family rules**: These should be few in number and limited to what is truly important. Safety is always non-negotiable. Health is non-negotiable to some degree.

**Family negotiation**: Sit down with your family to negotiate everything else. One key to getting teens to cooperate is respecting that in most areas of their life, they need to be in charge. This does not mean no boundaries. It means collaborative boundary-setting for things that are not safety-critical.

### 4. Foster Accountability Through Repair, Not Punishment

**The worry parents have**: That their child is not being "held accountable" if privileges are not revoked as punishment.

**The alternative - Reparations**: This is not a consequence (punishment) that you impose. This is when you ask your teen if there is something they can do to make the situation better now.

**Examples of repair**:
- If the teen says something mean to a sibling, they need to do repair work on that relationship
- If the teen breaks something, they help pay for a replacement

**Critical distinction about how repair is introduced**:
- If you think up the reparation and force it on the teen, they will reject it
- Instead, let this be an empowering opportunity for the teen to learn that everyone makes mistakes and that we can always take action to make things better
- The teen should generate or co-create the repair action

**Reframing from negative to positive**: Instead of taking something away (like a privilege) or adding something negative to the teen's life (like a punishment), think of repair as adding something positive to their life (a positive interaction with their sister to heal what went wrong between them).

### 5. Set Clear Expectations About What Matters to You

Expectations will vary for every family. The article's author provides their family's example:

- Teens are expected to treat parents and siblings civilly
- Teens are expected to be honest with parents
- Teens are expected to maintain good grades
- Because the teens travel an hour each way to school and have many after-school activities (gone 7am to 8pm), they are not expected to carry heavy household responsibilities during the school year
- They do help whenever asked
- They are expected to do more in the summer
- They are expected to hold summer jobs to earn money for college funds

This example illustrates that expectations should be realistic given the teen's circumstances and should be clearly communicated rather than assumed.

### 6. Give Whatever Support Is Necessary for Your Child to Meet Your Expectations

**The principle**: Expecting something without providing support for achieving it is setting up failure. Your teen will not necessarily know how to make things better. They need your help. You may not know either, but your partnership and willingness to step in to support them in figuring out the next step will reassure them that they are not alone and will go a long way toward solving the problem.

**Example - Teen not working hard in school**: The first step is diagnosis. Is the problem:
- **Lack of focus/distraction by social media?** The answer is your presence at homework time
- **Knowledge gap in a specific subject?** The answer is tutoring, and do not delay because they will fall further behind

**Example - Teen is rude to you**: Consider this a red flag that your relationship needs work. Address the relationship, not just the behavior.

### 7. Keep Your Sense of Humor While Sticking to Limits

**Expect testing**: Assume your teen will test you to see if you are serious. This is normal and developmentally appropriate.

**The approach**: Stay cheerful while you keep enforcing limits. For instance, be present during homework time and ensure your teen stays on track instead of connecting with friends or playing games, but find a way to do it with a light touch.

**Timeline for habit formation**: If you have previously relied on punishment rather than helping your teen develop inner discipline, establishing new patterns could take weeks or even months. But after a while, your rule will become a habit, your child will experience the benefits of it, and they will begin to WANT to do it on their own.

The progression is: External enforcement with light touch -> Habit formation -> Experiencing benefits -> Internalized motivation.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **Coming down like a sledgehammer**: Produces open rebellion
2. **Cracking down on rebellion without listening to the teen's reasons**: Produces lying and sneaking
3. **Relying on ever-stiffer punishments**: Does not work with strong-willed children or any teenager
4. **Yelling**: Models the disrespectful behavior you are trying to eliminate
5. **Imposing reparations without the teen's input**: Causes the teen to reject the repair process
6. **Setting expectations without providing support to meet them**: Sets up failure
7. **Expecting immediate compliance when shifting from punishment to relationship-based guidance**: The transition takes weeks to months

## Core Principles Summary

**You cannot control your teen; you can only influence them through relationship**: The illusion of control was always an illusion. Either your child chose to follow you out of respect, or out of fear. Fear stops working with teenagers.

**The teen's developmental task requires apparent opposition**: They need to determine what is right for THEM, which means they cannot simply comply because you said so. This is healthy development, not defiance.

**Repair is superior to punishment for building accountability**: Taking something away or adding something negative is less effective than adding something positive (repairing the harm done).

**Support is the companion to expectations**: You cannot expect without providing the help needed to succeed.

**Relationship is the mechanism of influence**: You cannot influence a teen who does not enjoy being with you.
