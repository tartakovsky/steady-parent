# When Your Child Isn't Making Friends in School

Source: Dr. Laura Markham, Peaceful Parent Happy Kids / Aha Parenting

---

## The Core Problem and Why Early Intervention Matters

When children struggle to make friends, parents often feel helpless watching their child's social difficulties. Dr. Laura Markham emphasizes that if you notice from an early age that your child is not making friends, you can and should intervene. The reason early action matters is that social skills are learned behaviors, and children who fall behind socially often continue to struggle unless they receive targeted help.

By elementary school age, children should be developing friendships. They don't need to be popular, but they do need the capability to form at least one meaningful connection at school. Having even one friend provides crucial social support and helps children feel they belong.

## Assessment: The Two Essential Conversations

Before taking action, parents must gather information from two sources to understand what's actually happening.

### Talk to the Teacher First

The teacher sees your child in the school social environment daily and can provide crucial observations about:
- Whether the friendship difficulty is actually occurring at school
- What specific behaviors might be causing problems
- Who your child interacts with and how
- Possible causes they've observed

This information is essential because what parents imagine is happening may differ significantly from the actual dynamics at school.

### Talk to Your Child

Have conversations to understand your child's perspective and identify barriers they're experiencing. This serves multiple purposes: it shows your child you care about their social life, it helps you understand their inner experience, and it may reveal specific obstacles you weren't aware of.

The combination of teacher and child perspectives gives you a fuller picture than either source alone.

## Common Behavioral Problems That Block Friendships

Dr. Markham identifies several specific behaviors that commonly prevent children from making friends:

**Bossy behavior** - When children try to control play and dictate what others should do, peers naturally resist and pull away. Other children want playmates, not commanders.

**Bragging tendencies** - Children who constantly boast about themselves or their possessions alienate potential friends who feel diminished by comparison.

**Emotional outbursts ("exploding")** - Children who have frequent meltdowns or angry explosions frighten peers and make others reluctant to play with them. Unpredictable emotional reactions create social anxiety in other children.

These behaviors often stem from underlying emotional regulation difficulties rather than character flaws. When children get these things under control, they can actually have a good social life and connect to other people their own age.

## Professional Support Options

If friendship difficulties persist despite parent intervention, Dr. Markham advocates seeking professional help. Modern resources include specialized groups for kids that help them develop both social skills and emotional regulation simultaneously.

These groups are valuable because:
- They provide a structured environment for practicing social skills
- Children learn alongside peers facing similar challenges
- Professional facilitators can identify and address specific skill gaps
- The group setting provides real-time feedback and coaching

## Practical Strategies for Parent Involvement

### Setting Up Playdates

Parents can actively intervene by arranging one-on-one playdates. These are more manageable than group dynamics and allow children to practice friendship skills in a controlled setting. Regular, consistent interaction with the same peers helps children build familiarity and deepen connections.

### Teaching Specific Skills

Some children need explicit instruction in social skills that other children pick up naturally. Key skills to teach include:

**How to join a group** - This is a crucial skill many struggling children lack. Rather than barging in or hovering awkwardly, children can learn to observe what others are doing, then contribute to the existing activity.

**Parallel play as an entry strategy** - When a child ignores your child's attempts to join, teach them to play nearby without demanding interaction. Start playing with similar toys alongside the other child, making comments they can listen to. Gradually initiate connection through play rather than direct conversation, as this feels safer to hesitant children.

### The Role of Structured Activities

Rather than relying solely on random playground encounters, enroll your child in structured groups where regular interaction with the same peers occurs under adult guidance. Options include:
- Sports teams
- Music classes
- Gymnastics
- Art classes
- Scouts or similar organizations

These settings work better than playgrounds because teachers and coaches typically establish social norms that reduce rejecting behaviors among group members.

## Parenting Approach During Social Struggles

### Active Listening Without Problem-Solving

When children face peer conflicts, resist the urge to direct solutions. If you tell your child what to do, you imply that they're incompetent, and you aren't helping them learn to solve problems themselves. Instead, help clarify their feelings and guide their own problem-solving process.

### Maintaining Neutrality About Other Children

Avoid demonizing other children when conflicts arise. Rather than taking your child's side and criticizing their peers, encourage perspective-taking. For example: "I wonder what was going on with Angelina that she said such mean things." This teaches empathy and helps children see that others' behavior often reflects the other person's struggles, not a personal attack.

### Validating Rather Than Negating Feelings

Validate your child's perceptions instead of dismissing them. Saying "It sounds like when Ayesha said that in front of the other kids, it really embarrassed and hurt you" is far more helpful than minimizing with "It's not a big deal." Full acknowledgment of emotions allows children to process feelings completely rather than staying stuck in anger or hurt.

### Teaching Non-Blaming Communication

Guide children to express their needs without attacking peers. When a child says "You're too bossy!" redirect them: "Can you tell her what you want, instead of what you think about her?" This teaches the crucial distinction between expressing needs and criticizing character.

### Coaching Assertiveness

Help children practice standing up for themselves through role-play and rehearsal. This is particularly important for sensitive or anxious children who may not instinctively know how to respond when peers treat them unfairly.

## Reframing Rejection for Children

When children experience rejection, help them understand it as information about the other child rather than a reflection of their own worth. You might explain: "They don't know how to make friends as well as he does" or "They are worried about having to share toys."

This reframing serves multiple purposes:
- It protects the child's self-esteem
- It builds empathy by showing that rejecting behavior usually stems from the other child's limitations
- It reduces the personal sting of social setbacks

## Addressing Bossiness Specifically

When your child tends toward bossy behavior, help them understand the trade-off through guiding questions: "Is it more important to you to play the game your way or to have Catherine play with you?" Then teach diplomatic scripts for reaching compromises that allow everyone to enjoy the play.

## When Physical Aggression Emerges

If your child is hitting, pushing, or being physically aggressive with peers, treat it as a signal that they need help processing underlying emotions rather than simply punishing the behavior. Avoid shame, which typically makes the problem worse. Instead, build empathy for how their actions affect others and help them take responsibility for repairing relationships.

## Parent's Own Emotional Work

Dr. Markham stresses that parents must address their own childhood rejection experiences. Children really do take on the issues of their parents, and resolving your own pain through supportive conversations or therapy will automatically lighten your child's social struggles.

This happens because:
- Parents who carry unresolved rejection pain may project anxiety onto normal childhood social situations
- Children pick up on parental worry and absorb it as their own
- A calm, confident parent presence helps children feel more secure in social settings

## The Kindergarten Transition

For preschool-age children struggling socially, it's helpful to know that school environments differ significantly from playground dynamics. Teachers establish social norms and actively work to include all children, which reduces the kind of random rejection that happens on public playgrounds.

## Facilitating Problem-Solving

Once emotions have settled after a social conflict, help children brainstorm multiple options for handling the situation. These might range from:
- Ways to reconcile with the other child
- Setting boundaries about acceptable treatment
- Finding new friends who are a better match
- Changing their own behavior that contributed to the conflict

Having multiple options prevents children from feeling trapped and teaches flexible problem-solving.

## Observing to Identify Specific Skill Gaps

Watch your child during play interactions to identify exactly where they struggle. Common areas include:
- Social cue recognition (not noticing when others are upset or bored)
- Turn-taking
- Respecting personal space
- Reading group dynamics
- Knowing when to lead versus follow

Once you identify specific gaps, you can use indirect teaching methods like stuffed animal scenarios or books to address concerns without blame or shame.

## Key Principles Summary

1. **Early intervention works** - Social skills can be taught and improved
2. **Gather information first** - Talk to both teachers and your child before acting
3. **Address underlying emotional regulation** - Many social problems stem from difficulty managing emotions
4. **Provide structured practice opportunities** - Playdates and organized activities give children chances to learn
5. **Coach without controlling** - Guide your child's problem-solving rather than solving for them
6. **Validate feelings fully** - Let children process emotions before moving to solutions
7. **Reframe rejection** - Help children see others' behavior as information about those others
8. **Do your own work** - Parent anxiety about social issues transfers to children
9. **Seek professional help when needed** - Social skills groups can provide targeted support
