# Teaching Kids to Share: Self-Regulated Turns

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids (Dr. Laura Markham)

---

## The Core Tension Parents Must Navigate

Parents face a genuine tension when teaching sharing. On one side, we want children to grow into **generous people who notice and respond to others' needs**. On the other side, we want children to be able to meet their own needs, including pursuing their own work and play, because that's how they develop mastery.

This tension creates competing goals that need to be balanced:
- We don't want kids to feel they should interrupt what they're working on just because another child asks for something
- We do want our child to notice when another child wants a turn and ensure that child eventually gets one
- When our child wants something another child has, we want her to control her impulses rather than grabbing, using words to work out an arrangement for future use

The ideal outcome is a child who can **advocate for her own needs**, **respect the needs of others**, and **delay gratification** to wait for what she wants.

---

## Why Forced Sharing Backfires

### The Research Foundation

**Nancy Eisenberg**, a leading researcher on children's social development, found that children become more generous by having the experience of giving to others and learning how good that feels. However, there is a critical catch: **the experience must be voluntarily chosen by the child**. When we force children to share, they walk away resentful rather than feeling generous. As a result, they become less likely to share in the future, not more.

This creates a counterintuitive dynamic: forcing sharing to teach generosity actually undermines the development of genuine generosity.

### Problems with Traditional Parent-Enforced Sharing

In most families, children are expected to share or take turns with most toys. The parent decides when one child has had a toy long enough, usually based on how loud the protest is from the sibling or friend.

This approach has multiple problems:
- It **reinforces competition between siblings** because children are pitted against each other for limited access
- It **disempowers both children** because neither gets to make decisions about giving or receiving
- It **teaches children that fussing gets results**, since the parent's decision often depends on who protests more dramatically
- It puts the parent in the unpleasant role of **judge, jury, and police officer**

---

## The Self-Regulated Turns Approach

### The Core Concept

The alternative approach is called **"self-regulated turns"** or in child-friendly language, **"long turns."** The child who has the toy decides how long she needs it, then gives it to the other child when she's ready.

### Contrasting the Two Approaches Through Outcomes

#### Traditional Approach (Option 1): Parent Takes Toy and Gives to Sibling

When we tell the first child he's had the toy long enough, take it away, and give it to the sibling, the second child (who received the toy) learns:

- **If I cry loud enough, I get what I want**, even if someone else has it - this reinforces fussing as a strategy
- **Parents are arbitrarily in charge of who gets what** - depending on their whim and how dramatically I beg for my turn
- **My sibling and I are in constant competition** - I don't like him because he's my rival
- **I guess I'm a greedy person, but that's what I need to be** to get what I deserve - this becomes part of identity
- **I had better "play fast"** because I won't have this item for long - undermines deep engagement with play
- **I won, but soon I will lose again** - I better protest loudly when my turn is up and start protesting again when it's my sibling's turn; making my parent miserable gets me more time with the toy

The key insight is that under this system, **the child's attention is barely on the toy he's won**. He can't play freely because all he can do is feel the clock ticking. Forced sharing undermines the ability of children to lose themselves in play.

Additionally, **neither child gets to experience the generosity of having their fill and giving to the other**. The positive feeling of voluntary giving never occurs.

#### Self-Regulated Approach (Option 2): Child Decides When Done

When we tell the second child that he can ask his sibling when she will be done, and assure him we'll help him wait until she decides she's finished, the second child learns:

- **I can ask for what I want** - sometimes I get a turn soon, and sometimes I have to wait
- **It's okay to cry, but crying doesn't get me the toy** - emotions are accepted but don't manipulate outcomes
- **I don't get everything I want, but I get something better**: my parent always understands and helps me when I'm upset - connection is the real prize
- **After I cry, I feel better** - emotions process through and resolve
- **I can use another toy instead and really enjoy it** - flexibility and finding alternatives is a skill
- **I'm getting better at waiting** - building self-awareness about growing capability
- **I don't have to whine and cry to convince my parent** - everybody waits for their turn, but everybody eventually gets a turn
- **I like the feeling when my sibling gives me the toy; I like her** - positive association with sibling replaces competition
- **I can use a toy for as long as I want; nobody will force me to give it up at a moment's notice** - security in ownership
- **When I'm done and give it to my sibling, I feel good inside - I like to give her a turn; I'm a generous person** - identity as a generous person develops

## Developmental Benefits of Self-Regulated Turns

### Building Impulse Control and Delayed Gratification

Every time you support a child through the wait for a toy, they build the **mental muscle to delay gratification**. This is active skill-building that happens through practice with parental support.

### Supporting Deeper Play

By not taking away the toy in the middle of the child's use of it, we allow children to lose themselves in their play. The pressure of a ticking clock disappears, enabling genuine engagement and mastery development.

### Discovering Genuine Generosity

When children voluntarily decide to give a toy to their sibling after they've had their fill, they experience what it feels like to be generous. This positive feeling, as Eisenberg's research shows, is what actually develops lasting generosity.

### Warmer Sibling Relationships

Reducing competition over toys supports warmer sibling relationships. Children associate their sibling with the good feeling of receiving a toy freely given, rather than with rivalry and losing.

## What to Expect During Implementation

### Initial Challenges

This approach can be challenging for both parents and children when first implementing it. The child waiting for a turn will likely protest loudly, which is inconvenient for parents and miserable for the child. It initially seems unfair that one child waits while the other uses the toy for as long as she pleases.

### The Transition Process

As kids learn two things, they get better at managing their impatience:
1. They will eventually get the toy
2. Their right to a toy they're using will also be protected

This security on both sides of the equation creates the conditions for patience to develop.

### Expected Outcomes

**Fairly quickly**, you will see your children:
- Start asking each other when they can have a turn
- Offer the toy to their sibling when they're done with it

That a child will offer her sibling a turn without parental enforcement seems hard to believe for most parents. However, if you adopt this policy, **it will become an everyday occurrence** in your home.

### Practical Caveats

Even with this approach, you will still need to:
- **Buy two of the most treasured items** when possible
- **Put favorites away before other children visit** to avoid high-stakes conflicts

The shift is not that all sharing problems disappear, but that instead of policing sharing, you find yourself **coaching your kids as they wait for their turn** and **admiring how often they navigate taking turns without you running interference**.

## The Parent's Changed Role

### Old Role (Traditional Sharing)
- Judge of who has had the toy "long enough"
- Jury deciding competing claims
- Police officer enforcing sharing rules

### New Role (Self-Regulated Turns)
- **Coach** who helps children wait for their turn
- **Emotional support** who validates frustration while holding boundaries
- **Observer** who watches children develop independent negotiation skills

---

## Key Framework Summary

**Goal**: Raise children who are generous people able to notice and respond to others' needs, while also meeting their own needs through pursuing their own work and play.

**Mechanism**: Generosity develops through voluntarily chosen experiences of giving and feeling how good that is - not through forced compliance.

**Method**: Self-regulated (long) turns where the child with the toy decides when to give it up, while parents support the waiting child through the emotional difficulty of waiting.

**Result**: Children develop impulse control, delayed gratification skills, genuine generosity, and warmer sibling relationships. They increasingly navigate sharing independently.
