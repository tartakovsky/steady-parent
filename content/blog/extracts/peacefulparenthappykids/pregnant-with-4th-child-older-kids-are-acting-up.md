# Children Acting Out During Pregnancy: Understanding and Responding to Regression

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

---

## Core Understanding: Why Children Act Out During Pregnancy

Children's behavioral changes during a parent's pregnancy are often reactions to either the pregnancy itself or to the parent's natural exhaustion. This creates a challenging dynamic: at precisely the time when parents have the lowest energy reserves, their children need them more than usual.

The key insight is that each child showing behavioral changes is communicating unmet needs. The behaviors are not defiance or "acting out" in a negative sense - they are signals that something is missing emotionally for that child.

---

## The Two-Pronged Approach: Preventive Maintenance + Targeted Support

The recommended strategy combines two complementary approaches:

1. **Preventive Maintenance** - general emotional support practices applied daily to all children
2. **Targeted Support** - specific interventions addressing each child's particular problem areas

These work together because preventive maintenance builds emotional reserves and connection, while targeted support addresses the specific ways each child's distress is manifesting.

---

## Preventive Maintenance Program

Preventive maintenance consists of three core practices that should happen daily:

### Daily One-on-One Time
Each child needs daily time alone with the parent. This is not family time or time in the presence of siblings - it is dedicated individual attention. The purpose is to fill each child's "emotional cup" and reinforce that they matter as individuals, not just as part of the family unit.

### Daily Roughhousing That Inspires Laughter
Physical play that gets children laughing serves a specific emotional function: it helps children work through pent-up emotions. The laughter itself is the release mechanism. This is not just "playing with the kids" - the goal is specifically to generate genuine laughter through physical play, because laughter processes and releases accumulated stress and tension.

### Scheduled Meltdowns
When children have deeper issues they need to process, allowing and supporting them through crying helps clear those emotions. This means creating safe opportunities for children to cry through difficult feelings rather than suppressing them. The parent's role is to be present and supportive during these emotional releases, not to stop the crying or distract from it.

This preventive program alone is often sufficient to clear up many behavioral issues, such as the somatic complaints (tummy aches) and sleep disruptions described in the original question.

## Understanding Specific Behaviors and Their Meanings

### School-Age Child (6-year-old boy) - Night Waking and Somatic Complaints

The "tummy aches" and waking to check if "mommy and baby are all right" represent anxiety manifesting physically. The child is processing worry about the pregnancy and the coming changes. While the behavior is "sweet," it disrupts everyone's sleep.

The preventive maintenance program is expected to resolve this because the underlying cause is emotional anxiety that needs processing. Daily special time, laughter through roughhousing, and opportunities to express feelings should address the root cause rather than just the symptom.

### Preschooler (4-year-old) - Multiple Behavioral Issues

**Critical reframe**: A 4-year-old is a preschooler, not a toddler. This distinction matters because it sets appropriate expectations - yes, 4-year-olds act impulsively, but the constellation of behaviors described indicates a child who is "really hurting," not just displaying age-typical behavior.

**Sneaking food** is usually a sign that children feel there is not enough to go around emotionally. The child is literally trying to get more of something, which represents a metaphorical hunger for emotional resources.

**Waking at night begging to watch TV** indicates emotional upset where the child wants to use screens to self-soothe and pacify difficult feelings. The child is seeking an external regulator because they cannot regulate internally.

**Drawing on walls, destroying things, breaking things** - while some impulsivity is normal at this age, combined with the other behaviors, this points to emotional distress rather than simple mischief.

**Fighting sleep and napping only in parent's bed** shows increased need for parental proximity and reassurance.

The recommended response includes daily special time, roughhousing with laughter, scheduled meltdowns to process emotions, and "lots of snuggling." Physical closeness and affection are part of the healing.

**Important warning**: If the preventive maintenance program is faithfully followed and the 4-year-old continues acting this way, seek parenting coaching or other support BEFORE the baby arrives. The arrival of a new baby will be another stressor on all children, so these challenges need resolution before adding that additional strain.

### Baby/Young Toddler (15-month-old) - Screaming and Sleep Dependence

#### On Screaming

**Screaming in babies around 15 months is very common**. This developmental stage is difficult for parents but does NOT indicate the child is becoming "willful" or "bratty."

The screaming happens because the child has:
- Big feelings they need to express
- No confidence in their ability to make themselves understood through other means

**How to respond**: Stay calm and don't over-react. Instead, translate what the child wants while modeling the words: "Oh, you want me to pick you up? Of course, come here... You can say 'UP!'"

**Why the child can't just use words yet**: Even if the child understands words like "up," putting that understanding into speech requires multiple cognitive steps that are difficult to execute, especially when emotionally overwhelmed (which babies this age often are).

**This phase is temporary** and will naturally vanish as language skills develop. The key is not over-reacting, which would reinforce the screaming as a communication strategy.

#### On Needing to Be Rocked/Cuddled for Sleep

The goal is helping the baby learn to fall asleep without being rocked and cuddled for extended periods (3+ hours is unsustainable, especially during pregnancy).

**Critical principle**: This transition must be gradual so the child does not develop other problem behaviors by repressing dependency needs. If pushed too fast, the underlying need doesn't disappear - it manifests in other ways.

**About the crying that will occur**: During this transition process, the baby will cry in the parent's arms while being told she'll be sleeping by herself. This crying is actually beneficial - it allows the child to "clean out those feelings that are keeping her from relaxing into sleep."

**Essential condition**: The crying is only beneficial if the parent is present to help the child feel safe. This is NOT the same as "cry it out" where the child is left alone. The parent provides safety and co-regulation while the child processes feelings about the change.

## Supporting Yourself as the Parent

A mother pregnant with her fourth child while caring for three existing children (including a baby) is carrying an enormous load. This section emphasizes self-care not as optional but as essential.

**Why parent wellbeing matters**:
1. For the parent's own sake
2. For the unborn baby, who experiences the parent's stress hormones
3. For the existing children, who depend on the parent as "the center of their universe, the person who helps them stay centered and regulated"

**Key framing**: Children use the parent as their regulatory anchor. When the parent is depleted, dysregulated, or stressed, children lose their primary source of stability, which explains why exhaustion in the parent often correlates with behavioral issues in children.

**Recommendation**: Do whatever is needed to stay "relaxed and happy" - this is not indulgence but necessity for the entire family system.

## Key Principles Embedded in the Advice

### Behaviors Are Communications
Every problematic behavior described - tummy aches, night waking, food sneaking, screaming, sleep resistance - is interpreted as communication about unmet needs rather than defiance or manipulation.

### Age-Appropriate Expectations Matter
The distinction between "toddler" and "preschooler" for the 4-year-old reflects the importance of having accurate developmental expectations. Mislabeling developmental stages leads to either too much tolerance of concerning behavior or too little grace for age-appropriate challenges.

### Prevention Over Reaction
The entire framework prioritizes proactive investment in connection (preventive maintenance) over reactive responses to individual incidents. This addresses root causes rather than symptoms.

### Emotional Processing Through the Body
Both laughter (through roughhousing) and crying (through scheduled meltdowns) are physical processes that clear emotional build-up. Emotions are not just talked through but processed through bodily experiences.

### Gradual Transitions Protect Development
Pushing children too quickly through transitions (like sleep independence) causes them to repress needs, which resurface as other problems. Gradual change honors the child's emotional timeline.

### Parental Regulation Enables Child Regulation
The parent's emotional state directly affects children's ability to stay regulated. Supporting the parent is not separate from supporting the children - it is foundational to it.

## Warning Signs and When to Seek Help

If the preventive maintenance program is implemented faithfully and the 4-year-old's behaviors persist, this indicates a need for professional support (parenting coaching or similar) before the new baby arrives. The birth will add another stressor, so unresolved issues should be addressed beforehand.

The phrase "faithfully follow the recommendations" suggests that inconsistent implementation would not be a fair test - the program needs genuine commitment before concluding it isn't working.
