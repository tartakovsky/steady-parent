# Managing Your Own Separation Anxiety as a Parent

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/Life-Preschooler-Mother-Separation-Anxiety

---

## Core Framework: The 12-Step Program for Parental Separation Anxiety

This framework addresses a common but rarely discussed problem: parents experience genuine separation anxiety when their children start school, and managing this parental anxiety is essential both for the parent's wellbeing and for the child's successful adjustment. The approach treats parental separation anxiety as natural and valid while providing practical strategies to handle it constructively.

---

## Step 1: Develop Goodbye Rituals

**Goodbye rituals** serve a dual purpose: they help the child know what to expect (increasing confidence) and they comfort the parent as well.

**Implementation requirements:**
- Do the same actions in the same order every day
- Consistency is what creates the sense of predictability and safety

**Example ritual:** A hug combined with a rhyme: "I love you, you love me, have the most fun ever and I'll pick you up at three!" followed by waving as you leave.

The power of the ritual comes from its predictability - the child can mentally prepare for the sequence, which reduces uncertainty and therefore anxiety for both parties.

---

## Step 2: Honor Your Feelings

The sadness and worry parents feel during separation is **biologically designed** - nature made you this way. Your job as a parent is to be there for your child and protect them. You work hard to have a close relationship. Given this, it is completely natural to feel sad when you separate and worried about whether they will be okay.

**Key reframe:** Do not be embarrassed about these feelings. They are a sign that the attachment system is working properly, not a sign of weakness or excessive attachment.

---

## Step 3: Manage Your Own Feelings Privately

There is a critical distinction between having feelings (Step 2) and expressing them to your child. You must manage your emotions privately so that you can reassure your child that there is nothing to be upset about.

**Why this matters:** Kids pick up parental cues. If you have tears in your eyes as you say goodbye, you cannot expect your child to look forward to playing with other kids at preschool. Your emotional state becomes their emotional reality.

**When separation triggers deeper issues:** If separating from your child triggers your own unresolved issues (perhaps related to your own childhood experiences), use this as an opportunity to work through those old issues with a counselor. The benefit is twofold: you will feel better, and you will be liberating your child from having to carry your emotional baggage.

---

## Step 4: Help Your Child Make a Smooth Adjustment

**Normal expectation:** Most kids have some separation anxiety they need to work through. This is normal, not a sign of a problem.

**Critical warning - do not over-react:** When your child wails and clutches at you, any parent gets upset. But over-reacting makes things harder for your child and for you. Your distress escalates their distress.

**What NOT to say:** Do not tell your child you will be in the parking lot in case they need you. This prevents them from settling into the classroom because part of their attention stays focused on you potentially being available.

**What TO say instead:** Tell them you will be back to pick them up at a specific time, such as "after story time." Concrete time markers help the child understand when to expect you.

**Covert monitoring strategy:** You can listen outside the classroom without your child knowing you are there. This allows you to hear when they stop crying and observe how the teacher handles their upset, giving you peace of mind without interfering with your child's adjustment process.

---

## Step 5: Have Faith in Your Child and in Nature

**The biological design of attachment:** Nature designed children to hang onto their parents for protection, but also to start exploring once they feel safe. The drive to explore is as natural as the drive to stay close.

**Reframe on worrying:** Worrying about leaving your child at school is implicitly saying you do not believe they can cope. Your worry communicates lack of confidence in their abilities.

**Prerequisites for faith:** As long as you have confidence in the caregiver (and why would you leave your child with a caregiver in whom you do not have confidence?), then you can have faith in your child's inner strength to rise to the occasion and grow.

---

## Step 6: Get to Know the Caregiver or Teacher

It is naturally hard to relax if you do not really know the person with whom you are leaving your child.

**Pre-enrollment conversation:** Before enrolling your child, have a discussion with the caregiver about how they handle separation sadness.

**What an experienced teacher knows:** Many children will naturally feel sad at saying goodbye, and those children need comfort. Once children bond with the teacher, they will feel much more comfortable letting go of parents. This bonding process is a normal part of adjustment, not a workaround for a problem.

**Ongoing relationship maintenance:**
- Engage in brief chats as you pick your child up
- Send notes of appreciation
- Let the teacher know about anything significant happening in your child's life

These touchpoints build trust and keep communication flowing, which reduces your anxiety and helps the teacher support your child better.

---

## Step 7: Be a Few Minutes Early for Pickup

**Why timing matters:** Not seeing you immediately will exacerbate any anxieties your child has and may panic them altogether. This panic then sets back your own adjustment because you see your child distressed.

**Understanding crying at pickup:** If your child cries when you pick them up, do not worry. What you are seeing is the stress of their having to keep it together all day. Your return signals that it is safe to be their baby-self again.

**The baby-self concept:** We all have baby-selves, but as we get older our executive selves assume more and more control, especially in the outside world. The child has been operating in executive mode during school. Your arrival releases them to feel the emotions they have been managing. This is healthy emotional processing, not a sign that something went wrong.

---

## Step 8: Reinforce the Bond After School

**Daily special time:** Make sure you spend special time every day after school with your child so they know they still matter to you.

**Why physical connection is especially important:** Your child has spent the day being as grown-up as they can. They need the reassurance of snuggle time with a parent to restore the attachment connection.

**Physical roughhousing recommendation:** Do some physical roughhousing to get your child laughing every afternoon. This helps both of you work through the tension of the separation. Laughter releases stress hormones and physical play reconnects you bodily after the separation.

---

## Step 9: Intervene if Your Child is Having Trouble Adjusting

**When your own feelings become a signal:** You are not likely to feel happy saying goodbye to your child if they seem to be dreading school. Your own distress is feedback about a real problem.

**First step - talk to the teacher:** Ask the teacher or caregiver about what they observe and discuss strategies.

**Specific interventions to suggest:**
- Ask if they can give your child a special job when they arrive (this gives the child something to focus on and a sense of importance)
- Suggest that they need to bond with the teacher more, and ask if there are ways to facilitate this

**Escalation:** If the problem drags on and you do not have confidence in the caregiver, consider other options. Persistent adjustment problems combined with lack of confidence in the caregiver is a signal to explore alternatives.

---

## Step 10: Get Organized at Night and Get Enough Sleep

**The connection between your state and your child's response:** If you are grumpy or rushing, you will be impatient with your child. It is hard for anyone to feel good about saying goodbye from a mood of emotional upheaval.

**The stress-clinging cycle:** On days when you are stressed and "cannot be late" for something important (like a big presentation), you can count on your child picking up your stress and clinging more. Your urgency creates their anxiety, which creates the very delay you feared.

**Solution for high-stakes mornings:** On days with important commitments, get up earlier yourself so you can be ready early. This allows you to stay relaxed as you get your child out of the house. Your calm creates their calm.

---

## Step 11: Make a List of Things You Cannot Wait to Do

**Purpose:** Transform the separation from pure loss into partial opportunity by identifying what you will gain.

**Examples of activities to add:**
- Catching up at work
- Meditating
- Working out more
- Tackling a big project you have been putting off

**Daily self-nurturing requirement:** Make sure you give yourself at least one act of true self-nurturing every day. Examples include:
- A long bath
- Lunch with a friend
- Reading a novel before bed

---

## Step 12: Get a Life - The Deepest Framework

**Standard understanding:** Most parents realize they need other things they are passionate about besides their children, so they are not living through them. Living vicariously through children is not good for the kids or for the parents.

**Taking it further - the hierarchy of parenting responsibilities:**

The author presents a specific framework about what matters most:

**Second most important job:** Being a parent, because you are responsible for nurturing a human being whose very brain is taking shape in response to your interactions.

**Most important job:** Nurturing yourself. The reasoning: growing up is never finished, and sooner or later we all have to pick up where our parents left off. Others can help, but you are the only one who has the job of making sure you are well taken care of.

**The logical chain:** Self-nurturing -> personal wellbeing -> emotional generosity with children -> better parenting

Without maintaining your own sense of wellbeing, you cannot be emotionally generous with your children.

**Reframe on separation:** If separating from your child gives you a little more time to put yourself back at the center of your life, that is a rare opportunity. Rather than viewing separation as loss, view it as a chance to become more loving and nurturing with yourself.

---

## Key Principles Underlying the Framework

**Emotional contagion:** Children pick up parental emotional cues constantly. Your emotional state shapes their experience of the situation.

**Attachment is bidirectional:** Both parent and child experience separation anxiety. Managing the parent's side is just as important as managing the child's side.

**Nature designed both attachment and exploration:** The drive to stay close and the drive to explore are both natural. Healthy development involves honoring both.

**Self-care is not selfish:** Taking care of yourself is actually a prerequisite for taking care of your child well. It is the foundation, not an optional add-on.

**Consistency creates safety:** Rituals, predictable timing, and reliable behavior all reduce anxiety by creating a predictable environment.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Showing emotional distress to your child during goodbyes** - tears in your eyes undermine the message that everything is fine
2. **Telling your child you will be nearby "just in case"** - this prevents them from settling in
3. **Over-reacting when your child cries at separation** - this escalates the situation
4. **Running late or being stressed during drop-off** - your stress triggers their clinging
5. **Being late for pickup** - this exacerbates anxiety and can cause panic
6. **Worrying visibly** - communicates to your child that you do not believe they can cope
7. **Neglecting your own needs** - depletes the emotional resources you need for parenting
8. **Interpreting crying at pickup as a sign something went wrong** - it is actually healthy emotional release
