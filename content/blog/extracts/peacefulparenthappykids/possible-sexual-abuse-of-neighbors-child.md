# Recognizing Potential Signs of Sexual Abuse in Children

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

---

## Understanding Red Flags vs. Normal Behavior

When evaluating whether a child's behavior indicates something concerning, the key is distinguishing between behaviors that have reasonable, innocent explanations and those that don't fit any normal developmental pattern.

### The "Could This Have an Innocent Origin?" Test

**Normal curiosity about adult behavior**: A five-year-old could see an open-mouthed kiss in a movie. Children are typically grossed out by such images because kissing seems disgusting to them at that age. A child might ask parents about what they saw, be told "grown-ups kiss that way, and it's beautiful," and then want to try such a kiss with dolls or friends out of curiosity or to work through feelings of disgust. This sequence, while perhaps surprising to another parent, has a completely logical, innocent explanation.

**When the explanation doesn't fit**: The statement "that's how princesses kiss" applied to open-mouthed kissing is a red flag specifically because:
- Parents wouldn't typically frame adult kissing as "princess behavior" to a five-year-old
- Princess imagery in popular culture doesn't include open-mouthed kissing
- The framing appears designed to encourage a child interested in princesses to want to kiss open-mouthed

The concerning element isn't the behavior itself but that **the explanation the child gives doesn't match any plausible innocent source**.

### Language That Sounds Repeated vs. Originated

**Normal child expression**: When children describe their feelings about parents, they typically express how THEY feel: "I love him!" This is child-originated language that reflects their emotional experience.

**Adult-sounding language as a warning sign**: A five-year-old saying their parent is "a good lover" stands out because:
- Most five-year-olds wouldn't describe parents as "cute" or "handsome" (though by itself this is merely odd, not alarming)
- The phrase "good lover" sounds like something heard from an adult and repeated
- Even if a child overheard such a comment in a movie or adult conversation, they wouldn't typically apply it to explain why THEY like their parent
- The application of this phrase to the child's own feelings about the parent suggests "inappropriate ideas about the parent-child relationship"

## The Statistics That Should Inform Our Awareness

**Prevalence of childhood sexual abuse**: About 20% of adult women in the US recall being sexually abused as children.

**Family perpetrators**: At least 30% of abusers are family members, with some estimates reaching 60%.

**Practical implication**: Given these statistics, each of us likely knows a child who is being abused, as difficult as that is to accept.

**Why mothers often don't know**: Most mothers whose children are abused by their partners are unaware it's happening because "it is just so hard for us to believe." This psychological barrier to recognizing abuse in one's own home is a key factor in why abuse continues.

## How to Respond When You Notice Red Flags

### Deciding Whether to Approach the Other Parent

This is described as a personal decision with no single right answer. The key considerations:

**Why you might say something**: If the child is in danger, you want to help.

**How to do it**: With "a light touch." The recommended approach:
- Simply tell the mom about the specific incident (the kiss your daughter gave you)
- Share what the child said (the "princesses kiss" comment)
- Don't make accusations

**Expected response**: The other parent will probably minimize it or say your child misunderstood.

**Why it's still worth doing**: Even if dismissed, "that plants a seed so she can be more aware as she observes what's happening in her home." The goal isn't to get the parent to agree with you immediately but to introduce awareness that might help them notice things later.

### Managing Your Own Child's Exposure

**Check what your own child has absorbed**: The article recommends getting a book (the specific title appears cut off in the source) to discuss body safety and appropriate boundaries with your child.

**Why children's play matters**: "Children work out their feelings using play." If the friend has issues based on her experiences, they might come up in play contexts. This means:
- Play can be a window into what a child is processing
- Observing play between children can reveal concerning patterns

**Practical approach to playdates**: You don't necessarily have to stop them entirely, but:
- Keep playdates at your own house (gives you control over the environment and ability to observe)
- Maintain "a watchful eye" during play

## What NOT to Do

**Don't make allegations lightly**: Sexual abuse is not something to accuse without clear evidence. The article explicitly states "these are not allegations to make lightly" even when red flags are present.

**Don't dismiss your instincts as paranoia**: If something feels wrong, you're "paying attention to those little red flags," not being paranoid. The distinction between paranoia and appropriate concern is whether the behavior has a reasonable innocent explanation.

**Don't assume popular culture explains everything**: While media exposure can introduce children to concepts, some things don't come from popular culture:
- Barbies don't kiss open-mouthed in mainstream media
- Young children don't conclude their daddies are "good lovers" from watching movies

---

## Teaching Appropriate Boundaries to Your Own Child

When your child displays concerning behavior learned from another child:

1. **Immediately correct the behavior**: "We do NOT kiss with open mouths"
2. **Ask where they learned it**: Get the child to identify the source
3. **Have a follow-up discussion**: Talk about who it is and is not appropriate to kiss

This models that parents can discuss these topics, that there are boundaries around physical affection, and that children can and should tell parents when they learn new things about bodies and relationships.
