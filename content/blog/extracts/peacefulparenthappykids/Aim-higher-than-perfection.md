# Aim Higher Than Perfection: Unconditional Love as the Real Standard

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/Aim-higher-than-perfection

---

## The Core Reframe: Perfectionism Is Too Low a Standard

The central insight is counterintuitive: **perfectionism is not aiming too high - it's aiming too low**. The reason is that perfectionism focuses on external standards and outcomes, while unconditional love focuses on acceptance and growth. Perfectionism sets up a standard that can never be met, creating a cycle of failure and self-criticism. Unconditional love, by contrast, provides the foundation from which actual growth becomes possible.

Brene Brown is quoted: "Perfectionism hampers success. In fact, it's often the path to depression, anxiety, addiction, and life-paralysis." This frames perfectionism not as a virtue that occasionally goes too far, but as inherently harmful - a path that leads away from wellbeing rather than toward excellence.

---

## The Self-Judgment Trap

Most parents judge themselves harshly against an impossible standard. Common examples of "failures" parents criticize themselves for:
- Oversleeping
- Eating cake (breaking diet rules)
- Forgetting to return phone calls
- Snapping at a partner
- Yelling at a child
- Not providing a hot breakfast
- Rushing the child so they forget homework

This self-judgment doesn't stay contained - it extends to judging the child. The observation: "While we're judging ourselves, how's the kid turning out? Not so perfect either?" reveals how parental perfectionism creates anxiety about the child's development. Nothing triggers parental anxiety more than seeing a child struggle or have difficulties.

---

## What Children Actually Need: Space to Be Imperfect

**The fundamental insight: Children need space to be imperfect, to be loved and accepted exactly as they are.**

This isn't about lowering standards or abandoning guidance - it's about recognizing that acceptance is the starting point for growth, not the endpoint. The reasoning: "That's the only place any of us can start from to grow." Growth requires a secure foundation of acceptance. Without that foundation, energy goes into self-protection rather than development.

---

## Two Modes of Guidance: Love-Based vs. Fear-Based

Parents can guide children from two fundamentally different emotional places, and the source matters as much as the content.

### Guidance with humor and understanding (love-based)
Examples of appropriate guidance delivered well:
- "Let's wait our turn at the slide..."
- "Here's how you work out with your sister how to share the toy..."
- "We brush our teeth every night..."

These examples show guidance that is:
- Clear about expectations
- Matter-of-fact rather than emotionally charged
- Framed as normal learning rather than correction of deficiency

### Guidance from fear (fear-based)
The internal question that signals fear-based guidance: "Is there something wrong with him?"

**The warning: Fear shades so quickly into criticism.** This is presented as a near-automatic progression - fear doesn't stay as private worry, it leaks into interactions and communications. The child receives the message that "somehow he just isn't quite good enough."

The distinction is not about what you're teaching but why and how you're teaching it. The same lesson delivered from fear versus love has entirely different impacts on the child's sense of self.

---

## The Alternative: Faith in the Child's Unfolding

Instead of fear-based guidance, the recommendation is to guide with faith that the child is "blossoming and growing all the time, becoming her best self."

This faith isn't naive optimism - it's a recognition that children are naturally developmental beings. The framing of "becoming her best self" acknowledges that the child isn't complete yet while maintaining that the trajectory is positive.

---

## Emotional Nutrients: What Children Need More Than Teaching

**Key concept: What children need more than teaching is "emotional nutrients to thrive."**

The four emotional nutrients identified:
1. **Unconditional love** - love that doesn't depend on performance or behavior
2. **Joy in who she is** - delight in the child's actual self, not just achievements
3. **Faith in the friendliness of the universe** - a sense that the world is fundamentally safe and welcoming
4. **Faith in her own goodness and ability to grow** - trust in the child's inherent nature and developmental capacity

This list prioritizes being over doing, relationship over instruction. The phrase "more than teaching" doesn't dismiss teaching but reorders priorities - the emotional foundation must come first.

---

## Practical Application: Using Challenges to Create Love

The action framework: "Use the challenges of life to create love where there wasn't any before."

This reframes challenges not as obstacles to good parenting but as opportunities. Each difficult moment is a chance to respond with love rather than criticism, thereby building the relationship and demonstrating unconditional acceptance.

### Specific practices recommended:
1. **Let go of fear and perfectionism** - an active releasing, not passive acceptance
2. **Choose love** - framed as a choice, implying it requires intention
3. **Embrace your imperfect self with compassion** - self-compassion precedes compassion for the child
4. **Meet your child heart to heart** - direct emotional connection rather than performance-based interaction
5. **Delight in who he is, imperfections and all** - active appreciation that explicitly includes flaws
6. **Focus on all the things you love about him** - attention management as a practice
7. **Chalk up "imperfections" to learning experiences** - reframing mistakes as developmental data
8. **Use imperfections to get back on track** - mistakes become navigation tools rather than verdicts

The sequence matters: self-compassion enables compassion for the child; acceptance enables effective guidance; focus on positives creates the emotional safety for addressing challenges.

---

## The Higher Standard: Unconditional Love

The conclusion reframes the entire perfectionism conversation: **"Start aiming higher than perfection. Aim for unconditional love."**

This positions unconditional love as more ambitious and more difficult than perfectionism, not as a relaxation of standards. Perfectionism is easier because it has clear (if impossible) metrics. Unconditional love is harder because it requires ongoing choice, emotional regulation, and acceptance of uncertainty.

The final message inverts the common assumption that accepting imperfection means lowering the bar. Instead, it argues that unconditional love is the higher standard - one that perfectionism falls short of.
