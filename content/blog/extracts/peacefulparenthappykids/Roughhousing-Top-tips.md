# Roughhousing: How to Make It Work Safely for Your Family

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/Roughhousing-Top-tips

---

## Why Roughhousing Matters for Children

Roughhousing is not just acceptable but **essential** for kids' development. The benefits work through multiple biological and psychological mechanisms:

**Laughter's physiological effects**: When children laugh during roughhousing, it transforms their body chemistry. Laughter reduces stress hormones and anxiety while simultaneously increasing **oxytocin** (the bonding hormone). This means that laughing together with another person actively strengthens that relationship at a biochemical level.

**Physical movement and brain development**: The physical activity involved in roughhousing helps children work out emotions stored in their bodies. This movement is also essential for brain development, not merely a pleasant side effect.

**Learning to manage aggression**: Like other young mammals, when kids engage in "play" fighting, they develop the ability to manage aggression. This practice makes them less likely to lash out when they're actually angry because they've learned to regulate those impulses in a safe context.

When kids wrestle, pillow fight, and roughhouse, it's genuinely good for them. The challenge is that it isn't always good for our houses, and parents naturally worry that someone will get hurt.

---

## The Core Principle: Proactive Rather Than Reactive Intervention

The central insight that runs through all the guidance: we cannot simply hope for the best. We need to actively help children learn how to roughhouse safely. This requires ongoing attention and teaching over time. Our job is to notice when kids are getting riled up in ways that signal danger, and then give them the tools to stay safe.

## Ten Strategies for Safe Roughhousing

### 1. Intervene Before You Get Angry

**The signal to act**: The minute you start getting worried that someone will get hurt, that worry is your signal to do something. Do not yell. Instead, intervene in a positive way to ensure safety.

**The common mistake**: Many parents try so hard to be patient that they let things get out of hand. The consequence is predictable: someone ends up crying, and the parent ends up yelling. That yelling is exactly the opposite of the emotional regulation you want to model for your children.

**Why timing matters**: By intervening early, while you're still calm, you can correct the situation in a way that teaches regulation rather than demonstrating dysregulation.

### 2. Assess the Actual Danger Level

Before shutting things down, honestly evaluate whether there's real danger present:

**It might not be dangerous**: Sometimes kids are being loud and exuberant but having a great time with no actual danger to anyone or to your home.

**Small changes might suffice**: Perhaps moving furniture would make the activity safe. For example, pushing the bed closer to the dresser so kids can jump onto the bed safely.

**Substitution can work**: If children are having a throwing contest with blocks, you can substitute stuffed animals instead of stopping the activity entirely.

### 3. Connect Before You Correct

**Why yelling across the room fails**: Yelling from a distance just adds to the frenzy rather than calming it.

**What to do instead**: Go physically to your children. When a child is spinning out of control, you cannot get through to her unless you move in close in a friendly way.

**The sequence**: Make a positive connection with your child BEFORE asking her to do something different.

**Example language**: "You two are having lots of fun with this roughhousing, aren't you?"

This approach acknowledges their experience first, which makes them more receptive to what comes next.

### 4. Set Clear Limits

Once you've connected, state your rule or expectation firmly and kindly. Both firmness and kindness are required together.

**Example language**: "This kind of play doesn't belong in this room. I'm worried that you might roll into the lamp or the TV."

Notice the limit is specific (this room) and the reason is given (danger to objects), rather than a blanket prohibition.

### 5. Empathize While Offering Alternatives and Choices

**Acknowledge the difficulty**: "I know it's hard to stop..." validates their experience rather than dismissing it.

**Provide alternatives**: "...but this kind of play belongs in the basement on the tumbling mat, or outside."

**Offer a choice**: "Your choice. Outside? Deal!"

Giving children a choice between acceptable options maintains their sense of autonomy while keeping everyone safe.

### 6. Check In With All Participants

Ask directly: "Is everyone still having fun with this?"

**What to watch for**: If one child is getting into a frenzy while the other seems tense, intervention is needed.

**How to facilitate**: Help children check in with each other. "Jaden, do you see that your brother isn't laughing? Let's stop for a minute and be sure everyone feels safe."

**Teach consent skills**: "Liam, you can tell Jaden to stop whenever you want. Do you want to practice it, right now?"

This teaches children to read social cues, notice others' emotional states, and practice asserting boundaries.

### 7. Help Kids Create Their Own Safety Rules

**Resist the urge to shut things down**: If you're worried someone's about to get hurt, fight the impulse to just stop the activity out of your own anxiety.

**Facilitate rule-making instead**: Help your kids make their own rules to keep everyone safe. This teaches them to think about safety themselves rather than just following imposed rules.

**Example dialogue**: "Play wrestling is great, as long as you have rules to stay safe. What are your rules? Oh, when someone yells 'Stop!' both people have to stop? And no hitting? Those sound like great rules! How are they working so far? Do you need any other rules?"

This approach validates the activity, prompts them to generate their own rules, affirms their ideas, and checks on effectiveness.

### 8. Understand That Tears Have Value

**Sometimes tears match the injury**: When kids get a bump during roughhousing and cry, sometimes the tears are proportionate to the injury. In these cases, a quick hug and they're ready to get back to playing.

**Sometimes tears seem like an overreaction**: When kids sob wildly in response to a minor bump, this is actually a good thing. The laughter has loosened up feelings that were stuffed in their **"emotional backpack"** (stored, unexpressed emotions). They're using this small injury as an opportunity to release deeper feelings they can't put into words.

**The result of a good cry**: After crying out these emotions, your child will be much more relaxed and happy because those feelings have been released.

**Reframe your response**: Instead of feeling like a bad parent because someone got hurt, relax. Take the opportunity to help your child with their big feelings and be grateful they got a chance to cry.

**After the tears**: Ask both kids if they need to add any new rules to keep everyone safer next time.

**The power of written rules**: Consider writing the rules down and posting them. This makes it easy to remind children when they start getting wild. Written agreements have extra power even for kids who can't read.

### 9. Help Them Wind Down

**When to redirect**: Sometimes you genuinely need to redirect to a calmer activity.

**Recognizing pre-meltdown states**: Often when kids are really wound up, they're actually on the verge of a meltdown.

**How to test this**: If you sense a meltdown brewing, move in close and set a limit. "OK, Bella, time to calm down now. That's enough rowdiness."

**Two good outcomes**:
- If she calms down, great.
- If she bursts into tears, that's also great. Better those feelings come out through crying in your arms than by her hurting her little brother.

Both outcomes are success. The goal isn't to prevent crying but to help emotions release safely.

### 10. Provide a Designated Space for Wild Play

**The biological need**: Like puppies or bear cubs, kids need to roll around, wrestle, climb, and jump. This is not optional or excessive; it's a fundamental need.

**Modern life's limitation**: Our contemporary lifestyles don't always provide opportunities for this kind of movement.

**Solutions**:
- A yard
- A basement with a tumbling mat
- Making their room safe for roughhousing
- Regular playtime at playgrounds or parks

**The consequence of not providing space**: If you don't give kids a designated space for wild play, your couch will start to look like a trampoline, and your lamps will be in constant danger.

## Key Mindset Shifts

**Setting limits early is easier**: Notice how much easier it is when you set the limit BEFORE you get mad. You can calmly, kindly, cheerfully do whatever is necessary to keep things safe from the start.

**Avoid overreaction**: Don't shut down all exuberant play. Wildness is normal for kids.

**Bumps and bruises are not emergencies**: The situation is usually not an emergency even when there are some injuries.

**Tears are not failure**: Tears aren't the end of the world. They often serve a valuable emotional release function.

**Laughter transforms**: Laughter is always transformative, so protecting children's opportunity to laugh together through roughhousing is worthwhile.

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **Waiting too long to intervene** because you're trying to be patient, then exploding when things go wrong
2. **Yelling across the room** instead of physically moving close to your children
3. **Skipping the connection** and going straight to correction
4. **Shutting down play from your own anxiety** rather than helping kids create safety rules
5. **Feeling like a failure** when tears happen, rather than seeing them as potentially valuable
6. **Not providing a designated space** for wild play, leading to conflicts over furniture and household items
7. **Imposing rules from above** instead of facilitating children creating their own rules
