# Giving Yourself the Support You Need: Self-Compassion as the Foundation of Parenting

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/support-self-compassion

---

## The Core Problem: Why Parents Struggle After Vacation

Many parents report being great with their kids on vacation but reverting to yelling and stress once normal life resumes. The pattern is common: when external pressures decrease (vacation), parenting improves naturally. When stress returns, so do the negative behaviors. This reveals that the issue is not skill or knowledge but the parent's internal state and capacity for self-regulation under pressure.

## The Foundational Insight: You Must Become Your Own Source of Support

When stressed, every parent wishes someone else would step in to take care of them, like an all-powerful, loving parent figure who would hug them, feed them, tuck them into bed, and handle the mess of their lives including childcare. This wish is universal and legitimate.

However, this external rescue is not going to happen. The adult reality is that **we must step in to do this for ourselves**.

The emotional work of adulthood consists of two sequential tasks:
1. **Grieving** that we never got the care we needed and deserved as children
2. **Learning** how to give that care to ourselves now

This process is called **reparenting**. Reparenting yourself is what allows you to give unconditional love to your kids. The healing chain works like this: loving ourselves -> healing our own wounds -> capacity to love our children unconditionally.

## Seven Strategies for Self-Support

### 1. Establish Healthy Physical Routines

Set up routines that ensure you give yourself the physical care you need:
- Adequate sleep
- Healthy food
- Regular exercise

The principle here is that self-support must be systematized, not left to chance. Routines remove the need for daily willpower decisions about basic self-care.

### 2. Become Your Own Nurturing Parent

Give yourself encouragement throughout the day, all day long. The specific mental technique: think of yourself as an angel on your own shoulder. Cultivate an inner voice that is loving rather than critical.

This is an active, ongoing practice, not a one-time mindset shift. The inner voice must be deliberately cultivated and maintained.

### 3. Build Resilience Through Daily Practice

Cultivate practices that restore your resilience, such as daily meditation. The key word is "daily" because resilience is not built through occasional intervention but through consistent practice that compounds over time.

### 4. Find Community with Shared Values

Find other people who share your parenting approach. This serves two purposes:
- Feeling less alone when you mess up (normalization and support during failures)
- Feeling less alone in your aspirations (validation that your goals are reasonable and achievable)

Community provides both accountability and comfort, depending on what you need in the moment.

### 5. Seek Regular Reinforcement

Find regular reinforcements that encourage you in both parenting and self-care. Examples include:
- Inspiring podcasts
- Emails or newsletters

The emphasis is on "regular" because one-time inspiration fades. Consistent reinforcement maintains motivation and keeps best practices top of mind.

### 6. Practice Continuous Self-Check-Ins

Take responsibility to check in with yourself throughout the day, every day. The practice involves:
1. Asking yourself what you need right now
2. Either giving it to yourself immediately, OR
3. Making a promise to yourself to provide it later, AND
4. Following through on that promise

This last point is crucial: promises to yourself must be kept. Breaking self-promises erodes self-trust, while keeping them builds it.

### 7. Practice Self-Compassion When You Mess Up

Messing up will happen many times a day. This is not exceptional but expected. When it happens:
1. Be compassionate to yourself
2. Remind yourself that every parent has felt the way you feel
3. Recognize that you can start now to make a repair
4. Remember that it is never too late to repair your relationship with your child

**Self-compassion might be the most important practice you can cultivate.** This is explicitly identified as potentially the highest-leverage intervention among all the strategies.

## Why This Matters: The Non-Negotiable Nature of Self-Care

Self-care is not selfish. It is essential for being a good parent. The reasoning:

- You can only give your child what you have inside
- If you are depleted, you have nothing to give
- Depletion is not sustainable and costs your children, not just you
- Therefore, self-care directly benefits your children

The common objection is that parents don't have time for self-care. The response: you must put yourself back on your own list. Not as an optional addition but as a requirement for effective parenting.

## The Central Role of Mental Self-Management

A significant portion of self-support is about managing your own mind. This is because the most important part of giving yourself support is the way you treat yourself inside your own head.

The binary choice: you can either be on your own side, or you can make yourself miserable. These are the only two options. There is no neutral position.

External circumstances are not controllable, but you always deserve your own support. That internal support is what helps you respond better to whatever happens, including your own mistakes.

## The Deeper Framework: Parenting as Personal Growth

The secret work of adulthood is that we are all still growing up. Parenting forces us to learn two parallel skills:
1. How to parent our child
2. How to parent ourselves

These are not separate projects but interconnected work. Progress on one enables progress on the other.

This work begins with learning to love and nurture ourselves. Self-love is the starting point, not an advanced technique to add later.

## The Ultimate Responsibility

Every person is responsible for their own peace of mind and how they spend their life. On your deathbed, there will not be anyone else to blame if you have been unhappy. This framing places full accountability on the individual while also granting full agency: if you are responsible, you also have the power to change things.

## Practical Application

The recommendation is to print out the seven strategies above and check them off as you solidify each type of support. Whenever you feel stressed and depleted, return to these principles as a reset and recharge mechanism.
