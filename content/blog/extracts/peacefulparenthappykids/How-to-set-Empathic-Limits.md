# Empathic Limits: Setting Boundaries While Maintaining Connection

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/How-to-set-Empathic-Limits

## Core Concept: What Empathic Limits Are

**Empathic limits** are a parenting approach that combines firm boundary-setting with emotional support for the child's feelings about those boundaries. The parent maintains the non-negotiable limit while simultaneously validating and accepting the child's emotional response to it. This stands in contrast to both permissive parenting (giving in to avoid upset) and authoritarian approaches (ignoring or suppressing the child's emotional reaction).

The fundamental principle: the two-year-old should not be making all the decisions for the family, but the child's feelings about those decisions deserve acknowledgment and support.

## The Process: A Step-by-Step Framework

### Step 1: Verbal Empathy and Acknowledgment First

Before stating the limit, acknowledge how the child must be feeling. The parent verbally names what they observe and validates the child's experience.

Example: "Avery is having so much fun on the swing. You must want to stay and swing for a long time."

This creates connection before introducing potential conflict.

### Step 2: State the Limit Clearly

After empathizing, state the boundary clearly with a reason.

Example: "But we need to fill our hungry bellies with a yummy lunch so we need to go home."

### Step 3: Offer Choices That Work for Both

Give the child options, but ensure all options are acceptable to you. This serves two purposes:
- It helps the child "save face"
- It gives them some sense of control within the boundary

Example: "You have a choice, you can jump down and walk with me, or I will pick you up and you can ride in the stroller."

### Step 4: Use Wish Fulfillment

When the child resists, acknowledge their wish in its fullest form. This is called **wish fulfillment** - validating the desire even when you cannot fulfill it.

Example: "Avery, you want to stay in the swing, all day, don't you?" followed by "I wish you could."

This demonstrates that you truly understand what they want, not just superficially.

### Step 5: Follow Through with Warmth

If the child cannot make a choice, gently take action yourself while framing it as helping rather than punishing.

Example: "Ok, Sweetie, I see it's too hard for you to leave the swing yourself. I will help you down and into the stroller."

## What NOT to Do When Your Child Cries (and Why)

### Don't Ignore the Crying

Traditional parenting advice suggests wrestling the child into compliance and ignoring their crying so we don't "reward" it with attention. This approach is problematic because:
- It breaks the connection with your child
- It is disrespectful (the test: would you do it to your spouse?)
- It sends the message that emotions are bad
- It communicates conditional love: "we will only attend to you if we like what you are expressing"
- The child is left alone with big, scary feelings

### Don't Distract

The temptation is to redirect: "Wow, Avery, look at that cute doggie right there!" However, distraction:
- Dishonors the child's experience
- Tells them their emotions are too scary for us to deal with
- Teaches emotional avoidance rather than processing

## The Power of Holding Space for Tears

When the child begins crying after you enforce the limit, the parent's role shifts to supporting them through the emotion.

### What to Say

"You are crying. You don't want to leave the swing. You are so sad and mad that we have to leave. I'm sorry you can't swing all day, but it is lunchtime. I will sit with you on this bench and hold you while you cry."

Key phrases that keep the tears coming (intentionally):
- "You are sad, you are crying"
- "I am right here"
- "You are safe"
- "You can cry"

If the child is angry and twists away, stay nearby and maintain connection with your voice: "I'm right here. I won't leave you alone with those big feelings."

### Why Crying is Good and Helpful

Despite the discomfort of having other parents stare at you, a crying child is not a sign of parenting failure. In fact:

- Crying is helpful for a two-year-old with big feelings
- They need to express emotions and show them to a caring adult
- The alternative - "stuffing" emotions - is harmful

### The Hidden Benefit: Processing Backlogged Emotions

When a child is held while crying, they often begin crying about other things beyond the immediate trigger:
- A new baby sibling
- A time when a parent snapped at them
- A scary experience (like a barking dog)
- A time they didn't cry but wanted to (like falling down in front of Grandma who praised them for being "brave" and said "big girls don't cry")

This is why holding a child through their tears is described as "a tremendous gift" - it gives them an opportunity to process accumulated emotional backlog.

### The "Picking Fights" Insight

A critical insight: children often **pick fights** by resisting our limits precisely to get the opportunity to cry like this. The resistance to leaving the swing may not really be about the swing at all - it's about needing emotional release. Understanding this reframes the behavior from defiance to a cry for help.

## After the Cry: What Happens Next

When the crying finally subsides:
1. The child will be snuggled in your arms
2. Give them a big hug
3. Name what happened: "You were crying. You were sad. Now you feel better."
4. Transition forward: "Let's go home and get those yummy sandwiches. Do you want a drink of water before you get in the stroller?"

### The Transformation Effect

After a good cry in a parent's loving presence, the child will:
- Be free of whatever feelings were making them stick rigidly to their position
- Feel relaxed
- Become cooperative

This is because **when kids are rigid and insist on getting their way, that's a red flag that they need to cry**. The same principle applies to adults.

## Initial Implementation: What to Expect

The first time you try this approach:
- Your child may cry for a long time (this is not bad - they're venting pent-up emotion)
- They may think crying will convince you to change the limit
- Important: empathizing with feelings does NOT mean rescinding a limit that is important to you

## Long-Term Outcomes

With consistent practice, children learn through experience that:
1. **Your limits are firm**, even when they cannot understand why they're important
2. **You really do care about their happiness** - demonstrated by your empathy
3. **Feelings are manageable** - they can be expressed and survived
4. **Disappointment can be weathered** - it's not catastrophic

These learnings form the beginning of **resilience** and **emotional intelligence**.

## Why Children Test Limits Repeatedly

Children who constantly test limits are usually showing that they don't feel safe. This seems paradoxical - why would a child who wants power feel unsafe?

The explanation: children may love the idea of being all-powerful, but it also terrifies them. They need to know that parents will be in charge and keep them safe. The constant testing is actually a search for boundaries.

Once children are convinced that the parent will maintain boundaries AND remain emotionally available, they no longer need to constantly test the limits to find out where the boundaries are.

## Summary Framework: The Dual Commitment

Empathic limits require holding two things simultaneously:
1. **Firm limit** - the boundary is non-negotiable to you
2. **Empathy for the response** - the child's feelings about the limit are valid and welcomed

This is different from:
- Giving in to avoid tears (permissive - teaches limits are negotiable)
- Ignoring tears while enforcing limits (authoritarian - teaches emotions are unwelcome)
- Distracting from tears (avoidant - teaches emotions are too scary to face)

The approach respects both the parent's authority to set necessary limits AND the child's right to have feelings about those limits.
