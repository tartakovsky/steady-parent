# Positive Discipline for Preteens

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/positive-discipline-preteen

---

## The Preteen Transition: Why Traditional Discipline Fails

The preteen years mark a fundamental shift in the parent-child dynamic. Children become heavily influenced by their desire for peer respect, which often conflicts with standards set at home. Simultaneously, parents lose the automatic respect that young children naturally give, and must now earn it through their behavior and relationship quality.

**Why punishment stops working at this age**: Parents who have relied on punishment (including timeouts and consequences) discover it no longer produces the desired results. The core reason is that it becomes impossible to control preteens when they're out of your sight, which they increasingly are. If you've relied on punishment as your primary tool, you've neglected to build the foundation that actually motivates a preteen to "act right."

**The two-part foundation for preteen cooperation**:
1. A strong parent-child relationship where the child genuinely WANTS to cooperate and doesn't want to disappoint the parent
2. Empathizing with feelings so the child develops the ability to regulate their own emotions, which in turn allows them to regulate their behavior

**Why punishment undermines both pillars**: Conventional punishment-based parenting erodes the parent-child relationship, making the child less motivated to cooperate. Additionally, punishment doesn't teach children to regulate their emotions, so they develop less self-discipline, not more. It's counterproductive on both counts.

**The evidence for non-punitive approaches**: Kids who aren't punished but are instead lovingly guided to make reparations and solve problems develop internal discipline and a strong moral sense earlier than their punished peers. Respectful, positive guidance from the start produces preteens who are respectful, considerate, responsible, self-disciplined, and delightful through the teen years.

## Catching Up: It's Not Too Late

If you've been using punishment (such as consequences) and your child is now around ten and acting disrespectful, the situation is recoverable, but you have work to do.

**The core principle for catching up**: Hold on to your high expectations, but instead of punishing your child for not meeting them, provide whatever support they need to meet them.

**Three types of support to provide**:

1. **Structural support**: Earlier bedtime, no screens during the week, or other environmental changes that remove obstacles to good behavior

2. **Emotional support**: Providing a listening ear, empathy, and validation so your child can make their best decisions rather than being driven by big emotions

3. **Executive function support**: Asking the right questions to help your child develop the prefrontal cortex skills needed for self-regulation and good judgment

## Strategy 1: Model a Respectful Tone

**The commitment**: If you're yelling, stop completely.

**Why yelling backfires**:
- Kids lose respect for parents who yell, which reduces your influence over them
- Children learn that yelling is how to express emotions and solve problems, so they yell too
- Yelling becomes unnecessary once you have a good relationship, so reliance on it signals relationship problems

---

## Strategy 2: Strengthen the Relationship Through Listening

**The goal**: Build a strong relationship where your child feels seen, heard, understood, and valued.

**Why this matters for behavior**: When your child feels truly valued, they will tell you the truth and want to share with you. When you set a limit ("Homework before screen time!") or express an expectation ("We speak civilly in this house"), your child will want to cooperate because they care about the relationship.

**Practical implementation**:
- Have one-on-one time with each child every day
- During this time, mostly listen rather than talk or advise
- Train yourself not to overreact when your child shares something with you (overreacting shuts down future sharing)

**If your child is "too busy" to connect**: Find alternative connection points. Give foot massages while talking. Connect through their passions, like listening to their music with them. The underlying truth is that you cannot have any influence if your kid doesn't enjoy being with you.

---

## Strategy 3: Develop Good Judgment Through Reflective Questions

**The principle**: Questions work infinitely better than lectures for developing executive function skills of the prefrontal cortex.

**Prerequisites for this to work**:
- Calm yourself first so your tone is warm, supportive, and curious
- If your child feels judged, shamed, or senses that punishment is looming, there will be no learning and probably no honest answers

**The question sequence for reflection**:

1. "What were you wanting to have happen when you did this?" - Gets at intent and helps child understand their own motivations

2. "Was there some part of you that said 'Don't do this'?" - Helps them recognize their internal warning signals

3. "What got in the way of you listening to that part of you?" - Identifies the barriers to self-regulation

4. "How did that work out for you?" - Encourages self-assessment of consequences

5. "How did it work out for other people involved?" - Develops empathy and perspective-taking

6. "What else could you have done?" - Generates alternatives for future situations

7. "Right, you could have done X... how do you think that would have worked out? What would have happened then?" - Mental simulation of alternative choices and their outcomes

## Strategy 4: Resist Threats and Punishment

**The psychology of self-discipline**: Self-discipline develops when the child CHOOSES to give up what they want for something they want more. What do they want more? To have a good relationship with you, to trust you, to know they can count on your love even when they mess up. This is the "roots" that allows them to spread their "wings" and learn to fly safely.

**"Consequences" are punishment in disguise**: When you "give" consequences, that's punishment with a different name.

**Example of the punishment approach (what NOT to do)**:
> "You should have done your homework last night. Next time I see you doing a rush job in the morning like this, you lose your screen time later today."

**Why this backfires (the unintended consequences)**:
- The child feels controlled, making rebellion more likely
- The child doesn't take responsibility for their behavior; instead, they get mad at how "unfair" you're being and blame you
- If this happens often, the child stops trying to do homework in the morning altogether and starts hiding the fact that they haven't done it

**The alternative approach using empathy and wondering**:
> "I see you're trying hard to get that math homework done this morning. Not much fun to have to rush like that, is it? It also means it's harder to do your best work and catch your mistakes. Let's figure out a way for you to finish your homework at night so you don't feel so stressed in the morning..."

**Why the empathetic approach works**: The natural result of not getting homework done the night before is having to rush in the morning. If you help the child notice that effect themselves, instead of using threats and control that trigger rebellion, they're more likely to take responsibility and act on that knowledge.

**When natural consequences aren't enough**: If your child doesn't step up and handle themselves responsibly (very common with screen usage), you'll probably need to make rules to limit screen time during the week so homework can get done. This is structural support, not punishment.

## Strategy 5: Create Agreed-Upon Family Rules

**Why agreement matters**: One key to helping preteens WANT to cooperate is letting them have some say in their lives.

**Implementation**:
- Discuss your family rules together
- Make a written agreement
- Stick to the important stuff: respectful tone, homework, family dinners, screen time limits

**Discuss what happens when rules are broken in advance**: But NOT punishment! Focus on REPAIR. Ask: How will your child make a repair and restore your trust?

**A surprising finding**: When you discuss consequences in advance, kids often volunteer heftier penalties than parents would. Your insistence on repair for infractions, as opposed to consequences, will help motivate your child to follow the rules.

---

## Strategy 6: Teach Repair Instead of Punishment

**Addressing accountability concerns**: If you're worried your child isn't being "held accountable," introduce the concept of reparations.

**The distinction between reparation and consequence**:
- A consequence is a punishment you impose
- A reparation is when you ask your child what they can do to make the situation better now

**Examples of reparations**:
- If he says something mean to his sister, he needs to do repair work on that relationship
- If he breaks something, he needs to help pay for a replacement

**Critical implementation detail**: If you think up the reparation and force it on the child, they will naturally reject it. Instead, let this be an empowering opportunity for them to learn that we all make mistakes, and we have the responsibility to take action to make things better.

**The milk-spilling metaphor**: It's like spilling milk. We don't cry over it, and there's no blame or shame, but we do have to clean it up when we spill it. This captures the no-shame, still-responsible approach.

---

## Strategy 7: Allow Natural Consequences

**The common parental impulse to protect**: When we worry about our child, we often intervene to protect them from natural consequences.

**Example**: If your child can't seem to remember to turn in the permission slip for the class trip, you might want to help them brainstorm how to remember when they get to school tomorrow.

**The boundary**: If they still forget, they'll learn an invaluable lesson. Don't rescue them. The natural consequence teaches what your intervention prevents them from learning.

---

## Strategy 8: Be Kind But Firm

**Expect testing**: Assume your child will test you to see if you're serious about the new approach.

**The stance to maintain**: Stay cheerful while you keep enforcing the limits.

**Practical example**: Be present during homework time and ensure your child stays on track with it instead of playing computer games with friends.

**Timeline expectation**: If you have relied on punishment rather than helping your child develop inner discipline, this transition could take weeks or even months. After a while of consistently enforcing your rule, it will become a habit. Your child will reap the benefits of the new structure and will begin to do it on their own.

---

## Expected Outcomes

The transition takes time and patience, but the result is:
- A more cooperative, respectful, and responsible kid
- A sweeter, stronger relationship with your child

This is described as a true gift at any time, but especially valuable before the teen years start, as it sets the foundation for that more challenging developmental period.
