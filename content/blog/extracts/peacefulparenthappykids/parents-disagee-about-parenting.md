# When Parents Disagree About Parenting Decisions

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/parents-disagee-about-parenting

---

## Core Principle: Parenting Partnership Requires Communication, Not Perfection

**Parenting is a partnership, and partnerships are hard.** Most people are not taught how to be good partners. When one parent stays home with the kids, the working parent often feels left out of parenting decisions and the day-to-day relationship with the children. This dynamic creates tension that needs to be addressed through direct communication, not avoidance.

## Key Insight: Complete Agreement Is Neither Possible Nor Necessary

**Parents do not have to always agree about parenting decisions.** A completely united front is both impossible to achieve and unnecessary for effective parenting. Children naturally learn that each parent has different areas of leniency. One parent might be more lenient about buying things, while the other might be more flexible about bedtime. This variation is normal and healthy.

The problem is not disagreement itself but rather how disagreement is handled in front of children.

## The Real Problem: Being Overruled in Front of Children

When one parent contradicts the other's decision in front of the kids (saying something like "No, it's okay if you do that" after the other parent said no), several damaging things happen:

1. **Mixed signals to children** - Kids receive contradictory information about what is and isn't allowed
2. **The overruled parent feels disrespected** - Even if the issue seems trivial, the pattern itself is hurtful
3. **Children learn they can go behind one parent's back** - Kids discover they can appeal to one parent to have the other parent's decisions revised
4. **Modeling disrespect** - Children may perceive that one parent disrespects the other, which affects how they view and treat that parent

The pattern matters more than any individual incident. Even when each instance involves a trivial issue, the cumulative effect undermines the overruled parent's authority and the partnership itself.

## Warning Sign: Avoiding the Conversation

When someone takes time to ask an outside party about a relationship issue but has not discussed it with their partner, this indicates the issue genuinely bothers them. Avoiding difficult conversations with a spouse while seeking outside advice signals a need to build comfort with direct communication. The goal should be increasing comfort in discussing difficult issues with your partner directly.

## Framework: How to Have This Conversation

### Step 1: Set Up the Conversation Properly

Tell your partner you have something important to discuss in private. Choose a time when you can talk without interruption:
- After the kids go to bed, pour a glass of wine and sit on the couch together
- Have coffee on Sunday morning while the kids watch cartoons
- Find any time when you can talk in peace

The setting matters because this conversation requires focus and privacy.

### Step 2: Lead with Affirmation

Start by telling your partner you love them and that you think they're a great parent. This establishes that the conversation comes from a place of care and appreciation, not criticism or attack.

### Step 3: State the Observation Neutrally

Describe what you've noticed without accusation: "I notice that sometimes when my instinct is to say no, you say yes." Frame it as an observation about a pattern, not a complaint about your partner being wrong.

### Step 4: Identify Your Two Concerns

Clearly articulate what specifically bothers you:

**First concern: Understanding the reasoning.** You want to understand why your partner makes these decisions so you can both be on the same page. Note: it's okay if your partner makes the decision, but you need to understand the reasoning behind it.

**Second concern: The emotional impact.** You feel embarrassed and hurt when you're overruled in front of the kids. You worry that the children won't take you seriously as an authority figure and will disrespect you because they witness this pattern.

### Step 5: Express Your Deeper Need

Explain that you want to be involved in your children's lives in an intimate way. Even though you're not the stay-at-home parent, you hope your partner will value your partnership in parenting. This connects the specific issue to the larger relationship dynamic.

### Step 6: Stop Talking and Listen

After stating your concerns, close your mouth and let your partner talk. This is crucial. Your partner may say some hurtful things, because:

- Many spouses feel their partner is oblivious and clueless when it comes to the kids
- Even if not objectively true, many stay-at-home parents feel they understand the children much better and should be the ones making decisions
- These feelings have built up without an outlet

**How to handle hurtful responses:** Do not take anything said personally. Acknowledge your partner's feelings. Their feelings and opinions are legitimate as feelings and opinions even if you disagree with them. Feelings don't have to be "correct" to be valid.

### Step 7: Collaboratively Create a Signal System

Ask your partner what the two of you can do on the spot when one of you disagrees with a parenting decision the other is making.

**Example signal system:** When one parent disagrees with a decision, they can tell the other parent they "need a hug" (or another code phrase) in private. After the two parents discuss the issue away from the children, the parent who made the original decision can return and tell the kids they've thought more about the issue and made a more thoughtful decision.

This approach:
- Maintains a united front in the moment
- Creates space for private discussion
- Models thoughtful decision-making for children
- Preserves both parents' authority

## Expected Outcome

This type of honest, vulnerable conversation typically strengthens both the relationship with your partner and your parenting partnership. The act of communicating openly about difficult topics builds connection and trust.

## Anti-Pattern: Not Discussing Because It's "Trivial"

Even when individual incidents involve trivial issues, the pattern itself is significant and worth addressing. Dismissing concerns because each instance seems small allows a damaging dynamic to continue unchecked. The cumulative effect of many "trivial" overrulings is not trivial at all.
