# Letting Go When Your Teen Heads for College

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/college-separation

---

## Core Framework: Extension vs. Separation

The traditional metaphor of "separating" from teenagers should be replaced with the concept of **extension**. This reframe comes from Michael Riera and captures a fundamental truth about the adolescent developmental process:

- During adolescence, teenagers need to **extend away** from their parents while simultaneously **staying connected** to them
- The teenager's job is to extend outward into the world
- The parent's job is to maintain connection

This is not separation in the sense of breaking ties. It's more like stretching a rubber band - the teen moves further out, but the connection remains intact. The parent's role shifts from being the center of the child's world to being an anchor point they can extend from and return to.

## Why Teens Pull Back from Communication

When a teenager goes to college and suddenly stops wanting to chat on the phone or share details about their life, this is not rejection. Several psychological mechanisms explain this behavior:

**Emotional self-protection against homesickness**: If a teen started having long, intimate conversations with parents, it would stir up feelings of missing them. Keeping conversations brief and logistical helps the teen avoid triggering homesickness. The teen is protecting themselves emotionally, not punishing the parent.

**Immersion in new experiences**: College represents a whirlwind of new experiences. The teen is genuinely absorbed in their new world. Even when they appear to have free time (reading in their dorm room), their mental and emotional energy is directed toward processing and engaging with their new environment.

**The subway analogy**: One example illustrates this beautifully. A son who had called his mother every single day after getting off the subway for six years - from age 12 through leaving for college - suddenly had no one to call. The mother worried: did he feel alone, abandoned? Her conclusion: he probably felt **liberated**. The absence of the check-in call wasn't loss to him; it was freedom.

## The 2am Phone Call Principle

Despite appearances of pulling away, the connection remains. The example that demonstrates this:

A son who seemed too busy to chat, who deflected personal questions, who never called his parents - one night at 2:30am called to say: "I was just on my way home from hanging with my friends, and I thought I would call to tell you I love you."

Key insights from this example:
- He didn't realize calling at 2am was unusual - the impulse to connect felt natural to him
- The connection was there all along; it just expressed itself on his timeline, not the parent's
- These moments of spontaneous connection are the reward for letting the teen take the lead
- The parent's response matters: being genuinely happy for the call, not scolding about the hour, reinforces that the connection is always welcome

## The Longitudinal Approach: Let Them Take the Lead from the Beginning

The 2am phone call didn't happen by accident. It was the result of a parenting approach practiced consistently from infancy through adolescence. The principle: **let the child take the lead on separations**.

**Infancy**: If the baby seemed comfortable, let someone else hold him. If he reached out for mom, take him back. The child signals readiness; the parent responds.

**Toddlerhood**: When he wanted to "do it myself" and climb to the top of the monkey bars, let him - while spotting in case he fell. Support without restriction.

**Age 5**: Starting sleepovers (because he was ready and wanted to)

**Age 12**: Commuting to school on the New York subway for an hour each way (a significant independence milestone, but led by his readiness)

**Age 16**: Going on a group trip to Spain for a month

**Age 17**: Coming home without a defined curfew in the wee hours of the morning

The pattern: each separation was initiated or welcomed by the child, not imposed by arbitrary rules. The parent followed the child's developmental signals rather than predetermined timelines.

## Why Separation is Harder for Parents Than Children

This is framed as a fundamental asymmetry in the parent-child relationship:

**For the child**: The parent is central - the child's whole world, especially when young. But as the child grows, the parent becomes increasingly peripheral. This is healthy development.

**For the parent**: The child remains central to the parent's life. This doesn't change as the child grows. The child leaving doesn't reduce their centrality to the parent.

This asymmetry creates pain: "As my child, he's central to me, to my life. But as his parent, I'm peripheral to him."

**Important reframes**:
- This is not personal rejection
- It doesn't mean they don't love us
- It's not something to be offended by
- We all know this from our own experience - as much as we may love our parents, they're usually not central to our lives after we leave home

## The Source of Parent-Teen Conflict

Much of the dramatic fallout between teens and parents stems from parents' difficulty with this asymmetry:

- We worry (natural, but can become controlling)
- We can't let them go (resisting the natural developmental process)
- We feel hurt that we're no longer needed (taking healthy development personally)
- **Result**: "We fight with them about stuff that doesn't actually matter"

The fights aren't really about curfews, grades, or chores. They're about the parent's struggle with becoming peripheral. Recognizing this can help parents disengage from unnecessary conflicts.

## The Bow and Arrow Metaphor (Kahlil Gibran)

The Gibran poem "On Children" captures the essence of this transition. Key elements:

**Children are not possessions**: "Your children are not your children. They are the sons and daughters of Life's longing for itself."

**They pass through you, not from you**: "They come through you but not from you, and though they are with you, yet they belong not to you."

**You cannot follow them into the future**: "Their souls dwell in the house of tomorrow, which you cannot visit, not even in your dreams."

**Don't try to make them like you**: "You may strive to be like them, but seek not to make them like you. For life goes not backward nor tarries with yesterday."

**The bow and arrow relationship**:
- Parents are the bows
- Children are the living arrows sent forth
- The archer (life/fate) bends the bow so the arrows fly swift and far
- "Let your bending in the archer's hand be for gladness"

**The critical asymmetry restated**: "To the bow, the arrow is its purpose. To the arrow, the bow is just a launching pad."

This is harsh but true. The parent's entire purpose is bound up in launching the child. The child's purpose is to fly toward their own mark. This isn't ungrateful - it's the design.

## The Time Paradox

A final reflection on the speed of this transition:

"When they're born, it seems like they'll be waking up at night forever. And then, before we know it, we're more and more peripheral to their lives."

The endless nights of infancy feel infinite. The years of growing independence pass instantly. This temporal illusion makes the transition harder because parents don't feel prepared - even when they've done everything right.

## The Reward for Following Their Lead

The closing insight ties everything together:

"Except, if we let them take the lead in navigating separations, and we're lucky. Then we might just get that occasional 2am phone call, just to tell us they love us."

The parent who:
- Lets the child signal readiness for each separation
- Doesn't force connection on the parent's timeline
- Doesn't take the growing distance personally
- Maintains openness without pressure

...creates the conditions where the child feels safe to reach back spontaneously. The connection is preserved precisely because it wasn't clutched.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**Taking their distance personally**: Their developmental need to extend is not about you. Interpreting it as rejection damages the relationship.

**Fighting about things that don't matter**: When you find yourself in conflict over trivial issues, check whether the real source is your own grief about becoming peripheral.

**Forcing connection on your schedule**: Insisting on long phone calls when they're not ready pushes them away. Brief logistical contact is still contact.

**Trying to make them like you**: The Gibran principle - life goes forward, not backward. They will be themselves, not copies of you.

**Not being available when they do reach out**: The 2am call works because the parent answered happily, not with "do you know what time it is?" Availability on their terms reinforces that connection is always welcome.
