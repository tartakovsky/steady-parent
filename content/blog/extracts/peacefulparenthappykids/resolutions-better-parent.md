# 10 Commitments That Will Make You a Better Parent

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids

---

## Core Insight: Self-Management Precedes Child Management

The fundamental premise is that resolving to "be more patient" rarely works as a parenting improvement strategy. The reason is that parenting effectiveness flows from the parent's internal state outward. **We can only give what we have inside.** If parents cannot manage their own emotions, they cannot teach children to manage theirs. This creates a dependency: self-regulation must come first, child guidance second.

The path to becoming a more inspired parent and happier person is completely possible, but it happens **step by step**, not through willpower alone.

---

## The Change Process Framework

### Step 1: Commit Yourself
Make an explicit commitment rather than a vague intention. Commitment creates psychological investment that sustains behavior change through difficult moments.

### Step 2: Envision the Outcome
Create a vivid mental image of what life looks like when you keep this commitment. Specifically notice:
- How much closer you feel to your child
- How much happier your child becomes
- How much more cooperative your child is

This visualization serves a specific purpose: **programming your subconscious**. The subconscious responds to emotional imagery, not intellectual decisions.

### Step 3: Daily Revisitation
Revisit your commitment every day, including the emotional feeling of keeping it. This repetition strengthens the neural pathways associated with the desired behavior.

### Step 4: Handle Setbacks with Compassion
When you mess up (which you will, because you're human), the response protocol is:
1. Offer yourself total compassion (not self-criticism)
2. Apologize to your child
3. Take a positive step in your desired direction

The key reframe: **Two steps forward, one step back still takes you where you want to go.** Progress does not require perfection.

### Step 5: Daily Micro-Improvements
Make a small positive change every day. Find external support and give yourself constant cheerleading. At first, you see small changes. But small changes compound into big changes over time.

## The 10 Commitments

### Commitment 1: Self-Care and Staying Centered

**Why this matters:** When your emotions are dysregulated, you're in fight-or-flight mode, and your child looks like the enemy. You cannot be the happy, patient, encouraging parent your child deserves if you're running on empty.

**Specific practices:**
- Go to bed earlier for better rest
- Eat healthfully to maintain stable mood
- Transform inner negative voices into encouraging ones
- Slow down your pace so you can actually enjoy life

**The critical behavior:** Calm yourself BEFORE engaging with your child. This is described as the most important element because emotional dysregulation makes effective parenting impossible.

---

### Commitment 2: Loving the One You're With

**The research finding:** The one thing we know for certain about child development is that kids who feel loved and cherished thrive. However, there's a crucial distinction: not kids who ARE loved, but kids who FEEL loved and cherished for exactly who they are.

**Why this distinction matters:** Plenty of kids whose parents love them don't thrive. The love must be communicated in a way the child can receive and recognize.

**The challenge:** Every child is unique, so it takes a different approach for each child to feel seen and loved. The hard work is accepting who your child is, "warts and all," and cherishing them for being that person, even while guiding behavior.

**The secret (three elements):**
1. See it from his perspective
2. Use a positive lens
3. Celebrate every step in the right direction

---

### Commitment 3: Staying Connected

**Why reconnection is necessary:** Separation happens naturally throughout daily life. Connection is not a one-time achievement but a cycle of separation and reconnection that must be actively maintained.

**Key principle:** Quality time is about connection, not teaching. This means it's mostly unstructured.

**Specific practices:**
- Hug your child first thing every morning
- Hug when you say goodbye
- When reunited later in the day, spend 15 minutes solely focused on your child

**What to do in those 15 minutes:** Listen, commiserate, hug, roughhouse, laugh, listen some more. The emphasis on listening appears twice, suggesting it's the primary mode.

**Evening practices:**
- Stop working before dinner time to devote evening to family
- Eat dinner together
- Have a chat and silent snuggle at bedtime every night with each child

---

### Commitment 4: Role Modeling Respect

**The goal:** Raise kids who are considerate and respectful, right through the teen years.

**The method:** Take a deep breath and speak to them respectfully. The deep breath serves as a physiological reset before engaging.

**The difficulty:** This is not always easy when you're angry.

**Three cardinal rules for managing emotions with kids:**
1. You're the role model (your behavior teaches more than your words)
2. Don't take it personally (their behavior isn't about you)
3. This too shall pass (perspective helps regulate your response)

---

### Commitment 5: Teaching Emotional Intelligence

Parents help kids learn to manage emotions through three mechanisms, in addition to modeling emotional self-management:

#### Mechanism 1: Teaching Self-Soothing

**Common misconception debunked:** Contrary to what you may have heard, little ones do NOT learn to self-soothe by being left to cry. That approach creates an overactive amygdala and panic response later in life.

**How self-soothing actually develops:** Soothing is a physiological process. When a baby cries and we soothe him, his body responds by sending out oxytocin and other soothing biochemicals. What you observe is that he calms down. What's happening biologically is that he's solidifying the neural pathways for these self-soothing hormones. This is how he develops the ability to soothe himself when upset later.

The key insight: **External soothing builds internal soothing capacity.** The child learns to self-soothe BY BEING soothed, not by being left alone.

#### Mechanism 2: Validating Feelings While Limiting Actions

Give children the message that their full range of feelings is understandable, even while their actions must be limited.

**Example script:** "You wish you could have a cookie."

This separates the feeling (which is valid) from the action (which may need limits).

#### Mechanism 3: Empathizing and Listening

Empathize with their emotions and listen when they have feelings to express.

**Verbal expression:** Occasionally feelings come out as words. It helps to give kids words for their feelings: "You're so mad!"

**Non-verbal expression:** More often, children just need the safety of a loving presence while they cry or rage to vent their feelings. They often won't be able to articulate what they're upset about, and it isn't necessary.

**Why this matters:** This process helps kids learn to accept and process their emotions, so they can move past them rather than having to act on them.

**Definition of "acting out":** We act on our feelings rather than simply tolerating them as they sweep through us and dissipate. The goal is to help children develop the capacity to experience feelings without needing to convert them into actions.

---

### Commitment 6: Looking for Needs Behind Behavior

**Core principle:** Your kid has a reason for whatever he's doing that displeases you. It might not be what you consider a good reason, but it's what's motivating his behavior.

**Why punishment fails:** If yelling at him about his behavior were going to change it, that would have worked already. Punishment addresses symptoms, not causes.

**What actually works:** Only by addressing the underlying need do we change a person's behavior.

**Pre-emptive approach:** Parents who address kids' needs pre-emptively by noticing problem areas are rewarded with kids who cooperate.

**Example:** "Hmm... looks like she wants to choose her own clothes, even if they don't match!" By naming and accommodating the underlying need (autonomy over clothing), you prevent the power struggle.

---

### Commitment 7: Guidance Rather Than Punishment

**Why punishment backfires:** Kids only behave to please us. When we constantly criticize and discipline, they harden their hearts to us. This undermines the very relationship that makes cooperation possible.

**Four elements of effective guidance:**

1. **Lead by loving example** - modeling rather than commanding

2. **Address needs rather than focusing on misbehavior** - see Commitment 6

3. **Redirect pre-emptively rather than punish**
   - Example: "You can throw the ball outside" (gives an acceptable alternative rather than just saying no)

4. **Set limits empathically**
   - Example: "You're mad and sad, but we don't hit. Let's use your words to tell your brother how you feel."
   - This acknowledges the feeling, states the limit, and offers an alternative action

**The outcome:** Parents who use this approach end up with self-disciplined kids who WANT to behave. The key word is "want" - intrinsic motivation rather than fear-based compliance.

---

### Commitment 8: Remembering What's Important and Gratitude

**Stay positive and choose your battles.** Not everything is worth addressing.

**The cost of negativity:** Every negative interaction with your child uses up valuable relationship capital. There's a finite amount of goodwill in any relationship, and criticism depletes it.

**Focus criteria:** Focus on what matters, such as the way your child treats her siblings (relationship patterns that will persist).

**Example of what NOT to battle over:** Her jacket on the floor may drive you crazy, but it probably isn't worth putting your relationship bank account in the red over. This uses the metaphor of a relationship as a bank account that can go into overdraft.

**The gratitude principle:** Be grateful for every single thing she does that you like, and you'll find her doing lots more of those things. Attention functions as reinforcement - what you notice and appreciate grows.

---

### Commitment 9: Radical Self-Acceptance and Compassion

**The path to feeling more love:** Give it to yourself first.

**Love as a verb:** Love can just happen, but we only make more (and feel more) by giving it away.

**The limiting factor:** We can only give our children as much love as our own hearts can hold. Self-love expands capacity; self-criticism shrinks it.

**The practice:** Every time you feel bad, for any reason, offer yourself love. This is described as "stretching your heart."

**The promise:** You'll be amazed how your life transforms when you practice consistent self-compassion.

---

### Commitment 10: Keep Perspective

**Accept imperfection:** Your kids will make mistakes, and so will you. There are no perfect parents, no perfect children, and no perfect families.

**But there are thriving families:** There are families who live in the embrace of great love, where everyone thrives.

**How thriving families are created:** By making daily choices that take you in that direction. It's not magic, just the hard work of course correction to stay on the right path.

**The navigation metaphor:** You can always find trail marks and support to beckon you onward. Just keep taking positive steps. Before you know it, you'll find yourself in a whole new landscape.

The emphasis is on direction and persistence rather than perfection - consistent small movements in the right direction eventually produce transformation.

## Key Themes Across All Commitments

### The Primacy of Connection
Multiple commitments emphasize that children cooperate because of their relationship with parents. Punishment and criticism damage this relationship, which then undermines the very foundation of influence.

### Feelings vs. Actions Distinction
A recurring pattern: validate and accept all feelings while setting limits on actions. This appears in emotional intelligence teaching, limit-setting, and guidance approaches.

### Proactive vs. Reactive Parenting
Pre-emptive approaches (noticing needs, redirecting before problems escalate) are consistently presented as more effective than reactive approaches (punishment after misbehavior).

### Self-Compassion as Foundation
The framework begins and ends with self-compassion. It's positioned as both the starting point (Commitment 1) and a dedicated practice (Commitment 9), suggesting it's considered the most important and most difficult element.

### Progress Over Perfection
The "two steps forward, one step back" framing and the emphasis on daily small changes both reinforce that sustainable improvement matters more than immediate transformation.
