# Helping Children with Back-to-School Anxiety

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

## Core Insight: Dual Emotional Reality

Many children experience both excitement and nervousness about returning to school simultaneously. This is especially true when circumstances include additional stressors like being placed in a class without their friends. Parents often share these first-day jitters, worrying about their child's well-being and what to do if the child refuses to go to school.

The approach outlined here serves two purposes: settling the parent's own worries while helping the child start school with genuine confidence.

---

## The Emotional Processing Framework

### Step 1: Elicit Multiple Feelings Through Direct Inquiry

**Ask your child to name three feelings they have about returning to school, then explore each one separately.**

The specific number (three) matters because it invites complexity rather than a single-word answer. Children rarely have just one emotion about significant transitions.

Example prompt: "Tell me three feelings you have about going back to school."

If a child responds with "Excited, scared, worried," acknowledge all three by reflecting them back: "I hear you. Excited, and scared, and worried."

Then explore each feeling individually: "Tell me about excited." This sequencing prevents feelings from blending together and ensures each emotion gets attention.

### Step 2: Acknowledge Rather Than Dismiss

**The goal is NOT to talk the child out of their fear or worry.**

Why this matters: Attempting to reassure too quickly or minimize the feeling makes the child feel alone with their emotion. They learn that this feeling is something to hide or suppress rather than process.

Instead, offer understanding and invite deeper exploration:
- "You're scared about that, huh? I hear you."
- "Tell me more."
- "What's the worst thing about that?"

The question "What's the worst thing about that?" is particularly powerful because it helps the child articulate the core fear beneath the surface worry.

**Common fears children express about school:**
- Social fears: "I'm scared that I'll feel left out because my friends aren't in my class and the other kids all know each other."
- Academic fears: "I'm scared that I've forgotten how to do the math so I won't understand it."
- Separation fears: "I'm scared that I will miss you a lot because I really liked being home with you this summer."

Each fear type requires different reassurance and problem-solving approaches.

### Step 3: Listen with Calm, Warm Presence

**What children need most when experiencing big emotions is a parent's calm, warm presence.**

The mechanism: This presence helps children feel safe enough to explore the feeling. Exploration is how feelings get processed and begin to recede. Without safety, the child keeps the feeling contained rather than working through it.

**Practical listening behaviors:**
- Pay full attention (not distracted)
- Nod to show engagement
- Restate what your child says so they feel heard

The restating technique serves as proof of listening. Children need evidence that their words landed before they feel safe to go deeper.

### Step 4: Offer Targeted Reassurance

Only after listening thoroughly should you reassure. The reassurance must be specific to what the child actually expressed, not generic comfort.

**Examples of targeted reassurance:**
- For missing a friend: "I know you will miss Sara. We can make sure that you get to see her after school to play. Maybe even the first day!"
- For social anxiety about new classmates: "Last year when you started in your class you didn't know anyone, and you made friends within a couple of weeks."

The second example uses the child's own past success as evidence. This is more persuasive than promises about the future because it references something they already know to be true about themselves.

### Step 5: Empower Through Confidence and Problem-Solving

**Shift from reassurance to empowerment by expressing confidence in the child's ability to cope.**

Key phrase structure: "I think you could handle it, if we think about it in advance and you feel prepared. You're pretty resourceful!"

This framing accomplishes several things:
- Normalizes the fear ("lots of kids feel that way")
- Reframes the fear as manageable ("it wouldn't be the end of the world")
- Expresses belief in the child's capability
- Makes preparation a collaborative effort ("we think about it")

Then invite the child to generate solutions: "What could you do to help yourself?"

### Step 6: Collaborative Problem-Solving

**Help the child take concrete action before the anxiety-provoking event.**

Example: "I wonder what you could do to connect with one of the kids you like, before you are back at school next week? Maybe we can have a playdate with your friend this weekend."

The timing matters - taking action before school starts gives the child a sense of agency and reduces the feeling that school is something happening to them.

## The Role of Laughter in Anxiety Reduction

**Laughter decreases stress hormones circulating in the body, directly reducing feelings of anxiety.**

This is a physiological mechanism, not just a distraction technique. The stress hormone reduction creates a calmer baseline from which the child can face the challenging situation.

### Effective Laughter-Inducing Games

**Bucking Bronco:** Child rides on parent's back while parent lurches around the room trying to (gently) toss them off. The shrieking laughter comes from the combination of physical sensation and safe danger.

**Airplane:** Parent zooms child wildly around the house. The movement and unpredictability generate giggles.

**Pushing Game:** Parent and child put palms against each other, child pushes parent across the room. Parent provides just enough resistance to make it effortful and fun, but always lets the child "win."

### Critical Warning: Tickling Does Not Work

**Tickling does not appear to release stress hormones and may actually increase fearfulness.**

Why: Tickling puts the child in a powerless position where they cannot control the sensation. This is the opposite of empowering. The laughter produced by tickling is a reflex response, not genuine joy.

**Key principle:** Go for any kind of play that gets your child giggling with as much warmth as possible - except tickling.

## Separation-Specific Games

If the child's anxiety specifically involves separating from the parent (common after enjoying summer together), specialized games address this directly.

### The "Please Don't Leave Me" Game

**Setup:** After reading or snuggling, when the child starts to get off your lap, pull them back.

**Script:** Tell them how much you love holding them. Say "please don't go away from me ever" and "I want to hold you always."

**Critical technique:** Keep your voice light and playful rather than needy. The child must feel free to pull away. Keep scooping them back with mock begging.

**How it heals:** In this game, the child gets to push the parent away and reassure the parent that it's okay to leave. This role reversal addresses the child's internal worry about letting go of the parent. By being the one who reassures, the child processes their own need for reassurance.

### The "Bye Bye" Game

**A simple hide and seek variant that triggers just enough separation anxiety to get the child giggling.**

**Setup:** Say "Let's play Bye-bye. If you want me, yell 'Peanut Butter'" (or any phrase the child finds funny).

**Gameplay:**
1. Hide behind the couch or door for just a moment
2. Before the child yells, YOU yell "Peanut Butter" and run out
3. Hug them and say "I missed you too much to leave!"
4. Say "Ok, I will be brave! Let me try that again."
5. Hide again, come back out before they yell for you
6. Play act being silly and excessively worried
7. Continue as long as the child is giggling

**The mechanism:** The child faces separation anxiety in a safe, controlled context. The parent expressing the fear (rather than the child) allows the child to be the brave one who reassures. This builds their internal sense of being able to handle separation.

**Key instruction:** Keep you being the one who yells first and not really leaving. The child should feel they can get you back whenever they want.

### General Principle for Laughter Games

Notice what makes your child laugh and do more of it, no matter how silly it seems. The content doesn't have to be explicitly about separation - all giggling defuses anxiety. The more giggling, the better.

## Reframing the Challenge

**Core mindset shift:** "My child can do hard things, with enough support."

The return to school IS a challenge for most children. This is normal, not a sign of a problem.

**The growth mechanism:** When children face problems with adequate support, they develop internal resources to manage those problems. This is how resilience develops, along with new skills and capabilities.

The implication: Protecting children from all difficult feelings prevents this growth. The goal is not to eliminate anxiety but to help children face it successfully.

**Reminder for parents:** And so can you. Parents also need to face their own anxiety about their child's struggles. The same principle applies - facing the challenge with support builds capability.

---

## Summary of the Complete Approach

1. **Elicit feelings** - Ask for three, explore each
2. **Acknowledge** - Don't dismiss or minimize
3. **Listen** - Calm presence, reflection, full attention
4. **Reassure** - Targeted to specific fears expressed
5. **Empower** - Express confidence, invite self-generated solutions
6. **Problem-solve** - Concrete actions before the event
7. **Play** - Laughter reduces stress hormones; use games that empower (not tickling)
8. **Address separation specifically** - Role-reversal games where child reassures parent

The sequence matters: emotional processing comes before reassurance, which comes before problem-solving, which comes before play. Each step creates the conditions for the next to be effective.
