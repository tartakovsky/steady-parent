# Preparing Your Child for Kindergarten

## The Case Against Academic Kindergarten Prep

**Research findings on play-based vs. academic preschools**: Children in play-based preschools perform better both socially and academically in kindergarten and later grades compared to children in preschools that focus on academics. This research implies that children who start kindergarten without knowing "readiness" material will likely learn it quickly once they're there.

The concept of kindergarten-readiness is based on problematic assumptions. The real markers of kindergarten readiness are:
- The child is ready to listen to the teacher
- The child is ready to work things out with other children

These social and emotional skills matter more than academic content.

## Why Parents Feel Pressure Anyway

Despite the research, the US kindergarten curriculum assumes children entering kindergarten will already know certain things. This creates anxiety for parents who want their children to be prepared. The good news: everything children are expected to know can be learned easily through daily life activities.

## How to Teach Pre-Kindergarten Skills Effectively

**The right approach**: Integrate learning into everyday interactions naturally. Example: "Do you want to count out how many forks we need for dinner?" This makes learning contextual and meaningful.

### Critical Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**Never quiz or test your child**: If you test a child, you will eventually reach the limit of their knowledge. When this happens, the child feels inadequate or "dumb." This damages their relationship with learning.

**Avoid flash cards**: Flash cards represent decontextualized learning that removes the meaning and fun from education.

### Why Context-Based Learning Works Better

Children always learn faster when they see things in action and understand the context. Example: "The letter T goes with that truck!" connects abstract symbols to concrete, meaningful objects the child already cares about.

**Making learning fun creates learning readiness**: The goal isn't just to teach content but to help your child be ready and eager to learn when they get to kindergarten. A child who associates learning with fun and connection will engage more readily with school.

## Specific Skills for Kindergarten Entry

(List from Stacey Kannenberg, author of "Homework for Parents")

### Colors
Basic colors to know: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple

### Shapes
Basic shapes to know: circle, diamond, rectangle, triangle, oval, square

### Money
Basic coins to recognize: penny, nickel, dime

### Numbers
- Ability to count objects to 10
- Know the numbers 1-10
- Recognize the first ten numbers out of sequence (example: 2, 5, 9, 8, 1, 3, 4)

### Letters
- Recognize the uppercase alphabet
- Be able to name letters out of sequence (example: B, D, X, K, J, M, O)

## Ongoing Homework for Parents

These habits support children's school success:

1. **Stop, Drop and Listen daily when reunited with your child**: This means giving full attention at pickup/reunion time rather than being distracted.

2. **Unload the backpack/homework folder with your kids daily**: This shows involvement and helps you stay aware of what's happening at school.

3. **Read with your kids every night for 15 minutes**: Consistent daily reading builds literacy foundations and connection.

4. **Play repetitive games with your kids**: Repetition helps children master concepts and skills.

5. **Ask open-ended questions to help your kids communicate**: Open-ended questions (not yes/no questions) develop language skills and thinking.

## The Parent's Role: Partner, Not Teacher

**Be your child's partner in learning, not their teacher.** This distinction matters because when parents care about what happens to their child in school, the child engages with school more and enjoys it more. The relationship dynamic of partnership (rather than instruction) preserves the child's intrinsic motivation and sense of ownership over their learning.
