# Managing Diaper Change Resistance in Babies and Toddlers

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

---

## The Core Problem: Why Babies and Toddlers Resist Diaper Changes

**Developmental autonomy** is the root cause of diaper change resistance in older babies and toddlers. By around 11 months, children want to be in charge of their own bodies and time. They do not want an adult to interrupt their activities, pick them up, and undress them when they are engaged with something else.

The fundamental mismatch: Unless a child is one of the rare ones who feels uncomfortable in wet diapers, they have zero incentive to want a diaper change. From the child's perspective, they are being forcibly removed from interesting activities to undergo something they did not ask for.

**The resistance is developmentally normal** - most little ones go through stages when they resist diaper changes. This is not a behavioral problem but a sign of healthy development and growing autonomy.

---

## The Foundation: Connection and Respect

### Slowing Down Changes Everything

The quality of the interaction during diaper changes directly affects cooperation. When you rush through a diaper change like it is something unpleasant, the child experiences being held down and subjected to something unpleasant - which is exactly what a rushed diaper change feels like.

**The physical experience matters**: Imagine having the intimate parts of your body touched in a brusque, rushed way. Children experience this same discomfort when diaper changes are hurried.

**The alternative**: Treating the diaper change as a chance to connect and enjoy your child makes them more likely to enjoy the connection and therefore cooperate.

### Mindful Presence During Diaper Changes

Mindfulness researcher **Cassandra Vieten** frames diaper changes as the "**Mindful Diaper Change Practice**" - a reframing that turns a mundane task into a mindfulness opportunity for parents who feel they no longer have time for such practices.

**Why presence matters**: Your ability to stay present and aware during a diaper change models for your child how they can stay grounded in the face of their own discomfort. The teaching here is not just about cooperation - it is about emotional regulation.

The practice involves bringing compassionate, open-hearted full presence to the diaper change rather than rushing through it.

### Connection Before Request

**The principle**: Children are always more likely to cooperate with us if we connect first.

**The sequence**:
1. Take a deep breath
2. Get on your child's level
3. Connect by commenting on what they are doing
4. Point out that their diaper is wet
5. Ask if they have noticed it

**Why ask if they noticed**: This gives the child an opportunity to check in with their body. This body awareness is a foundation block for eventual potty learning. It shifts the child from passive recipient to active participant.

**The relational effect**: When you connect first, the child feels like you are on their side. You are not just pushing them around, which would naturally make them feel resistant.

### Respect From Birth (RIE Approach)

**Magda Gerber**, founder of **RIE (Resources for Infant Educarers)**, taught that even though babies cannot understand words, they feel the difference when they are treated with respect.

**The practice**: From infancy, instead of just scooping babies up, move slowly and explain what is happening.

**Why this works linguistically**: Receptive language is about a year ahead of expressive language. Your child already understands much more than you think. Even tiny babies understand tone of voice.

**The long-term benefit**: If you do this from birth, children have better associations with diaper changes and do not build up such resistance over time. Prevention is easier than intervention.

## Control and Choice: Avoiding Power Struggles

### Always Offer a Choice

**Standard practice**: Always ask "Ready for a diaper change?"

**When they say no**: Offer a time-bound choice.
- "Your diaper is wet. Do you want to change it now or in three minutes?"
- If they choose three minutes: "3 minutes? Ok, let's shake on it!"

**Why this works**: The child has agency over timing even if not over the activity itself. The handshake creates a ritual agreement that the child has participated in creating.

### Depersonalizing With External Cues

When it feels like a power struggle, remove yourself as the authority figure by using an external timer.

**The script**:
- "When the alarm rings, it is three minutes and time for your diaper change, ok?"
- When the alarm rings: "Oh, listen, there's the alarm, it's been three minutes - Time for that diaper change!"

**Why this works**: The alarm becomes the authority, not you. The parent becomes an ally helping the child respond to an external requirement rather than an opponent imposing their will.

After the alarm, use one of the other transition strategies to help them move to the changing area.

## Minimizing Disruption to Play

### Understanding Play as Work

**Core principle**: Play is your baby's work. Naturally, they do not want to be interrupted.

This reframes the child's resistance as reasonable rather than defiant. They are not being difficult - they are trying to complete important developmental work.

### Bring the Change to the Child

Use a portable changing pad and change them where they are playing. This minimizes interruption to whatever they are working on.

**Why location matters**: Much of the resistance comes from being removed from an activity. If the activity can continue nearby, the diaper change becomes a minor interruption rather than a complete disruption.

### Standing Diaper Changes for Wet Diapers

**The technique**: Change wet (not messy) diapers with the child standing up.

**How to do it**: Pick a toy they like and put it on the couch, then stand them against the couch for stability.

**Why this matters**: Minimizing the times you ask them to lie down means they are more likely to cooperate when it is absolutely necessary for messy changes. You are saving the "lying down" request for when you truly need it.

**Dr. Laura's personal note**: She did this with her daughter beginning at 11 months until she was out of diapers. It is harder than lying down at first, but with practice you get good at it.

### Transition Objects

Help the child transition by taking an object they are involved with and carrying it with you.

**Example script**: "Let's drive the truck to the changing table!"

**Why this works**: The child's engagement continues rather than abruptly ending. The beloved object creates a bridge between play and the diaper change.

### Let Them Walk

Many kids object to being carried off to be changed. If you make the transition into a party and they are dancing alongside you into their room, they are actively taking part in the plan rather than feeling pushed around.

**The difference**: Carried = passive, done to them. Walking = active participant, part of the plan.

## Making Diaper Changes Fun and Engaging

### Pre-Change Laughter Session

**The science**: Laughter reduces stress hormones and increases bonding hormones.

**The strategy**: Getting your child laughing for ten minutes before you need their cooperation is always good preparation.

**How to do it**: Before starting the diaper change, roughhouse in a way that makes your child squeal with laughter. Chase them around the house, be completely silly. After ten minutes, make the diaper change part of the fun.

**Why ten minutes**: This is enough time to shift their physiological state through the release of bonding hormones.

### The Party Approach

Most kids cannot resist a party. This reframes the entire diaper change as celebration rather than interruption.

**Elements**:
- Grab a drum
- Have a conga line
- Sing and dance your way to the bedroom

**Example songs**:
- "Gonna change that diaper right off of your tush!"
- "Happiness is a clean diaper"
- Or whatever song gets them moving

### Live Entertainment During the Change

If the child is fussing, try singing to them very softly. They will usually stop fussing to listen to you.

**The full entertainment repertoire**:
- Sing
- Dance
- Kiss their belly
- Blow down their neck
- Make silly faces
- Make silly noises

**The stealth approach**: Somewhere in all the entertainment, get the diaper changed as unobtrusively as possible. The entertainment is the foreground; the diaper change is the background.

### Doll or Teddy Practice

**The sequence**:
1. First diaper the child's doll or teddy bear
2. Let the child help with the toy's diaper change
3. Shower admiration on Teddy during his diaper change
4. Then say: "Your turn! Are you ready like Teddy?"

**Why this works**: The child sees the activity modeled, participates in a low-stakes version, and then receives an invitation to be "like Teddy" - leveraging their natural desire for imitation and their relationship with the toy.

## Special Motivators and Rewards

### The Exclusive Toy Basket

When you absolutely have to ask them to lie down (for messy diapers), have a basket of toys ready that they only have access to during diaper changes.

**Making it extra special**: Wrap very small items in newspaper and put them in the basket. Every diaper change, they choose one wrapped present.

**Ideas for basket items** (things you have around the house or would have bought anyway):
- Plastic measuring spoons or funnel
- Small board books
- Little figures
- A block with a letter on it
- A roll of masking tape
- A broken cell phone
- A plastic cup
- Chapstick
- Colorful trinkets from Ikea
- Clay or playdoh with a plastic garlic press (for making "noodles")
- A puppet
- A tiny flashlight
- Little wind-up toys
- Stickers
- An unbreakable mirror

**Recycling tip**: You can re-wrap things the child has left lying around and forgotten about. Novelty matters more than newness.

### Sticker Decoration Wall

Keep a stash of stickers by the changing table. Every diaper change, let them choose one sticker they are allowed to put on the wall next to the table.

**Why this works**: The child has something to look forward to, a choice to make, and a visible accumulation of their participation over time.

### Audio Stories

Keep an audio that has an interesting story ready to turn on during changes. They might come to look forward to the next installment.

**Why this works**: Creates anticipation for the next diaper change rather than dread. The story becomes associated with the changing experience.

## Involving the Child as Helper

### Team Approach

Team up with your child to get the job done. This shifts the dynamic from something being done to them to something being done with them.

**Possible involvement**:
- Maybe they would like to take off their own diaper (kids love mastering new skills)
- Holding the wipes while you wipe them
- Putting their feet flat and lifting up their bottom so you can slide the diaper under

**The narration technique**: Tell them what you are doing at each step.

**Example script**:
- "I'm going to wipe you off now - do you want to hold the wipes?"
- "Ok, I'm going to lift your bottom now to put the diaper under you."

**When they decline to help**: Narrate what you will do instead. "Ok, I'm going to lift your bottom now" - they still have the information, they just did not choose to participate this time.

## Empathy During Discomfort

### Naming Physical Sensations

Example: "Does that feel cold on your bottom?"

**The approach when they get upset**: Try not to get reactive. Instead, soften and stay compassionate.

**What this communicates to the child**:
- It is not actually an emergency
- You understand their experience
- You are looking out for their best interests

**The regulatory effect**: Your calm presence helps them regulate their own distress.

## Critical Warning: The Consent Foundation

**The strongest statement in the article**: No little person should regularly be held down while their clothing is pulled off.

**Why this matters developmentally**: This is not a good foundation for learning consent as they get older.

**The futility of force**: Power struggles about someone else's body are not power struggles you can win. Even if you "win" in the moment by forcing the change, you lose in the relationship and in the child's developing understanding of bodily autonomy.

**The goal**: Diaper changing should not become a battle. The emphasis is on finding approaches that gain cooperation rather than forcing compliance.

---

## Implementation Approach

### Mix and Match Required

No one approach will always work. You have to mix and match and be willing to try different things.

**The suggestion**: Print this list and add to it as you come up with more solutions that work for you and your child. This positions the parent as an ongoing experimenter rather than someone seeking a single solution.

### The Long View

Keep your sense of humor. Remember that this too shall pass.

**Perspective shift**: It will seem like the blink of an eye before you find yourself trying to get your five year old to take their bath. The current struggle is temporary.
