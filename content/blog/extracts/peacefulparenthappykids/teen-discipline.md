# Setting Limits with Preteens and Teens

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/teen-discipline

---

## Core Philosophy: Connection as the Only Real Influence

The central premise is that **connection is the only power parents have to influence their teens**. Physical force stops working as children grow bigger. Intimidation eventually fails. The only sustainable leverage parents retain is the relationship itself.

When parents resort to nagging, yelling, or giving the cold shoulder ("not engaging"), they drive their teen further away. This creates a self-defeating cycle: as the barrier between parent and child grows, future cooperation becomes less likely, and the teen becomes more likely to manage the parent through lying. The teen begins to feel "my parents never understand."

Parents face a choice: develop a chip on their shoulder about how unreasonable their young person is, or recognize that connection is their only tool and work intensely to maintain it, even when frustrated. This requires significant emotional work from the parent, but produces far better outcomes.

## The Three Critical Parent Choices

### Choice 1: Connection vs. Disconnection

When frustration builds, parents can either pull away or lean in. Pulling away (not engaging, cold shoulder) feels justified but destroys the very influence the parent hopes to wield. The teen learns that conflict leads to abandonment rather than resolution.

### Choice 2: Questioning Assumptions vs. Assuming You're Right

Parents must ask whether their beliefs are facts or simply assumptions they've never examined. Can a parent allow their teen to disagree without interpreting that disagreement as disrespect?

**Key insight**: If every assertion of the teen's self is framed as disrespect for the parent, the teen will need to become more defiant for their own mental health. The parent's unwillingness to examine their own assumptions directly increases drama and conflict.

### Choice 3: Taking It Personally vs. Seeing Their Perspective

The common parental response "this is a lack of respect for me" is often a misreading of the situation. Parents can choose to see things from the teen's perspective instead.

## Understanding the Teen's Perspective (Using Hygiene as Example)

The article uses a specific scenario - a 13-year-old boy who resists showering - to illustrate how teen behavior that looks like laziness or disrespect often has different causes when viewed from the teen's perspective:

**Developmental factors:**
- Many boys in early adolescence genuinely see bathing as a waste of time
- Interest in daily showers often doesn't develop until they experience their first "crush" and realize grooming affects attractiveness
- The prefrontal cortex remains under construction until humans are in their mid-twenties, which means it's entirely possible for a young person to "forget" commitments, especially ones they never saw as necessary

**Autonomy and control factors:**
- Young people (like all people) want to be in charge of themselves
- They automatically rebel against control they consider unreasonable
- Our bodies are our most basic "selves," so body sovereignty challenges often come before teens assert control in other areas
- The teen's insistence on choosing what to do with their time and body has nothing to do with laziness or unwillingness to contribute

**The coercion problem:**
- When parents "express frustration" until the child commits to something, the child typically sees this as a promise made under duress - not a real commitment, just a way to get the parent to leave them alone
- When parents express frustration in a self-righteous way, they model that grown-ups work out difficulties with raised voices rather than calm appreciation for different opinions

## The Seven-Step Framework for Setting Limits with Teens

### Step 1: Connect

This step presupposes an existing relationship where connection is easy and warm. If this foundation doesn't exist, the subsequent steps won't work as intended. Connection must come first and be maintained throughout.

### Step 2: Set an Expectation (Not an Order)

With young children, setting limits is often direct: "No hitting!" With older children and teens, this works better as an observation and question that cedes at least some control.

**Example transformation:**
- Instead of: "Go take a shower."
- Try: "I notice you haven't showered in a few days and as you know, I think it's important to shower regularly. What's your plan to shower this weekend?"

The question format acknowledges the teen's autonomy while still establishing the parent's expectation. It invites the teen into problem-solving rather than positioning them as someone being ordered around.

### Step 3: Listen, Reflect, and Empathize

**Example response:** "It sounds like you really see showering as a waste of time. I hear you. You're a busy guy!"

**Critical warning about this step:** This is where most parents feel entitled to express their frustration because they assume they're right and the child is being unreasonable. But from the child's perspective, the parent is unreasonably asserting control over their body about something unimportant. **Any time frustration or force is used to get compliance, it provokes resistance.**

The parent must genuinely listen and reflect back understanding, even if they don't agree with the teen's position.

### Step 4: Stick to Your Expectation and Ask for Cooperation

This step comes with an important caveat: the parent should only stick to their expectation if they still think it's important after hearing the teen's perspective. If the teen provides a reasonable explanation (e.g., "a shower will wash the cool dye out of my hair, and I want to keep it one more day so everyone sees it Monday"), the parent may legitimately shift their expectation.

When the parent still wants compliance, they should:
1. Acknowledge the teen's perspective again
2. Explain why it matters to them (using reasoning, not authority)
3. Ask when would work for the teen

**Example script:** "I hear you. You hate showering and don't think it's important. That's because we humans don't really notice our own smell! But even though this isn't important to you, it's important to me, because it's good hygiene to shower regularly, just out of basic respect for others, so they don't have to smell you. When you're a grown-up, maybe you'll decide NEVER to shower, and you'll make it into the Guinness Book of World Records. But I would still like you to shower this weekend. When would be a good time for you to shower?"

**Key elements in this script:**
- Validates the teen's position ("you hate showering and don't think it's important")
- Provides reasoning without being preachy ("we don't notice our own smell")
- Frames it as "important to me" rather than an absolute rule
- Uses humor to lighten the mood (Guinness Book reference)
- Gives the teen control over timing ("when would be a good time")

### Step 5: Be Specific About the Agreement and Shake on It

Vague agreements invite "forgetting." Specific agreements with physical confirmation create accountability.

**Example:** "So you're committing to shower before bed, which means around 8:30pm, right? Thank you. I appreciate your willingness to do this for me, even though you don't yet share my feeling that it's important. Let's shake on it."

**The handshake ritual:** Starting from when children are very young, shaking on agreements and saying "We always keep our promises" builds a culture of commitment.

**Important caveat:** Children will remember every promise the parent breaks, even for good reason, and the parent will have to make repairs. However, this consistent approach means children will take promises seriously.

### Step 6: Remind Respectfully and with Connection

Even with a good agreement, teens may need reminders. These reminders should happen through connection, not nagging.

**Example (at dinner):** "What are your plans for the evening? I'm delighted to hear that your homework is done, and I know you plan to shower around 8:30pm. Do you have time to play a quick game with me before that?"

This weaves the reminder into natural conversation and connection time rather than issuing it as a separate command.

### Step 7: Remind with Humor

When the deadline passes and the teen hasn't followed through, humor can enforce the limit without creating conflict.

**Example:** When 8:30pm comes and goes and the teen is still on the couch, plop down on top of them. "I wonder why this couch is so lumpy? It can't be Alex. It's past 8:30, so I know he's in the shower. Hmm... what's going on here?... Oh, my goodness, that lump is you, Alex! I'm shocked, shocked to find you still on the couch at shower time!"

**Why this works:** The playfulness maintains connection while still enforcing the expectation. Both parent and teen end up laughing, and the teen heads to the shower without feeling controlled or shamed.

## What the Teen Learns Through This Approach

When parents use this framework consistently, teens internalize important lessons:

1. **Dad listens to me** - Feeling heard builds trust and willingness to engage
2. **We don't always agree, but he understands** - Disagreement doesn't mean disconnection
3. **Sometimes when I don't agree, I still do what he wants, because I like my dad and respect him** - Compliance comes from relationship, not fear
4. **When you get frustrated with someone, you can still see both sides. You can manage your frustration to work things out with each other** - Emotional regulation and conflict resolution are modeled
5. **I feel close to my dad. I think I can tell my dad anything** - The relationship supports future communication, especially about harder topics
6. **Showering isn't so bad** - The actual behavior gets accomplished

## Why This Requires Parent Self-Work

The article acknowledges this approach is hard because parents must work on themselves. The difficulty isn't in the technique but in the emotional regulation required to execute it. Parents must:

- Manage their own frustration rather than expressing it
- Question their assumptions rather than defending them
- Maintain warmth even when feeling disrespected
- Use humor when they want to use force
- Accept partial wins rather than demanding full compliance

However, this approach makes children better people, makes parents better people, and creates a home with less drama and more love.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**Expressing frustration to get compliance:** Even if this produces short-term agreement, the child sees it as a promise made under duress - not a real commitment. This makes "forgetting" more likely and teaches that raised voices are how adults handle disagreement.

**Not engaging / cold shoulder:** This feels like a justified response to disrespect but actually destroys parental influence and drives the teen away.

**Assuming every disagreement is disrespect:** This framing forces the teen into increased defiance as a mental health necessity.

**Demanding compliance based on authority alone:** Without reasoning and connection, this approach backfires as teens get older and physical or intimidation-based enforcement becomes impossible.

**Vague agreements:** Without specifics (exact time, clear expectations), "forgetting" becomes easy and probably genuine.

**Nagging reminders:** Reminders that aren't embedded in connection become white noise that teens tune out.
