# Understanding and Healing a Disrupted Parent-Child Bond

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

---

## The Crossroads Concept

When a parent feels worn out and in constant power struggles with a toddler, they stand at a crossroads. The relationship can go one of two directions: toward estrangement that becomes worse by the teen years, or toward genuine closeness and cooperation. The critical insight is that **the parent controls which outcome occurs** through their own behavioral changes.

This is not about fixing the child. It is about the parent changing their approach, which then changes the child's behavior in response. "You can't control your daughter's behavior. You can only control your own. But when we change our behavior as parents, our kids always change."

---

## Why Toddlers Are Challenging by Nature

Most two-year-olds are inherently challenging. The toddler developmental stage involves testing limits, asserting independence, and struggling with impulse control. Dr. Markham notes that "that's sort of the definition of a two year old" - they push boundaries, resist requests, and act stubborn.

What often separates a "difficult" toddler from a manageable one is whether the parent "skillfully avoids" power struggle behavior. The child's stubbornness may be typical for the age, but it becomes amplified when the parent-child bond is weak or the parent lacks strategies to sidestep conflict.

**The biological protection mechanism**: Mother nature equips parents with hormones that create fierce protectiveness of their children. These hormones begin at birth and are stimulated by every positive interaction with the child. This protectiveness creates compassion that helps parents through moments of anger. When this hormonal bonding process is disrupted, parents lose access to this natural emotional buffer.

---

## Signs of a Disrupted Bond

When a parent describes their toddler primarily in negative terms (stubborn, defiant, exhausting), this often signals that the parent-child bond has been disrupted. The warning signs include:

- Describing the child as "the most stubborn child I have ever been around"
- Experiencing every day as a power struggle
- Feeling pain, anger, and resentment more than protectiveness
- Difficulty accessing feelings of love for the child on an ongoing basis
- Fear of future estrangement

These feelings are natural and every parent experiences them sometimes. The problem arises when they dominate the relationship rather than being temporary moments buffered by love.

---

## Why Bonds Become Disrupted

Multiple factors can disrupt the natural parent-child bond:

1. **Bonding difficulties from infancy**: Some babies are difficult to bond with for various reasons
2. **Temperamental mismatch**: Parent and child may not be naturally suited to each other's personalities
3. **Ambivalence about motherhood**: The mother never fully embraced the nurturing role required when someone small depends completely on her
4. **Parent's childhood trauma**: Unresolved trauma from the parent's own upbringing
5. **Parental depletion**: The mother is simply worn out and her own needs are not being met, leaving nothing to give the child

Often multiple factors combine. A depleted parent dealing with financial strain, marriage problems, and isolation will naturally feel resentful, which then damages the bond with the child.

---

## The Vicious Cycle of Disappointment

**Why children cooperate**: Children behave well "because of who we are to them: their guiding star, the person whose love they live for, the person they don't want to disappoint."

**What happens when children sense disapproval**: When children sense parental disapproval or resentment, they conclude they have already disappointed their parent. At that point, they stop trying to please. "Instead, everything becomes a fight."

This creates a vicious cycle:
1. Parent feels disappointed in child's behavior
2. Parent shows disapproval (even subtly)
3. Child senses they have already failed
4. Child stops trying to cooperate
5. Child's behavior worsens
6. Parent feels more disappointed
7. Cycle intensifies

The perfectionist parent is especially vulnerable to this cycle. When expectations are very high, the child feels impossible to please and gives up trying.

---

## The Perfectionism Problem

**Perfectionism does not lead to perfection - it leads away from love.** Dr. Markham, a self-described "recovering perfectionist," explains that perfectionism:

- Never ends - there is always something more to improve
- Is insidious and creeps into every relationship
- Prevents happiness and creates constant not-good-enough feelings
- Teaches children that love is conditional on performance

**The dangerous path**: When a toddler gets mad because blocks do not fit together perfectly or because she cannot dress her doll correctly, she has already started internalizing perfectionism. The parent is modeling that mistakes are unacceptable.

**The perfectionist parent trap**: High expectations for yourself lead to feeling like a failure when your child is "stubborn and rebellious." Those high expectations then get placed on the child. The child picks up that love is based on "good performances and accomplishments rather than on her as my child."

**The antidote**: "Loving each moment is what gives us more wonderful moments, more love for ourselves, and more rewarding relationships." The phrase "This would be perfect if only that one thing improved" only creates more not-good-enough moments.

## The Stay-at-Home Parent's Resentment

Not every parent is suited to be a stay-at-home parent. Signs that staying home may not be working:

- Grumbling that "all I do is serve everyone else all day"
- Feeling that "my only purpose in life is to be a maid and a cook"
- Making comments like "My family is sucking the life right out of me"
- Feeling depressed when seeing toys scattered around the house
- Interpreting mess as evidence that "my life is not in order"

These feelings are not monstrous - they are signs that the parent's needs are not being met. However, taking frustrations out on the child makes everything worse.

**A nuanced view of dedication**: There is a tension between two approaches to motherhood. Some mothers dedicate their "whole being" to their children. Others believe motherhood should not mean having no life of your own. Both extremes carry risks. The mother who sacrifices everything may end up resentful (as the letter-writer's own mother did, regretting not pursuing her own happiness). But a mother whose needs are completely unmet has nothing left to give her child.

## The Ten-Step Prescription for Healing

### 1. Adopt a New Mantra

Use this mantra all day, every day, as many times as you think of it: **"I am more than enough exactly as I am. My daughter is more than enough exactly as she is."**

This directly counters perfectionism by affirming inherent worth rather than performance-based worth. Repetition is key - it must become automatic thinking.

### 2. Carve Out 15 Minutes Alone Daily

This is non-negotiable and must begin today, not "when things calm down."

**What to do**: Sit alone with a notebook or journal. Send yourself love. Simply be present, noticing what comes up. Accept any feelings or thoughts with great compassion for yourself. Write down anything that seems important.

**What NOT to do**: Do not fill the time with productive tasks like folding laundry. Do not fritter it away watching TV. This is specifically time for self-connection.

**Why it works**: Research shows that simply being with yourself creates more "friendliness" toward the self. That friendliness toward ourselves creates more love and healing in our hearts. You cannot give to your child what you do not have yourself.

### 3. Consider Returning to Work or School

Not every mother is cut out to be a stay-at-home mom. Some parents are better mothers when they are not home full-time.

**Benefits of working**:
- Helps financial pressures
- Creates more independence in marriage (allowing negotiation as equals)
- Provides identity and fulfillment outside the parenting role
- May reduce resentment of the caretaking role

**Recommendation**: Start with part-time work, which is better for both the child and the parent's stress level than jumping into full-time work immediately.

### 4. Stop Using the Child as a Scapegoat

When frustrated, do not take it out on the child. Instead:

- Work out problems in your marriage directly
- Stop cleaning up after your husband (address that issue separately)
- Stop dwelling on frustrations and making yourself more angry "just to be right"

**The reframe technique**: Every time you notice yourself looking at toys or messes and getting angry, say: "Why is she acting like a two year old?! Oh, right. She IS a two year old. How can I make this cleanup fun for both of us so she learns to like cleaning up and I won't be cleaning up after her when she's ten?"

This reframe accomplishes three things: it reminds you that the behavior is developmentally normal, it shifts focus from frustration to problem-solving, and it connects present actions to long-term goals.

### 5. Create One Orderly Refuge

Find one place in your house that you can keep clean and orderly. When you sit there, make sure your view is peaceful.

This addresses the real need (feeling like your life is in order) without requiring the entire house to be perfect or expecting a toddler to not make messes. You get your need met, and the child gets to be a child.

### 6. Organize Toys Systematically

If toys are chaotic, invest a full day (or whatever is necessary) into organizing them:

- Create a place for everything
- Put photos on the outside of boxes showing what goes inside, so the child can clean up herself
- **Rotate toys**: Put away anything she does not love into a box and store it in a closet. When you pull it out in a month, they will all seem new and entertaining

This reduces visual chaos while making cleanup achievable for the child.

### 7. Delay Additional Pregnancies

Do not get pregnant again until you are happy with your daughter, yourself, and your life - "all day, every day."

**Why this matters**: Additional children when you are already depleted will only worsen stress. The goal is to reach a genuinely good place first, which Dr. Markham affirms is possible.

### 8. Practice Gratitude Actively

Reframe the child's challenging traits as strengths:

- "A child who wants to spend lots of time playing with toys rather than whining and hanging on you? You're lucky."
- "A daughter who is smart and beautiful? Anyone would envy you."
- **"A child who is stubborn and determined and can't be pushed around? Count your blessings: she will be immune to peer pressure and more successful in her whole life."**

The stubborn child is often a strong-willed child, and strong will is a protective factor throughout life.

### 9. Take Responsibility for Your Own Mood

Every time you feel bad, notice it and find some way to improve your mood. Specific suggestions:

- Put on music you love and dance to it
- Take a hot bath (with your daughter if necessary)
- Call your mom or a friend
- Count things you are grateful for

**Why this is your responsibility**: "It's your responsibility to maintain a sense of well-being. You can't be the mom you want to be without that. And you deserve it."

This is not selfish - it is necessary. You cannot pour from an empty cup.

### 10. Mirror Practice: Say "I Love You" to Yourself

Look in the mirror, smile at yourself, and say "I LOVE YOU." Do this as many times a day as you can.

**Why it works despite feeling corny**: Research shows this practice can transform mood, and if done daily, can transform lives. The key is to savor the loving and loved feeling as it sinks in, not to rush through it dismissively.

## The Wholehearted Commitment Decision

Beneath all the practical strategies is a fundamental choice: **Are you prepared to wholeheartedly commit to being her mother? To accept her and adore her?**

This does not mean you will not have bad days. It means:
- You will see things from her point of view more often
- You will be committed to trying to make things better
- You accept her as she is, not as you wish she were

This commitment creates the foundation for everything else. Without it, techniques are hollow. With it, even imperfect implementation of techniques can heal the relationship.

---

## Key Reframes

**"Stubborn" becomes "determined"**: A child who cannot be pushed around will be immune to peer pressure and more successful throughout life.

**"Power struggles" become "developmental appropriateness"**: Two-year-olds are supposed to test limits. The question is not how to stop them from being two-year-olds, but how to work with this developmental stage.

**"I'm failing as a mother" becomes "my approach isn't working with this particular child"**: This shifts from identity-level shame to a solvable problem of fit and strategy.

**"She's wearing me out" becomes "my needs aren't being met"**: This redirects attention from the child as the problem to the parent's support system and self-care.

---

## Warning Signs in Children

A toddler getting mad when blocks do not fit together perfectly or when she cannot dress her doll correctly is concerning. This indicates she has already started internalizing perfectionism. The best way to prevent perfectionism in your child is to stop being a perfectionist yourself - children learn from what we model more than what we say.

---

## The Relationship Between Conditional Love and Rebellion

When parents praise children for obedience, accomplishments, and good performances, but show disappointment for failures and mistakes, children learn that love is conditional. This creates a paradox: the child may try harder initially, but eventually concludes that pleasing the parent is impossible. At that point, they stop trying and the relationship becomes adversarial.

The antidote is not to stop praising good behavior, but to ensure the child knows they are loved and accepted regardless of behavior - that the parent's fundamental view of them is positive even when their behavior needs correction.
