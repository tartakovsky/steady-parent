# The Family That Plays Together: Building Connection Through Play

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids
Age groups: Toddlers, Preschoolers, School Age
Topics: Connection, Family Life, Routines

---

## The Science Behind Why Play Builds Connection

**Play and laughter create bonding at a biological level.** When we laugh, our body releases oxytocin into our systems. This means that when you're laughing with someone, you're literally bonding with them through chemistry. This is why playing together is described as an "almost magical way to build connection" - it's not just emotional, it's physiological.

**Laughter transforms bad moods by decreasing stress hormones** circulating in your body. This dual mechanism - increasing bonding hormones while decreasing stress hormones - explains why playing together is one of the fastest ways to:
- Heal minor relationship stress
- Help people drop grudges
- Bring the family into sync
- Create a happy feeling in your home

Research shows that humor is invaluable for smoothing over the rough spots of life, from parental mistakes to simple bad luck. **Couples who can use affectionate humor to deflate anger have happier relationships.** This principle extends to parent-child relationships - children whose parents use silliness to keep the day flowing smoothly benefit greatly.

## Why Playfulness Works Better Than Direct Instructions

For younger children (toddlers and preschoolers), direct instructions often fail not because children are being defiant, but because **children are so engrossed in their own play that they don't even notice the parent.** Making instructions into a game captures their attention in a way that commands cannot.

### Practical Examples of Transforming Instructions into Play

**Instead of:** "Eat your breakfast now!"
**Try:** "Little Gorilla, it's time for breakfast, come eat your bugs and bananas!"

**Instead of:** "Get in the car!"
**Try:** "Don't you think your steam shovel wants to get in the car now so he can see the construction site on the way to school?"

The transformation works because it speaks the child's language - the language of imagination and play - rather than expecting them to shift into adult task-completion mode.

## Building Playfulness Into Daily Routines

The best way to cultivate more playful fun in your house is to **make it part of your routine.** The process is simple:
1. Try new playful ideas
2. If they work, repeat them
3. Repetition creates fun traditions the family can enjoy and look forward to

**Key insight:** Not all rituals need to be serious or spiritually joyous - some of the best traditions are silly. Don't wait for special times; any part of daily life can be made into a game.

### Everyday Playfulness Ideas

- **Use funny voices** during routine activities
- **Trade roles at the dinner table** so each family member acts as someone else (kids' portrayals of adults can be hilarious and provide insight into how they perceive you)
- **Have a race to get dressed and in bed for storytime.** Important: Instead of kids racing against each other (which worsens sibling rivalry), have kids work as a team racing against the adult or the clock
- **Compete at making baskets with dirty laundry** while doing household cleanup together
- **Make up funny song lyrics in the car**
- **Gentle physical roughhousing as children wake up** - for some kids, this puts them in a good mood for the day. Example: a competition to take off each other's socks
- **Start a pillow fight** when you're feeling dispirited and want to shift the mood and reconnect

## Critical Caveats for Playful Games

### 1. Don't Make Light of Serious Situations or Emotions

**The sequence matters:** Start by empathizing. Don't try to talk the child out of their feelings. But once they've vented, getting them laughing about the situation may be the most healing thing you can do.

**Example:** When your child has gotten past their upset over how unfair their science teacher was today, you can start to act out how YOU would have handled things - by messing up totally - or you might even be able to mildly mimic the science teacher, if you think it's appropriate.

The key is waiting until the emotional processing is complete before introducing humor.

### 2. Be Sure Opponents Are Well Matched

This can be accomplished by **handicapping adults and older siblings.** Without this adjustment, games become frustrating rather than fun for younger or less skilled participants.

### 3. Be Sure the Environment Is Safe

A family tickle match where there are breakables or sharp objects is almost guaranteed to end in disaster. Physical play requires a safe space.

### 4. Competition Itself Is Not Bad, But Valuing Winning Above the Game Is

For many children - and even some fathers and sons - **noncompetitive games work best.** The distinction is between enjoying the process of playing together versus focusing on the outcome of who wins.

### 5. What To Do When Someone Gets Hurt During Roughhousing

**Two scenarios to recognize:**

**Scenario A - Appropriate tears:** The child is ready to get back into the action after a quick hug. The tears match the injury level.

**Scenario B - Wild sobbing that seems like over-reacting:** This is actually a good thing. The laughter has loosened up feelings stuffed in their "emotional backpack," and they're taking advantage of this minor injury to share deeper wounds they can't verbalize. After a good cry, your child will be so much more relaxed and happy since those emotions will be released.

**The response protocol:**
1. Instead of feeling like a bad parent because someone got hurt, relax
2. Take the opportunity to help your child with their big feelings
3. Be grateful they got a chance to cry
4. Afterward, ask both kids if they think they need to add any new rules to keep everyone safer next time
5. Write the rules down and post them so you can easily remind them next time they start getting wild

**Important insight:** Written agreements have extra power even for kids who can't read. The act of formalizing rules gives them weight.

## The Importance of Play for Connection with Boys

Quote from Lawrence Cohen (Playful Parenting): "Play is children's main way of communicating. Playing is connection. Boys especially need empathy and emotional connection. You can't communicate to them that what they want to play is stupid and violent and antisocial, and then expect them to talk to you about their inner feelings."

This highlights that **dismissing children's play preferences - especially boys' preference for physical or "rough" play - damages the connection pathway.** If you want children to share their inner feelings, you must first validate their preferred mode of connection.

## Specific Game Ideas for Common Challenges

### "Relay-Race" Jammies (Getting Ready for Bed)

**How it works:** Child takes off one piece of clothing, runs to another room and touches a certain spot, runs back, takes off the next item or puts on the next bedtime item, and repeats until dressed.

**Why it works:** Gets out evening energy and takes the focus off "getting ready for bed."

**Critical follow-up:** Be sure to follow with a calming activity such as reading bedtime stories. The game releases energy; it doesn't wind down by itself.

### Airplane (Seatbelt Wars / Getting Out the Door)

**The problem it solves:** Seatbelt resistance and struggles getting out the door on time.

**How it works:** Pretend you are going on an airplane. Play the flight attendant:
- "Flight 1234 will begin boarding in 5 minutes. Please collect your baggage and proceed to the gate."
- Later: "All ticketed passengers should now be on board."
- Once in the "airplane," the flight attendant asks all passengers to make sure their seatbelts are buckled "low and tight across their laps."
- "Prepare for takeoff" on the driveway and off you fly.

The game reframes the mundane requirement (seatbelt) as part of an exciting adventure narrative.

### Code FUN! (Rushing When You're Running Late)

**Specific use case:** Only used when you're in a rush - this keeps the preparations fun.

**How it works:** Either the child or parent "rings the bell" to signal that it's time to rush. Then see how fast the kids can get into their clothes or get into the car.

**Critical constraints:**
- Only use this when you are almost ready to go (no one stops to run the dishwasher in an emergency!)
- Make sure everyone knows the difference between this and a real emergency

### Surprise Me (Getting Child to Complete Tasks)

**Origin:** Invented by a child.

**How it works:** The parent expresses doubt that the child is able to do something easy and then acts surprised when it happens.

**Variation:** Act surprised that something has changed: "Now how did that plate get from the table to the counter?"

**Critical constraint:** Only use this if it gets your child giggling, so you know they are in on the game - this prevents using it manipulatively. The child must understand it's play, not genuine doubt in their abilities.

### Time-It (Cleaning Up / Task Completion)

**Version 1:** Run a stopwatch to see how long it takes children to do a task (e.g., cleaning up all the toys). Instead of comparing to last time, just say "Wow - 3 minutes and 9 seconds!"

**Version 2:** Agree to see how many toys you can pick up together in a certain amount of time or until a particular piece of music ends. When the timer goes off, you're done picking up, even if some toys are still left.

**Why comparing to "last time" is avoided:** Comparison creates pressure and potential failure. Simply noting the time creates awareness without judgment.

**Constraint:** Make sure tasks are short enough that kids can stay focused and engaged.

**Benefits:** Both versions help kids gain a sense of time and responsibility.

### Let's Race! (Getting Dressed)

**How it works:** Race your child to see who gets dressed first, or let them try to get dressed before you finish something, such as making breakfast.

**For multiple children:** Have the children team up and help each other (rather than competing against each other, which increases sibling rivalry).

### The "Can-Do Team" (Solo Parenting Multiple Kids)

**Origin:** A working mom with three children invented this when her husband travels and it's hard to get all three kids out the door, dinner made, everyone to bed, etc.

**The core concept:** Tell your kids "We can do it together, because we're a 'Can-Do Team!'"

**Implementation:**
- Let kids help each other get dressed, brush teeth, get breakfast, pack backpacks, and load the car while you cheer them on
- Encourage each of them to communicate with each other to figure out when and where help is needed
- Everything is a team effort with all parties sharing ideas and suggestions on how to get something done
- When something breaks down, everyone stops what they're doing and problem-solves the breakdown

**Enhancement:** Let kids pick an actual team name ("Fireballs," "Eagles," "Howling Wolves," etc.)

**Example prompts:**
- "Can-Do Team, let's make dinner! What are we having?" (Let them create a menu)
- "Can-Do Team, who's setting the table? Ready?!? Go!"

### Top-to-Bottom Cleanup (Whole House Cleaning)

**Key insight:** It is more fun to do someone else's work than your own.

**How it works:** Work together to clean the house without attention to who made the mess. Take a laundry basket with you to put items that belong in other rooms, and ask your child to "drive" it from room to room, putting things away as you travel.

**Imagination enhancements:**
- The laundry basket can be a dump truck
- The vacuum can be a bulldozer

**Bonus benefit:** This game offers an opportunity to talk about how nice it feels when rooms are clean and how great it feels to work together.

## Core Principles Summary

1. **Play works biologically** - oxytocin release creates real bonding
2. **Speak children's language** - imagination and play, not commands
3. **Make play routine** - repetition creates traditions
4. **Sequence matters** - empathy first, humor after emotional processing
5. **Adapt for fairness** - handicap stronger players
6. **Environment matters** - physical play needs safe spaces
7. **Process over outcome** - enjoying play matters more than winning
8. **Tears during play can be healing** - let them happen
9. **Written rules have power** - even for non-readers
10. **Validate play preferences** - especially for boys and physical play
11. **Team kids together** - avoid sibling rivalry through competition
12. **Fun over perfection** - the timer ends, some toys remain, that's okay
