# When Partners Disagree About Having Another Child

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids (Dr. Laura Markham)

---

## The Core Dilemma

When one partner wants another child and the other doesn't, this creates a situation where the decision **must be made jointly**. Unlike many relationship decisions where compromise is possible, having a child is binary - you either have one or you don't. This makes partner disagreement particularly painful.

**Key principle**: If one partner is the designated stay-at-home parent, their vote carries even more weight in this decision because they bear more of the day-to-day childcare responsibility.

---

## Framework for Processing the Grief of Not Having Another Child

If the decision is ultimately made not to have another child, there is a three-step process to work through the longing:

### Step 1: Own the Choice

The first and most critical step is to **reframe the situation as your own choice** rather than something being done to you. Instead of feeling victimized ("He won't let me have another baby"), you consciously acknowledge: "I love my husband and choose to have a wonderful life with him and our daughter. I would rather be with him than have a second child."

**Why this matters**: Framing it as your choice prevents resentment from building. When you feel something was forced upon you, you carry that grievance indefinitely. When you own it as your decision, you can move through it.

**Important caveat**: This reframing may cause you to weep. That's expected and healthy - it leads directly to Step 2.

### Step 2: Grieve

The longing for a child you won't have is a real loss that deserves real mourning. This is not something to "get over" quickly or push aside. Grief takes time.

**Core truth**: Only grieving will ultimately bring peace with the decision. There is no shortcut around this emotional work.

### Step 3: Find Partial Fulfillment

After owning the choice and grieving, look for ways to partially fill the longing. One example: changing careers to work with babies (such as becoming a doula, childcare provider, or pediatric nurse) might help satisfy some of the drive to nurture infants.

**Key qualifier**: This is about *partial* fulfillment. It won't fully replace having another child, but it can help channel the nurturing energy.

## Why It May Be Too Soon to Make This Decision

Before committing to the grief process, consider whether the decision itself is actually final. Several factors suggest waiting:

### Reason 1: Child Spacing Research

**Research finding**: Spacing children closer than two years apart is a risk factor for the emotional development of both children. The presumed reason is that most parents cannot adequately meet the needs of two babies simultaneously.

**Practical implication**: If the baby is currently 9 months old, you wouldn't want to start trying to conceive for at least another 4 months anyway (to ensure at least a 2-year gap). This creates a natural waiting period during which the other partner's feelings might shift.

**Nuance**: Dr. Markham notes this doesn't mean it "can't be done" with closer spacing, just that it "takes heroic measures and is hard to pull off so everyone comes out ok."

### Reason 2: People Change Their Minds

The husband in this scenario had previously said he wanted no children at all, but came around after seeing his wife's seriousness and commitment (through her doula training and reading childbirth books). This pattern could repeat.

**Approach for ongoing dialogue**: Talk about the issue in a **non-accusatory way**, focusing only on expressing your desire and asking if there's any way he could eventually see himself being ready. Avoid pressure; focus on understanding.

**Timeline flexibility**: Even if the partner isn't ready in the next year, spacing kids a few years apart works well. Dr. Markham's own children are four years apart, which she describes as having "a lot of advantages."

### Reason 3: Re-examine the Current Arrangement

If the craving for another baby started when the first child was only 2-3 months old, this raises an important question: **Why wasn't the current baby fulfilling the "baby lust"?**

**The insight**: A 2-month-old is as little as a baby can get without being a newborn. If you're already craving another baby at that stage, it might not be about wanting *more* babies - it might be about wanting *more time* with the baby you have.

**Specific hypothesis**: If the mother works full-time and only sees the baby after work and on weekends while the father is the stay-at-home parent, perhaps the mother isn't getting enough of the baby experience. The longing for "another baby" might actually be longing for more connection with the current baby.

**Possible solution**: Consider renegotiating the work/caregiving arrangement. Being a full-time parent might "slake the babylust" by providing the intensive baby-care experience the mother is craving.

## Contrasting Example: When Roles Are Reversed

Dr. Markham describes a family where the father wants "as many children as possible" while the stay-at-home mother thinks one child is plenty and wants to return to her career.

**The mother's response**: She told him that if he would be the stay-at-home parent, she'd be happy to have more kids.

**What this illustrates**: The person doing the primary caregiving work gets proportionally more say in whether to expand that workload. This is about fairness and practicality, not about whose desire for children is more valid.

## Communication Approach for Partner Disagreement

When discussing wanting another child with a reluctant partner:

**Do:**
- Focus on expressing your own desire without blame
- Ask open-ended questions: "Is there any way you could see eventually being ready?"
- Give time and space for their feelings to evolve
- Recognize that their concerns (especially as primary caregiver) are valid

**Don't:**
- Make it accusatory
- Push repeatedly when they've said they're "tired of talking about it"
- Ignore the validity of their role in the decision
- Assume the current answer is the permanent answer

| Concept | Core Idea |
|---------|-----------|
| **Owning the choice** | Reframe giving up the dream as your own decision to prioritize the marriage, not something being done to you |
| **Grief as the path** | There is no shortcut - only grieving fully leads to peace with the decision |
| **Caregiver's weighted vote** | The stay-at-home parent's opinion carries extra weight because they bear more of the daily work |
| **Two-year spacing research** | Closer than two years creates developmental risks due to parents being stretched too thin |
| **Baby lust vs. baby time** | Craving another baby when yours is only months old might signal wanting more time with your current baby, not more babies |
| **Non-accusatory dialogue** | Focus on your feelings and ask open questions rather than pressuring |

## Warning Signs and Reframes

**Warning**: Feeling sad when others get pregnant, being unable to move past the longing despite journaling and hobbies - these indicate unprocessed grief that won't resolve through distraction alone.

**Reframe**: If the longing started when your baby was only 2-3 months old, examine whether the issue is really about having another child or about your current access to baby-care experiences.

**Important timing note**: Don't make permanent decisions about "never having another child" when the first child is still under a year old and the optimal conception window hasn't even opened yet. Give the situation time to evolve.
