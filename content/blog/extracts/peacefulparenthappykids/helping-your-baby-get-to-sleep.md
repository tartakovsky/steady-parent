# Helping Your Baby Get to Sleep

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids (Dr. Laura Markham, Clinical Psychologist)
URL: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/helping-your-baby-get-to-sleep

## Three Schools of Thought on Infant Sleep

### The Ferber Method (Cry It Out)

Advocates teaching babies over three months to sleep through the night by letting them cry for increasingly longer periods. While most babies eventually fall asleep, the process is often traumatic for parents and presumably for babies. It frequently needs to be repeated following any disruption in routine. Critics note that Ferber has no psychology training and question whether letting babies cry has permanent harmful effects.

### The Family Bed Approach

Holds that infants are hard-wired to sleep with their mothers and nurse at night for many months, probably until toddlerhood. Research indicates babies who sleep with their mothers are less likely to die of SIDS (Sudden Infant Death Syndrome). Mothers who co-sleep typically get more rest. Critics express concern about parents rolling onto babies, note that co-sleeping babies take longer to sleep through the night, and question whether toddlers will transition to their own beds.

### The No-Cry Approach

Represented by Elizabeth Pantley's No Cry Sleep Solution. Acknowledges that parents may desperately need sleep and agrees babies benefit from learning to fall back asleep independently, but argues this can be accomplished without the trauma of cry-it-out methods.

## The Core Problem: Sleep Association Dependency

Many infants who are helped to sleep by parents (nursing or rocking) cannot put themselves back to sleep when they re-awaken during the night. If they nurse to sleep, they may need to nurse again every time they partially wake. Eventually, if they do not figure out how to fall back asleep on their own, they will need gentle parental help to learn this skill.

**Important distinction**: Nursing to sleep is not a "bad habit" any more than using diapers. It is completely appropriate to nurse babies to sleep. The learning to fall asleep independently will come when they are developmentally ready.

## Key Principles

### On Letting Babies Cry

Leaving babies to cry is not recommended for emotionally healthy development. Children can learn to fall asleep without nursing or rocking in the safe comfort of a parent's arms, once they are old enough.

### On Finding Your Solution

There is a sleep solution that fits every unique family, from co-sleeping to baby bunks that attach to the parents' bed, to baby hammocks, to cribs. The goal is ensuring children know they can always ask for and get help, while also getting enough parental sleep to function well.

### On Parental Guilt

Exhausted parents who yell at their children may cause more harm than imperfect sleep arrangements. Do what works for your family and release the guilt.

## Practical Strategies for Getting More Sleep

### 1. Keep Baby Close at Night

Keep your baby near you while nursing at night so you do not have to fully wake up. Breastmilk is designed to be given every few hours. Human babies are biologically designed to require their mother's presence fairly constantly, unlike some animals whose higher-fat milk allows longer absences.

### 2. Co-Sleeping Safety

Mothers are biologically designed not to roll on their babies (unless impaired by drugs or alcohol). Evidence suggests co-sleeping babies may have lower SIDS risk because their sleep cycles synchronize with their mothers, and the maternal presence prevents overly deep sleep.

**Safety protocol**: Position baby between mother and wall rather than between parents, especially with very young babies. Some experts note fathers who have had alcohol could pose a risk. However, fathers do have hormonal changes after becoming parents that include protective instincts. Always follow a detailed safe co-sleeping checklist.

### 3. Use a Moses Basket, Cradle, or Baby Bunk

If co-sleeping in the same bed does not work, try a sleep space within arm's reach. Baby bunks can anchor to the bed at the same level and open so baby has their own space while mother can easily reach for night nursing.

### 4. Learn to Nurse Lying Down

This takes about a week to master but allows dozing during feeds. Position pillows behind you and between your knees for support. Place a folded blanket under baby if needed to raise them to breast level. Baby should be on their side facing you.

### 5. Help Baby Set Their Metabolic Clock

Baby does not yet know night is for sleeping. Speed the adjustment by ensuring baby does not sleep all day. Take them outside in the sun. Go for walks. Expose them to the wonders of the world. Fresh air and sunshine during the day improve nighttime sleep. Co-sleeping babies synchronize their REM cycles with mothers, reinforcing day-wake and night-sleep patterns. Keep nights dark and quiet. Nurse when baby wakes but do not make it playtime.

### 6. Nap When Baby Naps

This is the golden rule. Skip the shower if needed. You need this sleep.

### 7. Accept Morning Help from Partner

If your partner can take baby in the morning for an extra hour of sleep, accept it. Eight hours of interrupted sleep does not equal eight hours of continuous sleep.

### 8. Go to Bed Early

This is not the time to maintain an active evening life. You have the rest of your life for staying up late.

### 9. Encourage Sleep Associations Baby Can Control

Begin gently and gradually helping baby learn to fall asleep without sucking or being held. This is not about forcing independence but about slowly building the skill over time while always responding when baby communicates hunger or need for comfort.

## Night Feeding Duration

Babies typically need to nurse at night for a minimum of six months and probably until about one year old.

## Transition to Independent Sleep

The process of helping babies or toddlers learn to fall asleep without nursing or rocking should happen when they are developmentally ready. This learning occurs in the safe comfort of parental arms, not through being left alone to cry. The transition is a gradual, gentle process that respects the child's developmental stage and emotional needs.

## Three Consolidated Takeaways

1. Babies are biologically wired to need frequent feeding and parental proximity at night. This is normal development, not a problem to be fixed through cry-it-out methods.

2. Sleep solutions must fit each unique family. The goal is balancing baby's needs for closeness with parents' needs for adequate rest to function well.

3. Teaching independent sleep is a gradual skill-building process that happens when baby is developmentally ready, always in the context of parental comfort and responsiveness.
