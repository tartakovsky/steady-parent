# What Children Really Want for the Holidays: Shifting from Presents to Presence

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/What-Every-Child-Wants-for-the-Holidays

---

## The Core Insight: Children's "Greed" Is Actually a Deeper Longing

When children seem greedy during the holidays, something deeper is being triggered - a longing for a happy, perfect life where they feel completely enveloped by parental love. Adults share this same fantasy. The holidays carry a promise of transformational love, which is part of their wonder.

The human mind naturally craves more, more, more. Children (like many adults) haven't yet learned how to manage those yearnings and direct them toward what will actually fulfill them.

**Research shows that true fulfillment comes from five sources:**
1. Connection
2. Creativity
3. Contribution
4. Gratitude
5. Meaning

**The material abundance paradox:** Research demonstrates that having lots of material possessions usually makes us value them less. When we aren't as grateful, we aren't as happy. Therefore, material abundance absent gratitude often makes us more unhappy, not less.

**The key reframe:** It is possible to fill children's deep longings - not with excessive presents (which generally leave kids feeling unfulfilled) but with the deeper meaning of the holidays and the magic of love.

## Framework: Eight Strategies for Meaningful Holidays

### Strategy 1: Establish "Presence Over Presents" as the Core Philosophy

**The principle:** Explicitly tell your child that your December holiday is about "presence" (time together), not "presents." Yes, there will be some presents, but that's not the focus and not what matters most.

**Cultural touchstones to reference:**
- The miracle of Hanukkah had nothing to do with gifts
- The Grinch's realization: "What if Christmas, he thought, doesn't come from a store. What if Christmas... perhaps... means a little bit more!"

**Critical follow-through:** You must keep this promise through your actions. When children ask you to do something with them:
- Leave dishes in the sink
- Leave email unanswered for now
- Skip the homemade present or wreath if you can't do it WITH your child

**The perfection trap:** If you do crafts with your child, it won't look perfect, but you'll treasure it forever - as will they. The imperfect result made together is more valuable than the perfect result made alone.

---

### Strategy 2: Manage Expectations Through the Four Gift Categories

**The framework:** Ask your child to carefully consider their desires and tell you four gift ideas:

1. **Store-bought gift** - Must be within your means (this may require back-and-forth discussion)
2. **A book** - Something they want to read
3. **A "together" present** - Something you will do with them (example: going to the zoo)
4. **A "giving" present** - Something they can gift to someone else (example: stuffing stockings for kids in a shelter)

**Allowing for surprise:** If you want more surprise, let them give you two possible options for each category.

**What to avoid:** Discourage long list-making. Long lists stir up cravings and fantasies that can't possibly be met. The structure of four specific categories naturally limits this tendency.

---

### Strategy 3: Fill Hearts Through Love-Based Family and Community Activities

**The principle:** Every day, do one thing to bring your family together and/or a small act of kindness for your community.

**Specific activities for family connection:**
- Enjoy twinkling holiday lights together in the dark
- Go around the dinner table and share appreciations
- Make a paper chain together, writing an appreciation on each link (examples: "Dad makes the best pancakes," "Michael let me use his truck," "Grandma has a beautiful laugh")
- Write love notes of appreciation to each other (anonymous or signed) and hide them around the house to be found

**Specific activities for community kindness:**

**Why this works:** When we act kindly toward others, it makes us happy inside.

- Volunteer to walk dogs for the local shelter
- Pick up trash in the park
- Write notes of appreciation to teachers, store keepers, crossing guards - anyone in your community

**Research-backed benefit of gratitude notes:** Studies show that expressions of gratitude are good for the recipient's well-being, but also make the child who writes the notes happier for months afterwards.

**The "Beam a Blessing" game:** As you walk down the sidewalk or drive in your car, play a game with your child where you beam a blessing of love to everyone you see. Notice how great you feel after two minutes of this - that's because you're activating those feel-good love hormones.

**The underlying mechanism:** Let your child experience how acts of love create feelings of more love. The action generates the feeling, not the other way around.

---

### Strategy 4: Create the Experience of Abundance Within Values and Budget

**The key insight:** You can let your child revel in that feeling of abundance while still sticking with your values and your budget. Abundance is an experience, not a dollar amount.

**Techniques for creating abundance feelings:**

**Wrap experiences as gifts:**
- If gifting a trip to the zoo, print out a photo of their favorite zoo animal and a simple certificate, wrap it complete with ribbon

**Multiply small items:**
- If they love chapstick, buy four flavors and wrap each one separately (same cost, more unwrapping experiences)

**Document and celebrate their contributions:**
- If you and your child picked up trash in the park, take a photo
- Print it out with a "Commendation for Citizenship" and put it under your tree with a candy cane
- Opening this envelope will probably bring a thoughtful smile to your child's face, especially when you tell everyone how dogged your child was in transforming that part of the park

**Create "evidence packages" of their generosity:**
- If you baked and decorated cookies together to take to older folks when visiting Aunt Sue, take photos
- Print out a "Commendation for Generosity" with your child's name on it
- Include a photo of a happy senior citizen cookie-eater and your child
- Wrap it with a ribbon and a cookie in a baggie
- This will probably bring as big a smile to your child's face as a toy, especially when you regale everyone present with a story about how happy your child made the senior citizens

**Why this works:** The combination of recognition, documentation, and public storytelling transforms an act of service into a treasured gift. The child receives both the internal reward of contribution AND the external validation of being celebrated for it.

---

### Strategy 5: Allow Desire Without Requiring Acquisition

**The principle:** Children can desire things without that meaning they "need" the item. Wanting and getting are not the same thing, and wanting can be enjoyed on its own.

**How to respond when your child says "I want that!":**

**Respond positively, even if you won't be buying it:**
- "That game looks like so much fun, doesn't it?"
- "Oh, this stuffed animal feels so soft and cuddly, I see why you like it!"

**Why this works:** Often, this acknowledgment is all the child needs to enjoy their pleasure in the item without needing to pursue it. You're validating the desire itself rather than denying it.

**The magic words for persistent wanting:** If your child does have a hard time putting the item back on the shelf, try "You wish..." as in:
- "It sounds like you really wish you could have one of those... I understand. I love the way it..."

**The mechanism:** By naming the wish explicitly and with empathy, you help the child process the feeling rather than fight against it. The wish is honored even though it isn't fulfilled.

---

### Strategy 6: Respond to "Misbehavior" with Playfulness

**The principle:** Give your child the gift of playful responses to things that you'd normally get irritated about.

**Specific techniques:**
- When she resists your instructions, be mock horrified
- Scoop her up and throw her around, making a rambunctious game of it
- Interpret every "misbehavior" as a request for loving connection: "Hey! Are you out of hugs again?!"

**Critical timing note:** If you need to "teach" appropriate behavior, do it AFTER you connect. Connection first, correction second.

**Why this matters:** This is described as one of the greatest gifts you can give your child.

**The reward for parents:** You'll be so pleased by how much more cooperative she is that you might adopt this approach permanently. Playful responses reduce resistance and increase cooperation.

---

### Strategy 7: Model Values Through Holiday Activities

**The principle:** Prioritize family activities that savor the deliciousness of your holiday. Minimize shopping and busy-ness and focus instead on living, with your child, the values that are part of your holiday.

**Specific value-centered activities:**
- Read and talk about books on holiday themes
- Have dinner discussions about your holiday and what it means to you
- Open holiday cards together at dinner and discuss what you love about the sender
- Think of each holiday activity (gift wrapping, baking, decorating) as a chance to connect and create a little more love in the world

**The reframe:** Routine holiday tasks become opportunities for connection when approached with this mindset. Gift wrapping isn't just a chore - it's a chance to be together and spread love.

---

### Strategy 8: Minimize Stress and Fill Your Own Cup

**Why this comes last but matters most:** You need to be in a good mood, living the spirit of the season and spreading love and good cheer. You can't give what you don't have.

**Specific guidance:**
- Pare back on your expectations
- Let go of comparisons
- Prioritize getting sleep
- Take care of yourself so you can give your child your best - not just what's left

**What children actually want:** Your kids don't want a magazine-spread holiday. They want YOU in a good mood so:
- Your joy is infectious
- You're emotionally generous when things go wrong (which they inevitably will)

## The Core Truth About Children's Wants

No matter what they think, kids don't need the latest toy or the latest electronic gadget. Those are just strategies they think will help them feel good.

**The secret to lasting good feelings:** It has to come from one of five sources:
1. Love
2. Contribution
3. Creativity
4. Meaning
5. Gratitude

Toys and gadgets are attempts to access these feelings through shortcuts, but the shortcut doesn't work.

---

## The Advertising Trap

**Lawrence Kutner's observation:** "Television advertisements for toys and games often exploit children's underlying needs and desires. Many commercials show a child playing with a game or toy with her parents. The message is clear to young children: Ask for this product and your mother and father will pay attention to you. It is an offer they cannot resist."

**What this reveals:** Children aren't actually craving the toy - they're craving the parental attention and connection depicted in the commercial. Advertisers know this and exploit it. The product is just a prop in a fantasy about parental engagement.

## Self-Reflection Questions for Parents

**The memory test:** Consider the memories your kids are shaping this December. When they look back, will they describe a parent who communicated the spirit of the season with:
- Laughter
- Warm embraces
- Gracious patience
- Joyful presence

**The identity affirmation:** You ARE that parent, inside.

**The support question:** What kind of support could you give yourself, so that you can EXPRESS that parent, more and more each day?

This frames self-care not as selfishness but as enabling the best version of yourself to show up for your children.
