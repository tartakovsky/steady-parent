# Preparing an 18-Month-Old for Separation During Labor and New Baby Arrival

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids (Dr. Laura Markham)

---

## Core Reassurance: The Child Will Be Okay

**A strong attachment foundation provides resilience.** When a parent has built a close relationship with their toddler up to this point, that foundation gives the child the strength to weather temporary separations, even difficult ones. The love and attention provided before and after the challenging event makes all the difference in the child's ability to handle it.

**Even if the worst happens, recovery is possible.** If the child cries extensively or even cries themselves to sleep during the separation, they will recover and be okay. This is not ideal, but it is survivable because of the secure relationship that exists.

**The parent's presence during labor matters too.** Studies show that labor advances faster when women have support from a loved one. While the partner could stay home with the toddler instead of attending the birth, having that support present during labor has measurable benefits. This creates a legitimate tension that needs to be managed rather than ignored.

---

## The Critical Mistake: Exposing Toddler to Multiple Caregivers

**Leaving an 18-month-old with different people to "get used to separation" does not work.** This approach actually traumatizes the child rather than building tolerance. The parent in the original question had been trying short and long trips with various people (neighbor, grandma, others), but nobody could calm the child down.

**Why this fails:** 18-month-olds do not "get used to separation" in the abstract. They do not develop a generalized coping skill for being away from their primary attachment figure. Instead, they need a specific person they trust to help them through difficult emotions.

---

## The Correct Approach: Building a Single Secondary Attachment

**Pick ONE person who will care for the child during labor and focus all preparation on that relationship.** Stop exposing the child to separations with other people. The goal is not separation tolerance; the goal is relationship building with one specific caregiver.

**Why this works:** The only thing that helps a toddler cope with the absence of their primary attachment figure is the presence of someone else they trust. Trust with toddlers is built through repeated positive interactions with the same person, not through exposure to many different caregivers.

### What the Chosen Caregiver Needs to Learn

The preparation period should be used to help the chosen caregiver learn how to calm this specific child. Every child is different, and the caregiver needs to know:
- What comfort objects the child responds to
- What routines help the child settle
- What the child's cues look like

### What Success Looks Like

**It is okay if the child cries.** The goal is not to prevent all crying. The goal is to ensure the child has someone present to comfort them while they cry. The key distinction is between:
- A child who cries while being held and comforted by a trusted person (acceptable, even healthy)
- A child who is left to cry themselves to sleep with no comfort (not ideal, though survivable)

## Preparing the Child Cognitively: The Homemade Book Technique

Even though 18-month-olds do not have many words yet, they understand a great deal. Reading a personalized book repeatedly helps prepare them for what will happen.

### Recommended Book Structure

**Page 1 - Anchor in Love:**
- Words: "Mommy LOVES [child's name]"
- Image: Picture of parent and child together
- Purpose: Establishes the emotional foundation before introducing any challenging content

**Page 2 - Introduce the Event:**
- Words: "Mommy is having a baby. Mommy goes to the hospital to have the baby."
- Image: Picture of pregnant mom, possibly outside the hospital
- Purpose: Names what is going to happen in simple, concrete terms

**Page 3 - Name the Separation and Caregiver:**
- Words: "[Child's name] goes to stay with [caregiver name] while Mommy has the baby."
- Image: Picture of child and caregiver looking happy together
- Optional addition: Extra pages showing them doing fun activities together
- Purpose: Creates positive associations with the specific person who will provide care

**Page 4 - Acknowledge the Hard Feelings:**
- Words: "[Child's name] misses Mommy but [caregiver name] loves [child's name] and tells him that Mommy will be home soon."
- Image: Picture of child being held by caregiver
- Purpose: Validates that missing the parent is expected and normal, while showing the caregiver providing comfort

**Page 5 - The Reunion:**
- Words: "Mommy comes back! Mommy always comes back!"
- Image: Parent hugging child
- Purpose: Reinforces the critical message that separations are temporary

### Why This Works

The book accomplishes several things:
1. Uses the child's receptive language (what they understand) even though their expressive language (what they can say) is limited
2. Creates a narrative framework the child can hold onto
3. Establishes the core reassurance message ("Mommy always comes back") through repetition
4. Normalizes the emotional experience in advance

---

## Verbal Preparation Running Alongside the Book

In addition to reading the book, the parent should talk to the child about the upcoming events:
- "You will go to the hospital to have the baby"
- "You will go to [caregiver's name]"
- "I will come to pick you up and take you home soon"
- Emphasis on: "I ALWAYS come back to you"

The phrase "Mommy always comes back" is the key message that needs to be reinforced repeatedly through both verbal conversations and the book.

---

## Comfort Objects as Transitional Tools

**Identify objects the child is already attached to** (stuffed animals, bottles, blankets, etc.) and:
1. Ensure these objects will be available during the separation
2. Have the chosen caregiver practice using these objects to comfort the child before the separation occurs

This gives the caregiver additional tools and creates continuity between the home environment and the temporary care situation.

---

## Timing Considerations

At 35 weeks pregnant, there is still time to implement this approach, but the window is limited. The recommendations involve:
- Repeated practice sessions with the chosen caregiver (not one or two, but "as often as possible")
- Multiple readings of the homemade book
- Building genuine relationship and trust

This is not a quick fix but a relationship-building process that takes weeks, not days.

---

## Summary of Key Principles

1. **Trust in the foundation you have built.** A secure attachment provides resilience.
2. **Stop the multi-caregiver approach.** It does not build separation tolerance; it creates trauma.
3. **Focus on one relationship.** Pick the person who will provide care and help that specific relationship develop.
4. **The goal is comfort during distress, not prevention of distress.** Crying with support is different from crying alone.
5. **Use cognitive preparation tools.** Homemade books and repeated verbal messages help even pre-verbal children understand what is coming.
6. **Leverage comfort objects.** They provide continuity and additional soothing options.
7. **Emphasize reunion.** The message "Mommy always comes back" is the anchor.
