# Handling Aggression from Other Children: Helping Your Child Find Their Voice

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/handling-aggression-from-kids-at-the-playground

---

## Core Philosophy: Responding to Aggression with Love

When another child acts aggressively toward your child, the goal is not simply to protect your child in the moment, but to help them develop the ability to stick up for themselves without attacking others. This requires staying grounded in your own dignity and compassion while dealing with unhappy people. The underlying belief is that by adding to the love rather than the pain, both you and your child help transform the world.

We cannot fully protect our children from aggressive encounters with other children. Since these incidents will inevitably happen, the practical focus should be on equipping children to handle them constructively.

---

## The Six-Step Approach for Playground Aggression

### Step 1: Model an Appropriate Response to the Other Child

**Why this works:** Most people who act rudely simply want their position acknowledged. Once they feel understood, they feel less threatened and therefore become less threatening. This principle applies to children as much as adults.

**The technique:** Verbalize what you think the other child might be feeling and what they seem to want.

**Example in action:** When a 3-year-old says "We will kill you" to your toddler who is playing in puddles, you might respond: "You sound mad! Are you saying that you don't want other kids playing in the puddles?"

Then follow up by acknowledging their perspective while offering reassurance: "I guess it's hard to share the puddles, isn't it? Don't worry, there are plenty of puddles for all of us to enjoy!"

**Expected outcomes:** The more kind and empathic you can be, the more the other child will soften. They might even smile and begin playing with you. At minimum, most children will shrug and move on once they feel heard.

**Important nuance:** You would not normally engage with an enraged stranger. However, when the aggressor is a child who appears to have some reason to feel unhappy, acknowledging their point of view can often resolve the situation.

---

### Step 2: Reassure Your Child That They Are Safe

Turn to your child and explicitly state that you will keep them safe. Example language: "She's mad that we're playing in the puddle... Don't worry... I will keep everyone safe... There are plenty of puddles for everyone."

**Key insight:** It is fine, even beneficial, if the other child overhears this reassurance. The reason is that children who act aggressively are often scared by feeling at the mercy of their own aggression. Hearing that an adult will "keep everyone safe" can be reassuring to the aggressive child as well.

---

### Step 3: Set Limits if the Behavior Continues or Escalates

If the other child says something like "No! These are OUR puddles!" or moves physically toward your child, you need to take protective action.

**Physical positioning:** Step between the two children to create a physical barrier.

**Verbal response:** Restate your limit in a voice that is both strong and kind. The combination matters because you are modeling how to be firm without being aggressive.

**Example language:** "I know it's hard to share, AND these are EVERYONE's puddles... We will stay over here so you can have those puddles where you were playing before."

**Why the "AND" matters:** The word "AND" acknowledges the other child's difficulty while maintaining the limit. This is different from "BUT" which tends to dismiss what came before it.

---

### Step 4: Support Your Child to Find Their Voice

When another child physically infringes on your child (pushing, grabbing something), give your child explicit permission to speak up for themselves.

**Critical nuance:** Do this without acting like it is an emergency. Your calm demeanor teaches your child that while the situation requires a response, it is manageable.

**How to do it:** Offer your child specific words they can use. Example: "Are you okay with that? You can say 'Don't push my body' or 'Hey, I'm playing with this... I will give it to you when I'm done.'"

**Why scripting matters:** Young children often do not know what to say in these moments. Giving them exact phrases to use empowers them to respond.

---

### Step 5: Appeal to the Accompanying Adult if Necessary

**How to approach:** Smile at them and directly appeal for them to step in. The goal is to enlist their cooperation, not criticize their parenting.

**What to avoid:** Do not get into a position of fighting with the other adult or implying that they are doing a bad job.

**Example language:** "It sounds like your girl loves these puddles and needs a puddle of her own! Our family is using this puddle right now. We'll be happy to let her have this puddle once we're done with it."

**Expected outcome:** At this point, most adults will move their child to another location.

**On teaching other parents:** Should you explicitly "teach" the other adult better parenting? The answer is that you already are teaching them. **Modeling is always the strongest teaching. Love is always the strongest teacher.** Demonstrating calm, kind, and effective responses teaches far more than criticism would.

---

### Step 6: Help Your Child Process Their Feelings Afterward

Being the target of someone else's aggression is scary for a child. After the other family has left, create space for your child to process what happened.

**Opening the conversation:** "That was hard, wasn't it?... I think that girl must have been having a tough day! What did you think?"

**Role-playing for empowerment:** If your child seems upset, role-play the scenario so they can experience saying assertive things they may not have said in the moment. Example: Have them practice saying "Excuse me?! I was playing in that puddle! You can have it as soon as I'm done." Encourage them to use their strongest voice.

**Why role-play helps:** The child gets the experience of being assertive in a safe, supported context. This builds the neural pathways and confidence for responding differently next time.

**Using laughter to dispel anxiety:** After processing, help your child laugh about the incident to release any lingering anxiety. You can do this playfully, such as by asking if you can share their puddle or inviting them to share yours. Physical play like splashing together helps discharge the tension.

---

## Key Principles Underlying This Approach

**Acknowledge before redirecting:** People (including children) feel less threatened when their position is acknowledged. This makes them less likely to continue aggressive behavior.

**Safety is foundational:** Children need to know they are safe before they can learn from a situation. Reassurance comes before skill-building.

**Model strength without aggression:** Being firm and kind simultaneously shows children that they do not have to choose between being assertive and being compassionate.

**Give children tools, not just protection:** Protecting your child in the moment is not enough. The goal is to equip them with language and skills they can use independently.

**Processing matters:** Children need help making sense of difficult encounters. This happens through conversation, role-play, and physical release of tension.

**Perspective-taking transforms situations:** Acknowledging that the aggressive child "must have been having a tough day" helps your own child develop empathy while making sense of the other child's behavior.

---

## The Bigger Picture

The approach described here does more than just handle the immediate situation. It operates on the belief that how we respond to aggression shapes the world. By demonstrating compassion rather than retaliation, by adding to the love rather than the pain, parents help their children become people who make the world a better place "just by being themselves."

The aggressive child is not portrayed as a villain but as someone who is struggling. This framing matters because it shapes how your child will view difficult people throughout their life - not as threats to be defeated but as people who may be suffering and need understanding.
