# How to Provide Reassurance and Stability When Things Go Wrong

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/when-things-go-wrong

## Key Concepts

### The Parental Foundation
- Events beyond our control (elections, personal challenges, tragedies, wars) create stress, anxiety, anger, or disappointment
- Parents who cannot manage their own overwhelm cannot be present for their children
- Children need reassurance from emotionally regulated adults
- These moments are opportunities to model self-nurture and resilience

### The Four-Step Framework

#### 1. Manage Your Own Emotions
- Children take emotional cues from parents; strong reactions signal danger and reduce children's sense of safety
- Self-regulation strategies:
  - **Limit media exposure** - Constant news and social media amplify anxiety; periodic check-ins are sufficient
  - **Breathe and ground** - Deep breaths signal no emergency to the nervous system; sensory grounding (see, hear, touch, smell, taste) returns focus to the present
  - **Physical reassurance** - Big hugs help regulate a dysregulated nervous system after shock
  - **Social connection** - Talking with supportive people helps process emotions and reduces isolation
  - **Self-compassion** - Acknowledge emotions kindly rather than judging them
  - **Attention management** - Focus on gratitude and goodness despite difficult circumstances; what you focus on grows

#### 2. Reassure Your Child
- Children need to know their world is safe and adults are handling the situation
- It is not the child's job to worry about crises
- Communication approach:
  - **Start with open-ended questions** - Ask what they have heard or how they are feeling to understand their perspective and potential misconceptions
  - **Listen and reflect** - Paraphrase their thoughts and feelings without interrupting to show understanding
  - **Correct misinformation** - Gently address inaccurate conclusions; avoid conjectures about the future
  - **Admit uncertainty** - It is acceptable to say you do not know; model how to fact-check and evaluate sources together
  - **Prioritize safety reassurance** - Regardless of the situation, emphasize that you will keep them safe and the family will find a way through

#### 3. Model Resilience and Values
- Children learn resilience by observing parental responses to challenges
- Techniques:
  - **Acknowledge emotions calmly** - Share feelings like disappointment while demonstrating emotional management
  - **Focus on controllables** - Demonstrate agency through quality time, helping others, and positive choices at home
  - **Share values** - Express how your values guide your response to the current moment
  - **Take positive action** - Do something constructive to improve the world, even if you need to regroup first

#### 4. Provide Safety and Stability
- Turbulent external circumstances increase the importance of internal home stability
- This supports the child's nervous system in relaxing so development can continue
- Strategies:
  - **Maintain routines** - Predictability of daily life reinforces normalcy
  - **Increase emotional generosity** - Children who feel unsafe will act out; they need to "feel better" to be their best selves, while boundaries remain consistent
  - **Prioritize connection activities** - Games, reading together, conversation reinforce family safety and support

## Core Principles

1. **Self-regulation precedes co-regulation** - Parents must manage their own emotions before helping children with theirs
2. **Children mirror parental calm** - Emotional reactions from adults signal danger to children
3. **Safety is the message** - Beyond information, children need reassurance that they are protected
4. **It is not the child's responsibility** - Adults handle crises; children should not carry that worry
5. **Stability counters uncertainty** - Home routines and connection provide anchoring during external chaos
6. **Values guide response** - Difficult moments are opportunities to live and demonstrate family values

## Practical Insights

- Media consumption during crises should be intentionally limited rather than constant
- Physical touch (hugs) serves a nervous system regulation function, not just emotional comfort
- Children often draw alarming conclusions from overheard information; listening reveals these misconceptions
- Saying "I don't know" followed by collaborative fact-checking teaches critical thinking
- Even teenagers benefit from explicit safety reassurance during uncertain times
- Acting out behavior during stressful periods reflects emotional dysregulation, not defiance
- Modeling self-kindness during difficulty teaches children how to treat themselves during hardship
- Constant parental love provides security regardless of external circumstances
