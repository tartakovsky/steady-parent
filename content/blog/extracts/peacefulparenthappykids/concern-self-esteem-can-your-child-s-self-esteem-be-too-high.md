# Can Your Child's Self-Esteem Be Too High?

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/concern-self-esteem-can-your-child-s-self-esteem-be-too-high

---

## Core Thesis: There Is No Such Thing as Self-Esteem That's "Too High"

The central argument is that genuine **high self-esteem** cannot be excessive. Self-esteem means seeing yourself as good and capable, secure in your value and worthiness. The concern parents have about "too high" self-esteem reflects a confusion between self-esteem and something else entirely: **grandiosity**.

**Grandiosity is the opposite of self-esteem, not an extreme form of it.** Grandiosity manifests as an over-inflated view of one's abilities or a conviction of being more important than others. This arises from insecurity, not confidence. Low self-esteem can express itself as self-deprecating behavior, but more commonly shows up as arrogance or pridefulness, a need to believe we are better than others. This is a defense mechanism against the deep fear of not being good enough, which creates a compulsion to constantly measure against others and come out ahead.

**People with high self-esteem don't need to compare themselves to others or inflate their abilities because they already feel they are "more than enough, just exactly as they are."** They are secure in their sense of value without external validation or competition.

Any psychological measure that identifies traits like entitlement, superiority, narcissism, or being "full of themselves" is measuring grandiosity, not self-esteem. These traits indicate the opposite of healthy self-esteem.

## The Myth That High Self-Esteem Reduces Effort

Some parents fear that a child with high self-esteem will stop trying at school, sports, piano, or other pursuits. **This is a myth.**

When children genuinely love an activity (a sport, instrument, or learning), they practice and hone their skills naturally. They don't require bribes, badgering, or being made to feel they're falling short. Intrinsic motivation from loving the activity drives continued effort.

However, when parents pressure children to achieve, the child often begins to feel they aren't good enough. This terrible internal feeling triggers defensive responses:

- **They may stop trying altogether** (avoiding the activity that creates the painful feeling)
- **They may become so competitive they need to put others down** to feel good about themselves
- **They may exaggerate accomplishments** (e.g., "I made 30 baskets at practice!")

**The cure is ensuring your child knows they are MORE than enough, exactly as they are.** Communicate confidence in their ability to practice, learn, grow, and improve at whatever they choose. Growth is exciting, and parents should share excitement about achievements. But those achievements should be framed as a natural unfolding that develops from the child's love of applying themselves to their interests.

**Critical framing:** There is nothing the child could do to make you more proud or to get more love from you. You could never love anyone or anything more than you love them. This removes the conditional quality that creates pressure and defensiveness.

## Two Components of Self-Esteem

Self-esteem consists of two distinct components that must both be developed:

1. **Goodness** - The sense that you are of value, regardless of what you do, regardless of whether you succeed or fail
2. **Capability** - The sense that you can meet your needs and achieve your own aspirations

---

## Component 1: Goodness - Developing Stable Internal Happiness

The core of self-esteem is **"Stable Internal Happiness,"** a term coined by **Martha Heineman Pieper**.

While some people have natural tendencies toward better moods and more optimism, stable internal happiness can be fostered in any child through unconditional love. **Simply telling children we love them is not sufficient.** The child must **feel** that she is loved unconditionally.

### What Unconditional Love Parenting Looks Like

#### 1. Parenting that communicates the child is appreciated and adored exactly as they are

The child knows she inspires love just by being herself. She doesn't have to prove herself, work harder, or be better behaved. It doesn't matter if she wins or loses, succeeds or fails. **She knows that you could never love her more or less than you do now.** This removes any transactional quality from the parent-child relationship.

#### 2. Parenting that is responsive to this unique child's needs and emotions

**Responsiveness to a child's needs is one of the most important predictors for raising a child who feels safe and valued.**

Key elements of responsiveness:
- Stay connected even while the child becomes increasingly independent
- Accept and affirm all of who the child is, including messy, challenging, human needs and feelings

**The challenge:** All parents encounter times when staying positive seems almost impossible - with a tantruming toddler, recalcitrant eight-year-old, or rude preteen. Parents are tempted to withdraw into anger.

**Why withdrawing fails:**
- Giving a child the cold shoulder doesn't teach anything positive about building relationships
- It teaches the child that your love is conditional on acting a certain way
- **When kids are at their least lovable is when they need our love the most**

**The key to being responsive:** Ask yourself what support your child needs to meet your expectations. Then give that support. This reframes discipline as support rather than punishment.

#### 3. Parenting that stays connected while guiding

**Punishment always undermines self-esteem.**

Children need limits. No, he can't pee on the rug, run around in the restaurant, call his mother a poopyface, or hit his brother. But **how** those limits are set matters enormously.

**Setting limits with empathy** helps kids learn to manage their emotions and therefore their behavior. Example: "You're mad! Tell me in words. Hitting is never okay." This approach helps children see themselves as good and capable.

**Punishment, by contrast:**
- Does not help kids learn to manage their emotions
- Worsens the tangle of angry feelings they already can't control
- Makes them feel like bad people who can't manage themselves, much less the world
- Children experience punishment as a parent intentionally hurting them (physically or emotionally)
- No matter what a parent tells the child, punishment doesn't make the child feel loved
- On some level, children interpret punishment as evidence that they aren't good enough to cause their parent to love them

The mechanism: Punishment damages self-esteem because it communicates rejection and intentional harm, which children internalize as evidence of their own unworthiness.

#### 4. Parenting that gives lots of unconditional positive encouragement

Affirmation, encouragement, and acknowledgment are essential for children to feel seen, heard, respected, appreciated, and valued - all part of developing healthy self-esteem.

**Critical distinction - what encouragement does NOT mean:**
- It does NOT mean praising a child for traits or abilities they don't have
- **Praise for specific traits (as opposed to specific behavior) seems to undermine self-esteem**

**Example of trait-based praise failing:**
Telling a child he's smart puts pressure on him to always be smart. This is impossible to consistently achieve, so it creates insecurity. The child becomes anxious about maintaining the label rather than confident in their abilities.

**Example of behavior-based acknowledgment succeeding:**
Acknowledging that a child has figured something out by himself reinforces his sense of himself as someone who is smart and capable enough to figure things out. The focus is on the action and capability demonstrated, not on a fixed trait.

### Outcomes of Stable Internal Happiness

Kids who experience unconditional love and acceptance develop stable internal happiness early, possibly by age ten or twelve.

**How stable internal happiness manifests:**
- Setbacks from the outside world (lost ball games, flubbed tests, even family moves that leave friends behind) throw them for much briefer times than other kids
- They return more quickly to their normal happy state
- They have greater resilience

**Reality check:** Only a handful of lucky people develop this early. Many don't reach this state until their twenties. Others work their whole lives to get there.

### Why Unconditional Love Matters

The reason to unconditionally love children and appreciate who they are is that it helps them accept and appreciate themselves. This creates a cascade of benefits:
- Confers happiness
- Develops the ability to love others
- Gives children resilience to pursue their goals and meet their needs
- Greater success in pursuing goals creates additional self-esteem (and happiness)

This creates a positive feedback loop: unconditional love -> self-acceptance -> resilience -> goal achievement -> more self-esteem.

## Component 2: Confidence - Experiencing Capability

**As a child grows, he needs to experience himself as capable of meeting his needs and successfully pursuing his goals.**

Self-esteem begins with unconditional love, which has nothing to do with accomplishment. The unconditional love provides the stable internal happiness foundation that enables the child to pursue and accomplish goals.

### The Philosophical Framework

All humans have intrinsic value as human beings, not because of what we accomplish, but merely because of who we are. But who we are can only be perceived by what we do, how we interact with our environment.

**All humans find themselves tested by their environments.** We all have:
- Tasks to master throughout our lives
- Growth that is demanded of us
- Practice and training required
- Hurdles and tests to face

This is what shapes who we are, what brings our selves into expression. Self-esteem requires feeling loved, but it also requires feeling capable.

### Secondary Self-Esteem Through Confidence

**Secondary self-esteem** comes from the confidence of knowing, deep inside, that we can take care of ourselves and meet our needs, that we have what it takes to bring our dreams into reality.

The sequence matters: Self-esteem starts with feeling loved because **only kids who feel completely loved are able to tackle and master hard things.** But it also requires the person to feel they can take care of themselves and achieve their desires.

### The Parent's Role in Building Capability

Building capability means tackling hard things. As parents, this means encouraging children to do some hard things.

**Critical distinction:** This must be about the child pursuing his own goals.

**Healthy example:** "Dad really supports me to work hard at my soccer because he knows it's important to me."

**Unhealthy example:** "Dad loves me more if I score a goal." This represents the parent making the child work hard at goals the parent has set, which shades into conditional love.

### Three Parenting Styles That Undermine Capability

**Tiger Parenting** - Pushing a child inappropriately. This makes kids worry that they aren't good enough.

**Helicopter Parenting** - Rescuing children or doing things for them. This robs the child of opportunities to develop confidence in their own capability.

**Snowplow Parenting** - Moving all obstacles out of the child's way. Like helicoptering, this removes the challenges that would build capability and confidence.

### The Right Approach: Targeted Support for Self-Directed Goals

**Parenting for high self-esteem means stepping back, paying attention, and giving your child targeted support to develop his own competence and his own feelings of being capable and powerful.**

This is distinct from either pushing (tiger) or removing challenges (helicopter/snowplow). It involves:
- Stepping back to allow the child to face challenges
- Paying attention to what the child actually needs
- Providing targeted support rather than taking over
- Supporting the child's development of their own competence
- Fostering feelings of being capable and powerful

### Mastery

Children who are able to apply themselves in pursuit of a goal are developing what is called **Mastery.** This is the culmination of the capability component of self-esteem - the demonstrated ability to pursue and achieve self-directed goals through sustained effort.

## Key Quotation

From **Alfie Kohn:** "Unconditionality...a solid core of belief in yourself, an abiding sense that you're competent and worthwhile - even when you screw up or fall short - that creates a more stable form of self-esteem, which, in turn, carries a range of psychological and social advantages."

This captures the essence of the framework: unconditional self-regard creates stability, which creates psychological and social benefits. The key word is "unconditionality" - the self-esteem is not contingent on performance or outcomes.
