# Don't Get Hijacked: Taking the High Road in Parenting

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/parent-mindful

---

## The High Road vs. Low Road Framework

Dan Siegel's concept: "When emotional reactions replace mindfulness, you're on the low road and it is very unlikely that you will be able to maintain nurturing communication and connection with your child."

**The High Road** is the state you're in when you're feeling good. Nothing fazes you. You respond to your child's difficulties with patience, understanding, and a sense of humor. From this elevated perspective, you can see your child's point of view, and compassion naturally emerges.

**The Low Road** is the state you're in when you're stressed, exhausted, or resentful. On the low road, you insist on being right, you demand apologies from your child, your fuse is extremely short, and you feel justified in having your own tantrum. You're in the grip of fight-or-flight emotions, so your child starts to look like the enemy rather than someone you love.

The key insight about the high road: when we're in a state of love, not much upsets us. By contrast, when we're on the low road, everything upsets us. This means what bothers you intensely today might make you shrug a year from now - the difference isn't the behavior, it's your internal state.

---

## The Root Cause of Low Road Reactions

All challenging emotions that flood us and push us onto the low road can be traced to four core feelings at their source:
1. **Fear**
2. **Shame**
3. **Powerlessness**
4. **Grief**

These feelings are so unbearable that we can't tolerate them. So instead of experiencing them, we start blaming and lashing out. We become resentful, angry, disappointed, and disconnected - both from our child and from our own ability to love.

The critical insight: Yes, we're reacting to our child's behavior. But the reason we rage is so we won't have to feel those unbearable underlying emotions. The anger is a defense mechanism against experiencing the deeper pain.

This doesn't mean there aren't valid reasons to get upset. It means there are far fewer valid reasons than we typically believe. The threshold for what "should" upset us shifts dramatically based on our internal state.

## Strategy 1: Use Your Pause Button

**Your number one parenting responsibility** is monitoring your own emotions so you can regulate yourself when facing childish behavior. This enables you to use the **Stop, Drop, Breathe** technique:

- **Stop** what you're doing
- **Drop** your agenda (just for this moment)
- **Breathe** (this signals to your body that it isn't an emergency)

Why this works: Simply bringing awareness to your thoughts and emotions is enough to prevent you from being in their grip. You shift from being controlled by the emotion to observing it.

**What "bringing awareness" means practically:** Notice what's happening in your body right now. Every time you take a deep breath and feel the sensations in your body, you're practicing mindfulness. You're pressing the pause button so you aren't merely getting triggered. This creates choice - you get to decide which road to take.

**Support practice:** A regular meditation practice makes it significantly easier to find the pause button when you're upset. The skill of noticing and pausing strengthens with practice outside of conflict situations.

## Strategy 2: Accept Feelings and Take Loving Action BEFORE Getting Mad

The typical pattern that leads to the low road: We tolerate behavior that bothers us. We try to be patient, but we're gritting our teeth. So we get increasingly annoyed until we finally get hijacked by our anger.

The solution: Practice accepting feelings while taking loving action to set limits - before you're near the edge.

### Three Response Patterns Compared

**Pattern 1: Tolerating without action**
Example thought: "I wish she wouldn't call her sister names."

What's happening: You're tolerating behavior that violates your values without accepting the child's feelings or taking action.

Why this fails:
- No limit is set
- The child doesn't get help with the feelings driving the unkind behavior
- It makes you resentful of your child
- It makes you more prone to snap later

**Pattern 2: Reacting without helping**
Example response: "Stop that name calling right this minute or you'll get punished!"

What's happening: You're indulging your own reaction without helping the child with the feelings that drove the behavior.

Why this fails (even though a limit is technically set):
- The response escalates the problem
- The response reinforces the problematic dynamic
- The child now blames their sibling for the punishment
- The child feels angry at your unfairness and doesn't want to cooperate
- The child gets no help with their underlying feelings

**Pattern 3: The effective approach**
Example response: "You sound mad! The rule in this house is that we speak to each other with respect, and no name calling. You can tell your sister what you're mad about without attacking her."

What this accomplishes:
- Sets a clear limit on behavior
- Avoids threats or punishment
- Accepts and acknowledges the child's feelings
- Guides the child to express feelings directly
- Shows the child how to communicate without hurtful behavior

## Strategy 3: Don't Get Hijacked by the Low Road

**The warning sign to watch for:** When you notice you're shaking with anger, that is NOT a signal that your child needs to be taught a lesson right now. It's a red warning flag telling you to STOP because you're getting hijacked by your upset and sliding down onto the low road.

**The immediate response:** Disengage from your child.

**The internal work:** Shift yourself out of being triggered by:
1. Noticing the emotion
2. Resisting the urge to act on it
3. Breathing through it
4. Recognizing that you aren't that emotion - you are observing that emotion
5. Understanding it will pass
6. Melting the rage away by letting yourself feel the fear, sadness, and disappointment that lies underneath the anger

**Long-term healing:** If getting hijacked happens often, give yourself whatever support you need to heal your own issues. This is framed as universal - "who doesn't have issues?"

## Strategy 4: Remember That the Low Road Never Leads Where You Want to Go

From the low road, your child appears so clearly wrong. But the wider view available from the high road reveals your child's perspective, and compassion naturally emerges.

**The common scenario:** Your child is objectively, totally, completely off-track. This happens frequently to young humans because they have big feelings and immature brains. It's developmentally normal.

**The critical truth:** Your child can only join you on a better path if you're reaching out from the high road. You cannot lead them to better behavior from the low road.

**What never helps a child become a better person:**
- Blame
- Shame
- Anger
- Criticism

The rhetorical challenge: Do these things help you become a better parent? (Implied answer: No.)

**The compass:** Your heart. Getting in touch with your love always gets you back on the high road.

---

## Strategy 5: Consciously Choose the High Road

**The signal that a child needs help:** Children who feel ugly inside act ugly. When you see ugly behavior, recognize it as a signal that the child needs your help, not your punishment.

**The choice you always have:** Will you join your child on the low road and escalate the upset? Or will you embrace them with your love so they can get back onto the high road with you?

**Realistic expectations:** You can't live on the high road all the time if you're human. Nobody takes the high road constantly.

**The growth process:** The more you get used to choosing the high road, the faster you'll notice when you're off-track. You're actually rewiring your brain through this practice.

**The simple (but hard) truth:** It's difficult, yes, but it isn't complicated.
- The high road is love
- The low road is fear
- Choose love as often as you can
- Choose it unconditionally

## Key Concepts Summary

**Emotional monitoring as primary responsibility:** Parenting starts with managing your own emotional state, not managing your child's behavior.

**Observation creates choice:** By noticing your emotions rather than being consumed by them, you create space to choose your response.

**Underlying emotions drive surface reactions:** Anger is typically a cover for fear, shame, powerlessness, or grief. Addressing the surface anger without reaching the underlying emotion doesn't resolve the pattern.

**Limits plus empathy:** Effective parenting combines clear behavioral limits with acceptance of the child's emotional experience.

**State determines perception:** The same child behavior will seem intolerable or manageable depending on whether you're on the high or low road. The behavior isn't the problem - your state is the variable.

**Neuroplasticity through practice:** Choosing the high road repeatedly actually rewires your brain, making future high-road responses more accessible.

**Ugliness signals need:** Children acting badly are communicating that they feel bad inside and need help, not punishment.
