# Helping a Young Child Cope with a Parent's Suicide

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/helping-child-with-fathers-suicide

---

## Core Principle: The Child's Wellbeing Depends on the Parent's Healing

**The child will be fine as long as the surviving parent is fine.** This is the foundational insight - a young child's ability to process grief and trauma is directly tied to the emotional state of their primary caregiver. The parent's healing is not separate from helping the child; it IS helping the child.

This means that prioritizing your own mental health and emotional processing is not selfish - it is the single most important thing you can do for your child. A parent who is drowning in unprocessed trauma, guilt, and anger cannot provide the stable emotional foundation a grieving child needs.

## Working Through Your Own Trauma First

### Why Professional Support is Essential

Witnessing a spouse's death by suicide, especially finding the body, constitutes **major trauma**. This is explicitly described as "not something you can cope with alone." The recommendation is clear: seek professional counseling support.

The parent in this example is dealing with multiple layers:
- The visual trauma of finding the deceased spouse
- Guilt (which is common in suicide survivors)
- Anger (also common and valid)
- Grief for the lost relationship and future

All of these must be processed because they will inevitably affect how the parent interacts with and supports the child. An emotionally dysregulated parent cannot consistently provide the calm, secure presence a grieving toddler needs.

## Age-Appropriate Approaches for Very Young Children (Around Age 2-3)

### What a Toddler Can Understand

At 2.5 years old, a child:
- Will repeatedly ask if the deceased parent will come back (this is developmentally normal, not a sign of confusion)
- Can understand simple, concrete explanations
- Benefits from consistency in messaging
- Needs the surviving parent's emotional presence more than perfect explanations

### Books as a Tool for Processing

Reading books about death and loss with the child serves multiple purposes:
1. **Introduces concepts gradually** - The child encounters the idea of death in a structured, age-appropriate way
2. **Creates opportunities for conversation** - Books provide natural openings to talk about the deceased parent
3. **Benefits the parent too** - Reading these books is healing for the adult as well
4. **Normalizes the experience** - Seeing characters deal with loss helps the child feel less alone

The specific recommendation is to explore books designed to explain death to children.

## Creating a Personalized Memory Book

### Timing Matters

The memory book project should wait until **the surviving parent has regained emotional equilibrium**. Attempting this too soon, while still in acute grief and trauma, could:
- Be overwhelming for the parent
- Transfer unprocessed emotions to the child
- Result in a book that reflects raw pain rather than love and acceptance

### Structure of the Memory Book

The suggested format moves through a narrative arc:

1. **Connection and love**: Photos showing the child with the deceased parent, with captions like "Daddy loved [child's name] very much"

2. **Explanation of what happened**: Age-appropriate language about the death - "Daddy had an illness that made him very sad, so sad he did not want to live." This framing:
   - Presents suicide as the result of an illness (which depression is)
   - Avoids blame
   - Is honest without graphic details
   - Gives the child a framework for understanding

3. **Concrete reality of death**: A photo at the grave site with simple explanation - "When Daddy died, we buried him." This:
   - Makes death concrete and real (important for young children who struggle with abstract concepts)
   - Shows the child that death is permanent
   - Provides a physical place associated with the deceased

4. **Emotional acknowledgment**: A photo of parent and child together, hugging - "We miss Daddy, and we will always love him." This:
   - Validates the grief
   - Shows that love continues after death
   - Models that it's okay to miss the person
   - Demonstrates connection between the survivors

5. **Positive remembrance**: A photo of the deceased smiling, with specific positive memories - "We remember him for the things we loved about him: his sense of humor, his love of music, how much he loved us." This:
   - Ends on connection rather than loss
   - Gives the child positive associations with the parent
   - Models healthy remembrance

### How to Use the Memory Book

- Read it whenever the child wants (following the child's lead)
- Read it on significant dates like the father's birthday
- Consider rituals like lighting a candle on the birthday

## The Critical Importance of Speaking Positively About the Deceased

**Whatever the surviving parent's own feelings about how the spouse ended their life, they must find ways to talk positively to the child about their father.**

This is emphasized because:
- The child's identity is partly formed through their relationship to both parents
- Negative messages about the deceased parent become internalized as negative messages about the self ("I am part of someone bad")
- The child deserves to have positive memories and feelings about their parent
- The surviving parent's anger and complicated feelings should be processed with a counselor, not transferred to the child

This does not mean lying or pretending the death didn't happen the way it did. It means focusing on the love, the positive qualities, and the relationship when talking to the child.

## Framework for Explaining Suicide to Young Children

The language suggested - "Daddy had an illness that made him very sad, so sad he did not want to live" - demonstrates a specific approach:

1. **Frame it as illness**: Depression and mental illness are framed as medical conditions, not character flaws or choices
2. **Keep it simple**: A toddler doesn't need details about the mechanism of death
3. **Be honest about the outcome**: "Did not want to live" acknowledges the reality without graphic specifics
4. **Avoid euphemisms that confuse**: Don't say things like "went to sleep" or "went away" which could create fear of sleep or abandonment anxiety

## The Role of Ritual and Ongoing Remembrance

Creating ongoing rituals helps both parent and child:
- Reading the memory book on birthdays
- Lighting a candle
- Visiting the grave

These rituals:
- Keep the deceased parent as part of the family story
- Give the child permission to remember and feel
- Provide structured ways to process ongoing grief
- Create moments where talking about the deceased is expected and welcomed

## What the Surviving Parent Needs

The closing wish is for "healing and forgiveness, for yourself and your husband." This acknowledges:

- **Self-forgiveness** is needed - survivors of suicide often carry guilt ("Could I have prevented this? Did I miss signs?")
- **Forgiveness of the deceased** may also be needed - there can be anger at being left behind, at the manner of death, at the impact on the child
- **Healing** is a process that takes time and support

This emotional work must happen for the parent's sake and for the child's sake, as the two are intertwined.
