# Building on Your Parenting Strengths

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/whats-great-about-your-parenting

---

## Core Philosophy: Strengths-Based Parenting Growth

The fundamental premise is that you don't need to be a perfect parent - you need to build on what you already do well. Transformation happens not by focusing on failures, but by identifying and amplifying existing strengths. This approach works because appreciation lifts mood and creates emotional generosity, which naturally leads to better parenting moments.

The key insight: **"You don't have to be a perfect parent at all, to be the perfect parent for your child."** Perfection is impossible for humans, so the goal is continuous growth from a foundation of self-appreciation rather than self-criticism.

---

## Step 1: Create a Written Strengths Inventory

Make a physical written list of what you're most proud of about yourself as a parent. The writing matters - it creates tangible evidence you can return to.

**What to include:**

- Things no one else ever notices (the invisible labor of parenting)
- Things that look simple from outside but take extra effort for you specifically (acknowledging your personal challenges)
- **Moments when you don't completely pull it off, but notice things going downhill mid-interaction and turn things around** - these are described as your **"growing edge, where transformation happens"**

The growing edge concept is significant: catching yourself mid-mistake and course-correcting is itself a parenting strength, not a failure. These moments represent active growth in real-time.

**Target numbers:** At least 10 items minimum, with 20-30 being better. The high number forces you past obvious items into deeper appreciation.

**Examples of strengths given:**
- Being a good listener
- Keeping things organized so family life runs smoothly
- Losing it sometimes but always apologizing and reconnecting afterward
- Creating joyful moments like family dance parties that make children laugh

---

## Step 2: Practice Self-Appreciation

Read through your list and actively give yourself appreciation for every item.

**Handling the inner critic:** Your inner critic will likely offer counterpoints about what you don't do well. The response is:
1. Acknowledge you're human and perfection is impossible
2. Offer yourself compassion for this limitation
3. Let go of what you're still working on - for now, just for today
4. Focus entirely on what you do right

**Why this works:** Appreciating yourself lifts mood and helps you feel more emotionally generous. When you feel appreciated (even by yourself), you have more emotional resources to give to your children.

---

## Step 3: Identify Your Personal Success Conditions

Consider what helps you be at your best as a parent. This is diagnostic work to understand your own patterns.

**Important reframe about children's behavior:** While it's tempting to say "I'm a better parent when my child behaves," children will reliably exhibit childish behavior - you can count on it. And honestly, a lot of how kids act depends on how parents are showing up with them. So the question shifts from "how do I get my child to behave" to "what conditions help ME show up well?"

**Three diagnostic questions:**
1. What helps you be your personal best as a parent, even when your child isn't at his or her best?
2. What support would help you be that parent more often?
3. How can you give yourself that support every single day, until it becomes a habit?

The progression matters: first identify what works, then identify what's missing, then build a daily habit to provide it.

---

## Step 4: The Three R's Framework for Difficult Moments

When you have moments you don't feel good about, use the **Three R's: Reflect, Repair, Responsibility.**

**Critical mindset:** Resist the urge to beat up on yourself. Use difficult moments as motivation to do better, not as evidence of failure. The cheerleader metaphor: "That's alright, that's okay, we're gonna do it anyway!"

### Reflect
- Think about what happened
- Replay in your mind how you WISH you had handled things
- This mental rehearsal alerts your subconscious to your intention for next time
- Offer yourself compassion - parenting is hard and everyone messes up sometimes

The mental replay is a form of visualization that programs future responses.

### Repair
- Repair the relationship with your child after difficult moments
- This includes apologizing and reconnecting (referenced as a separate detailed process)

### Responsibility
- Take responsibility to give yourself whatever support you need so you can do better next time
- This connects back to Step 3's diagnostic questions

**After the Three R's:** Let those tough moments go and move on to better interactions. Don't dwell.

---

## Step 5: Become Your Own Cheerleader

Give yourself an internal hug or high five every time you notice yourself acting toward your child in a way that makes you feel good about yourself.

**Addressing the "swelled head" concern:** This isn't arrogance - it means you're working hard to be your best and valuing that effort. Self-appreciation for effort is distinct from unearned ego.

**The cycle of positive effects:**
1. Appreciating yourself makes you happier with how you parent
2. You find you enjoy parenting more
3. Children notice the difference (watch for: commenting on changes, cooperating more, becoming more affectionate)
4. You're role modeling for your child how they can grow, love, and be their own personal best

**Unique insight:** You're not just parenting better - you're teaching your child self-appreciation and growth through your example.

## Supporting Quotes and Principles

**Jack Canfield on self-appreciation:** "By taking the time to stop and appreciate who you are and what you've achieved - and perhaps learned through a few mistakes, stumbles and losses - you actually can enhance everything about you. Self-acknowledgment and appreciation are what give you the insights and awareness to move forward."

This frames self-appreciation not as self-indulgence but as the foundation for insight and forward movement.

**Abraham-Hicks on appreciation:** "Appreciation and self-love are the most important tools that you could ever nurture. Appreciation of others, and appreciation of yourself, is the closest vibrational match to your Source Energy."

This positions self-appreciation as fundamental, not optional.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Transformation happens at the "growing edge" - those moments when you catch yourself mid-mistake and course-correct
- Self-criticism depletes emotional resources; self-appreciation replenishes them
- Children's behavior often reflects how parents are showing up
- The goal is not perfection but continuous growth from existing strengths
- Visualizing how you wish you'd handled something programs better future responses
- Self-appreciation is role modeling - children learn to value themselves by watching you value yourself
