# Five Research-Backed Principles for Raising Great Kids

Source: Peaceful Parent, Happy Kids (Dr. Laura Markham)

This article synthesizes decades of longitudinal research tracking children from babyhood to adulthood to identify what actually works for raising self-disciplined, resilient, and happy adults.

---

## 1. Secure Attachment as the Foundation for Everything Else

### What Secure Attachment Is and How It Develops

**Secure attachment** forms when children experience consistent emotional responsiveness from at least one loving adult. This attachment develops through:

- **Listening to the unique baby** and responding to their specific needs in the first year
- **Accepting the full range of who the child is** - including the messy parts like neediness and anger - throughout toddlerhood, childhood, and the teen years
- Being warm and nurturing so children feel safe and soothed in your presence

The core requirement is **meeting the child's needs rather than acting from your own needs** - whether those parental needs are to control, to keep the child from getting upset, or to avoid conflict.

### Why Secure Attachment Matters for Discipline and Influence

The close relationship created through secure attachment is what motivates children to cooperate with their parents and accept their rules and role-modeling. This is the crucial insight: **without that bond, parents lose their influence as soon as children begin interacting with peers**. Children who lack secure attachment will seek to satisfy those unmet needs through their peer relationships instead, making parents' guidance irrelevant.

### Attachment Practices vs. Attachment Security

Do you need to practice formal "attachment parenting" (baby-wearing, co-sleeping, nursing) to raise a securely attached child? No. Before these specific practices became popular in the US, approximately **60% of toddlers** were still securely attached. What determines attachment security is the parent's **emotional responsiveness to the child's needs**, not specific parenting practices.

However, many parents report that attachment practices increase their responsiveness, and research is beginning to confirm this - at least for baby-wearing. Attachment parents can and should still set limits; attachment parenting is not permissive parenting.

## 2. Self-Discipline Comes From Limits With Empathy

### The Problem With Permissive Parenting

Children raised without limits don't get opportunities to practice self-discipline. They don't necessarily learn to:
- Be considerate of others
- Manage themselves through unpleasant tasks

This is why permissive parenting can produce undisciplined kids - not because limits themselves are harmful, but because the absence of limits deprives children of practice opportunities.

### The Problem With Authoritarian Parenting

Here's the crucial nuance: if limits are imposed in ways that provoke resistance (example: "Don't you sass me, young lady!"), the child still doesn't learn self-discipline. The reason: **the child doesn't internally accept the limit**.

When a limit feels harsh or unfair:
- Kids don't actually learn self-discipline
- They can't manage themselves without external discipline
- They become more susceptible to peer pressure (because they never developed internal regulation)

### Why Punishment Undermines Self-Discipline

All punishment undermines self-discipline. The common assumption that time-outs teach responsibility is wrong. A child sitting on the naughty step is not "taking responsibility and considering how to be a better kid." They are doing what any normal human would do:
- Reviewing why they were justified in their behavior
- Plotting revenge

This is not learning self-discipline; this is building resentment.

### How Empathic Limits Actually Work

When limits are imposed with empathy - example: "I see you're mad! Shoes are not for throwing, no matter how mad you are... Tell me in words!" - kids may not like the limit, but **they don't get stuck in resistance**.

The mechanism:
1. Child feels understood, supported, and connected
2. This connection makes them willing to live with the limit
3. The parent also accepts the child's upset about the limit (this is important - the child can be mad about the limit)
4. Child builds self-discipline by stopping themselves from pursuing what they want because there's something they want even more: **a good relationship with you**

### The Deeper Lesson Beyond Compliance

Through empathic limits, the child learns: "I can't always get my way, but I get something better - someone who loves me exactly as I am."

This unconditional positive regard becomes:
- The core of unshakable positive self-esteem
- The foundation of stable internal happiness
- The basis of resilience

## 3. Self-Soothing Is Learned Through Being Soothed

### The Neuroscience of Self-Soothing

**Self-soothing** develops because the neural pathways that release soothing biochemicals are formed when the baby is soothed by the parent. This is not metaphorical - the physical brain structures that enable calm are built through the experience of being calmed by another person.

### Why "Cry It Out" Backfires

Leaving little ones alone with their big emotions does NOT teach them to self-soothe. Instead, it makes them shut down, creating a nervous system that has to work harder for them to calm themselves throughout their lives. The child who learns to stop crying when left alone has not learned to self-soothe - they have learned to suppress.

### Which Children Need Extra Support

Children who are explosive, anxious, or "dramatic" need extra support in the form of parental calming. They also need safe opportunities to show their emotions (see emotion-coaching section below).

### Why Self-Soothing Matters

The skill of self-soothing is essential for children to learn to manage their:
- Anxiety
- Emotions
- Behavior

This self-soothing only develops from being **"emotionally held" by a calm adult**. The parent's calm presence is the template the child's nervous system learns from.

## 4. Emotion-Coaching Teaches Emotional Management

### What the Research Shows

Decades of research show clearly that when parents emotion-coach (instead of shutting down emotions), kids are healthier and more successful in every measurable way.

### The Four Components of Emotion-Coaching

**Emotion-coaching** means:
1. The parent **notices** the child's emotions
2. The parent **sees emotions as an opportunity** for intimacy, reassurance, or teaching (rather than as a problem to fix or suppress)
3. The parent **acknowledges the child's perspective and empathizes**
4. Once the child has had a chance to express the emotion, the parent **supports the child to problem-solve**

### Why Emotion-Coaching Works

The parent helps the child feel safe enough to feel the emotions, so they can be experienced and begin to dissipate. Through this process, the child learns that:
- Emotions aren't dangerous
- Emotions can be managed

### The Signal Behind "Bad" Behavior

Kids who are uncooperative, angry, or fearful aren't choosing to be uncooperative. They're signaling that they need help with big feelings they can't manage. To move past those feelings, they have to go through them - suppression doesn't work.

The parent's loving presence is what helps the child feel safe enough to let themselves feel the emotions and let them go.

### Children Who Can "Feel" Don't Store Up Feelings

Children who know their feelings are "allowed" don't store them up. This is why they're better able to manage their emotions and behavior - they're not dealing with accumulated unexpressed emotion on top of current challenges.

### What Acting Out Tells You

If you're connecting with your child and setting limits with plenty of empathy, and your child is still acting out, she's signaling that she needs help with her emotions. The behavior is the symptom; the unexpressed emotions are the cause.

## 5. Children Learn What They Live

### The Principle of Modeling

This is the simplest principle to state and often the hardest to implement:
- If we **delight** in our child, they learn they are of value
- If we're **considerate and respectful** to a child, they become respectful, considerate people
- If we **yell** at them, they learn to yell, and they'll be yelling back at us by the time they're eight

### The Origin of Rudeness

Kids who are rude and disrespectful learned it somewhere. However, if they bring rudeness into the house from outside and we politely remind them that we don't relate that way, they don't adopt that style. The key is our consistent modeling of how we want them to behave.

### The Ultimate Factor

Ultimately, what your children experience as they grow up with you will depend on who you are, and that will be more important than any parenting philosophy. The techniques matter, but they flow from the parent's own emotional regulation and values.

---

## The Hard Truth About This Approach

This kind of parenting requires you to manage your own emotions. That's the hardest work there is. The approach is simple to understand but demanding to implement because it requires the parent to do their own emotional work.

---

## The Generational Impact

Giving your child a good start in life means sending ripples for generations into the future - not just your children, but their children, and their children, and their children. The investment in becoming a regulated, responsive parent pays compounding dividends across generations.
