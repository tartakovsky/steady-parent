# Ten Tips to Become a More Peaceful Parent

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/use-positive-discipline

---

## The Three Interlocking Components of Peaceful Parenting

Peaceful parenting operates as an integrated system with three components that work together, not in isolation:

**1. The parent commits to regulating their own emotions.** This is the foundation because parents are always radiating how they feel. Children pick up on parental emotional states and respond accordingly. Children cannot calm down when parents are dysregulated. This makes emotional self-regulation a prerequisite for everything else.

**2. The parent prioritizes maintaining and strengthening the parent-child connection.** This is positioned as "the only reason children cooperate." Connection is not a nice-to-have but the core mechanism that makes cooperation possible.

**3. The parent coaches the child rather than using control or manipulation.** This specifically excludes "love withdrawal" and other punishment techniques like yelling, threats, punishment, or rewards. Instead, the parent offers loving guidance and emotion coaching so the child learns to manage emotions, and therefore behavior. The causal chain here is explicit: emotional management leads to behavioral management.

## Why Peaceful Parenting Instead of Conventional Approaches

**Conventional parenting relies on punishment**, which produces three specific negative outcomes:
- Undermines the parent-child relationship
- Leaves kids feeling worse about themselves, and they often act worse as a result (feeling bad about self -> acting badly)
- Interferes with the development of true self-discipline

**Peaceful parenting** helps children feel safe, valued, and understood. This enables them to:
- Regulate themselves
- Cooperate
- Grow into their best selves

## Tip 1: Start With Regulating Your Own Emotions

The reasoning: You need to be the patient, emotionally generous parent you aspire to be, and that every child deserves. You are always radiating how you feel. Your kids pick that up and respond accordingly. They cannot calm down when you are dysregulated.

**Practical actions:**
- Take responsibility to notice your own moods
- Practice strategies to move yourself back into well-being when you get out of sorts
- **Self-compassion is highlighted as "one of your most reliable tools"**
- Remember to take care of yourself

The sequence matters: Your regulation comes first because it enables everything else.

---

## Tip 2: Evaluate All Teaching Based on Whether It Strengthens or Weakens Your Relationship

**Core principle:** The most effective discipline strategy is having a close bond with your child. Kids who feel connected to their parents naturally want to please them.

**Framework: Loving Guidance vs. Punishment**

**Punishment** is destructive to your relationship with your child and ultimately creates more misbehavior. This is a key insight: punishment does not just fail to help, it actively makes behavior worse by damaging the relationship that makes cooperation possible.

**Loving guidance** is defined as setting limits and reinforcing expectations as necessary, but in an empathic way. The empathic framing helps the child focus on improving their behavior rather than on being angry at the parent.

This creates a test for any intervention: Does this strengthen or weaken our relationship?

---

## Tip 3: Start All Correction or Redirection by Reaffirming the Connection

**The underlying principle:** Children misbehave when they feel bad about themselves and disconnected from us. Therefore, correction must begin with reconnection.

**Concrete techniques with examples:**

- **Stoop down to her level and look her in the eye:** "You want your brother to move, so you pushed him. No pushing; pushing hurts! Tell your brother, 'Move please!'"

- **Pick her up:** "You wish you could play longer but it's time for bed."

- **Make loving eye contact:** "You are so upset right now."

- **Put your hand on her shoulder:** "You're scared to tell me about the cookie."

Each of these examples combines physical connection (positioning, touch, eye contact) with verbal acknowledgment of the child's internal state.

---

## Tip 4: Set Limits With Empathy

**Key distinction:** You still need to enforce your rules. The shift is not about removing limits but about how you hold them.

**Why this works:** When kids feel understood, they're more able to accept our limits. Understanding is the prerequisite for acceptance.

**Example scripts that combine firmness with empathy:**

- "You're very, very mad and hurt. And still, no biting! Tell your brother what you want."

- "You wish you could play longer, and now it's bedtime. I know that makes you sad."

- "I hear that you don't want Mommy to say No, but the answer is still No. We don't say 'Shut Up' to each other. It's ok to be sad and mad, and you can tell me how you feel. I will always understand and try to help."

- "You are scared, but we always tell the truth to each other."

**Pattern in these examples:** Each one first acknowledges the child's emotional state or desire, then states the limit clearly, and often offers an alternative acceptable action.

---

## Tip 5: In Physical Danger Situations, Intervene AND Connect Simultaneously

When physical safety is at stake, you must act immediately to set limits. But even in these moments, you can simultaneously connect by empathizing.

**Example:** "The rule is no hitting. You can tell your sister what you want and how you feel without attacking her."

The limit is stated clearly (no hitting), and an alternative is offered (tell her what you want and feel).

---

## Tip 6: Defiance Is Always a Relationship Problem

**Key insight:** When a child does not accept your direction ("I don't care what you say, you can't make me!"), this is always an indication that the relationship is not strong enough to support the teaching.

This happens to all of us from time to time. The critical response is to stop and think about how to strengthen the relationship, not how to make the child "mind."

**Warning:** Turning the situation into a power struggle will just deepen the rift between you. The temptation to escalate and force compliance is counterproductive because it further damages the relationship that makes cooperation possible.

---

## Tip 7: Avoid Timeouts - They Create More Misbehavior

**Timeouts are positioned as "another version of punishment by banishment and humiliation,"** even though they are infinitely better than hitting your child.

**Why timeouts fail:**
- They leave kids alone to manage their tangled-up emotions, which undermines emotional intelligence
- They erode rather than strengthen your relationship with your child
- They set up a power struggle
- They only work while you're bigger (not building internal motivation)

**Strong characterization:** Timeouts are described as "a more humane form of bullying than physical discipline."

---

## Tip 8: Consequences Teach the Wrong Lesson When Parents Engineer Them

**The conceptual distinction:**

**Natural consequences** (when the parent is not involved) can be a terrific learning experience. Examples:
- If they don't study and flunk their test
- If they don't brush and get a cavity

However, there are caveats even with natural consequences:
- You need to be able to handle the bad result
- You don't want it to happen more than once, or their self-image becomes that of a person who flunks tests and gets cavities - they have learned an unintended lesson

**Parent-engineered consequences** are different. Most kids whose parents use "consequences" don't think of them as the natural result of their own actions ("I forgot my lunch today so I was hungry"), but as threats they hear through their parents' clenched teeth: "If I have to stop this car and come back there, there will be CONSEQUENCES!!"

**The core problem:** If parents are in charge of consequences, then the consequences aren't the natural result of the child's actions, but simply punishment.

**Effectiveness assessment:** To the degree that consequences are seen as punishment by kids - and they almost always are - they are not as effective as positive discipline to encourage good behavior. Using them on your kids should be considered a last resort and a signal that you need to come up with another strategy.

## Tip 9: What You Think and Feel Is More Important Than What You Say

**Core principle:** Kids will do almost anything we request if we make the request with a loving heart.

**Technique: Find a way to say YES instead of NO even while you set your limit.**

**Extended example demonstrating the "YES" approach:**

"YES, it's time to clean up, and YES I will help you and YES we can leave your tower up and YES you can growl about it and YES if we hurry we can read an extra story and YES we can make this fun and YES I adore you and YES how did I get so lucky to be your parent? YES!"

The child will respond with the generosity of spirit that matches yours. This is a reciprocity principle: your emotional orientation shapes their response.

---

## Tip 10: How You Treat Your Child Is How They Will Learn to Treat Themselves

**The internalization principle:**
- If you're harsh with her, she'll be harsh with herself
- If you're loving with her while firm about setting appropriate limits, she'll develop the ability to set firm but loving limits on her own behavior

**Why this matters beyond just happiness:**

Harsh discipline and punishment, ironically, interfere with the child's ability to develop self-discipline. The problem with internalizing harshness is not just that it makes for unhappy kids and eventually unhappy adults - it is that it does not work.

**Strong claim:** Kids who are given discipline that is not loving never learn to manage themselves constructively.

**How internalized harshness manifests in adults:**

To the degree that we're harsh with ourselves because of the way we were parented, we respond to it by:
- **Rebelling** (how many times do we cheat on our diets?)
- **Martyring ourselves** (trying hard to be good girls and boys but building up resentment and lashing out at those we love, or not giving ourselves a break and ultimately breaking down)

**The positive alternative:**

To the degree that we can accept our own loving guidance because we've learned from our parents to treat ourselves that way, we are able to set goals and use our self-discipline to attain them.

**Ultimate outcome:** Loving guidance and peaceful parenting result in the child developing what is described as "the holy grail toward which all child-raising is aimed: the child's own self-discipline."

## Meta-Guidance on Implementation

This process takes time, and you can expect to make mistakes because you're human. But every step in the right direction makes it easier. This acknowledges that perfection is not the goal and that incremental progress compounds over time.
