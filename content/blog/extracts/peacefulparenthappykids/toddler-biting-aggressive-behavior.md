# Understanding and Responding to Toddler Biting and Hitting

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/toddler-biting-aggressive-behavior

## Core Reframe: Why Toddlers Bite and Hit

### It Is Not About Attention or Being Spoiled

A common parental interpretation is that biting and hitting are done "for attention" or because the child is "spoiled." This framing is incorrect and leads to ineffective responses. The child is not biting because they have too much attention or because they enjoy causing pain. Even children who receive abundant one-on-one time and constant engagement will exhibit these behaviors when they have unmet emotional needs.

### The Real Cause: Unexpressed Feelings

**Toddlers bite and hit because they need something emotionally that they cannot yet articulate or process.** The behavior is a signal that difficult feelings are building up inside the child. These feelings need an outlet. When the child cannot express them through words or tears, the tension manifests as physical aggression.

### Why the Child Laughs

When a toddler hits or bites and then laughs, parents often interpret this as enjoyment or mockery. **The laughter is actually a tension-release mechanism.** The child is not laughing because they find the parent's pain funny. They are laughing because the body is trying to discharge the uncomfortable emotional tension that led to the aggressive act in the first place. This is an important distinction because misreading the laugh as enjoyment leads parents to think the child is being defiant or manipulative, when in fact the child is struggling with overwhelming feelings.

## Why the Standard Approach Fails

### The Typical Response Pattern

A common parental approach involves:
1. Taking the child's hands and holding them down
2. Using a stern voice
3. Saying "NO, we do not hit, you need to use gentle hands"
4. Demonstrating gentle touching
5. Repeating the correction when the behavior recurs

### What This Approach Gets Right

Setting a firm limit is correct. The child needs to understand that hitting and biting are not acceptable behaviors. Stopping the child physically when possible, and always responding to the behavior, are appropriate actions.

### What This Approach Misses

**The limit-setting addresses the behavior but not the underlying cause.** The approach tells the child what they cannot do, but it does not help them with the feelings that are driving them to hit and bite in the first place. Because the emotional pressure remains unaddressed, the behavior continues. The child will keep trying to release the tension through physical aggression because they have no other outlet.

The sternness itself can be counterproductive. A stern voice may cause the child to shut down emotionally rather than open up. The child needs to feel safe enough to access and express the difficult feelings beneath the behavior.

## The Recommended Approach

### Step 1: Prevent When Possible

Try to avoid being bitten or hit when you can see it coming. This is not always possible, but when the parent notices the buildup, intervening before the hit lands is preferable.

### Step 2: Stop the Behavior

Always respond by physically stopping the child when they hit or bite. The limit must be consistent. The child needs to know that this behavior will always be interrupted.

### Step 3: Shift from Stern to Kind

Instead of using a stern voice, use a **kind voice**. The purpose of the kind voice is to help the child feel safe enough to get in touch with whatever feelings are beneath the hitting. A stern voice triggers defensiveness; a kind voice creates the emotional safety needed for the child to access vulnerable feelings.

### Step 4: Make Eye Contact

Look into the child's eyes while setting the limit. **The child will likely try to avoid eye contact.** This avoidance is significant. It indicates that the child is trying to keep the difficult feelings at bay. When parents insist on gentle, caring eye contact, it breaks through the child's emotional defenses.

### Step 5: Invite the Feelings

Use language that invites the child to express what is going on internally. An example phrase: **"You have some big feelings in there you need to show me. Look at me, Sweetie. I am right here. You are safe."**

This language accomplishes several things:
- It names that feelings exist ("big feelings")
- It communicates that the parent wants to see those feelings ("you need to show me")
- It offers connection ("I am right here")
- It provides safety ("You are safe")

### Step 6: Allow the Tears

If the parent kindly and clearly insists that hitting is not okay while simultaneously offering emotional presence and safety, **the child will almost certainly begin to cry.**

The laugh or eye avoidance may come first, but with gentle persistence, the tears will follow. **The cry is the goal.** The tears are how the child releases the emotional pressure that was driving the aggressive behavior. A good cry allows the feelings to come out, and once they are out, the child will not need to hit anymore because the internal pressure has been relieved.

## Key Mechanisms

### Aggression as Emotional Overflow

The hitting and biting are symptoms of emotional pressure with no acceptable outlet. The child does not have the verbal skills or emotional regulation capacity to process difficult feelings on their own. The feelings build until they overflow into physical action.

### Laughter and Eye Avoidance as Defenses

Both laughing and avoiding eye contact are ways the child keeps the vulnerable feelings locked inside. They are protective mechanisms. The child may not consciously choose these behaviors, but they function to prevent the deeper feelings from surfacing. The parent's job is to gently but firmly move past these defenses.

### Tears as Release

Crying is the natural mechanism for emotional release in young children. When a child can cry in the presence of a safe, connected adult, the feelings move through and out. This is why the approach aims for tears rather than compliance. Compliance without emotional release just postpones the next aggressive outburst.

### Safety Enables Vulnerability

The child will only access and express vulnerable feelings if they feel safe enough to do so. Sternness, punishment, or disconnection make the child feel less safe, which causes them to hold the feelings in more tightly. Kindness, presence, and eye contact create the conditions where the child can let go.

## What to Avoid

### Do Not Interpret Laughter as Enjoyment or Manipulation

Treating the child's laugh as evidence that they find hitting funny or are trying to provoke will lead to harsher responses that make the problem worse.

### Do Not Rely Solely on Limit-Setting Without Emotional Connection

Telling the child "no" and demonstrating gentle hands addresses only the surface behavior. Without addressing the feelings underneath, the behavior will persist.

### Do Not Use a Stern Voice as the Primary Response

Sternness may feel appropriate given the seriousness of hitting, but it closes the child down emotionally when the goal is to open them up.

### Do Not Avoid Eye Contact or Allow the Child to Avoid It

Eye contact is the mechanism that breaks through the child's defenses. Allowing avoidance leaves the emotional pressure intact.

## Summary of the Intervention Sequence

1. Prevent the hit/bite if possible
2. Stop the behavior when it occurs
3. Hold a clear limit (hitting is not okay)
4. Use a kind voice rather than a stern one
5. Seek and maintain eye contact
6. Name the feelings and invite their expression
7. Offer safety and presence
8. Allow and support the resulting tears
9. The emotional release resolves the pressure that was driving the aggression
