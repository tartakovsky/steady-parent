# Losing Patience with Adopted Kids

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/losing-patience-with-adopted-kids

---

## Core Context: Why Adoptive Parenting Requires a Different Approach

Newly adopted children, particularly those who are older (ages 6-7 in this case), come with **substantial baggage** from their previous life experiences. This creates a fundamentally different parenting situation compared to raising children from birth. The key insight is that **connecting without history takes time**, and for children to connect back requires **healing on the part of the kids** first. This means parents cannot expect normal bonding timelines or typical child behavior patterns.

The neediness these children display is **understandable** given their circumstances. No matter how much attention they receive, they want more - this is not a character flaw but a symptom of attachment wounds that need to heal. The exhaustion parents feel (both emotional and physical) is a natural consequence of trying to meet needs that feel bottomless.

---

## The Support Infrastructure Requirement

**This is definitely not something you can do alone.** The work of parenting adopted children is described as "the work of the angels" - meaning it is both noble and extraordinarily demanding. The emphasis on support is not optional but essential to sustainability.

### Specific Support Mechanisms

**Adoptive family support groups** provide connection with others who understand the unique challenges. These communities offer both practical advice and emotional validation that friends and family without adoption experience cannot provide.

**Spousal support rotation** means trading off with your partner so that **both parents get some R&R time daily**. This is not occasional relief but a daily requirement built into the family routine.

**Extended family involvement** through grandparents or godparents (if possible) should be structured to give the parents time to connect with each other. The recommendation is specifically **a few hours each weekend** for the couple to reconnect. The relationship between the parents needs maintenance alongside the parent-child relationships.

---

## The One-on-One Connection Strategy

**Each child needs time alone with each parent every day.** This is a structural recommendation, not a nice-to-have. The reasoning is that attachment forms through individual attention, and children who have experienced loss or disruption need consistent evidence that they matter as individuals to each parent specifically.

## Work and Capacity Considerations

There is an explicit acknowledgment that **it would be very hard to hold a job and offer the parenting these kids need**. The statement that "there is no way to recharge your batteries in that situation" makes clear that trying to maintain full work responsibilities while parenting newly adopted children with attachment needs creates an unsustainable situation.

**Giving your kids what they need is a full-time job for now** - the "for now" is important, suggesting this is a phase rather than a permanent state, but one that demands full attention during the critical attachment period.

## Expectation Management as Self-Care

**Offer yourself empathy.** The framework here is recognizing that when a child enters a family, there is always a huge adjustment period. When **two kids** join simultaneously, and they arrive with substantial baggage, the adjustment is exponentially more demanding.

The critical insight: **Life can't possibly go on as usual. Everything has to change.** This is not a failure but an inevitable reality. The stress everyone feels is proportional to the magnitude of change, not a sign that something is going wrong.

**Adjusting expectations about what should be happening** is framed as a practical tool that will help. The gap between "how things should be" and "how things actually are" creates suffering - closing that gap through realistic expectations reduces distress without changing external circumstances.

---

## The Daily Recharge Requirement

Implementing a **daily family routine that guarantees alone time every single evening** is essential for parental sustainability. This is not flexible - the word "every single evening" emphasizes consistency.

### What Recharge Time Should NOT Include

**Resist the urge to fold laundry or do paperwork.** Productive tasks that feel like accomplishing something are actually draining in this context. The temptation to use "free time" for catching up on tasks undermines the purpose of recharging.

### What Recharge Time Should Include

Call it a **daily mini-vacation** and ask yourself: **"What would nurture me at this moment?"** Specific examples given:
- A bubble bath
- Calling a friend
- Reading a novel

The common thread is activities that fill your emotional tank rather than complete obligations.

---

## The "Do Over" Technique for Patience Recovery

When you find yourself losing patience with your kids, the recommended intervention is a **"do over"**:

1. **Stop** - physically and mentally pause the current interaction
2. **Take a deep breath** - physiological regulation before attempting cognitive reframing
3. **Ask yourself: "What's most important here?"**

The insight that follows this question is consistently the same: **The only thing that ever REALLY matters is love.** The emphasis on "REALLY" and the capitalization indicate this is the ultimate filtering question that cuts through whatever immediate frustration is occurring.

**Loving yourself, your kids, and your husband has to be at the top of your list, no matter what.** The inclusion of self-love first is significant - depleted parents cannot give from an empty cup. The order (self -> children -> spouse) suggests a priority of sustainability.

**Everything else is secondary.** This framing helps parents release attachment to outcomes, behaviors, timelines, or expectations that are causing the patience loss in the first place.

---

## Key Warnings and Anti-Patterns

**Trying to do this alone** is explicitly identified as unsustainable. The cultural narrative of self-sufficient parenting is dangerous in the context of adoptive families with traumatized children.

**Maintaining pre-adoption life patterns** is unrealistic. Everything has to change - attempting to preserve "normal" creates conflict with reality.

**Using recharge time for productivity** defeats the purpose. The urge to accomplish tasks is strong but counterproductive.

**Expecting immediate connection** ignores that the children need to heal before they can fully bond. Time and consistency are required, not just effort.

## Underlying Philosophy

The response acknowledges the difficulty and nobility of adoptive parenting simultaneously. There is no minimizing of the challenge, but also no catastrophizing. The tone suggests that with proper support structures, realistic expectations, and self-care practices, the family can navigate this transition successfully.

The emphasis throughout is on **sustainability through support** rather than **heroic individual effort**. The parent's wellbeing is treated as a prerequisite for the children's flourishing, not a competing priority.
