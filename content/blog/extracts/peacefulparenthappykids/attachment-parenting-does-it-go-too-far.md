# Attachment Parenting - Does it Go Too Far?

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/attachment-parenting-does-it-go-too-far

## Summary

This Q&A exchange between a parent (Ingrid) and Dr. Laura Markham addresses concerns about attachment parenting being overly prescriptive. The parent pushes back against what she perceives as rigid attachment parenting rules while describing her modified approach that worked for her family. Dr. Markham responds with nuance, acknowledging that responsive parenting should be individualized while still citing research supporting certain practices.

## Key Parenting Insights

### Responsive Parenting Over Rigid Rules
- Good parenting attends to the needs of the individual baby, whether it is for solid food at five months or to be held
- Every baby is different and responsive parenting responds to the needs of that unique child
- There are no ironclad rules that all parents need to follow
- Parenting is an art, and every child is different

### Sleep Training Considerations
- Being left alone to cry is identified as a risk factor for babies
- Having a parent present to comfort during any crying mitigates potential harm
- Some children adapt easily to sleep training while others may not be ready
- Parents should know their limits and have clear boundaries about what is acceptable during sleep training

### Balance Between Structure and Responsiveness
- Children should not be "in charge" in their families
- The basic rule for raising great kids is to set limits, but to do it with empathy
- Attachment parents who are afraid to set limits do not represent the ideal approach

### What Research Supports
According to Dr. Markham, research supports these beneficial practices:
- Holding/wearing the baby frequently (though not necessarily all the time)
- Breastfeeding into the toddler years
- Co-sleeping
- Delaying solids until approximately six months
- Not letting babies cry alone
- Avoiding punitive discipline

### Nuanced Perspective on Attachment Parenting
- Babies don't need to be carried when they are happy to be put down
- Babies need tummy time to learn to use their muscles
- Following a baby's cues (like readiness for solid food) is more important than following strict timelines
- Co-sleeping works for some families but isn't necessary for all

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Individualize your approach**: What works for one family may not work for another
2. **Presence matters during distress**: If using sleep training, staying present to comfort reduces potential harm
3. **Set limits with empathy**: Structure and warmth are not mutually exclusive
4. **Read your baby's cues**: Developmental readiness varies between children
5. **Avoid extremes**: Neither rigid attachment parenting rules nor complete permissiveness serves children well

## Research Reference

The article references a Harvard study suggesting that being left alone to cry may create susceptibility to PTSD (requiring another trauma later in life to manifest) and may be a risk factor for panic and anxiety disorders. However, having a parent present to comfort changes this dynamic.

## Notable Quote

"All parents benefit from real discussion of these issues rather than sanctimonious judgments."
