# Why Consequences Don't Work for Aggressive Behaviors Like Hitting: A Better Approach

Source: Dr. Laura Markham, Peaceful Parent Happy Kids

---

## Core Principle: Hitting Is an Expression of Overwhelming Emotions, Not Willful Defiance

When a three-year-old hits parents or caregivers during a meltdown, this behavior is not a discipline problem requiring consequences. It is a **cry for help** with emotions the child cannot manage. The hitting happens because the child feels anguish that is unbearable to them, and they lack the emotional regulation skills to handle it any other way.

**Why young children hit:** When a three-year-old feels very upset - like the anguish of a parent leaving for work - he doesn't even know what he's feeling. He just feels overwhelming distress that is unbearable to him. So he lashes out. The hitting is not calculated misbehavior; it is the primitive response of an underdeveloped nervous system to emotional overwhelm.

---

## Why Time-Outs and Punishment Backfire

### What Happens Inside the Child During Time-Out

If you put a child in timeout after hitting, he will calm down externally. **But those feelings will still be inside, stuffed down, seething.** Because the feelings are not under conscious control, they will burst out at some other time. The underlying emotional distress is not resolved - it is merely suppressed.

### The Damaging Messages Punishment Sends

When a child is punished for hitting, the lessons he internalizes are not what parents intend:

- **"I'm a bad person... I have to be punished"** - The child identifies as fundamentally flawed rather than as a good person who had a difficult moment.

- **"I have feelings I don't understand, and I don't know how to manage them"** - Without help understanding and processing emotions, the child remains stuck with overwhelming feelings he cannot handle.

- **"My mom doesn't understand and she doesn't help me with those feelings"** - The parent-child connection is damaged rather than strengthened.

- **"I hurt people and I don't know how to stop... It just gets worse"** - The child feels helpless about his own behavior, which increases anxiety and makes future hitting more likely.

---

## The Effective Alternative: Emotional Safety and Processing

### Step 1: Stop the Hitting and Create Safety

The immediate response should establish a clear limit while creating emotional safety:

- **"I won't let you hit"** - A clear statement of the limit.
- **"I will keep everyone safe"** - Reassurance that the parent is in control of safety.
- **"You must feel so upset to hit like this"** - Acknowledgment that the hitting signals overwhelming distress.

This approach is firm about the behavior while compassionate about the underlying feelings.

### Step 2: Allow the Child to Cry and Process the Feelings

Once the child feels safe enough to move past the defensive anger, he will often cry. **This crying is exactly what he needs** - it allows the awful feelings to begin to evaporate. The parent should offer comfort during this crying rather than trying to stop it.

### Step 3: Help the Child Understand What Happened

After the child has calmed down and processed the emotions through crying, talking can happen:

- Connect the behavior to the feeling: **"You were so upset about Daddy leaving that you hit me, and also your Dad, and also your nanny."**
- Reinforce the limit clearly: **"Ouch! Hitting hurts. Hitting is never okay, no matter how upset you are."**
- Offer an alternative: **"Next time you feel upset, can you tell me? Can you say 'I'm mad!' or 'I'm sad!'? I will always understand."**
- Practice the alternative: **"Let's practice that right now, okay?"**

---

## The Critical Distinction: Repair, Not Punishment

### What Actually Teaches the Lesson

Parents often feel that aggressive behavior requires a "consequence" to teach a lesson. The concern is understandable - how will the child learn that hitting is wrong without some kind of punishment?

**The answer is repair.** Instead of punishment, the child is empowered to see the effect of his actions and to make things better. This teaches responsibility far more effectively than punishment.

### How to Facilitate Repair

After the child has calmed down (not before), help him understand the impact of his actions and take responsibility:

- **"When you hit Nanny, it hurt her body, and also her feelings."**
- **"I wonder what you could do to make things better?"**
- **"Could you draw her a picture? Give it to her with a big hug?"**

**Important:** This is not punishment. It is empowering the child to repair what he has broken.

---

## The Lessons Each Approach Teaches

### Lessons from Punishment (Time-Out, Consequences)

- I am bad
- I have uncontrollable feelings
- My parents don't understand me
- I hurt people and can't stop

### Lessons from the Empathy and Repair Approach

- **"I can always use words to say how I feel"** - The child learns that emotions can be communicated verbally rather than acted out physically.

- **"Hitting hurts and I don't want to do that"** - The child develops internal motivation to stop hitting rather than external fear of punishment.

- **"My mom always understands and I don't want to disappoint her"** - The connection with the parent becomes a positive motivator for good behavior.

- **"When I hurt a relationship, I can make things better"** - The child learns that mistakes can be repaired and that he has agency to fix problems he creates.

---

## Two Key Outcomes of the Recommended Approach

### First: Emotional Processing

When the child feels safe enough to experience the awful feelings - rather than stuffing them down through punishment or distracting from them - **the feelings begin to evaporate.** This is how human emotion works: once we allow ourselves to feel emotions fully, they dissipate naturally.

### Second: Emotional Understanding

Through the parent's empathic reflection ("You must feel so upset to hit like this") and later discussion ("You were so upset about Daddy leaving"), the child begins to understand his feelings. This is a step toward emotional regulation - you cannot regulate what you do not understand.

---

## Why This Approach Feels Counterintuitive to Parents

### The Fear of "Letting Them Get Away With It"

Many parents worry that responding with empathy instead of consequences means the child is "getting away with" the behavior. This concern conflates two different things:

1. **Permitting the behavior** (which the approach does not do - the limit is clear and immediate)
2. **Responding to the underlying need** (which the approach does - the emotional distress is addressed)

The child is not getting away with anything. He is being taught, through lived experience, that hitting is not okay AND that his feelings can be managed in better ways.

### The Desire for Immediate Behavioral Change

Punishment can produce immediate compliance, which feels like success. But the approach described here produces something more valuable: **actual emotional development** that prevents future hitting rather than merely suppressing it in the moment.

---

## Summary: The Sequence That Works

1. **In the moment:** Stop the hitting immediately and firmly. Acknowledge the overwhelming feelings. Create safety for the child to move from anger to the vulnerable feelings beneath.

2. **During the emotional release:** Allow the child to cry while offering comfort. The crying is the processing of the difficult emotions.

3. **After calm is restored:** Talk about what happened. Help the child understand the connection between his feelings and his behavior. Teach words for feelings. Practice alternatives.

4. **For relationship repair:** Help the child see the impact of his actions on others and empower him to make things better through his own actions (not through forced apology or punishment).

This approach addresses both the immediate behavior (which must be stopped) and the underlying cause (overwhelming emotions without adequate coping skills). The result is a child who learns emotional regulation, maintains a strong connection with caregivers, and develops internal motivation to treat others well.
