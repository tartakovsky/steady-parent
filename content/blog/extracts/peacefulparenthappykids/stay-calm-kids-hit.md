# Staying Calm When One Child Hurts the Other

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/stay-calm-kids-hit

---

## The Core Challenge: Why This Scenario Is So Hard for Parents

Most parents report that when one child hurts another, it is the **hardest time for them to self-regulate**. This difficulty is compounded when a parent has actively tried to intervene (like calling out "I'm coming to help") but the child still hurts the sibling before the parent arrives.

### Understanding the Child's Perspective

A four-year-old in this situation faces multiple challenges:
- **Cannot yet trust** that the parent will arrive in time to help
- **Cannot control impulses**, especially when feeling powerful and convinced they're right
- **Developmentally unable to see sibling's perspective** - part of being four is being certain you're right
- Experiences a sibling "wrecking his castle" as a competitor destroying something he worked really hard to create

The younger sibling (two-year-old) deserves protection, but this puts parents in an impossible position of needing to be in two places at once.

---

## Understanding Your Own Reaction: The Psychology of Parental Rage

### The Raw Truth About Your Instinct

When your child is hurt, your instinctual response is to become a "raging lunatic" who will do anything to protect them and destroy anyone who threatens them. This is a primal protective response.

### Why This Instinct Fails When the Aggressor Is Also Your Child

The neighbor-child thought experiment reveals the solution: If the neighbor's child threw your daughter to the ground, you wouldn't waste time screaming at him. You would fly to your child and help her. You would ignore the aggressor. Once you were calm, you would figure out how to protect your child from another incident.

### The Real Source of Your Rage

**The feeling of powerlessness is what drives the "losing it" reaction.** The rage makes you feel better because it counteracts the powerlessness. With siblings, you can't "build a fence to keep your own child out of your yard" - you can't simply separate them permanently, which increases frustration that you can't prevent these incidents despite trying so hard.

### What Your Rage Is Actually About

The rage at your son is a **defense mechanism** against something even more primal: your anguish that you haven't been able to protect one of your children. This is your most foundational responsibility as a parent. The authentic feeling underneath the rage is your deep need to keep your children safe.

## What Doesn't Work: Ineffective Responses

### Spanking
**Research shows spanking makes children more aggressive** - the exact opposite of what you're trying to achieve. (Citation: Gershoff, E.T., 2002, "Corporal punishment by parents and associated child behaviors and experiences: a meta-analytic and theoretical review," Psychological Bulletin)

### Shaming and Punishing
**Research and common sense indicate that shaming and punishment increase sibling rivalry and worsen the relationship.** (Citation: Brody, G. H., 1998, "Sibling Relationship Quality: Its Causes and Consequences," Annual Review Psychology)

### Expressing Rage Directly at the Child
If you tell your child you're furious at him for hurting his sister, he becomes confirmed in his fear that you don't love him - because of her. This puts him on the defensive and prevents him from being able to change.

---

## The Effective Framework: Expressing Need vs. Attacking

### The Key Distinction

**Express your fierce need to keep all your children from hurting each other** rather than attacking or blaming. This reframes the situation from "I'm mad at you" to "I have a need that affects everyone."

### The Script

"I need all of my children to be safe. I get so upset and angry when one of my children is hurt. I get even more upset and angry when one of my children hurts the other!"

### Why This Works

- You're clearly expressing anger - your child has no doubt you're angry
- Your voice can be passionate
- You haven't blamed your child
- He isn't on the defensive
- He's more open to taking responsibility for what he did
- He experiences your words as an expression of your need rather than an attack on him
- He becomes more likely to respond by wanting to meet your need

## The Underlying Solution: Prevention Through Connection

### Why Prevention Is the Only Real Solution

**The only thing that will stop such incidents from happening is prevention.** This includes:
- Helping your son with his jealousy
- Helping him with his panic when you're on the way to help but he isn't sure you'll get there in time
- Separating children during tough times of day when your son's internal resources are low

### The Connection Requirement

For your child to want to work with you on this problem, he needs to:
1. **Feel connected to you**
2. **Feel understood**

Empathize explicitly: "Sometimes it's hard to have a younger sibling."

Make connecting a priority. Once your child really believes that you couldn't possibly love anyone more than you love him, he doesn't need to be jealous of his sibling.

### The Crying Process

Your child may need to cry to work through all of the painful feelings that are driving him to hurt his sibling. This crying is therapeutic and helps process emotions.

---

## Practical Adaptations: The Impulse Control Analogy

### The Street-Crossing Comparison

You wouldn't expect a four-year-old to cross the street by himself because he doesn't yet have the impulse control to keep himself safe. Similarly, he doesn't yet have the impulse control to manage himself when a competitor destroys something he has worked hard to create.

### Temporary Environmental Solutions

If one child only seems to hurt the other when she touches his things, you may need a temporary solution to keep her away from them. This isn't giving in - it's matching your expectations to the child's developmental capacity.

---

## Summary: The Complete Response Sequence

1. **In the moment**: Go to the injured child first (like you would if a neighbor's kid caused the injury)
2. **Express your authentic need** (not your rage): "I need all of my children to be safe"
3. **Later, when calm**: Work on prevention
4. **Ongoing**: Build connection so the child doesn't need to be jealous
5. **Environmental**: Create temporary solutions that match the child's actual impulse control capacity
6. **Emotional processing**: Allow and support the child's crying to work through underlying painful feelings
