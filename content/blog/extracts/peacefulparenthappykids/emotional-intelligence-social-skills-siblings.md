# 7 Powerful Ways to Help Kids Develop Social Intelligence Skills Every Day

Source: Dr. Laura Markham, Clinical Psychologist, Peaceful Parent Happy Kids

## Core Conceptual Framework

**Social and emotional intelligence skills** are developed through practice, repetition, and daily experiences. Parents must explain, model, and repeat themselves continuously. The key insight: rather than viewing sibling conflicts as problems to eliminate, parents can welcome them as **teachable moments** that accelerate learning.

### The Learning Mechanism

Children learn social-emotional skills through:
1. Parental explanation and modeling
2. Repeated practice in real situations
3. Reflection on experiences without defensiveness

The sibling relationship provides a natural laboratory for this learning because conflicts arise frequently and create immediate opportunities for skill-building.

## Strategy 1: Talk About Feelings Daily

### The Core Practice

When parents talk to children on a daily basis about what everyone in the family feels and needs, siblings become:
- More sensitive and emotionally generous to each other
- More likely to understand each other's point of view

### Developmental Evidence

This works even with very young children. Research shows that when mothers talk to toddlers about what the baby might be feeling, the toddler develops more empathy for the baby and experiences less jealousy.

### Critical Sequencing Principle

**Always begin by empathizing with the older child first**. Only after establishing that connection can you wonder aloud about what the younger sibling might want or need. This cannot be delivered as a lecture; empathy must precede perspective-taking requests.

## Strategy 2: Ask Coaching Questions About Feelings, Needs, Wants, and Choices

### The Principle

People learn by having the chance to reflect, but only if they do not feel defensive. Questions that help children explore and discover work better than direct instruction.

### The Coaching Question Sequence

A powerful sequence of questions to use after conflicts:

1. "How did you feel?"
2. "What did you want?"
3. "What did you do?"
4. "How did that work out?"
5. "Did you get what you wanted?"
6. "Did your brother (or friend) get what he wanted?"
7. "How do you think he felt?"
8. "Would you do the same thing next time, or do you think you might try something different?"
9. "What do you think you might try?"
10. "What would happen then?"

### The Parent's Role During Questioning

- Listen actively
- Nod to show understanding
- Repeat back to confirm you understand
- Stay warm and non-judgmental
- Maintain your sense of humor

### Handling Provocative Responses

When a child says something like "Next time I'll smash him!", respond neutrally with curiosity: "Hmmm... what might happen then?" Do not jump in to evaluate or lecture.

### The Underlying Principle

**Reflection is how children develop judgment. Good judgment often develops from bad experience.** The questioning process allows children to discover consequences through mental simulation rather than requiring additional real-world mistakes.

## Strategy 3: Explain and Model (With Repetition)

### The Scripting Technique

Provide exact words for children to use in conflict situations:

**Example script for turn-taking:**
- To Child A: "Jake, say... 'Excuse me Sofia, when you're finished may I have a turn please?'"
- Wait for the child to repeat the words
- To Child B: "Sofia, say... 'Sure, Jake.'"

### Realistic Expectations

This must be done many, many times. The payoff comes when children eventually use these exact words unprompted to resolve issues themselves. This represents the internalization of the skill.

## Strategy 4: Practice Finding Win-Win Solutions

### Daily Opportunities

Every family encounters daily situations where needs differ. These represent opportunities for collaborative problem-solving practice.

### The Win-Win Prompt

When conflicts arise over differing preferences, pose the problem as a puzzle to solve together:

**Example**: "Hmmm... You want to go to the pool and he wants to go to the park... How can we find a win-win solution?"

This teaches children that conflicts are problems to be solved collaboratively rather than battles to be won.

## Strategy 5: Model "I" Statements

### The Core Distinction

**"I" statements** express what you need rather than judging or attacking someone else. They shift communication from accusation to self-expression.

### Transformation Example

- **Attacking statement**: "Well, you're stupid, too!"
- **"I" statement alternative**: "I don't like it when you call me names."

### The "I" Statement Formula (Marshall Rosenberg's Framework)

"I feel ______ because I want (or need) _______ and I observe that _______."

### Application With Children

After stating your feeling, need, and observation, provide a clear instruction rather than asking if the child is willing to comply. Asking implies compliance is optional, which is rarely accurate when a parent is upset.

**Complete example**: "I feel worried because I want to get there on time and I see that you aren't ready to leave yet... Please put on your shoes now."

## Strategy 6: Model Pro-Social Behavior

### The Environmental Influence

The way adults in the home relate to each other sets a powerful example for children. This can be leveraged intentionally through role-playing desired behaviors between adults.

### Modeling Examples

**Sharing model**: Say to your partner, "There's only one banana left; shall we split it?"

**Limit-setting model**: Say with a smile and a hug, "Excuse me, I was using that. You can have it as soon as I'm done!"

Children observe and internalize these interaction patterns, then apply them to peer and sibling relationships.

## Strategy 7: Teach Repair

### The Universal Need for Repair

In every relationship, there are times when someone feels hurt. Sometimes this is unintentional. Sometimes we knowingly prioritize our immediate needs over the other person's feelings. Even adults sometimes say hurtful things to partners.

### The Family Policy

Make it a family policy that there be a repair as soon as possible after someone is hurt, and definitely before bedtime.

### The Repair Prompt

**Script**: "Your brother was very upset that you knocked down his tower. I wonder what you could do to make things better with him?"

### Why Repair Is Not Punishment

The distinction matters for emotional outcomes:

- **Children who are punished** feel resentful
- **Children who aren't punished and don't repair** feel guilty
- Both categories blame the sibling

- **Children who repair** feel empowered
- The sibling relationship is strengthened

### The Psychological Mechanism

Once humans are no longer angry, they often look at the wreckage caused by their anger and wish they could make things better. Parents should help children get in this habit early.

### Hero Framing vs. Apology Extraction

Instead of trying to extract an apology (which makes the child feel like they "lost"), help them feel like a hero - someone who makes things better.

This reframing preserves dignity while achieving the relational repair goal.
