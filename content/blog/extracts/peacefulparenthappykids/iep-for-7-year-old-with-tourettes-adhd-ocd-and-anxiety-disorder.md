# Navigating IEPs for Children with Complex Diagnoses

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Dr. Laura Markham

## Context

This guidance addresses parents navigating the IEP (Individualized Education Program) process for a child with multiple diagnoses including Tourette's Syndrome, Generalized Anxiety Disorder, ADHD, and OCD. The specific scenario involves a child who was punished at school for behaviors related to these conditions (motor and vocal tics, compulsive wall-touching) even after the school was informed of the diagnoses, because formal paperwork had not yet been processed.

## Key Recommendations for IEP Navigation

### Get an Advocate

**Call the school and request a referral for an advocate.** This is the critical first step because an advocate will know what resources are available in your specific school district and educational system. Parents often don't know what accommodations exist or what they're entitled to request. The advocate fills this knowledge gap.

**Be persistent in getting this referral quickly.** Schools may not immediately provide this information, so follow up repeatedly until you have a name and contact.

### Position Yourself as a Full Team Member

**You are a full-fledged member of the IEP team**, not a passive recipient of decisions made by school personnel. This mindset shift is essential because:

1. You know your child better than anyone else on the team
2. The school professionals are resources for your son, but you are the expert on him as an individual
3. You have the right to understand and agree to everything in the plan

**Ask whatever questions you need** to fully understand all discussions and recommendations. There are no "dumb questions" when it comes to advocating for your child. If something is unclear, request clarification until you understand it completely.

**Do not agree to the IEP until you are comfortable with it.** You are not obligated to accept a plan during the meeting. Take time if needed to review, consult with your advocate, or request modifications.

### Address Emotional Harm from Prior Punishment

**If your child has been punished for behaviors related to their conditions, they may need counseling.** This is a critical but often overlooked consideration. When a child is repeatedly disciplined for things they cannot control (tics, compulsive behaviors), it can cause significant psychological harm including:

- Shame about their conditions
- Anxiety about school
- Damaged self-esteem
- Confusion about why they're being punished for something they can't help

**This counseling is typically not a standard part of an IEP**, but it should be pursued anyway. The advocate can provide referrals for appropriate counseling.

### Framing the Counseling Request to the School

**Frame the counseling as addressing the child's reaction to how the school handled his issues.** This is strategic for two reasons:

1. It establishes that the school bears some responsibility for the emotional harm
2. It creates a legitimate argument that the school should cover the expense

**Additionally, point out that counseling will help him behave in class in the future.** This gives the school a practical, outcome-focused reason to support and fund the counseling, rather than making it purely about remedying past mistakes.

## Specific Accommodations Mentioned

The evaluation report for this particular child recommended:

- **Occupational therapy** - to address sensory and motor needs
- **Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)** - particularly relevant for OCD and anxiety management

These are starting points that come from professional evaluation, but the IEP can and should include additional accommodations as needed.

## Underlying Principles

### The Gap Between Diagnosis and Accommodation

Schools may acknowledge a diagnosis verbally but resist formal accommodations until paperwork is complete. During this gap, children remain vulnerable to inappropriate discipline. This is why having an advocate and being assertive matters - institutional processes often move slowly unless pushed.

### Children Cannot Control Condition-Related Behaviors

Punishing a child for tics, compulsions, or other manifestations of neurological conditions is fundamentally inappropriate because these behaviors are not within the child's control. Understanding this distinction is essential for both school personnel and for protecting the child's emotional wellbeing.

### Parent as Expert

While school professionals have expertise in education and special services, parents have irreplaceable knowledge of their individual child. The IEP process works best when parents bring this expertise confidently to the table rather than deferring entirely to school staff.
