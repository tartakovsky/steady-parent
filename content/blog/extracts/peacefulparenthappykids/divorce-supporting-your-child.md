# Helping Your Child Cope with Divorce

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/divorce-supporting-your-child

## Core Framework: Divorce as Loss

Divorce is fundamentally a loss, regardless of any positives that may eventually result. This framing matters because it sets the appropriate emotional weight for how parents should approach the situation. The child experiences this as the death of their family, which means they need space to grieve in the same way they would grieve any significant death. Understanding divorce as loss rather than merely "change" or "transition" shapes every subsequent recommendation.

## How to Tell Your Child About Divorce

### The Joint Announcement Approach

Both parents should tell the child together. The reason for this is that it prevents the child from blaming just one parent for "breaking up the family." When both parents are present and share responsibility, the child sees the decision as mutual rather than one parent being the "villain."

### What to Communicate

**Present a united front on responsibility**: Tell the child that you both did your best to save the marriage and that the decision to live apart was made by both of you. This distributes responsibility and prevents the child from developing resentment toward one parent.

**Frame the future positively without apologizing**: Don't apologize for the decision. Instead, communicate that even though there may be a tough transition, the divorce will give everyone a better life in the long run. Apologizing signals that the decision might be wrong or reversible, which creates false hope and prolongs uncertainty.

**Reference conflict if visible, happiness if not**: If the child has witnessed fighting between parents, you can reference that as context for the decision. If they haven't seen conflict, simply say that you are happier living apart, which will make you better parents. The key is connecting the decision to something the child can understand.

**Emphasize continued family structure**: Stress that each parent remains in a family with the child, and that you will stay connected as the child's parents. Children need to understand that the parent-child relationship is not being divorced, only the marriage.

**Address self-blame directly**: Reassure the child explicitly that they are not responsible for the separation. Children often believe they caused family problems, so this needs direct verbal reassurance, not just implication.

**Close the door on reversibility**: Emphasize that this is a decision that will not be reconsidered. While this seems harsh, it prevents the child from spending emotional energy hoping for or trying to engineer a reconciliation. Clarity, even painful clarity, is kinder than prolonged uncertainty.

### Anticipate Concrete Questions

Children will have very practical concerns that need answers. Before the conversation, think through answers to questions like:
- Where will he sleep?
- Will both parents be at his birthday?
- If there is a pet, who will have the pet?

Having concrete answers prepared demonstrates that the child's practical world has been thought about and will remain stable.

### Maintain Stability in the Child's External Life

As much as possible, keep the child's life the same: home, bedroom, school, activities, friends. The reasoning is that during a period of massive internal upheaval, external stability provides an anchor. The more things stay the same in the child's daily life, the more capacity they have to process the core change.

### Don't Force the Child to Choose

Make decisions for the child rather than putting them in the position of choosing between parents. Asking a child to choose creates loyalty conflicts and guilt that compound the grief of the divorce itself.

## Handling the Child's Emotional Reaction

### Expect Volatility

A young child (the example given was a 5-year-old) will react in volatile ways. This is normal and should be expected rather than avoided or suppressed.

### Meet Anger with Compassion

When the child gets angry, respond with compassion rather than defensiveness. The child is experiencing genuine loss and is entitled to their feelings about it.

### Allow Full Expression of Grief

**Let them rage, cry, and vent.** The phrase used is that this will "feel like a death to him, and in fact it is: the death of his family." Suppressing or minimizing these emotions prevents necessary processing.

### Resist Defending the Decision

When the child expresses anger or sadness, resist the urge to defend your decision. The moment is not about the parents' rationale; it is about the child's experience. Taking the child's reaction personally or becoming defensive shifts focus away from their needs.

### The Child is Entitled to Their Feelings

This principle is stated explicitly: "He's entitled to his feelings." This means parents should not try to talk the child out of feeling sad, angry, or upset. The feelings are appropriate responses to a genuine loss.

### Expect Repetition

Because young children cannot absorb all the information at once, expect to have variations of the conversation multiple times over an extended period. This is not a one-time talk but an ongoing process.

### Use Indirect Processing Methods

**Acting out with stuffed animals**: Use toys to dramatize scenarios of families separating. This gives the child a safe, externalized way to explore and express feelings.

**Tell stories**: Share stories about children whose parents got divorced, who felt sad, but both parents still loved them and everything worked out. Stories provide a narrative framework the child can use to understand their own situation.

## Five Pillars of Ongoing Support

### 1. Reaffirm Shared Parenting Priority

Make an explicit agreement with your ex that your child's emotional health is your mutual priority. You may no longer be marital partners, but you will always be child-raising partners.

**The maintenance practice**: Every time you get angry at your ex, reaffirm this priority to yourself. Model maturity in all interactions with your ex. The parent's ability to maintain a positive relationship with the ex-spouse is key to the child's adjustment. This is framed as a causal relationship: positive co-parenting -> better child adjustment.

### 2. Pursue Shared Custody

It is in the child's best interest to arrange shared custody so both parents stay equally involved in the child's life.

**The sacrifice required**: This means giving up half the custody. The benefit is that it dramatically increases the chances that the child will grow up emotionally intact.

**Why this matters**: The question of whether one parent would be a "great single parent" is explicitly called irrelevant. What damages children is feeling abandoned by the other parent. Even if you believe you are the better parent, the child needs both parents.

**What this means practically**: You will have to help your child through the separations and the back-and-forth dislocations of visitation. These transitions are hard, but they are preferable to the alternative of reduced contact with one parent.

### The "Child Did Not Get Divorced" Principle

This is stated as a key reminder to keep in mind. Most of the time when a parent loses contact with a child after a divorce, it is because of conflict between the ex-spouses, not because of anything the child did or wanted.

**The child's ongoing needs**: The child is still related to and needs both parents. This biological and emotional reality doesn't change because the marriage ended.

**Behavioral implications**:
- Be happy when the child has fun with the other parent (rather than feeling threatened or jealous)
- Don't make the child feel guilty for loving their other parent
- Actively nurture the child's relationship with the other parent

**Access promise**: Tell the child they can see each parent whenever they want. Make this easy. The goal is stability and as much time with each parent as possible.

### 3. Expect and Accept Big Emotions

This is framed as a major adjustment requiring major emotional processing. When the child cries about small things, recognize they are "carrying around a backpack full of grief and anger that needs to come out somehow."

**What not to do**: Don't make the child feel analyzed by telling them they're really mad about the divorce when they're upset about something small.

**What to do instead**: Simply empathize with the surface issue: "You are so upset because you really want to do X and I won't let you... I'm sorry you can't do X, Sweetie."

**Let feelings exist without argument**: Don't try to argue the child out of their feelings. Listen and reflect back what you hear: "Sounds like you're pretty mad at me that we're getting divorced."

### 4. Maintain Family Routines, Rules, Schedules, and Structures

Consistency in daily life provides stability during emotional upheaval. This includes maintaining family rituals and keeping both parents involved for big occasions. The structures of normal life become an anchor when the family structure has changed.

### 5. Help the Child Develop a Positive Understanding

Help the child construct a narrative that makes sense of the transition in a way they can live with.

**The Memory Book Method**: Create a book for the child that:
1. Starts with when they were born, using family photos
2. Emphasizes how much both parents love them
3. Explains that Mom and Dad decided they could not live in the same house any longer
4. Shows Dad's new place with photos of child and dad happy there
5. Highlights positives about the new place (what the child loved there, activities with dad)
6. Acknowledges that sometimes the child was sad and missed the old days
7. Shows continuity (mom and child still doing Friday night activities together)
8. Shows both parents celebrating birthday together
9. Mentions any genuine positives of the new arrangement (a puppy, a goldfish, having two of a special toy - one for each house)
10. Ends with how Mommy and Daddy are much happier now and both love the child very much

The purpose of the book is to give the child a coherent, positive story about their life that acknowledges the loss while emphasizing continuity, love, and eventual happiness.

### Play Therapy

Play therapy is recommended as a way for children to process big emotions and develop a positive understanding of what is happening. This can be done professionally or at home using stuffed animals to act out scenarios of families separating and feeling sad.

## Summary of Key Principles

**Divorce is a death** - Treat it with the emotional seriousness of grieving a death, because for the child, it is the death of their family.

**United front** - Both parents tell together, both share responsibility, both commit to co-parenting as priority.

**Stability anchors processing** - Keep external life (home, school, friends, routines) as stable as possible so the child has capacity to process internal upheaval.

**Feelings are valid** - Let the child rage, cry, vent. Don't defend, don't argue, don't analyze. Just empathize.

**Both parents matter** - Shared custody is in the child's best interest. Nurture the child's relationship with the other parent. The child did not get divorced.

**Create narrative** - Help the child develop a story about their life that acknowledges loss but emphasizes continuity, love, and a better future.
