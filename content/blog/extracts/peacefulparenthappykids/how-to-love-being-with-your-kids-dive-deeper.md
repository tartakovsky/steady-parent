# How to Love Being with Your Kids: Dive Deeper

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/how-to-love-being-with-your-kids-dive-deeper

---

## Core Insight: The Secret to Transforming Tedium into Joy

The central teaching is that parenting feels tedious not because the tasks themselves are inherently boring, but because of how we show up to them. The secret to transformation is simple: **dive deeper and be fully present**.

### Why Parenting Feels Tedious

Most parents find it wearying to make yet another snack, change yet another diaper, mediate yet another sibling fight. Even activities designed to be fun - board games, art projects - can become tedious. This creates a feeling of being trapped in an endless cycle that gnaws away at aliveness.

However, the tedium comes from a specific pattern: **being only half present**. When our mind is elsewhere - thinking about the to-do list, worrying whether our child is developing properly, cataloging our own failings - the caretaking tasks will always feel burdensome.

### The Vicious Cycle of Partial Presence

When parents are physically present but mentally elsewhere, two problems compound:

1. **Children become more demanding**: Kids sense when we're not fully there. They keep asking for "more, more, more" because they're not actually receiving the full presence they need. The neediness is a signal that their emotional cup isn't being filled, not evidence of inherent greediness.

2. **The inner critic gains power**: Staying in our heads rather than being embodied in the present moment leaves the door open for the inner critic to wreak havoc. That critical voice finds fault with everything we do and everything our child does. Mental distraction feeds self-judgment.

### The Reframe: Being with Kids Doesn't Have to Be Boring

This is stated as a direct challenge to the assumption that childcare is inherently tedious:

**"When we bring ourselves fully into each present moment of experience, with no agenda, we find it brimming with life."**

This is actually the definition of play, of being "in the zone." Creative possibilities become endless when we're fully present. The key insight: **It's our resistance that's the burden, not our kids.** The heaviness comes from fighting the moment, not from the moment itself.

---

## The Experiment: Testing Full Presence

A concrete experiment is proposed to verify this teaching:

1. **Turn off your cell phone** - Remove the primary source of distraction and divided attention
2. **Show up 100%** when with your child - Complete presence, not partial
3. **Observe the results**:
   - Notice how much more joy and delight you find in nurturing and guiding
   - Notice how much easier everything becomes
   - Recognize it as a gift to yourself: **you emerge replenished**, not depleted

This reframes time with children from an energy drain to a potential energy source. The difference is entirely in the quality of presence brought to the interaction.

## Five Specific Practices for Diving Deeper

### Practice 1: Throw Yourself into the "Zone"

**The technique**: During any routine activity (giving a bath, reading a book, cleaning up toys), forget about everything except this one moment. Don't just move your child through the schedule mechanically.

**The intention setting**: Consciously set an intention to enjoy yourself and to connect. This mental shift changes the experience fundamentally.

**Why it works**: Your child feels "the juice" of your full presence. The response is happiness and cooperation. Children are highly attuned to whether they're receiving real attention or going-through-the-motions attention. When they get real presence, their behavior transforms.

### Practice 2: Connect Physically

**The technique**: Hold your child on your lap. If they're too big for that, snuggle up on the couch. The physical positioning matters.

**The deeper instruction**: Bring your full presence into your touch. "Soak her in through your body." This is about embodied connection, not just physical proximity.

**What happens**: When you dive deeper into the moment, you embrace more fully - the quality of physical connection improves. The child sinks into you. Both parent and child experience nervous energy draining away into stillness. The body becomes a conduit for calming both people.

### Practice 3: Watch the Sunset Together

**The technique**: Find somewhere to watch the sunset with your child - even sitting in the car works. Really appreciate "the show" as the sky changes and the sun sinks below the horizon.

**Key instruction**: Don't feel you have to say much. Words aren't the point.

**Why it works**: Your child will feel the sacredness of shared awe. Importantly, this experience leaves your inner critic speechless. The beauty and presence of nature bypass the mental chatter that normally runs interference on connection.

### Practice 4: Bring Sunset-Inspired Awe to Your Next Interaction

**The technique**: Transfer the quality of presence from the sunset experience into ordinary moments with your child. The feeling of awe isn't limited to nature - you can cultivate it toward your child directly.

**The insight**: Whether your child is five months, five years, or fifteen years old, she is a miracle. Watch closely: how she communicates, how she moves, how she learns, how she seeks your love.

**Why it works**: Really taking in the miracle of your child fills you with love. It also puts all those things you worry about into perspective. Worries shrink when genuine appreciation expands.

### Practice 5: Bypass the Inner Critic by Diving Deeper

**The situation**: The inner critic will show up even while you're enjoying your child. This is normal and expected.

**The technique**: Instead of fighting the critic or believing it, bypass it by diving deeper into presence. Go further into the moment rather than engaging with the mental commentary.

**Important distinction**: You do need time just for yourself to replenish. But time with your child can ALSO replenish you - as long as you're fully present with no agenda, just enjoying the creative act of connecting and playing.

**What children need**: Your presence is described as "the sunshine she needs to flower." The child requires time with you fully showing up and engaging, not just physically being in the same space.

## Three Tactics to Support Presence

These are specific mental moves to help maintain presence:

1. **Resist the lure of the to-do list**: It will still be there after the kids are in bed. The tasks aren't going anywhere, but this moment with your child is fleeting.

2. **Remember the practical benefit**: Once your child's cup is filled through genuine connection, he'll be less needy and more cooperative. Investment in presence pays off in reduced demands later.

3. **Reframe the meaning**: Remind yourself that you're engaged in the greatest possible creative act - helping your child blossom. This isn't mundane maintenance; it's profound creative work.

## Supporting Wisdom from Other Sources

**From Zen Habits**:
"Instead of focusing on how much you can accomplish, focus on how much you can absolutely love what you're doing. Be there completely. While doing this, you'll find that you naturally enjoy those seemingly tedious tasks much more (like washing the dishes). It's amazing how much non-resistance and presence changes everything."

This reinforces that the transformation isn't about changing what you do, but changing how completely you're present while doing it. The shift from accomplishment-focus to love-focus changes the entire experience.

**From Jan Marie Dorr**:
"When our mind is neither in the past or the future and we are completely present, our experience changes in a significant way. Suddenly life seems more spacious, and more peaceful."

This names the temporal dimension: tedium comes from mental time travel (past regrets, future worries). When attention is fully in the present, spaciousness and peace emerge naturally.

## Key Concepts Summary

**Presence as the variable**: The quality of parenting experience depends almost entirely on the quality of attention brought to it, not on the nature of the tasks themselves.

**Children sense partial presence**: Kids respond to real presence with satisfaction and cooperation; they respond to partial presence with increased demands.

**The inner critic thrives on distraction**: Mental absence creates space for self-judgment. Presence starves the critic.

**Parenting as replenishment, not depletion**: When done with full presence and no agenda, time with children can restore rather than drain the parent.

**Presence is bypassing, not fighting**: The way through the inner critic is to go deeper into experience, not to argue with critical thoughts.

**Physical connection as embodied presence**: The body is a channel for presence. Touch with full attention calms both parent and child.
