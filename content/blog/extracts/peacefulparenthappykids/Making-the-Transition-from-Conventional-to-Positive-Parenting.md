# Making the Transition from Conventional to Peaceful Parenting

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/Making-the-Transition-from-Conventional-to-Positive-Parenting

## Core Framework: Why the Transition Is Hard and Why It Gets Easier

The transition from conventional (punishment-based) parenting to peaceful parenting is genuinely difficult at first, but it becomes easier over time for specific reasons:

1. **You are learning new skills that work better** - the techniques become more natural with practice
2. **You are literally rewiring your brain** - building new neural pathways that support emotional regulation
3. **You are healing old wounds** - both your child's and your own - which reduces the emotional intensity over time

The payoff is clear: less drama and more love in the family dynamic.

## Understanding Why Children "Act Worse" Initially

A common concern is that children seem to act out more when parents first adopt peaceful parenting. This is not a sign of failure - it's actually part of the healing process.

**What's really happening:** When a child acts out during this transition, they are showing you feelings from the past - from times when they were yelled at or punished and felt alone and misunderstood. These old feelings have been stored inside and are now surfacing because they finally feel safe enough to express them.

**The reframe:** Think of it as healing old hurt feelings so they stop driving new bad behavior. The child's misbehavior is not defiance - it's an opportunity for healing. Your empathic response to these outbursts is what heals those old hurts so everyone can move forward.

## Handling Parental Guilt

Many parents feel guilty about how they acted before discovering peaceful parenting. However, guilt is counterproductive:

- **Feeling bad does not help you act good** - this is equally true for parents as it is for children
- You should ditch the guilt because you are already paying the price and making amends by helping your child heal those old hurt feelings
- Self-compassion enables better parenting; guilt undermines it

## The 13 Core Practices for Transition

### 1. Start With Yourself - The Foundation of "Peace"

The "peace" in peaceful parenting originates from you, specifically from your commitment to regulate your own emotions.

**The Stop, Drop, Breathe technique:**
- **Stop** when you feel upset
- **Drop** your agenda (temporarily)
- **Breathe** and notice the sensations in your body

**Why noticing body sensations matters:** This keeps you more conscious and prevents you from getting hijacked by anger. When anger takes over, it triggers "fight or flight" responses that make your child look like the enemy.

**The key principle:** Whenever possible, delay taking action until you feel more calm. Never act from the triggered state.

**Why this is difficult:** This requires practice both in tough moments with your child and in general life as you develop awareness of your own thoughts and emotions. It is described as "really, really hard."

**The neurological payoff:** Every time you practice this, you are building gray matter in your brain that develops impulse control. You are also uncovering and processing your triggers, so you won't get upset as often in the future.

### 2. Focus on Connecting First - The Non-Negotiable Foundation

**Critical principle:** Peaceful parenting does not work without connection. Before changing anything else with your child, you must build up your bond.

**The sequence matters:** If you drop punishments without first establishing connection, your child won't feel motivated to "do right" and you'll just see more testing behavior.

**The specific practice:** Spend at least **15 minutes** connecting one-on-one with each child daily, following their lead and pouring your love into them.

**The result:** You will be amazed at the difference in how your child responds to your requests. Connection creates intrinsic motivation to cooperate.

### 3. Explain What's Happening to Your Child

**Timing:** Wait until you already see more connection and cooperation before having this discussion. Don't rush it.

**The conversation template:**
- Acknowledge the old pattern: "You know how I used to yell at you and send you to your room when you broke the rules?"
- Name the change: "Have you noticed that I've been yelling a lot less?"
- Apologize specifically: "I'm so sorry that I've gotten into a bad habit of yelling so much."
- Affirm their worth: "I love you so much, and I know you try hard. You don't deserve to be yelled at, no matter what. No one does."
- Clarify that rules still exist: "We still have all the same rules. So it is never okay to lie or break promises or hit your brothers."
- Introduce the repair concept: "We think you'll learn more from cleaning up your messes and repairing your mistakes than from being punished."
- Offer support: "When you're upset, we want to help you with whatever problem you're having."
- Create shared ownership: "Let's begin by having a family meeting about what household rules are important to us."

### 4. Ask for Cooperation - Making It Reciprocal

**Frame the central rule:** "Our most important rule is that in this house we treat each other with kindness."

**Model the commitment:** "I'm going to work very hard not to yell at you, and to really listen and be kind."

**Invite their participation:** "Do you think you can work on this rule, too, and be kind to your sister?"

**Critical warning:** You can count on your child losing control sometimes and breaking the kindness rule. Resist the temptation to use that to justify your own yelling. You are the role model - hold yourself to the higher standard regardless of your child's behavior.

### 5. Offer Support and Model Win-Win Solutions

**The framework:** Acknowledge the child's legitimate feelings while holding the behavioral boundary, then collaborate on solutions.

**Example dialogue:**
- Validate: "I know your little sister gets on your nerves sometimes, and she always wants to play with your things. That's really annoying to you."
- Affirm their right: "You deserve to be able to keep your treasures safe."
- Hold the limit: "But it isn't okay to yell at your sister or hit her."
- Collaborate: "Why don't we work together to find a safe place for your treasures where your sister can't get at them?"
- Build skills: "And if you start getting annoyed at her, what can you do instead of yelling?"

### 6. Keep Setting Limits - Flexibility Does Not Mean Permissiveness

**What changes:** You become more flexible as you see situations from your child's point of view more often - and that's a good thing.

**What doesn't change:** You will still need to set plenty of limits.

**The timing secret:** Set the limit BEFORE you get angry, while you still have a sense of humor and can empathize with their perspective.

**Example dialogue:** "You wish you never had to stop playing and get ready for bed, don't you? I bet when you grow up, you'll play all night every night, won't you?! And right now, it's time for your bath."

**Why this works:** Acknowledging your child's perspective as you set the limit is what helps them cooperate with you. The limit is still firm, but it doesn't feel adversarial.

### 7. Teach Reparations - The Alternative to Punishment

**The parent's internal struggle:** If you've been punishing, you'll feel unfinished when your child breaks a rule and you don't punish. This feeling is real but should be overridden.

**The mindset shift:** Train yourself to think in terms of repair instead of punishment.

**The process (in sequence):**

**Step 1: Wait for calm and reconnection**
Everyone needs to have calmed down and be feeling reconnected before having the repair discussion.

**Step 2: Have a private discussion**
Be patient, listen, and really empathize. This is what helps them move past the incident.
Example: "You were pretty mad when he did that...I hear you."

**Step 3: Let them express the upset fully first**
Resist the urge to teach until after your child has opened up to show you all the upset that caused them to act out. The teaching comes only after they feel heard.

**Step 4: Point out the cost without shame or blame**
Example: "When you said that to your brother, it really hurt his feelings....I wonder if it made him not feel as close to you."

**Step 5: Ask them to identify the repair**
Example: "I wonder what you could do to repair things with your brother?"

**Step 6: Empower rather than punish or force**
Do NOT force an apology. Instead, empower them to see they can repair their mistakes.
Example: "You know we always clean up our own messes, right? This is just a different kind of mess, like spilled milk. I know you'll think of just the right thing to make things better with your brother....I can't wait to see what it is."

**The spilled milk analogy:** Just as with matter-of-factly cleaning up spilled milk, the process of cleaning up relational messes teaches the child that they don't want to cause those hurts to begin with. The learning comes from the experience of repair, not from punishment.

**Important parameters:**
- This is NOT a punishment
- They DO need to make a repair
- The repair needs to happen by the end of the day
- What they choose to do is their choice
- If the sibling doesn't see the repair as sufficient, you'll need to intervene again
- The point of repair is to make things better with the person they've hurt

**What to do if the child resists making a repair:**
Resistance means they need more help from you to heal their upset before they can move on to repair. Double-check that you aren't lecturing and that you're really seeing their perspective so they feel heard and can work out those big feelings. If old resentments are creating a chip on their shoulder, make a commitment to start repair work today to melt those resentments.

### 8. Expect Emotions - Why "Acting Out" Increases Temporarily

**The dynamic under punishment:** When children are punished, they learn that the big emotions driving their misbehavior get them into trouble. They develop the habit of stuffing those "bad" feelings down.

**Why stuffing doesn't work:** The jealousy, frustration, and need are still there in the child's "emotional backpack," popping out at the slightest provocation. The only reason children keep these feelings under wraps is fear.

**What happens when you stop punishing:** Those stuffed emotions are bound to bubble up to get healed. If you can make it safe for your child to show you the more vulnerable feelings underneath the anger, you won't see as much anger.

**The temporary increase in acting out:** When a child "acts out," they are acting out feelings they can't express in words. The internal experience might be something like: "All those times you yelled at me, and I was so scared...I acted like I didn't care, but I was terrified inside....That fear is still inside me and it eats away at me and feels awful....So I lash out to keep those feelings down."

No child could articulate that, so they act it out instead.

**Critical reframe:** Acting out is not a personal challenge to you. Emotions are never the problem - humans will always have big emotions. This doesn't give your child license to hurt anyone else, but it should change how you interpret misbehavior.

**The key practice:** Train yourself to see misbehavior as a cry for help, and set limits calmly and patiently to guide behavior. Help your child work through the hurts and fears under their anger so these no longer drive their behavior.

**The three tools for working through emotions:** Connection, laughter, and tears.

### 9. Create Safety for Emotional Expression

**Your role when your child shows upset:** Stay calm. Don't take it personally. The more you stay compassionate and accepting, the more they will feel safe enough to show you the woundedness behind their anger.

**Understanding anger:** Anger is just the body's fight response to threatening feelings. It's defensive, not the core issue.

**What healing looks like:** Expressing tears and fears is healing. Once your child shares them with you - and they don't even need to know what the feelings are about, or use words - those upsetting feelings will evaporate and they won't need the chip on their shoulder to protect themselves anymore.

**What to do if your child is stuck in anger:**
- First, create more safety by being as compassionate as you can about what's upsetting them
- If that isn't enough to help them cry and they stay angry, it's a sign they need more daily empathy and more daily laughing with you
- Both empathy and laughter build the trust needed for deeper emotional release

### 10. Help Your Child Make Sense of Their Experience With a Story

**Example narrative:** "When you were little, I was having a hard time... I yelled a lot... I didn't know what else to do... That frightened you.... So you got very very mad sometimes... Nowadays I work really hard to be kind, and not to yell.... You don't get so frightened.... And you are learning better ways to show me when you are sad or scared or mad..... We work together to solve problems in our family..... Everyone gets upset sometimes.... We try to listen to each other and be kind.... Then we always repair things between us.... There is always more love."

**Why this helps:** All children benefit from using words and stories to understand their emotional life.

**Critical warning:** Empathize, don't analyze. The goal is for the child to feel understood, not invaded or lectured. The story should validate their experience, not dissect it.

### 11. Model Apologies - Why Forced Apologies Backfire

**The problem with forced apologies:** Forcing a child to apologize in the moment when they're angry leads to resentment, not genuine repair.

**The better approach:** Expect repair by the end of the day (empowering the child to heal relationships), while modeling apology yourself. Your child will learn to follow your example.

**How to model taking responsibility:** When something goes wrong, take as much responsibility as you can to demonstrate how to step up.

**Example dialogue:** "I see two upset kids... I'm so sorry I wasn't here to help you work this out before you both got so upset and started hitting... and then I got worried someone was getting hurt, so I started yelling, too... I'm so sorry.... Let's all try a do-over.... I know you don't want to hit each other, hitting hurts... And I hear how mad you are.... Let's start over so you can tell each other what you need without attacking each other."

**Why this works:** There is no blame or shame in this approach, which makes it easier for everyone involved to consider how they might have contributed to the problem and to acknowledge that voluntarily.

### 12. Expect Setbacks - Self-Compassion as a Core Practice

**The reality:** You're human, so you aren't perfect. Some days will be a huge struggle. You will make mistakes.

**The secret of making this transition work:** Having compassion for yourself, just as you do for your child.

**The trajectory:** Parenting is hard, and peaceful parenting can be even harder when you begin. But it gets easier because:
- You're learning new skills that work better
- You're rewiring your own brain
- You're healing your child's old wounds and your own
- The result is less drama and more love

### 13. Every Morning, Make the Commitment - The Daily Choice

**First-person account of the practice:**

"For me, this type of parenting is a daily choice. Every morning I have to make the commitment not to yell, to stay calm, to choose love. And there is something very empowering about that."

**The ripple effects of modeling:**
- "I apologize to my kids when I make mistakes and slip"
- "When they accept my apology, they feel empowerment and generosity of spirit"
- "This influences their behavior with each other - there are more kind words and gestures, more 'I'm sorry' and more 'Don't worry, I know it wasn't your fault' that they extend to each other than before"

**Acknowledging difficulty while affirming progress:**
"There are days when things are a big struggle, but I really feel that something is changing deep within our hearts AND I feel us grow closer together when we choose love, and when in the middle of a tantrum I hug my child and genuinely tell him that I hear his pain and that I'll help him work through it."

**The path metaphor:** You're on a path that leads to a happier, more peaceful family. Two steps forward, one step back still gets you where you want to go. Soon you'll find yourself in a whole new landscape.

## Key Concepts and Principles

### The Emotional Backpack

Children carry accumulated feelings - jealousy, frustration, need, fear - that haven't been processed. These feelings are stored and come out at the slightest provocation. Punishment teaches children to stuff these feelings down out of fear, but they remain in the "backpack" driving behavior. Peaceful parenting creates safety for these feelings to surface and be healed.

### Repair vs. Punishment

**Punishment teaches:** Fear, resentment, and that might makes right.

**Repair teaches:**
- Taking responsibility for one's actions
- The impact of behavior on relationships
- The ability to make things right
- That mistakes are fixable

**The key distinction:** Repair is not punishment. The child chooses how to repair. The deadline (end of day) provides structure without being punitive.

### The Connection-Cooperation Link

Without connection, dropping punishments just leads to more testing behavior because the child lacks intrinsic motivation to cooperate. Connection must come first - it creates the relational context in which limits feel like guidance rather than control.

### Misbehavior as Communication

Acting out is expressing feelings that can't be put into words. It's a cry for help, not a personal challenge. This reframe is essential for responding effectively rather than reactively.

### The Role of Laughter and Tears

Both build trust. Both are needed for healing. Laughter and tears are explicitly named as tools alongside connection for helping children work through difficult emotions.

### Why Anger Is Not the Core Issue

Anger is the body's fight response to threatening feelings. The real issue is the vulnerability underneath - the fear, hurt, or need. Creating safety helps children access what's under the anger, which is what actually heals.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Dropping punishment without building connection first** - leads to more testing behavior
2. **Using your child's rule-breaking to justify your own yelling** - you are the role model
3. **Forcing apologies in the moment of anger** - creates resentment rather than genuine repair
4. **Lecturing instead of empathizing** - prevents children from feeling heard
5. **Analyzing instead of empathizing when telling stories** - makes children feel invaded
6. **Punishing yourself with guilt** - doesn't help you act better
7. **Interpreting acting out as a personal challenge** - it's a cry for help
8. **Setting limits only after you're already angry** - you lose your ability to empathize and use humor
9. **Expecting immediate behavioral improvement** - the transition includes a temporary increase in emotional expression
10. **Giving up when there are setbacks** - two steps forward, one step back still gets you there
