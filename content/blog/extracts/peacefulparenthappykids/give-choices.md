# Giving Choices as a Parenting Tool

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - "When Kids Won't Cooperate: Give Choices"

## Core Concept: Giving Choices as a "Magic Wand"

**Giving choices** may be the single most useful tool parents have for managing life with young children. It functions almost like a magic wand, particularly effective until children are about five years old. However, the tool remains valuable even into the teen years, where choices help children learn to manage themselves.

## Why Giving Choices Works

The effectiveness of giving choices stems from creating a **win-win solution**:

1. **Parent satisfaction**: You offer only choices that are acceptable to you, so you remain happy with any outcome
2. **Child satisfaction**: The child gets to pick an option that works for them, so they feel satisfied
3. **Power struggle avoidance**: You sidestep conflict because you aren't forcing the child to do something - they are choosing
4. **Sense of agency**: The child is in charge within your parameters

The fundamental psychological principle: **No one likes to be forced to do something.** When a child chooses, they cooperate because the decision feels like their own.

## The "Parenting Aikido" Framework

Giving choices functions like **Parenting Aikido** - instead of meeting your child's resistance with force (which creates a power struggle and ultimately produces a more resistant child), you affirm their right to some control while maintaining the boundaries you set.

The contrast:
- **Force approach**: Creates power struggle -> Results in more resistant child
- **Choice approach**: Affirms child's control within bounds -> Results in happier, more cooperative child who knows you're on their side

**Long-term benefit**: Children who are given choices get good at making healthy choices themselves.

## Six Practical Guidelines for Giving Choices

### 1. Give Limited Choices

Make choices as palatable as possible to the child, but **eliminate any options that are unacceptable to you**. This ensures you can genuinely accept whichever option the child selects.

### 2. Use Either/Or Choices for Young Children

For young children or any child who is easily overwhelmed, an **either/or choice** (two options) works best. This prevents decision paralysis while still providing agency.

**Example**: "We have to leave now. Do you want to put on your shoes yourself or do you want me to put them on for you?"

The non-negotiable (leaving) is established, but the child controls how the shoe-putting-on happens.

### 3. Increase Complexity for Older Children

As children get older, choices can become more complicated, reflecting their developing capacity for complex decision-making.

**Example**: "You can quit soccer if you want, but what sport or physical activity do you think you'd like to try? You need to choose one physical activity."

Here the requirement (physical activity) is maintained, but the child has latitude in how to fulfill it. The child can even exit a specific commitment (soccer) while still meeting the underlying need.

### 4. Use Choices to Teach Self-Management

Choices can be used to help kids learn to manage themselves by connecting their choices to outcomes they care about.

**Example**: "As soon as your homework is done, I'll help you carve that pumpkin. Your choice, but I know you want to start on the pumpkin as soon as we can."

The child technically has the choice to procrastinate on homework, but you're helping them motivate themselves to tackle it now by linking completion to a desired activity. This teaches self-regulation rather than imposing external control.

### 5. Use Choices to Teach Consequences

Choices can teach children about natural consequences of their decisions.

**Example**: "You know your piano recital is coming up. Extra practice will help you feel more confident, but that's your choice."

**Critical caveat**: Don't offer choices you can't live with. If you aren't willing to let the child make a fool of themselves at the recital, you may need to help them structure their practice effectively rather than leaving it entirely to choice. The choice must be genuine - don't offer an option if you'll override it when they choose "wrong."

### 6. Combine Empathy with Choices

**Empathy doubles the effectiveness of giving choices.** The mechanism:
- Empathy helps the child feel understood
- Feeling understood makes them less upset
- Being less upset makes them less resistant
- Reduced resistance means they're more likely to actually make a choice and move on

Empathy is not just a nicety - it's a practical multiplier for the effectiveness of the choice strategy.

## Example of the Complete Pattern

The bedtime example demonstrates the full framework:

"Do you want to go to bed now or in five minutes? Five minutes? Ok, do we have an agreement that in five minutes you'll go to bed no matter what?"

Elements at work:
1. **Either/or choice** appropriate for young children
2. **Both options acceptable to parent** (bed now or bed in five minutes)
3. **Child agency** in selecting their preference
4. **Agreement confirmation** reinforces that the child is committing to their choice
5. **Clear parameters** (bed will happen - only the timing is negotiable)
