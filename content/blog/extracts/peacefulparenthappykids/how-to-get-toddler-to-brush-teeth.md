# How to Get a Toddler to Brush Teeth

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/how-to-get-toddler-to-brush-teeth

---

## Core Philosophy: Why Force Backfires

**Holding a child down to brush their teeth is always a bad idea.** This approach is guaranteed to make them hate brushing their teeth and undermines the parent-child relationship. The parent is the person the child trusts most, and using force violates that trust.

The common dentist recommendation of having two adults hold a child down while one brushes is problematic. Consider how an adult would feel if this were done to them - it would be traumatizing. The same applies to children.

**Why toddlers resist brushing:**
- They don't understand why they need to brush their teeth, no matter what stories we tell them about cavities
- Having someone else put something in your mouth and move it around is inherently scary, or at minimum uncomfortable
- Most adults don't enjoy dental visits every six months, yet we ask children to open their mouths for someone to move something around twice a day
- This resistance is completely natural and expected

Despite resistance, parents should not give up on tooth brushing. The key is to **start small and keep at it**, just as with every other habit. All children eventually brush their teeth.

---

## The "Copycat" Method: Building Independence

Most kids at toddler age enjoy learning by copying adults but want to "do it themselves." Use this developmental drive by brushing together in front of a mirror.

**How it works:**
- Let the child "brush" her own teeth while you brush yours
- She copies you in the mirror
- She won't do a thorough job, but that's acceptable at this stage

**Why this works:**
- It teaches her that SHE brushes (establishing ownership)
- She doesn't have to rebel totally against brushing even during periods where she won't let you brush her teeth
- Frame it as a fun game rather than a chore

## The "Checking" Framework: Reframing Your Role

This is the central strategy for actually getting teeth brushed while maintaining child cooperation.

### Phase 1: Let the Child Lead

In the beginning, she will mostly just chew on the toothbrush. This is acceptable because it gets her comfortable with the toothbrush being in her mouth. Comfort must come before technique.

### Phase 2: Parent "Checks" (Not "Brushes")

After she "brushes," you help her finish up. The language matters: you're not brushing her teeth, you're "checking" to make sure no food is hiding.

**The exact script:**
"Yay! You brushed your own teeth! Now I'm going to check to make sure there is no food still hiding."

As you swipe each side, say what a great job she did brushing and add something like: "I see a tiny piece of pasta hiding right there! I'll get that for you."

**Why this framing works:**
- All children are motivated by **mastery** - let her feel good about her achievement of brushing, even though you're really the one doing it
- She feels successful and capable rather than acted upon
- Over time, she'll genuinely get better and better

### Keep It Very Short

Having someone poke around in your mouth can seem interminable even to an adult. In the beginning, your "check" should be just a quick swipe over all the teeth.

**Gradual progression:** You can lengthen brushing time over time by using songs and timers as she gets more used to it and develops greater tolerance.

## Making Brushing Fun: Specific Techniques

### Imagination Games During "Checking"
While brushing her teeth (the "check"), add imaginative elements:
- Say you see a giraffe or a tiger in there that you have to catch
- Chase the "sugar bugs"
- Count her teeth

### Singing
Songs serve multiple purposes:
- Increase the fun level
- Reinforce the routine
- **Most importantly:** Assure the child that brushing is time-limited because they can count on it ending when the song ends

**Suggested songs:**
- "This is the way we brush our teeth, after we eat our dinner"
- "The toothbrush in the mouth goes round and round"

### Sound Prompts
Encourage her to make sounds that facilitate brushing:
- Say "Teeee" (opens mouth for front teeth)
- Say "Ahhhh" (opens for back teeth)
- Roar like an animal so her mouth is open wide

This also makes the process feel like an exciting game rather than a chore.

## Handling Resistance: The Turn-Taking Strategy

Toddlers are beginning to understand the concept of "My turn!" Leverage this developmental milestone:

**The sequence:**
1. "Baby's turn to brush Mommy!"
2. "Now it's dolly's turn!"
3. "Now it's Mommy's turn to brush Baby!"

This creates a predictable, fair-feeling rotation rather than the parent always being in control.

---

## Pre-Brushing Warm-Up: Getting Giggles First

For kids who resist, start the brushing task with something fun. Anything that gets her giggling will make her more amenable to cooperating.

**Specific techniques:**
- Let her brush the stuffed animals' or dolls' "teeth"
- Brush all over her body - her arm, her ear - while asking "Is this where I should brush?"

**Why giggles matter:** Giggling releases the same pent-up emotions as crying. This helps the child work out their feelings about brushing in a playful context, reducing resistance when actual brushing happens.

---

## Using Proxies: Puppets, Dolls, and Stuffed Animals

**The proxy technique:** Instead of having your hand "finish up" her teeth, offer a choice of who will do it - her favorite stuffed animal, a doll, or a puppet.

**Implementation notes:**
- Puppets make it easier for you to hold the toothbrush
- She may prefer her doll, so offer the choice
- You can also offer a choice between two puppets
- Hold the doll or puppet and let them "finish up" her mouth
- It's awkward, but it gets the job done

The proxy creates psychological distance between the parent's control and the brushing action.

---

## Offering Choices: Building Cooperation Through Autonomy

Offering choices helps kids cooperate. Let her be in charge of everything possible about brushing:
- The toothbrush she chooses
- The toothpaste (or whether to use toothpaste at all)
- The song
- The position she is in
- How many times you have to jump up and down before she's done
- Anything else she can control

**The dual-brushing distraction:** Many parents report that letting the child brush their (the parent's) teeth WHILE the parent brushes hers is the best distraction. This simultaneously gives her a sense of control and keeps her occupied.

---

## External Modeling: Books and Videos

### Books About Toddlers Brushing Teeth
When kids read about brushing teeth, the whole process is normalized instead of feeling like a hardship they have to endure. This passive exposure builds acceptance.

### Videos Showing Toddlers Brushing
Many parents find videos motivate kids even better than books because most toddlers want to mimic other kids. Seeing peers brush normalizes the behavior.

---

## Timing and Routine Optimization

**Make brushing teeth a reliable part of the routine.** Experiment until you find the right timing.

**Options to try:**
- Brush BEFORE the bath so she is not so tired (the original question described brushing after bath when the child was exhausted and "crazy")
- Brush during the bath - more awkward for the parent, but the child will be more playful and relaxed

The original scenario had brushing as "one of the last things we do after the bath and before bed, when she is tired" - this timing sets up failure because the child is already depleted.

---

## The Daytime Giggling Strategy: Processing Resistance

If the child still resists, get her giggling about brushing to help her work out her feelings about it.

**Key implementation details:**
- Do this during the day, at a time when you aren't actually brushing
- She should feel more relaxed during this play
- Example: Make a game of letting her brush your teeth

**Why this works:** Reversing the power dynamic and getting her giggling releases the same pent-up emotions as crying. This is referenced from Hand in Hand Parenting's approach - processing emotions through play reduces resistance during the actual task.

---

## Toothpaste Considerations

**Most dentists say skipping toothpaste is fine at this age.** Since most kids don't like toothpaste, experiment to see if eliminating it makes your child more open to brushing.

**Alternative approach:** Buy multiple kids' toothpastes (Tom's of Maine is mentioned as having more natural ingredients), try them one after another, and give her choices. She might love one, and that preference could give her incentive to brush.

---

## When She Refuses: Avoiding Power Struggles

**Critical principle: Don't get into a power struggle.**

**What to do instead:**
1. "Play" toothbrushing a little bit using the games described above (for instance, brushing her elbow)
2. This shows her it's still on the agenda
3. It also gives her a chance to work out some of her resistance by giggling about your game
4. Then try again the following night

The goal is to maintain the expectation without creating a battle. Persistence over time, not force in the moment.

---

## Summary of Key Principles

1. **Force destroys trust and creates lasting resistance** - never hold a child down
2. **Toddlers have legitimate reasons for resisting** - the sensation is uncomfortable and they don't understand the purpose
3. **Reframe parent brushing as "checking"** - this preserves the child's sense of mastery
4. **Start very short and gradually extend** - build tolerance over time
5. **Giggles release resistance** - laughter and play process difficult emotions
6. **Offer control wherever possible** - choices create cooperation
7. **Use the child's developmental drives** - copying, turn-taking, mastery motivation
8. **Normalize through books and videos** - external modeling reduces the sense that brushing is a hardship
9. **Optimize timing** - don't brush when the child is already exhausted
10. **Maintain consistency without battles** - keep it on the agenda, try again tomorrow
