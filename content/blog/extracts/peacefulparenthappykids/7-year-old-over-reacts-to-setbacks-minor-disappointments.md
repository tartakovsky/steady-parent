# Helping Children Who Over-React to Setbacks and Minor Disappointments

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/7-year-old-over-reacts-to-setbacks-minor-disappointments

## Context and Problem Definition

The scenario involves a 7-year-old girl with an intense emotional personality who over-reacts to minor setbacks. Key characteristics include:
- When happy, everything is great; when upset, it feels like the end of the world
- Bright, articulate, and fairly sensitive
- Convinced her way is the right way
- Reacts out of proportion to minor disappointments (e.g., wailing for 10 minutes when asked to wait 5 minutes for the computer)
- Remembers small slights and perceived unfairness for months (still upset about a classmate getting a special job 5 months earlier)
- Strong sense of justice that manifests as complaints about unfairness
- Cannot let go of perceived injustices even when she intellectually understands the reasons

The core parenting goal is to give the child more ability to regulate her mind and emotions.

## The Four Reasons Some People Over-React to Disappointments

Each person experiences setbacks, but responses vary dramatically. Four factors explain why some people over-react while others don't:

### 1. Carrying a Full Backpack of Unprocessed Negative Feelings

**The mechanism**: When people carry around accumulated negative feelings that haven't been processed, they become more "ready to blow" when something goes wrong. The new disappointment triggers not just the current upset but also all the stored feelings.

**Why feelings get stuffed instead of processed**: All humans experience big feelings that scare us. The natural tendency is to bottle these feelings up to keep them at bay rather than letting ourselves feel them. Adults have fully-developed cerebral cortexes and life experience that allow them to manage emotions more easily - they can keep from automatically acting on emotions and they know they can weather disappointments and come out okay.

**Why children struggle more**: Young humans have a much harder time with this because their emotions are big and scary. They often "stuff" big feelings rather than letting themselves "feel" those emotions. The critical insight is that feeling the emotion is the only way for it to dissipate.

**The role of witnessing**: When we have a big emotion, we need a witness. Having a witness helps us feel safe enough to let ourselves feel the emotion. This explains why you're more likely to cry when sad if someone you love hugs you and strokes your hair. Children need parents to accept and empathize with their feelings so those feelings can bubble up. When parents aren't around (at school, with peers), upsets get stuffed.

**The release mechanisms**: Unless children have regular opportunities for raucous laughter (which releases pent-up emotion) and good cries, they end up carrying a full backpack of big feelings. Giggling discharges the same emotional energy as tears.

### Pattern in Attachment Parenting Families

In some (not all) families using attachment parenting practices, parents rush in to stop kids from crying. The child gets the message that the feelings behind tears are to be avoided at all costs. This prevents healthy emotional release.

### Pattern in Punitive Discipline Families

Children raised with punitive discipline often don't burst into tears as easily but are quick to anger. They have the same full backpack of unprocessed feelings but because feelings are generally not "safe" in their homes, they defend against feelings bursting out by getting angry instead.

### 2. Anxiety and Difficulty Self-Soothing

**The mechanism**: When something goes wrong, some people have a harder time soothing themselves than others.

**How self-soothing develops**: Children learn to self-soothe by being soothed by their parents. When parents physically soothe a crying infant, toddler, or preschooler, the child responds by strengthening the neural pathways to soothe themselves. This is a learned skill built through repeated experience of being soothed.

**The developmental window**: Kids who tend to be more anxious may need extra physical soothing when things go wrong, even past the preschool years. While unusual for a 7-year-old to need extensive soothing for small things, it is within the realm of normal.

### 3. Negative Thinking Patterns

**The optimist vs. pessimist distinction**: When something goes wrong, optimists see it as a temporary setback that they can overcome and prevent in the future. Pessimists conclude that everything will always go wrong for them and things are hopeless.

**The emotional consequences**: Negative thinking patterns lead to depression or anger (which are related states). A child's repeated tendency to tell herself a negative story about a past event can become entrenched.

**The key insight**: This negative default tendency can be interrupted by teaching the child to notice and correct her mind's automatic negative interpretations.

### 4. Processing Emotions to Make Sense of Experiences

**Humans as meaning-makers**: When something big happens emotionally, humans are designed to look for a lesson in it. We all draw conclusions about life, ourselves, and others from emotional experiences.

**The integration process**: Processing involves reviewing the emotions of an event and using cognitive processes to "understand" the event. This links two very different parts of the brain - the thinking part and the feeling part - to learn.

**Why this matters**: Without help processing, children may draw unhelpful conclusions that stick with them. With guidance, they can reframe experiences and draw healthier conclusions.

## Practical Interventions

### Intervention 1: Daily Laughter Games

**Purpose**: To help the child vent emotional energy through giggling, since giggling discharges the same energy as tears.

**Implementation**: Play daily games that get the child giggling. Games for bonding and emotional intelligence work well.

**Tailoring to the child**: For a child who loves to be right, play games in which the parent is wrong. This lets the child enjoy being right while also getting the laughter release.

### Intervention 2: Allowing and Supporting Crying

**Reframe the over-reaction**: When the child sobs about not being able to use the computer, this is a good thing, not a bad thing. It's an opportunity to release stored emotions.

**What NOT to do**: Don't try to stop the crying.

**What TO do**: Help her cry by empathizing. Example script: "It is so hard for you to wait. It seems like this waiting will never end. I'm sorry it's so hard."

**The technique**: Don't talk too much, just enough to help her cry as much as she needs to. The over-reaction is a great opportunity to let out all kinds of sadness and frustration that have been stored up.

**The underlying principle**: Her feelings are just feelings. She's allowed to have them. The more opportunities she has to giggle and cry, the less she will over-react to small slights.

### Intervention 3: Teaching Self-Soothing Techniques

**The approach**: Develop a practice the child can do when upset that soothes her, preferably one she can do in public.

**Recommended technique - Tapping (EFT)**: Tap the acupressure point at the base of the hand (where you would hit your hand if you used it to karate chop). This has been shown to be effective in calming the body's arousal, and the more one does it, the more effective it becomes.

**Recommended technique - Mantra**: Help the child develop a little mantra like "This will be ok in the end. If it isn't ok, it isn't the end" or whatever works for her to help herself get calm.

**The sequence of support**:
1. Empathize and let her vent
2. Hold her and soothe her
3. When she is able to move on emotionally, help her calm down with tapping and a mantra
4. Then help her work with her mind (reframing)

**Why this builds independence**: Over time, the child will adopt the soothing techniques to soothe herself when parents aren't there to help, and even as she begins to get upset. The techniques become associated with producing a calm state from all the practice with parents.

### Intervention 4: Correcting Negative Thinking Patterns

**The goal**: Teach the child to notice and correct her mind's default tendency to see things negatively.

**For entrenched negativity**: May need more than basic techniques. Cognitive therapy with a child-focused therapist can be very effective, especially when the parent is in the room to learn how to talk with the child about these issues.

**Timing advantage**: At age 7, the child is still young enough to get quick results with cognitive therapy intervention.

### Intervention 5: Retelling Stories to Reframe Them

**The technique**: Help the child retell any story that is bugging her.

**Example process**:
1. Get her to tell you the story of the event that upset her (e.g., the child who received special treatment last May)
2. Validate her feelings about it
3. Extend the discussion: How else could she see the story? Is there another conclusion she could draw from it?

**Why this works**: It engages both the feeling and thinking parts of the brain to help the child make new meaning from the experience.

## Important Developmental Context

**What's normal for a 7-year-old**: Most 7-year-olds are pretty good at regulating their emotions. A 7-year-old should generally be able to manage emotions enough to wait for something, even if she isn't happy about it. Having big reactions to small disappointments is not usual but is within the spectrum of normal.

**Holding grudges**: Holding a negative conclusion about something and continuing to dwell on it months later does not help a child become optimistic and resilient.

**The concern**: If a child carries grudges about small things, this pattern could affect future mental health.

---

## What the Parent is Doing Right

The scenario describes good parenting foundations:
- Focusing on attachment
- Spending 20-30 minutes most days playing with or doing something with the child to foster connection
- Using attachment parenting practices (child sleeps in parents' room when needed)
- Trying to validate feelings and give hugs when child has setbacks
- Not using punishment

**The gap**: Despite good parenting, the child is still struggling because she needs specific help developing resilient, optimistic, self-regulating capabilities.

---

## Key Mindset Shifts for Parents

**Shift 1**: Instead of judging whether the child is over-reacting, focus on helping her solidify the "habit" of soothing herself.

**Shift 2**: What seems small to adults may not seem small to the child. Let go of the judgment about proportionality.

**Shift 3**: Recognize that every child has different strengths and weaknesses. A child may have many strengths but need specific help in the area of emotional regulation.

**Shift 4**: Over-reactions are opportunities for emotional release, not problems to be stopped.

---

## The Complete Framework

The overall approach combines four parallel tracks:

1. **Emotional release through laughter and tears** - Daily giggling games plus allowing/supporting crying when it happens
2. **Building self-soothing capacity** - Teaching tapping and mantras, practicing them during co-regulation until they become automatic
3. **Cognitive reframing** - Teaching the child to notice negative thinking and consider alternative interpretations
4. **Story retelling and processing** - Helping the child make new meaning from upsetting experiences

All four tracks work together to give the child more ability to regulate her mind and emotions.
