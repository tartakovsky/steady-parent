# Nurturing Your Child's Unique Temperament

Source: Peaceful Parent, Happy Kids - "Love the One You're With: Nurturing your child's unique temperament"

---

## The Nature vs. Nurture Reality

**About half of who we are comes from genes, and half from environment.** Environment includes peers, school, and the outside world, but the most critical traits are shaped by a child's earliest relationships. These critical traits include:
- The ability to give and receive love
- The ability to maintain inner happiness regardless of life's difficulties

The key insight from researchers Martha and William Pieper (authors of "Smart Love") is that **the stability or instability of a person's inner happiness is entirely determined by the nurture a child receives**, not genetics. This means parents have enormous influence over their child's foundational emotional wellbeing, even though they cannot control inherited genes.

### The Shy Chimp Study: How Nurturing Transforms Genetic Tendencies

A powerful example of gene-environment interaction comes from research on shy baby chimps. Shyness is believed to be strongly influenced by genes, and shy children face disadvantages in outgoing cultures because they are highly sensitive to stimuli and struggle to relax and connect with others.

However, when shy baby chimps were given to **extremely nurturing mothers**, they became leaders in their group. Their shy siblings raised by less responsive mothers remained shy and fearful throughout life.

**Why this happened:** The responsive mothering helped shy chimps learn to calm themselves and manage their reactions. Their heightened sensitivity, rather than being purely a liability, made them more responsive to the needs of other chimps and better at negotiating group situations. The trait itself did not change, but its expression and usefulness transformed entirely based on environment.

## The Nine Innate Temperament Traits

Research by **Stella Chess and Alexander Thomas** identified nine apparently innate traits observable in babies:

1. **Activity level** - how physically active the child is
2. **Regularity** - predictability of biological functions like sleep and hunger
3. **Approach or withdrawal** - initial response to new situations
4. **Adaptability** - ease of adjusting to changes
5. **Responsiveness** - sensitivity to stimuli
6. **Intensity of reaction** - energy level of responses
7. **Positivity or negativity** - general mood
8. **Distractibility** - ease of being diverted from activities
9. **Attention span or persistence** - ability to stay focused on tasks

Infants can be rated on a continuum from high to low on each trait. **These traits are unlikely to change throughout life.** However, what CAN change is how the trait manifests, depending on how the person learns to modulate it.

## Core Framework: Strengths and Weaknesses Are Flip Sides

**Almost all weaknesses can be understood as the flip side of a person's strengths.** This is a crucial reframing that helps parents accept their child's difficult traits.

### The Stubborn Child Example

A child who is incredibly stubborn, arguing with parents until she wears them down, has a trait that:
- Is hard to live with
- May alienate friends, teachers, and employers
- BUT the flip side is **dogged persistence**

This persistence will serve her well in careers requiring tenacity: detective, scientist, novelist, and almost any profession.

**The parent's job is not to eliminate the trait but to help the child:**
- Recognize that while the trait is an asset, it can drive others crazy and make them angry
- Learn to modulate and use the trait intentionally, rather than letting it control her
- Manage herself to best meet her overall goals

**Helping children know themselves well and manage themselves toward their goals is one of the most helpful gifts any parent can give.**

## The Seven-Step Framework for Nurturing Temperament

### Step 1: Know Your Child

Pay attention to two things:
- What you love about your child
- What drives you crazy about your child

The "negatives" may emerge primarily with you (parents have special privileges where children feel safe to show their full range), but these traits probably appear in the rest of the child's life too. Knowing both sides allows you to help effectively.

### Step 2: Accept Your Child's Weaknesses

**Everyone has weaknesses.** The key is reframing them through the strength-weakness flip side lens:

- If he has trouble controlling anger when his brother disrespects him, ask: Is he an equally passionate fighter against other injustices?
- If she dawdles constantly, ask: Is this a sign of immense imagination? Like Calvin from Calvin and Hobbes secretly being Spaceman Spiff?

Acceptance does not mean passivity. It means understanding the trait's value while still helping the child learn to manage it.

### Step 3: Help Your Child Understand Her Strengths and Challenges

**How to have this conversation:**

1. **Start positive**: Begin with how you've noticed this wonderful thing about her
2. **Be honest about the flip side**: Explain how this trait also creates challenges
3. **Show the impact on her**: Point out how it ends up being a problem for her when she impacts negatively on others
4. **Invite collaboration**: Ask if she has ideas about how to manage it to get benefits without drawbacks
5. **Share your own experience**: If you or the other parent has the same trait, talk about your journey learning to manage it. Make your story positive and hopeful to help her feel less alone and more optimistic

**Critical caveat:** Your goal is to help her notice something about herself and learn to manage it, NOT to label her in a way that makes her feel locked into your expectations. Always make clear that everyone changes, and as we grow up, it gets easier to manage ourselves.

### Step 4: Own Your Reactions

**What bothers one parent might not bother another.** A high-energy child might fit right in with some families but exhaust others. Your reaction to a trait says something about YOU, not just about the child.

**Frame requests as "I" statements rather than criticism:**

Instead of: "You're being too loud and hyper!"

Say: "I see you feel like jumping around right now. I'm tired and a little cranky, and the noise is too much for me. Do you want to go outside and play or down in the basement to jump on the old mattress?"

This approach:
- Validates the child's energy and needs
- Takes ownership of your state and limits
- Offers constructive alternatives
- Avoids framing the child as "wrong"

### Step 5: Look in the Mirror

**Often what drives us crazy about our child is something we cannot acknowledge about ourselves.**

Examples of projection:
- If you think your child is obstinate, examine who they are pushing up against. It takes two to have a power struggle.
- If you think she is a "drama queen," consider whether you had to cut off your own big feelings when your parents told you to stop overreacting

**Intentionally softening your reaction to your child often uncovers and heals your own triggers.** The child's behavior serves as a mirror reflecting unresolved issues from your own upbringing.

### Step 6: Contextualize

Children need to know they do not make mistakes because they are bad, but because they are human, and often because they are children (developmentally immature). This is especially important when the child slips and feels bad about her progress.

**Example language:**
"I know you didn't mean to yell at your friend when you got upset. You've been working hard not to lose your temper. I had a hard time managing my temper when I was eight too. It will get easier as you keep practicing."

This approach:
- Separates the behavior from the child's identity
- Acknowledges effort and progress
- Normalizes the struggle through your own experience
- Provides hope that things will improve with practice

### Step 7: Be Patient

**Results vary:** Sometimes self-awareness and clear intention create remarkable changes quickly. Sometimes you need to keep talking, keep reminding, and keep exploring management strategies. What works will evolve as the child develops.

**The endpoint:** Once you have found a way to help your child see his whole self positively, he is on the road to learning to manage even the most challenging character traits. Even more important, he will feel cherished for who he is. This unconditional love is what every child needs to thrive.

**Celebrate every step in the right direction.**

## Foundational Mindset: The Unlabeled Seed Metaphor

"Try to see your child as a seed that came in a packet without a label. Your job is to provide the right environment and nutrients and to pull the weeds. You cannot decide what kind of flower you will get or in which season it will bloom."

This metaphor captures:
- You do not control who your child becomes
- You control the conditions for growth
- Your job is nurturing (environment, nutrients) and protecting (pulling weeds)
- The outcome and timing are not yours to determine
- Acceptance of the unknown is part of parenting

## Key Takeaways

1. **Genes set the stage, but environment determines the play.** The most important traits - capacity for love and inner happiness - are shaped by early relationships.

2. **Traits do not change, but their expression can.** A shy child can become a leader if given responsive parenting that helps them learn self-regulation.

3. **Weaknesses are strengths in disguise.** Reframing helps both parent acceptance and child self-understanding.

4. **Self-management is the goal, not trait elimination.** Learning to modulate strengths and weaknesses is a major task of maturation that challenges many adults.

5. **Your reactions reveal your own issues.** The traits that drive you crazy often mirror unacknowledged parts of yourself.

6. **Unconditional love requires seeing the whole child positively.** This is the foundation that allows children to thrive and learn self-management.
