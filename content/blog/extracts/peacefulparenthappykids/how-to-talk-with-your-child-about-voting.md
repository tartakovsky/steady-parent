# How to Talk with Your Child about the Election

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/how-to-talk-with-your-child-about-voting

---

## Core Philosophy: Voting as a Teaching Opportunity

The central premise is that electoral politics provides a valuable opportunity to teach children about citizenship, critical thinking, and values. Even though politics may seem like "adult business," children are inevitably hearing about elections and forming their own conclusions. Rather than letting children absorb information passively (often with anxiety-inducing misconceptions), parents can guide their understanding deliberately.

**Voting itself is a teaching moment.** Taking children to the polling place demonstrates civic participation. If there is a line, frame it as part of the lesson: voting is such a privilege that people are willing to endure discomfort for it. People around the world have fought and died for the right to vote. Bring masks, umbrellas, snacks, and books about voting to read while waiting. After voting, take a photo outside the polling place and post it on social media with #FamilyVote to inspire other parents.

## General Guidelines for All Ages

### 1. Control the Media Environment

**Turn off the news.** Checking in periodically is more than sufficient. The media creates a "roller coaster ride of rumors and fears" that should not set the tone in your home, even on election day.

The reasoning: constant news exposure amplifies anxiety and creates an atmosphere of crisis that children absorb, even when they don't understand the specifics.

### 2. Manage Your Own Emotional Reactions

**Watch your tendency to react strongly in front of your child.** Kids take their cues from adults. Parental overreactions make children feel less safe. Children should know that adults "have got this" and that worrying about the election is not the child's job.

**Calm yourself before talking with your child.** This is a prerequisite, not optional. Your emotional state transfers directly to them.

### 3. Start by Listening, Not Telling

**Open discussions by asking what your child has heard.** If your child initiates by asking you a question, ask them what they have heard about the issue before you answer.

Why this matters: Children often draw upsetting conclusions from fragments they've overheard. You cannot address their actual fears until you know what those fears are. Your goal becomes correcting misinformation and alleviating anxiety, but you first need to understand what misinformation they've absorbed.

### 4. Safety Reassurance Comes First

**Your priority is to assure your child that you will keep them safe.** This applies to children of all ages, including teenagers. More than giving information, you want to:
1. Listen to their worries
2. Acknowledge those worries
3. Reassure them that whatever happens, you will keep them safe and your family will find a way through

The sequence matters: listening and acknowledgment must precede reassurance, or the reassurance feels dismissive.

### 5. Share Values While Allowing Disagreement

**Express your opinions but back them up with facts.** Don't be afraid to share your values, but allow your child to express opinions that differ from yours.

**Model evidence-based civic conversation.** Kids need to learn that civic conversations can be conducted with civility and should be evidence-based. This is especially important given the current civic atmosphere where they may be seeing the opposite modeled elsewhere.

### 6. Avoid Demonizing the Other Side

**Resist the urge to demonize voters who disagree with you.** When children see public discourse that is disrespectful, they learn that adults are not actually serious about kindness and respect - that those are just things adults say to kids but don't practice themselves.

**Explain the information ecosystem problem.** Many of us wonder "How could they even think such a thing?" about people who disagree with us. But everyone forms opinions based on the information they receive from news sources and social media. People who disagree are often seeing completely different information on their feeds and even from news sources, leading to completely different conclusions about what is true.

**Frame this as a problem for their generation to solve.** Point out to your child that our generation has not been able to solve the fractured information ecosystem. This positions it as a civic challenge they may help address, rather than simply a reason to despair or hate the other side.

### 7. Explain Election Integrity Safeguards

**Elections follow strict rules** to ensure fairness, that every vote is counted, and that only valid ballots are included. While some candidates and supporters have claimed elections "weren't fair," these claims can be fact-checked.

### 8. Explain Vote Counting Timelines

**Legally, the election is not complete until all votes are counted.** Traditionally in the US, votes were counted and a winner declared on election night. But because some states don't start counting mail-in ballots until election day, results may take up to a week.

**Frame patience as civic responsibility.** Ask your child: "What can we do to help everyone stay patient and peaceful until all the votes are counted, to protect the democratic process and ensure fairness?"

### 9. Model How to Handle Not Knowing

**When you don't know an answer, say so.** Invite the child to look it up with you. As you research together, model how to fact-check and evaluate sources.

## Age-Specific Approaches

### Preschoolers

**Principle:** Kids this young should not be exposed to news, but they will notice signs and excitement. Explain political issues in terms they can relate to, like fairness.

**Specific Language and Framing:**

- **Voting as decision-making:** "Voting is one way that people make decisions about how we will live and work together. For instance, let's vote on whether to have pizza or pasta tonight." This makes the abstract concept concrete through immediate experience.

- **The President role:** "The President is the most powerful leader in the government. We all vote so that everyone gets to say who they think should serve as President. If only certain people got to decide, then that wouldn't be fair, would it?" This connects to the fairness concept preschoolers understand.

- **Why voting matters:** "Why do you think voting matters? That's right, if someone doesn't vote, they are letting everyone else decide for them." Asking the question first, then affirming, builds their reasoning.

- **Political signs:** "All those signs in front of people's houses are showing who they plan to vote for. They're hoping that their sign will make you want to vote for the person they like. What do you think about that?" This introduces the concept of persuasion without judgment.

- **Choosing leaders:** "How would you decide who to vote for? That's right, by whether you think they are a good leader, who has good ideas, listens to others, treats people fairly and kindly, and tries to help everyone work together." This establishes criteria for evaluating leaders.

- **Disagreement doesn't end friendship:** "People can disagree about whether or not something is a good idea for our country, and still be friends with each other." This is foundational for healthy civic engagement.

### School-Age Kids

**Principle:** Build on preschooler concepts with more complexity about how democracy functions.

**Specific Language and Framing:**

- **Personal stake:** "If you were 18, who would you vote for? How would you make that decision?" This makes it personally relevant and exercises decision-making.

- **Taxes and public goods:** "In our town, we will be voting about whether to fix the roads. That will mean paying more taxes, because we have to pay for the roads somehow. Taxes also pay for schools, libraries, hospitals and playgrounds. If we don't all chip in, who would pay for these things? Do you think that is worth it?" This introduces the concrete trade-offs in policy decisions.

- **Checks and balances:** "Our democracy is pretty strong, with a lot of checks and balances, and it's set up so that lots of people are involved in making decisions. Who is President is important, but there are limits to their power. It is also important who is elected to Congress." This provides reassurance through understanding of systemic safeguards.

- **Voter fraud concerns:** "No, people can't cheat and cast a ballot who aren't allowed to. There are many precautions in place to be sure that everyone who votes is a U.S. citizen and has the right to vote." Direct factual reassurance about a common concern.

- **Sore losers analogy:** "Sometimes people who lose elections say that the election wasn't fair, just as kids who lose boardgames say that the game wasn't fair." This uses a relatable comparison to put claims of unfairness in perspective.

- **Leader responsibility:** "What responsibility do our elected leaders, the candidates, and the news media have for telling the truth and role-modeling respectful civic engagement? Do you think lying to get elected is okay?" This develops critical evaluation of public figures.

### Preteens

**Principle:** Introduce more sophisticated concepts about how the system works and how to evaluate information.

**Specific Language and Framing:**

- **Political advertising skepticism:** "Do you think all political ads tell the truth? How could you fact-check what they say? How do you know which sources to trust?" This develops media literacy skills.

- **Electoral College explanation:** "All the votes get added up, and that is called the popular vote. But that isn't necessarily who wins the presidential election. The people in each state vote for who they want to be president, and they send 'electors' to vote for that person in the Electoral College. There are 538 electors all together, so how many of those would you need to vote for you if you were running? That's right - 270, which is half plus one." This explains the actual mechanics with a calculation exercise.

- **Electoral College debate:** "Some people think the Electoral College isn't fair because the candidate who wins the electoral college vote may not win the popular vote, which means that the majority of Americans didn't actually vote for that person. Other people think the Electoral College is a good way for states with fewer people to have an impact on the election. What do you think?" This presents both sides and invites the child to form their own opinion.

- **Vote counting timeline:** "No, there is no rule that all the votes need to be counted on election day. The law is that all votes need to be counted that were postmarked by the deadline, even if that takes weeks! That's one of the reasons the newly elected president isn't inaugurated for a few months after the election. Do you think it's important that everyone's votes get counted?" This addresses a common misconception directly.

### Teens

**Principle:** Focus on information literacy, manipulation awareness, and personal civic responsibility.

**Specific Language and Framing:**

- **Social media source evaluation:** "When you see political information on social media, how do you know the source of it? What responsibility do you have for determining whether something is true before you repost it? How would you fact-check something? What sources do you trust?" This establishes personal responsibility for information sharing.

- **Attention economy manipulation:** "You are likely to see things on your social media feeds that alarm you. That's intentional, so that you'll keep clicking. The more time you spend on social media, the more money they make. Youtube made billions on videos they knew were inaccurate in this election cycle. Since the information you're seeing often is not true, you have to fact-check it. How does it make you feel to know that you're being manipulated that way just so someone can make money?" This reveals the profit motive behind alarmist content and creates healthy skepticism.

- **Foreign influence:** "Why do you think other countries have tried to influence our voters? What do they get out of it?" This introduces geopolitical dimension of information warfare.

- **Voter fraud statistics:** "Experts agree that voter fraud is very rare, because there's a five year prison sentence for committing it! The Heritage Foundation, a conservative think tank, says there have been 'only a handful of voter fraud cases over the past few years.' The Brennan Center for Justice, a liberal public policy center, 'found that Americans are more likely to be struck by lightning than impersonate someone else at the polls.'" **Note:** Citing sources from both conservative and liberal think tanks models how to build credible arguments using diverse sources.

- **Historical perspective and activism:** "There are many times in American history when voters elected leaders who made unfair policies. In every case, there were advocates and activists who worked hard to protect civil liberties and the rule of law. Do you know any examples of this?" **Examples given:** Jim Crow laws, Japanese Internment Camps, McCarthy hearings. This provides both realism (bad things have happened) and hope (citizens have successfully fought back).

## Key Patterns Across All Ages

**Progression of complexity:** The same core concepts (voting, fairness, truth, civic responsibility) are revisited at each age with increasing sophistication.

**Questions over lectures:** Most talking points are framed as questions, which:
- Engage children's thinking rather than just transmitting information
- Reveal what children already believe or misunderstand
- Build reasoning skills
- Give children ownership of their conclusions

**Concrete before abstract:** At every age, abstract concepts are connected to concrete experiences the child can relate to (pizza voting, board game sore losers, paying for roads).

**Both sides presented:** On genuinely contested issues (like the Electoral College), both perspectives are offered rather than only the parent's view. This models intellectual fairness and prepares children for a pluralistic society.

**Empowerment over fear:** The overall tone is one of agency - children can learn to evaluate information, understand systems, and eventually participate. Anxiety is acknowledged but not amplified.
