# Staying Connected to Your Toddler as a Working Parent

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids (Dr. Laura Markham, Clinical Psychologist)

## Core Insight: The Real Cause of Evening Meltdowns

When toddlers in daycare have meltdowns every evening after being picked up, this is not primarily about "not getting enough attention." It is about the child needing to process and communicate accumulated emotions from the day spent away from their parent.

**Reframe**: Evening meltdowns are the child's way of saying "I need to tell you what's been happening with me since I last saw you."

## Three Factors That Determine Success for Children in Daycare

Research identifies three essential components that determine whether children thrive in nonparental care:

1. **The relationship with the parents**: The quality of connection during the hours spent together
2. **The relationship with the caregiver**: A loving, attached relationship with whoever provides care during the day
3. **The number of hours in care**: Fewer hours in nonparental care generally supports better outcomes

## Why Evening Meltdowns Happen

**The emotional backpack concept**: Children accumulate emotions throughout the day that they cannot fully process or express while away from their primary attachment figure. When they reunite with their parent, they finally feel safe enough to release these stored feelings.

**Signs that a child needs to empty their emotional backpack**:
- Whining or tearfulness
- Controlling behavior (needing things done exactly a certain way)
- Extreme fragility where small disappointments trigger major reactions

**What controlling behavior signals**: When a child desperately needs you to sit exactly where they want you to, they are communicating that their world feels so fragile that if you do not do exactly what they want, everything will fall apart.

## Strategy 1: Prioritize Intensive Connection Immediately After Reunion

**What to do**: When your toddler is having daily meltdowns from arrival home until bedtime, prioritize the child completely during this period. Do not expect to be able to cook dinner.

**Practical adjustments**:
- Order takeout
- Have the other parent cook
- Use convenience foods temporarily

**Why this works**: If you can really focus on your child and really listen to their meltdowns, they will get all that crying out and will have a much happier evening. They will be able to connect with both parents. And the tearfulness will not persist every night.

**Long-term benefit**: This helps the child adjust much better to missing their parent because they are not carrying around a full backpack of upset feelings they cannot express.

## Strategy 2: Allow and Support the Meltdown Instead of Preventing It

**The counterintuitive approach**: Instead of trying to prevent meltdowns by catering to every demand, gently and compassionately set a limit that will trigger the feelings the child needs to release.

**Example script**: "No, Honey, I can't come play with you yet."

**What happens next**: This will trigger all of the built-up feelings of wanting you and not being able to have you. Expect a complete meltdown.

**Why preventing meltdowns backfires**: Even if you successfully cater to the toddler to prevent a meltdown, your whole evening will be spent managing a child on the verge of falling apart. The child's attempts to control their inner turmoil by controlling you will not be successful and will leave everyone miserable.

## Strategy 3: Hold Space During the Meltdown

**Physical presence**: Hold the child, or if they will not let you, stay close.

**Verbal approach**: Do not say much. Just be warm and loving.

**What to communicate**:
- "You're safe"
- "I'm right there"
- "You really wish I would always play with you"

**The goal**: Honor all of the pain and anguish as they pour it out. That level of distress represents how much they want you when you are not there all day long.

**Timeline for improvement**: If you do this consistently for about a week, expect to see the child become completely different in the evening.

## Strategy 4: Use Physical Roughhousing to Release Emotions

**The principle**: Physical play that produces laughter helps children release tears and fears through a different channel.

**Separation games**: Play games that specifically address the separation theme in a playful way.

**The ByeBye Game**:
1. Tell your child you want to play the ByeBye Game
2. Explain you are going to go ByeBye behind the couch (or bathroom, or another location)
3. Agree that if either of you misses the other, that person should yell a silly word (like "Tyrannosaurus")
4. Start to go behind the couch, but before the child can even miss you, jump out yelling your silly word and hugging them
5. Tell them you just missed them so much
6. Repeat, very gradually making the separation times a bit longer
7. Always make sure you miss them before they can miss you

**How to know it is working**: The child is giggling.

## Strategy 5: The Fix Game for Partner Involvement

**Purpose**: Helps the other parent connect with the child and develop empathy for the child's evening struggles.

**How it works**: Both parents playfully "fight" about which of them gets to kiss and hug the child because they both love them so much.

**Benefit**: Increases the non-primary parent's connection and helps them engage more positively during difficult evenings.

## Strategy 6: Weekend Special Time

**What to do**: On weekends, have intensive, low-key special time where you simply stay with the child while they play, pouring your love into them.

**Why it matters**: This is what the child needs from you to build the closest relationship possible and helps compensate for weekday separation.

## Optimizing Work Schedule If Possible

**Best approach if negotiating reduced hours**: Cutting back at both the beginning and end of the day is preferable to taking a full day off.

**Reasoning**:
- Fewer hours of each day in someone else's care matches what the child needs
- Less rushed mornings reduce stress during an already hectic time
- Earlier pickup reduces evening meltdown intensity

## Why Positive Discipline Matters More for Children in Daycare

**Research finding**: Toddlers who spend the most hours in nonparental care tend to have more behavior problems. However, by preschool age, this effect is no longer measurable.

**Implication**: The toddler may act out a bit more than they otherwise would during this period.

**Critical warning**: Responding to this acting out with punishment, including timeouts, is the fastest way to erode the parent-child relationship.

**Better approach**: Use positive parenting methods that maintain connection while guiding behavior.

## Managing Parental Guilt and Grief

**Acknowledge the grief**: If you feel sad about missing time with your child, take time to mourn what you have lost. Journal about it, talk about it, cry about it, and really acknowledge your grief.

**Why this matters**: If you do not process your own feelings, your child will pick up on the unresolved emotion. Processing grief allows you to be the parent you want to be.

**Reframe the choice**: Even in situations that feel like constraints, recognize that you are choosing the current arrangement. Feeling like a victim undermines your sense of agency and well-being.

## Signs of a Good Caregiver Arrangement

Indicators that a nanny share or daycare situation is working well:
- The child talks about the caregiver and other children when at home
- The child appears happy at pickup
- The caregiver is loving and affectionate
- There is a small child-to-caregiver ratio
- The routine is calm and predictable

## Core Reassurance

Working full time does make the parenting job harder because it is harder to stay connected to the child and because parents are inevitably more stressed and tired. But it is entirely possible to stay deeply connected to your child.

**Key understanding**: When the child feels disconnected, it is not just the time apart but the emotions they have built up and need to express. Once they express those feelings, they can reconnect. Over time, they will gain maturity and will be better able to handle the separation.

## Summary of Key Principles

1. Evening meltdowns after daycare are about processing accumulated emotions, not just wanting attention
2. Children need to empty their emotional backpack after being away from their primary attachment figure
3. Prioritize intensive connection immediately after reunion rather than trying to accomplish other tasks
4. Allow meltdowns to happen rather than preventing them through constant accommodation
5. Physical play and laughter help release stored emotions
6. Separation games played in a silly way help children process separation themes
7. Fewer hours in care per day generally supports better outcomes than fewer total days
8. Parents must process their own grief about working to be fully present
9. Positive discipline becomes even more important because punishment erodes the connection needed to maintain attachment
10. Deep connection is entirely possible while working full time if parents prioritize quality of interaction
