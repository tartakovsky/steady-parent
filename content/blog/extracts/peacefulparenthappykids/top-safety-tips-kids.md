# Family Safety Rules: Teaching Children to Keep Themselves Safe

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids - Safety Rules Every Family Needs

---

## The Core Philosophy: Safety Through Gradual Independence

The fundamental tension in child safety is balancing protection with autonomy. When children are babies and toddlers, parents provide constant supervision or trusted adult care. But as children grow older, parents must "hold their hand less and less." This creates understandable anxiety, especially when children begin navigating sidewalks or public transit independently.

However, overprotection does not serve children. The parental responsibility is to **gradually give children more and more independence**, which requires teaching them how to keep themselves safe in the world without undermining their trust that the world is a good place. Safety education should build competence, not fear.

---

## Rule 1: Prioritize Connection as the Foundation of Safety

**The single most protective factor** against virtually every risk - abuse by predators, bullying, drug use, premature sexual activity - is maintaining a close relationship with your child.

### Why Connection Protects

Safety experts Ric Bentz and Christine Allison explain the mechanism: "Every time you respond to your child's cry of hunger or pain or discomfort, you raise a child who knows he will be heard." Children who feel heard and taken seriously are much more likely to:
- Stick up for themselves
- Fight back when necessary
- Ask for help when they need it

### Practical Connection Strategies

1. **Put down your phone and listen** - undivided attention signals that the child matters
2. **Sit down to dinner together** as many nights as possible
3. **Schedule one-on-one time with each child daily** - aim for at least 15 minutes per child
   - This time should be unstructured (not for homework or reading to them)
   - The purpose is to see what "bubbles up" naturally
   - It creates space for the child to express problems and upsets

### Warning Sign: Defiance or Distance

If you notice your child becoming defiant or distant, treat reconnection as a high priority. These behaviors often signal that the relationship needs repair before safety conversations can be effective.

## Rule 2: Teach Street Crossing as a Practiced Ritual

Adults cross streets so automatically that they often fail to recognize that children need explicit instruction. This skill must be taught through repetition and modeling.

### The Teaching Method for Young Children

When your child is young enough to hold your hand, stop every single time you cross and announce the full process aloud:
- "Let's cross safely"
- "First check the signal - it shows the person walking, so we can cross"
- "Now we look left, then right, then left again"
- "Any cars? Okay, now we can cross!"

This verbalization makes the implicit explicit and builds the habit through repetition.

### When Children Resist Hand-Holding

Older children often rebel against having their hand held. The solution is to **give your child some control** within the safety framework:
- Ask the child to take charge of the ritual and direct you
- Allow alternatives like holding onto your purse strap or wrist
- By the time the child can cross independently, safe habits will be engrained

### The Phone Rule

Looking at your phone while crossing the street is "terrible modeling." Once your child is old enough for a phone, they need the discipline to put it away while crossing - just like you do. This requires parents to model the behavior first.

## Rule 3: Ensure Your Child Knows Contact Information

If you inadvertently get separated in a crowd, your child needs to know:
- Your phone number
- Your home address
- Their own full name

### Making Practice Enjoyable

Turn memorization into a game where the child recites their name, address, and your phone number. Reward correct answers with a funny animal noise or game show gong noise. This makes practice feel like play rather than drilling.

### For Children Who Wander

Some children, particularly many neurodiverse kids, are "runners" who take off without initially realizing they can't see their parent. For these children, consider placing a tracker in their shoe as an additional safety measure.

## Rule 4: Build Anti-Bullying Resilience

### Who Bullies Target

Bullies prey on children whom they perceive to be vulnerable. The best prevention is ensuring children have:
- High self-esteem
- Strong relationships at home
- Strong relationships with peers

### How Bullying Begins: The "Testing the Waters" Pattern

Bullying behavior often starts with a mean remark. The bully is "testing the waters" to see if they can goad the target child into showing hurt or upset. This reaction signals vulnerability and often leads to escalation.

### Preparing Your Child Through Role-Play

If your child is being bullied, role-play how they can:
- Stand up to a bully with quiet dignity
- Walk away

### Reframing Fear and Help-Seeking

Children need explicit reassurance that there is no shame in:
- Being frightened by a bully
- Walking away
- Telling an adult
- Asking for help

The key message: "Bullying situations can escalate, and saving face is less important than saving their life."

## Rule 5: Know Where Your Child Plays and What Happens There

### Getting to Know Other Families

Before playdates, get to know the parents at households where your child spends time. Have ongoing conversations about what goes on at friends' houses.

### Questions to Discuss with Your Child

Prepare your child by discussing scenarios they might encounter:
- Are the kids unsupervised on the computer?
- Are they allowed to walk to the store alone?
- Would they recognize if their friend's mother was inebriated?
- What would they do if their friend's father touched them inappropriately?
- What if their friend suggested looking at porn, playing a "secret" game involving touching, or sniffing markers?

### The Gun Safety Protocol

**Before your child plays at a new friend's house**, ask if they keep guns and how they are secured.

Teach children to leave any room and house immediately if a gun appears, loaded or not.

### Giving Children Social Permission to Leave

It would be ideal if your child could say: "I'm not allowed to be in a room with a gun." However, children will be under great social pressure in that moment, and that statement invites a discussion the child will get sucked into.

A better approach: Any child old enough to be on their own at a playdate understands social lies. Give them permission to use face-saving excuses like: "Oops, I just remembered I have a dentist appointment!"

This works because:
- It doesn't require the child to confront or challenge anyone
- It provides a quick exit without debate
- Children will be grateful for permission to use this kind of social lie

## Rule 6: Replace "Stranger Danger" with Instinct-Based Safety

### Why "Stranger Danger" Fails

Teaching your child to be afraid of strangers does not actually protect them because **most sexual predators are known to the child**. The "Stranger Danger" framework is misguided.

### The Better Framework: Trust and Empowerment

Instead of teaching fear of strangers, teach that:
- Most people are okay
- There are a few people out there who do bad things and could hurt them
- **It is more important to stay safe and trust themselves than to be polite or nice**
- It is okay to question, disobey, and even run away from someone whose behavior makes them acutely uncomfortable

### Predators Give Signals

Predators give warning signals - your child just needs your support to trust themselves in reading those signals. The goal is developing the child's own threat recognition ability.

### What Constitutes Improper Adult Behavior

Explicitly teach that it is inappropriate for adult strangers to:
- Offer children treats
- Ask children for directions

The child's reaction should be to walk away immediately.

### Response Training

Train children that if threatened, they should:
- Fight back
- Shout: "Help me! This is not my parent!"

If they're in a public place and get worried, teach them to run to a mother with a child, who can generally be counted on to help.

## Rule 7: Prevent Sexual Abuse Through Consent, Communication, and Education

### The Statistics

The data is stark:
- One out of every three girls will have suffered some unwanted sexual touching by the time she is sixteen
- For boys, it's one out of six
- According to the National Center for Missing and Exploited Children, **molestation is usually perpetrated by someone the child knows and trusts**

This is why "Stranger Danger" completely misses the point and does not protect children.

### Strategy 1: Teach Consent from Early Childhood

If you want your child to stand up for herself in an abuse situation, **she must be allowed to make her own decisions about who touches her body from an early age**.

**House rules to establish:**
- "We ask people before we touch their body"
- "When someone says STOP!, we stop"

**Parental behaviors to adopt:**
- Don't "steal" kisses or hugs if your child says no
- Never force your child to be touched by a relative or friend if she doesn't want contact

**The Balance:** The child must be respectful. If she doesn't want to hug Grandpa, you can ask her to blow a kiss instead. But the principle is clear: **she must be in charge of her own body**.

### Strategy 2: Establish the "No Secrets" Rule

**Family rule:** In your family, no one ever keeps secrets.

**Why this matters:** Molesters usually begin "grooming" by seducing kids into complicity with mild secrets like: "Don't tell your mom I gave you candy."

**The protocol:** Your child needs to know that anytime anyone asks her to keep a secret, she is to tell you **immediately**.

**Important extension:** This rule applies to kids as well as adults. Children who have been inappropriately touched often "act out" that trauma onto other children. Frequently, it's another child - older in years or experience - who "teaches a secret game" to a child, with tragic results.

**The unconditional message:** Make sure your child knows he can tell you anything, and that you will love him no matter what he's done.

### Strategy 3: Educate About Private Body Parts

Teach your child that every part of their body covered with a swimsuit is private, belonging only to them. Use books designed for this purpose and read them regularly.

### Strategy 4: Protect Through Trusted Supervision Only

Don't leave your child with anyone - even your boyfriend - unless you completely trust them.

**The statistics explained:** The good news and bad news about abuse is that most of it is not perpetrated by strangers.

**Bad news interpretation:** It happens at the hands of family members or the mother's boyfriend. Almost all the rest is perpetrated by trusted intimates such as coaches, religious leaders, or teachers. These are people your child trusts.

**Good news interpretation:** Because abuse is committed by known people rather than random strangers, it's a risk you can usually avoid if you trust your instincts and pay attention to your child.

**Specific guidance for blended families:** Stepparents should never have the responsibility of disciplining their partner's children. This is one of many reasons for this boundary.

Every child should know how to swim. This is presented as non-negotiable basic safety.

## Rule 9: Make Helmets Non-Negotiable

Helmets are required for:
- Cycling
- Skating
- Skateboarding

**The key statistic:** Helmets reduce the risk of brain injury by 90%.

## Rule 10: Car Safety Protocols

### For Parents of Young Children: Prevent Hot Car Deaths

If transporting a little one in the back seat, **train yourself to check the car before you get out** to be sure your child is out. This prevents the tragedy of sleep-deprived parents spacing out and forgetting a sleeping child. Every year, babies and toddlers die when left in cars.

### Train Children to Buckle Up

This should be automatic behavior.

### Escaping Drunk Drivers

Teach children to get out of any car immediately if the driver is drunk. Role-play what they can say to get out of the car and to a safe place.

**The social lie approach:** Saying "I'm carsick! I'm going to throw up! Stop the car quick!" may not be strictly true, but will be much easier for a child to say than "You're behaving erratically and I think you may have been drinking. Please let me out of the car."

**The unconditional promise:** Make sure she knows she can always call you for a ride regardless of the situation. No questions asked, no punishment - just a safe ride home.

### Teen Driver Safety

**The stakes:** Car accidents are the leading cause of death among teens.

**Use personal stories:** Once your teen starts driving, make sure he hears any personal stories you have about kids who've died in car accidents. That story could keep him alive.

**Address distracted driving:** When you see a news story about an accident caused by texting, discuss it at the dinner table. Admit that you're tempted too. Then role model the solution: turn off your phone and put it in your bag in the back seat.

**Navigation:** Need directions? Print them out in advance or pull off the road to check them.

## Rule 11: Gradual Introduction to Public Transit

### The Progression

Public transit independence should be eased into through stages:

1. **Travel with your child** - they observe while you handle everything
2. **Stay near but let them travel "alone"** - you're present but they make the decisions
3. **Let them travel with a friend** - peer support while building independence

### Role-Play Scenarios

Extensive role-play is critical. Scenarios to practice:
- What happens if you and your friend get separated?
- What if someone pulls a knife and demands your money? (This actually happened to the author's 13-year-old on one of his first solo subway trips)
- What if your cell phone falls on the subway tracks?
- What if some guy stares at you and it gives you the creeps?

### Communication Protocol

Buy them a cell phone and establish a check-in routine:
- Call before getting on the bus
- Call after getting off

### Avoiding Being a Target

Be sure they don't use their phone or other electronics en route - **these devices make them a target** for theft.

## Rule 12: Develop Good Judgment as the Ultimate Safety Tool

### The Limits of Supervision

There is no substitute for:
- Supervision
- Knowing what's going on in your child's life

But as your child becomes increasingly independent, they'll need to be aware of their own instincts about what's safe and follow them.

### The Teen Brain Problem

**The challenge:** The brain of a teen is primed to be influenced by peers. This means they can easily override that "still, small voice within" if all the other kids are doing something risky.

**The escalating stakes:** Daredevil behavior is bad enough in a six-year-old, but in a sixteen-year-old it can be deadly.

### The Parenting Task

Consciously help your child develop:
- Good judgment
- Social intelligence

These skills enable them to resist the lure of social pressure when they need to.

## Rule 13: Talk Constantly, Listen More

### The Dual Purpose of Listening

1. **Listening keeps you connected** and helps your child feel safe
2. **Listening helps your child talk to you more** - and when kids talk about something, they're thinking about it

### Using Questions to Develop Judgment

Introduce topics that help your child think, reflect, and develop good judgment. Ask questions like:

- "What do you worry about the most?"
- "If you got into really big trouble, how do you think I would respond?"
- "What are the different kinds of courage? How do you define bravery?"

These open-ended questions prompt reflection and reveal what's on your child's mind.

## Key Recurring Themes

### Social Lies as Safety Tools

Throughout the article, permission to use "social lies" appears as a key safety strategy:
- At playdates with guns: "I just remembered I have a dentist appointment!"
- In a car with a drunk driver: "I'm carsick! I'm going to throw up!"

The reasoning: Children face enormous social pressure in dangerous situations. Confrontational honesty ("You're drunk" or "Guns are dangerous") invites debate and conflict. Face-saving excuses allow quick exits without social cost.

### Role-Playing as Preparation

Role-play appears repeatedly as the preparation method:
- Standing up to bullies with quiet dignity
- Escaping cars with drunk drivers
- Handling public transit emergencies
- Responding to uncomfortable situations

The implicit principle: Children need to practice their responses before they need them. In the moment of danger, they won't be able to think clearly enough to improvise.

### Unconditional Love as Safety Infrastructure

Multiple rules emphasize that the child must know:
- They can tell you anything
- You will love them no matter what they've done
- They can always call for help regardless of the situation

This unconditional acceptance removes barriers to seeking help and reporting problems.

### Trusting Instincts Over Politeness

A core teaching: **Safety takes priority over politeness**. Children must have explicit permission to:
- Question adults
- Disobey adults
- Run away from adults
- Trust their uncomfortable feelings

Without explicit permission, social conditioning toward obedience and politeness can override survival instincts.
