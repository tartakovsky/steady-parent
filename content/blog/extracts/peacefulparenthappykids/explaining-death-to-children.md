# Explaining Death to Children (Toddlers)

Source: Dr. Laura Markham, Peaceful Parent Happy Kids

---

## Core Understanding: How Toddlers Process Death

**Toddlers don't understand the finality of death.** You cannot expect a young child to grasp that their loved one won't return or be available anymore. This fundamental limitation shapes everything about how to approach the topic with them.

The **biggest danger** when a loved one dies is that the child will feel abandoned - that the person chose to leave them or stopped loving them. This fear of abandonment, not the abstract concept of death itself, is what can cause lasting harm.

---

## The Central Principle: Maintaining the Relationship

Children who are able to **maintain a relationship with the person they've lost** while also having **permission to go on living fully and joyfully** make the healthiest adjustment to loss.

This means the goal is not to help the child "get over" the death or "move on" in the sense of forgetting. Instead, the healthy approach involves:
- Keeping the deceased person present in the child's internal world
- Normalizing ongoing connection even after death
- While simultaneously affirming that life continues and joy is allowed

---

## Practical Framework: What to Tell a Toddler About Death

### The Physical Explanation

Use concrete, body-focused language that a toddler can grasp:

1. **Start with sickness and the body:** "Grandpa is very sick. He is old and his body doesn't work so well anymore, so it was weaker and got a disease."

2. **Explain the progression:** "That disease is slowly shutting his body down. He will get weaker but will still be able to hear us even when he is lying very still."

3. **Explain what death means physically:** "Eventually his body will shut down totally. When that happens we will bury his body in the ground."

4. **The body-spirit distinction:** "You will be able to see his body and you will see that it doesn't really look much like him because his wonderful loving, laughing spirit is not there. We honor the body that is left, but it is more like a home where Grandfather lived, more like a shell he has cast off because he no longer needs it."

### The Meaning and Context

**Why death exists:** "That is one of the great mysteries of life. But look around at nature. Everything alive is born, grows up, has a full life, gets old, and dies. It makes room for new life."

**Honoring the life:** "Grandfather had a full life and one of the best parts was having his granddaughter to love."

---

## The Ongoing Connection: How to Keep the Relationship Alive

### Internal Communication

Encourage the child to **speak with the deceased inside their own head**. This is not denial - it's a healthy adaptive mechanism that helps children maintain the relationship while accepting the physical absence.

"When Grandfather's body goes, he will stay in our hearts. If we listen we will be able to feel him smiling at us and even speaking to us."

### Physical Objects as Anchors

**A small object belonging to the deceased** helps the child maintain connection. Having something tangible to hold facilitates the internal relationship.

### Storytelling and Ritual

"One way to express our love is to tell stories about him and honor the life he led, so that is what we do at the funeral."

Funerals and memorial rituals are framed as expressions of love and ways to honor the person's life, not just sad events to endure.

---

## Handling the Child Seeing Adults Cry

### The Reassurance Framework

Children may be deeply concerned seeing their parents cry. Address this directly:

1. **Normalize the crying:** "We are all very sad because we love him so much and when his body shuts down we will never be able to hug him again. That's why we cry so much."

2. **Use analogy they understand:** "It helps us to cry, just like it helps you to cry when you fall down and get hurt. You cry and then you feel better. Because this is such a big hurt, we do a lot of crying."

3. **Give them a role without burden:** "If you see me crying it is ok, it is because I am sad and miss my Dad. You can always give me a hug, but you don't need to take care of me. I will be ok."

The critical phrase here is **"you don't need to take care of me."** This prevents the child from taking on an inappropriate caregiving role or feeling responsible for the parent's emotions.

---

## The Constant Reassurance: Love Doesn't End

Every explanation should reinforce: **his love will always be there inside her heart**.

Love continues after someone dies. The relationship transforms but doesn't end. This directly counters the abandonment fear that is the primary emotional danger for toddlers facing loss.

---

## Young Children and Seeing Deceased Loved Ones

There's an interesting phenomenon: **younger children are more likely to report seeing or communicating with deceased loved ones** after death. This happens because there's no cultural overlay telling them it's not possible.

Whether this is "real" or a psychological defense mechanism doesn't matter - it's described as **"a very adaptive response that has only healthy effects on the child."** Parents should not discourage this if it happens.

---

## The Life-Affirming Message

**"Grandpa would want us to keep living and enjoying life. We have to live extra full and good lives because we are living not just for ourselves now, but also for Grandfather, since he is in our hearts."**

This framing gives the child permission to feel joy without guilt, while also honoring the deceased by "living for them too."

---

## Timing: Before vs. After Death

**More connection before death is better** - for both the dying person and the child. The context of this advice was a grandfather with cancer who has time before death arrives. Maintaining and even increasing connection during the dying process, rather than shielding the child, supports both parties.

---

## Viewings and Funerals with Toddlers

The advice addresses concern about viewings being traumatic:

- Prepare the child that the body won't look like the person they knew
- Frame it as seeing "a shell" or "home" the person no longer needs
- The spirit/essence is what made them who they were, and that's not in the body anymore
- This preparation reduces the shock of seeing a loved one's body

---

## Key Warnings and Anti-Patterns

**Don't leave the child feeling abandoned.** The emotional danger isn't the death itself - it's the child interpreting the absence as rejection or abandonment.

**Don't burden the child with caretaking.** Explicitly tell them they don't need to take care of grieving adults, even while allowing them to offer comfort like hugs.

**Don't discourage ongoing "relationship" with the deceased.** Whether the child talks to them, sees them, or just thinks about them - this is healthy, not denial.
