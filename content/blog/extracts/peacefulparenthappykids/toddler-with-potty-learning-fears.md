# Helping Toddlers Through Potty Learning Fears

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/toddler-with-potty-learning-fears

## Core Insight: Potty Training Anxiety Shows Up in Unexpected Ways

When a toddler begins potty training, even one who is physically ready and succeeding, the emotional challenge can manifest as seemingly unrelated behavioral changes. A child who is doing well with the actual potty use may simultaneously become clingy, cry more, want to nurse more frequently, throw things, or even hit themselves. These behaviors signal that the child is carrying fear and anxiety about this major milestone, even when the surface-level performance looks successful.

The key understanding is that potty training is a huge milestone that naturally generates fear, and this fear needs an outlet. The anxiety does not just disappear because the child is physically managing the skill. It accumulates and comes out sideways through these other behaviors.

## Primary Strategy: Laughter as Fear Release

**Giggling is the best way for toddlers to release stored anxiety.** The therapeutic mechanism here is that laughter allows children to process and discharge fears they cannot articulate or consciously address.

### The Parent-Models-Fear Game

The most powerful specific technique is for the parent to play out potty fears in an exaggerated, silly way:

1. **Pretend you (the parent) need to use the bathroom urgently**
2. **Dance around dramatically while saying you're too scared to go**
3. **Let the child help you get to the bathroom and succeed**
4. **Express exaggerated relief afterward**

The entire sequence should be performed in an over-the-top manner designed to generate giggles. If the child does not immediately recognize you are playing, wink at them at the beginning to signal this is a game.

**Why this works:** When the parent models the fear in a safe, silly context, the child can laugh at the very thing causing them anxiety. The power dynamic is reversed: instead of feeling scared themselves, they are in the helper role assisting the scared parent. This gives them a sense of mastery over the fear.

### Extending the Play

Build on this foundation with any potty-related silliness that generates laughter:
- Sing nonsense songs about potty topics
- Wear toilet paper as a hat
- Wear a diaper as a hat
- Any absurd potty/bathroom humor that makes the child giggle

The content matters less than the emotional release through laughter.

### Addressing Specific Fears Through Dialogue in Play

During these games, weave in reassuring dialogue that targets common potty fears. Use a question-and-answer format between yourself and another adult (or ask the questions rhetorically):

**Example fear to voice:** "What if I can't make it to the bathroom? Oh no! That would be a disaster!"

**Example reassuring response:** "Don't worry, I will love you no matter what. Everyone has accidents sometimes."

**Why this specific approach matters:** The fear of not making it in time, of having an accident, is a central anxiety for potty training children. By voicing this fear explicitly in play and pairing it with unconditional acceptance, you are directly addressing what the child cannot articulate: that they fear accidents mean something bad about them or will cost them your love.

## Secondary Strategy: Physical Edge-of-Scary Games

Beyond potty-specific play, any physical games that are "just on the edge of scary" help children process generalized fear:

- "Boo" games (mild startle followed by laughter)
- Riding on a parent's back while the parent gallops around the house
- Any physical play that creates a tiny thrill followed by laughter

**Why this works even without potty connection:** These games have no obvious connection to potty issues, but they help discharge fear in general. Fear is fear - releasing it through one channel (physical play) reduces the overall burden the child is carrying, even if the source (potty anxiety) is different from the trigger (being startled or going fast).

## Third Strategy: Welcoming Emotional Meltdowns

Sometimes the stored fear comes out not through laughter but through intense crying or tantrums over seemingly trivial matters. This is equally valuable as the laughter route.

### How to Facilitate This Release

If the child tries to throw something, catch their arm gently and say: **"I won't let you throw that, Sweetie."** This kind, clear limit can unlock the feelings the child has been holding.

**What to expect:** The resulting meltdown may include flailing and struggling. This physical movement during distress is specifically how children let out fear they have stored up.

**What to do:** Welcome the feelings. Do not try to stop the crying or distract from it. Hold space for the emotional release.

**What happens afterward:** Once the child has off-loaded the tension through this crying/meltdown, you will see them become more relaxed and return to their usual sunny temperament.

### The Mechanics Explained

The sequence is: mild external trigger (the limit) -> emotional dam breaks -> fear releases through crying/flailing -> child feels lighter and returns to baseline.

The "fit" about something inconsequential is actually about the bigger fear. The small issue just provides the opening for the bigger feelings to come through.

## Critical Warning: Parental Pride Can Create Pressure

**This is the most important caveat in the entire piece.**

Even highly loving, attentive parents can inadvertently create pressure through their visible pride in accomplishments. When children sense that parents care intensely about specific achievements like using the potty, this creates a burden of responsibility that is too heavy for a toddler.

**The specific dynamic:** When a child feels that "the sun rises and sets with her accomplishments," she experiences this as pressure, not just love. The parent's joy in her success becomes something she must maintain, which adds anxiety rather than reducing it.

**The distinction:** You cannot give a child too much love. But you can make them feel too much responsibility for your emotional state through your investment in their performance.

**The prescription:** Be a bit more relaxed about expressing pride in accomplishments. This relaxation in the parent leads to relaxation in the child. This applies not just to potty training but throughout childhood.

**Why this matters for potty training specifically:** If the child senses that successful potty use is incredibly important to the parents, accidents become higher stakes. The fear of disappointing becomes layered on top of the physical challenge.

## Summary of the Three Release Mechanisms

1. **Laughter** - through silly potty play where parent models fear
2. **Physical play** - through edge-of-scary games that generate giggles
3. **Crying/meltdowns** - through welcoming emotional release after setting kind limits

All three accomplish the same goal: helping the child discharge the anxiety that potty training naturally generates.

## Context and Reassurance

The behavioral changes (clinginess, increased nursing, throwing things, hitting self) during potty training, even when physical progress is good, are normal. The child is "doing just fine" even with these behaviors. They indicate fear that needs release, not a problem with the potty training approach itself.

The solution is not to stop potty training (especially if the child refuses to go back to diapers) but to provide outlets for the emotional burden that accompanies this milestone.
