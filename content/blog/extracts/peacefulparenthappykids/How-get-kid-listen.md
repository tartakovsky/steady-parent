# How to Get Your Child to Listen

Source: Peaceful Parent Happy Kids (Dr. Laura Markham)

## Why Children Don't Listen - The Underlying Neuroscience

Children genuinely struggle to hear and process speech in ways adults don't. This isn't defiance or disrespect - it's neurology.

**Immature auditory processing**: Until age sixteen, children's brains are still learning to make sense of the sounds they hear. Research shows that when asked to listen to someone and repeat back what they heard, children struggle significantly when other people are talking in the background. This isn't willful ignoring - their brains literally cannot filter speech from noise as effectively as adult brains can.

**Background noise impact**: Even white-noise sounds like washing machines and dishwashers diminish speech-discernment accuracy for children under age ten. An air conditioner, music, or conversations in the background create real auditory interference.

**Brain rewiring periods**: At age six and again at age twelve, the brain undergoes significant rewiring. During these periods, children often feel overwhelmed by outside stimuli and naturally tune out more than usual.

**Competing priorities and focus**: Children have a lot occupying their minds - school tests, sports tryouts, video games, exploration. Parents rank dismally low on their priority list. Toddlers are especially absorbed in their "job" of exploring (tearing the house apart). When a child is focused on something, their brain actively works to block out extraneous noise. Calling from across the room simply won't register.

**The real question**: When parents ask "how do I get my child to listen," they aren't really asking about auditory perception. They want to know how to get their child to take in what they say AND take action. This requires specific techniques beyond just speaking louder.

## The Three Core Principles

All specific techniques reduce to three foundational practices:

1. **Calm yourself** - Your emotional regulation comes first
2. **Connect** - Establish relationship before communication
3. **Engage cooperation with empathy** - Work with human nature, not against it

These principles work for communication with anyone of any age. Parents forget them with children because we unconsciously believe children should simply do what we say when we say it. But children are humans, and humans don't like being ordered around. **You'll always get better cooperation when you connect before you direct.**

## Technique 1: Connect Before Speaking

**The principle**: Don't start talking until you have your child's attention. Barking orders from across the room does not work and should not be expected to work.

**The process**:

1. **Move in close** - Physical proximity is required
2. **Get down on their level** - Match their eye height
3. **Touch lightly** - Physical connection signals importance
4. **Observe and acknowledge what they're doing** - Make a comment about their activity ("Wow, look at that train go!"). This isn't manipulation; it's acknowledging and respecting what matters to them
5. **Wait for them to look up** - Don't speak until you have eye contact
6. **If they don't look up, request attention explicitly** - Ask "Can I tell you something?" and wait for them to look at you
7. **Only then start talking** - After eye contact is established

**Why this works**: Brain research shows that when we feel connected to another person, we become more open to their influence. By connecting first, you make it neurologically easier for your child to receive your message.

**The reciprocity principle**: Don't be surprised when your child starts using this technique to get YOUR attention. And if you want them to keep listening to you, you'll need to listen back when they do this.

## Technique 2: Don't Repeat Yourself

If you've asked once and gotten no response, repeating the same words is futile. The lack of response means you don't have their attention. **Return to Technique 1** - the connection step - rather than raising volume or repeating instructions.

Repetition without connection teaches children to ignore the first several requests because nothing happens until you escalate.

## Technique 3: Use Fewer Words

**The problem**: Most parents dilute their message and lose their child's attention by using too many words. Children tune out during long explanations.

**The solution**: Use the minimum words necessary to convey your instruction.

**Example approach for a jacket on the floor**:

1. Smile warmly, point to the jacket, say "Julia! Jacket please!"
2. Stand there smiling and pointing until she moves
3. If no response, pick up the jacket and hand it to her, still smiling: "Jacket goes on hook, please!"
4. If still ignored, make a game of it - put the jacket over whatever she's focusing on

**The underlying insight**: Kids don't see demands like hanging up jackets as important. When we stay cheerful while expressing expectations and setting firm, clear, kind - and even fun - limits, they become much more likely to cooperate. The tone and brevity matter more than elaborate explanations.

## Technique 4: Acknowledge Their Point of View

**The empathy test**: If you were busy with something you enjoyed and your partner ordered you to stop and do something that wasn't a priority to you, how would you feel? Would you tune them out?

**The key distinction**: Your child doesn't have to share your priorities - they just have to accommodate your needs. You don't have to share their priorities either, but acknowledging what they want helps immensely.

**Language example**: "I know it's hard to stop playing, Honey. You're really enjoying that, and I can see why! And now it's time to..."

This acknowledgment doesn't mean caving to their wishes. It means validating their experience before redirecting their behavior.

## Technique 5: Engage Cooperation (Don't Give Orders)

**The resistance principle**: No one wants to listen to someone giving orders. Orders stimulate resistance in humans of all ages. Think about how YOU feel when someone orders you around - do you cooperate enthusiastically?

**Instead**:
- Keep your tone warm
- Give choices when possible

**Choice example**: "It's bath time, Sweetie. Do you want to go now or in five minutes? Ok, five minutes with no fuss? Let's shake on it."

**Following through example**: "We agreed to go inside in five minutes, and it has been five minutes. I know, you wish you could stay outside and play all night. When you grow up, I bet you'll play all night every night! Now, it's time to go in. Let's go."

Notice how following through combines firmness with empathy - acknowledging their wish while maintaining the boundary.

## Technique 6: Stay Calm

**The neuroscience of upset**: When we get upset, kids feel unsafe and go into fight or flight mode. In their effort to defend themselves or fight back, they become LESS effective at listening and lose sight of your message entirely. Your emotional escalation creates a neurological barrier to comprehension.

**Practical application**: If your priority is getting everyone in the car, don't waste time and energy lecturing them about why they didn't listen and get ready when you first asked. That lecture will make everyone more upset, including you.

**Instead**: Take a deep breath. Help her find her shoe. Help him with his backpack. Get in the car. THEN, once everyone is calm, ask them to help brainstorm ways to get everyone out of the house on time next time.

**Critical note**: Problem-solving conversations are more productive when focused on solutions, not blame. The debrief should be collaborative, not punitive.

## Technique 7: Set Up Routines

**The nagging problem**: Most parent-to-child communication consists of nagging. No wonder children don't listen - they've learned to filter out repetitive commands.

**The solution**: Routines reduce power struggles and eliminate the need for you to be a drill sergeant. Routines are regular habits that become automatic.

**Example routine elements** (before leaving the house): brush teeth, use toilet, pack backpack, put on shoes

**Visual support technique**: Take photos of your child doing each task and create a small poster. Over time, your child learns the sequence. Put them in charge of their own routine.

**The benefit**: Your child gains a new skill and sense of mastery. Your role shrinks to asking questions: "What else do you have to do before you leave the house? Let's check your schedule. Great!"

This transforms you from order-giver to supportive guide.

## Technique 8: Model Listening

**The reciprocity problem**: If you stare at your screen while your child tells you about their day, you're demonstrating how communication works in your family.

**The solution**: If you want your child to listen to you, stop what you're doing and listen to them. It only takes a few minutes.

**Long-term payoff**: Start this when they're a preschooler and they'll still be willing to talk to you when they're a teenager. The investment in attention now builds the relationship that keeps communication open later.

## Technique 9: Watch for Understanding Issues

**The distinction**: Most of the time when kids don't "listen," they simply haven't tuned in. This is normal and addressed by the techniques above.

**The warning sign**: If your child REPEATEDLY seems unable to process your instructions even when you have their attention, there may be an **auditory processing disorder**.

**Diagnostic approach**: Adopt the connection techniques and experiment with giving multi-step instructions. If concerns persist, consult your pediatrician for referral to an audiologist.

## Technique 10: Reduce the Volume of Orders

**The workplace analogy**: If you worked for someone who constantly badgered you with orders, would you feel like cooperating?

**The principle**: You don't want every interaction with your child to be an order. This trains them to tune you out as background noise.

**The balance**: Maximize loving, happy interactions. Minimize orders. Pare down your demands to only what's truly non-negotiable.

When orders are rare, they carry more weight.

## Technique 11: Use Playfulness

**Parent testimony**: "Kids not listening is like my parenting kryptonite. I can keep my cool in the face of many things but this is the hardest. I inadvertently discovered this week that my kids are much better at doing what I want when I use a hand puppet to ask them to do it. At first I was like WHAT IS THIS MAGIC but reading this article I can see that the puppet is much better than I am at a lot of these things - connecting before speaking, staying calm, engaging cooperation... basically, I should just take parenting lessons from my own hand puppet!" - Robin M.

**The insight**: Play and puppets work because they naturally incorporate all the core techniques - they create connection, maintain calm (it's hard to be angry through a puppet), and engage rather than demand. The playful approach makes it easy to implement the principles that are harder when we're frustrated.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**Yelling from across the room**: Won't penetrate the child's auditory filtering, especially with any background noise

**Repeating yourself**: Signals you didn't have attention; return to connection step instead

**Using too many words**: Dilutes message, loses attention

**Giving orders in demanding tone**: Stimulates resistance rather than cooperation

**Getting upset**: Triggers fight/flight, blocks listening

**Lecturing in the moment**: Wastes time, escalates everyone's emotion, doesn't teach

**Focusing on blame during debriefs**: Less productive than focusing on solutions

**Constant nagging**: Trains children to tune you out

**Expecting immediate compliance without connection**: Ignores how human communication actually works

**Ignoring your child when they talk to you**: Models the behavior you don't want from them

## Summary Framework

**Before speaking**: Connect (proximity, eye level, touch, acknowledge their focus, wait for eye contact)

**While speaking**: Few words, warm tone, choices when possible

**When they don't comply**: Check - did you have connection? Return to step one. Stay calm. Make it a game if possible.

**Overall relationship**: Model listening. Minimize orders. Maximize positive interaction. Use routines to reduce the need for commands.

**The fundamental truth**: Children are humans. Humans don't like being ordered around. Connection before direction is not a parenting trick - it's how effective communication works with people of any age.
