# Handling Sexual Curiosity and Potty Talk Obsession in Young Children

Source: https://www.peacefulparenthappykids.com/read/6-year-old-obsessed-with-sex-potty-talk

## Understanding Normal vs. Concerning Sexual Behavior in Children

### The Spectrum from Curiosity to Obsession

**Normal curiosity** about bodies and potty-related topics is developmentally expected in young children. Three-year-olds typically go through a phase of potty talk as they work to "master" concepts related to toileting. This is a healthy developmental process.

**Preoccupation** - spending significant time thinking about these topics - also falls within normal range. Children naturally work through new concepts by focusing on them.

**Obsession over several years**, however, crosses into concerning territory. The key distinction: children normally master each developmental concept and move on to the next. When a child remains stuck on the same material for years (in this case, from age 3 to 6), it signals something is blocking the normal mastery process. The child is trying hard to work through something upsetting but cannot resolve it internally.

**External feedback matters**: When a teacher expresses concern about the frequency or intensity of behavior, this is a significant data point that the behavior exceeds typical developmental ranges.

### Concerning Sexual Behaviors That Require Attention

Certain behaviors in children are distinctly unusual because they require knowledge that young children do not naturally possess. **Children do not spontaneously think about kissing genitals** - this concept would seem gross to them without external exposure. The idea must be "planted from somewhere."

This is different from general body curiosity. It is common for children bathing together to become curious about each other's bodies. They might even engage in "I'll let you touch mine if you let me touch yours" play when unsupervised. But specific sexual acts like kissing or oral contact with genitals indicate exposure to sexual content or experiences.

### Nursing-Related Behavior: A Nuanced Case

If an older child offers to "nurse" a younger sibling, this is "actually fairly normal" in the context of ongoing breastfeeding in the home. The dynamics behind it include:
- Jealousy of the younger child receiving nursing
- Wishes to have capabilities like mom (produce milk, get pregnant)
- General curiosity about the nursing relationship

However, the **timing** matters for interpretation. If nursing has been ongoing for a long time but the offer to nurse a sibling is new, the question becomes: why now? New behaviors warrant investigation into what prompted them.

## Possible Sources of Sexual Knowledge in Children

When a child displays sexual knowledge they shouldn't have, the ideas came from somewhere. Sources to consider:

**Media exposure**: Magazines, websites, movies, or photos containing sexual content. Even brief exposure to images of sexual acts can be "very stimulating to a little guy" and cause obsessive thinking.

**Peer interactions**: Another child at school may have suggested sexual behaviors or described sexual scenes. Children share information with each other, sometimes information that came from their own problematic exposures.

**Sexual abuse**: This is a possibility that must be considered, though it is uncomfortable. Abuse does not have to be extreme to cause lasting impact - it "would not have to have been a 'big' thing." The specific behaviors a child later reproduces often mirror what they experienced (e.g., asking to "kiss" rather than "suck" may reflect the specific language or action used with them).

The key principle: **children act out what they have encountered**. When you see behavior that seems to require specific knowledge, trace backward to identify the source.

## Sexual Orientation: Not the Concern Here

**Sexual orientation appears to be "hard-wired before birth"** for men and probably largely for women as well. A young child engaging in sexual play with a same-sex sibling is not an indicator of future gay or straight orientation.

**Children this age "don't care about the gender of who touches their penis"** - they are responding to sensation and curiosity, not gender-based attraction.

While repeated sexual encounters can create associations with stimuli involved (the example given: "lots of early sex with a woman in a red dress may make red an erotic and arousing color"), this conditioning mechanism is not relevant to early childhood sibling play.

**The actual priorities**: protecting younger siblings from inappropriate sexual contact, and helping the child experiencing obsession work through whatever is causing it.

## Intervention Strategies

### Immediate Safety Measures

**Supervise children constantly when together**. If you believe constant supervision "is not possible," you need to make it possible. Practical approach: keep either the younger or older child with you at all times.

**Eliminate high-stimulation situations**. Bathing together, even though the children enjoy it, is "too stimulating" when one child is exhibiting sexual preoccupation. Remove the opportunity rather than rely on behavioral control.

### Therapeutic Play: Daily Special Time

Commit to **20 minutes of daily special time** with the child exhibiting concerning behavior. Frame it as "a new game you want to play with him." The goal is to help the child release tension and move through whatever issue is blocking normal development.

#### Game Types and Their Purposes

**"Who puts what where" game** (from Larry Cohen's Playful Parenting): One person begs the other to let them put their finger in their ear. The action is not actually completed, but the play induces giggles around themes of "what's gross" and "what goes where." This allows the child to process bodily boundaries through humor.

**Stuffed animal roleplay for potty talk**: Act out scenarios where stuffed animals engage in potty talk. Allow the child to say "as many potty words as he wants" through his stuffed animal character, but only in this contained context (his room, in private, with parent present). **Follow where the child takes the game** - this reveals what is on his mind.

**Stuffed animal roleplay for sexual content**: Progress to having the stuffed animals "talk about penises." The parent's animal acts "dense" and asks questions. Let the child, through his animal, share "everything he knows about penises."

Key questions to work toward through the stuffed animal dialogue:
- "Who has penises?"
- "Who can touch someone else's penis?" (Answer to establish: "no one, not even a parent, once you no longer need help in the bathroom")
- "Has anyone ever touched your penis?"
- "Kissed your penis?"

The stuffed animal medium allows the child to disclose information that would be too threatening to share directly.

**Power games**: Games where the child wins, overcomes the parent, or demonstrates strength. The reasoning: any experience involving the child's penis may have "made him feel humiliated or small." Power games restore a sense of agency and counteract feelings of powerlessness. Resources: Lawrence Cohen's book and website, and Dr. Laura Markham's website.

### Establishing Boundaries Around Potty Talk

**After special time (not during)**, make clear that the potty-talk games are only for special time with the parent, not for other contexts.

**Responding when the child uses potty words at other times**:
1. Move in close
2. Get on his level
3. Make eye contact
4. Say firmly: "That's only for our special time, sweetie. Not for now."

### When Limits Trigger Emotional Release

If the child resists the limit about potty talk, this is diagnostic. Two possibilities:

**Power struggle pattern**: If the child generally has difficulty accepting limits across contexts, this is a broader discipline issue to address (see resources on discipline and power struggles).

**Signaling for help**: If defiance is not a typical pattern for this child, resistance to limits on potty talk means the underlying issue is "too big to just let off tension" - the child needs to do emotional healing work.

**The meltdown is the healing**. If setting the limit leads to raging and crying, this is "great" - it means the child is "getting to the big hurt or fear that is under his obsession." Allow the tantrum. Be present but do not try to stop the emotional release.

The **combination of games plus limits/releasing** will "release your son's tension and will ease his obsession."

## Timeline for Improvement and When to Seek Professional Help

**One month** is a reasonable timeline to see "fairly quick improvement" in the obsession when using these strategies consistently.

If improvement does not occur within one month, consider therapy. The strategies may not be sufficient for the underlying issue, or professional support may help the parent navigate complex material that emerges.

**During the process**: If material comes up in the games that the parent does not feel comfortable addressing, seek coaching support rather than trying to handle it alone.

## Reassurance and Perspective

**The most likely explanation** for unusual sexual behavior in a child without obvious media exposure: the child had an interaction with another child that included either actual kissing of a penis or discussion of such, and the child "left that interaction obsessed because he couldn't make sense of it."

**The games will "heal" the hurts and fears**, and thus the obsession. The child is showing that he needs help, and once he receives appropriate support, "he will be fine."

**Emotional regulation for the parent**: "Stay calm. Breathe." The child needs the parent's help now, and the parent's ability to remain regulated supports the child's healing process.
