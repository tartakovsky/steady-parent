# The Do's and Don'ts of Toddler Discipline: Complete Extract

Source: Big Little Feelings

## Core Reframe: Discipline Means Teaching, Not Punishment

The word "discipline" derives from "to teach." The goal is to teach children how to shift from unwanted behavior to wanted behavior, not to punish them into compliance. This distinction fundamentally changes how parents should approach behavior correction.

## Why Punishment-Based Discipline Fails

**Fear is a terrible teacher** and does not produce long-term behavioral change.

When discipline tactics are rooted in punishment (fear-based approaches), they appear to work in the moment because fear activates the **fight, flight, or freeze response** in the brain. The toddler stops the behavior immediately (freeze response), but after the punishment ends, they repeat the same behavior.

This happens because fear short-circuits learning. The child's brain goes into survival mode rather than processing the lesson about why the behavior was wrong.

## The Developmental Reality of Toddler Brains

**Toddler brains are continually under construction**, trying to make sense of how the world works. Key limitations include:

1. **Difficulty connecting dots between experiences**: Toddlers struggle to link one event to another, especially across time gaps
2. **Time is an extremely hard concept**: This causes toddlers to feel overwhelmed and confused
3. **Memory limitations**: It is difficult for toddler brains to recall events from something that happened hours earlier

Evidence of this: A toddler "forgets" that bedtime comes after bath time despite this sequence happening identically every single day. If they cannot retain this simple routine, expecting them to connect a park incident to a dinnertime consequence is unrealistic.

## Why Unrelated Consequences Do Not Work: The Park-to-Dessert Example

**Scenario breakdown:**

A toddler throws a ball in another child's face at the park. The parent threatens: "If you throw the ball one more time, you won't get dessert tonight!" The toddler throws the ball again. At dinner, dessert is withheld from the offending child.

**Why this fails (two reasons):**

1. **Temporal disconnect**: The toddler brain cannot recall events from five hours earlier with enough clarity to connect them to a current consequence
2. **Zero logical connection**: There is no relationship between misusing a ball and losing dessert. What do those things have to do with one another?

**Result**: The toddler experiences the consequence as arbitrary and incomprehensible, leading to epic meltdown and no learning. Even after the parent explains why dessert was withheld, the child still does not understand because the connection was never there to begin with.

**The foundational principle**: Without connection between behavior and consequence, there will be no correction. Once children start to grasp the connection between what they do and what happens as a result, they become empowered to make different choices.

## The Solution: Related Consequences

**Related consequences** (also called logical consequences) are completely and directly related to the undesired behaviors. They work because they create clear cause-and-effect understanding in the toddler's developing brain.

### Key Characteristics of Effective Related Consequences

1. **Quick**: Delivered immediately or very close to when the behavior occurred
2. **Relevant**: Directly connected to the specific misbehavior
3. **Clear**: Easy for the toddler brain to understand the link

### The Correct Approach: Park Example Reimagined

**The situation**: Toddler throws ball in another child's face.

**The response**: Walk over to the toddler. Using a calm, cool voice, say: "Balls are for bouncing. Balls are not for throwing at faces. I see you're having a hard time playing with the ball, so the ball is going bye-bye. We'll try again tomorrow. Let's check on [the other child] now to make sure he's okay."

**Why this works**: The consequence is both quick and relevant, setting up the toddler's brain for success. The synapses fire and the child processes: "When I used the ball to smash brother's face, I didn't get to play with the ball anymore. So if I want to play with the ball, I can't smash it in people's faces."

### Examples of Related Consequences

| Undesired Behavior | Related Consequence |
|---|---|
| Running into the street | Holding hands or going in stroller |
| Throwing the iPad | iPad goes on the shelf for the rest of the day |
| Drawing on the walls with markers | Take away markers for the afternoon |

### Building the Skill

The more you practice related consequences, the faster you will come up with them in the moment. Consistency with this approach produces multiple benefits:

- Fewer unwanted behaviors over time
- Child develops **problem-solving** abilities
- Child develops **self-awareness**
- Child develops **self-confidence**

### Repetition Is Expected

It may take a few more encounters with the related consequence for your toddler to really absorb the lesson. This is normal and does not mean the approach is failing. Research confirms that related consequences work, but learning takes time.

## The Parent's Role: Calm, Confident Leadership

The recommended approach positions the parent as the **calm, confident leader of the home**. This means:

- No yelling
- No shaming
- No threatening
- Avoiding becoming the parent you swore you would never be

The calm, cool voice delivery is not just about tone but about modeling emotional regulation while enforcing boundaries.

## What to Avoid: Time-Outs and Spanking

**Time-outs and spanking should not be part of your discipline plan.**

Effective discipline teaches three things:

1. All feelings are OK
2. Certain behaviors are not acceptable
3. Better coping skills for next time

Neither time-outs nor spanking accomplishes these goals. Both techniques are:

- **Ineffective** at producing lasting behavioral change
- **Potentially counterproductive**: Can lead to more bad behavior
- **Harmful to development**: Can prevent your toddler from developing healthy coping skills

## Compassion for Parents

Many parents discipline the way they were parented themselves. Sometimes you do not know what you do not know. If you have used time-outs and spanking in the past, or if you have lost your composure with your child, do not beat yourself up about it. Tomorrow is a new day, and awareness of new tools enables change.

## Additional Consideration: Dessert as Reward

Elevating dessert as a reward or using it as punishment leverage creates separate issues around mealtime battles. This is noted as a distinct problem from the discipline approach itself.

## Summary of Core Principles

1. Discipline means to teach, not to punish
2. Fear-based approaches produce freeze responses, not learning
3. Toddler brains cannot connect temporally distant events
4. Unrelated consequences produce confusion, not behavior change
5. Related consequences must be quick and directly connected to the behavior
6. The parent's demeanor should be calm and confident
7. Consistency with related consequences builds the child's internal problem-solving abilities
8. Time-outs and spanking do not meet the criteria for effective discipline
9. Past parenting mistakes do not define future parenting capability
