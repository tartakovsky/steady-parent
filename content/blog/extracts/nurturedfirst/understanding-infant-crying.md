# Understanding Infant Crying: A Comprehensive Guide

**Source:** Nurtured First
**Author:** Jess VanderWier, MA, RP (Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** February 13, 2023

---

## Core Reframe: What Infant Crying Actually Means

### The Fundamental Mindset Shift

**Crying is not a parental failure signal.** When a baby cries, they are not communicating that the parent is bad or failing them. Instead, crying represents a message of trust: "I trust you, and need you to get curious about what is going on for me."

**Crying is normal infant development.** It is how babies make sense of the world. This is a biological reality, not a problem to be eliminated.

### Why the "No Crying" Mindset Fails

The goal of preventing all infant crying is:
- **Exhausting** for parents
- **Unattainable** because babies will inevitably cry
- **Anxiety-producing** when parents internalize this impossible standard

Babies are inherently unpredictable. They may sleep on various schedules and have unique individual needs that defy standardization.

---

## The Natural Stress Response to Crying

**Parental stress when hearing crying is biologically normal.** The sound triggers a stress response by design. This response exists because infant crying is often outside of parental control, which can be triggering for many people.

**The desire to stop crying indicates healthy parenting instincts.** Wanting to help the baby stop crying demonstrates love and care, not weakness or overreaction.

## Anti-Patterns: What to Avoid

### Commercial Exploitation of Parental Anxiety

Many products, courses, and materials are marketed with an implicit or explicit message that:
- Infant crying is inherently wrong or abnormal
- Parents who cannot immediately soothe their babies are doing something wrong

This marketing creates unnecessary anxiety. The reality: Parents can use every soothing technique available (shushing, swinging, etc.) and babies will still sometimes cry.

### The Rush-to-Soothe Trap

When parents rush to stop crying as quickly as possible, they:
- Fail to take time to tune into what the infant actually needs
- Miss the opportunity for genuine curiosity about the baby's state
- Prioritize noise elimination over understanding

## The Three-Part Response Framework

### 1. Crying as a Call to Tune In (Not a Call to Silence)

**Reframe the cry:** Think of it as an invitation to tune into what the baby needs, not as an alarm requiring immediate silencing.

**The response sequence:**
1. Take a breath
2. Calm your own emotional reaction (acknowledge that crying is difficult to listen to)
3. Get curious about what the baby is trying to communicate

**Diagnostic questions to consider:**
- Are they hungry?
- Do they need to be changed?
- Are they cold?
- Are they in pain?
- Are they simply frustrated and expressing unhappiness about how they feel?

**Key instruction:** Release yourself from the pressure of instant soothing. Instead, slow down your own body's response to the crying.

### 2. Communicate with Your Baby

**Start authentic communication early.** Even though babies may not understand the specific words being used, verbal communication serves important developmental functions:
- Sets the stage for emotional regulation skills
- Helps babies learn to process big feelings as they grow older
- Models that emotions are safe to express

**Important distinction:** Use authentic communication, not stereotypical "baby talk." Speak to the baby as you would communicate meaningfully.

**When all needs are met but crying continues:**
1. Hold the baby
2. Provide physical comfort
3. Verbally acknowledge their state

**Example script:** "I see you crying right now, you are feeling upset. I am not sure how to help you, so I am going to hold you."

**What this communicates to the baby:**
- You are present and attentive
- You can handle their emotions
- Their emotions are safe with you, even now in infancy
- Solutions are not always required; presence is sufficient

### 3. Prioritize Parental Mental Health

**Causal chain:** When parents do well, babies can do well.

**Mental health challenges intensify the crying response.** Feeling anxious or depressed can cause the sound of baby cries to send the parent's body into high alert, making the experience significantly more difficult to manage.

**Action required:** Do not hesitate to reach out for support if struggling with mental health during the postpartum period.

## The Mutual Learning Framework

**Core affirmation for parents:**

"Just like my baby is learning the world for the first time, I am learning their world for the first time too. It is ok if we don't have it all figured out. I can give myself and my baby grace as we learn from each other and learn how to figure out this new world."

**Key principles embedded in this framework:**
- Both parent and baby are beginners in this specific relationship
- Not having all answers is acceptable and expected
- Grace toward self and baby is essential
- Learning is bidirectional: parent learns about baby while baby learns about world
- The relationship is a collaborative discovery process

---

## Summary of Key Recommendations

1. **Normalize crying** as a healthy part of infant development rather than treating it as a problem
2. **Resist the pressure** from commercial products/courses that frame crying as parental failure
3. **Slow your own stress response** before attempting to address the baby's needs
4. **Get curious** about what the baby might be communicating rather than rushing to silence
5. **Speak authentically** to your baby, acknowledging their emotional state verbally
6. **Hold and comfort** even when you cannot identify or solve the underlying issue
7. **Prioritize your mental health** because parental wellbeing directly affects capacity to respond to infant needs
8. **Practice self-compassion** for not having all the answers immediately
