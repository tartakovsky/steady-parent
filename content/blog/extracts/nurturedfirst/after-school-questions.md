# How To Encourage Your Child To Open Up About Their School Day

**Source:** Nurtured First (Shannon Wassenaar, Registered Psychotherapist)
**Published:** March 5, 2024
**Reviewed by:** Nurtured First's team of child development experts

## Core Problem

Parents frequently struggle to get more than single-word answers ("Good," "Yes," "Fine") when asking children about their school day. This creates discouragement for parents who want to connect with their children but cannot elicit meaningful responses.

## Underlying Causes of Child Silence

1. **Overwhelm from broad questions**: When children are asked vague questions like "How was your day?" they do not know how to answer because so much happened between morning and afternoon. The breadth of the question causes them to "freeze up."

2. **Feeling unheard**: When children do not feel listened to, they often stop talking entirely. Signs that make children feel unheard include parents being on phones, unpacking bags, or doing chores while asking questions.

3. **Conversation feeling forced or awkward**: Direct interrogation-style questioning can feel unnatural and pressured, causing children to shut down.

4. **Lack of framework for expression**: Some children struggle to share because they do not know how to put their experiences into words.

5. **Feelings being minimized or misunderstood**: When parents respond with dismissive phrases, children learn that sharing feelings is not safe.

6. **Timing issues**: Bombarding children with questions immediately upon arrival home can be overwhelming before they have had time to decompress.

## Strategy 1: Ask Detailed Questions

### Core Principle
Detailed questions yield detailed answers. Breaking down the day into smaller, manageable parts reduces overwhelm.

### Why This Works
Children feel overwhelmed by broad questions because the scope is too large to process. Specific questions feel more manageable and typically result in more detailed responses.

### Implementation Techniques

**Break down the day into smaller parts:**
- Instead of: "How was school?"
- Try: "Who did you sit with on the bus this afternoon?"

- Instead of: "What did you do today?"
- Try: "What game did you play during recess?"

**Use their school schedule as a conversation starter:**
- Get familiar with your child's class schedule, routines, and extracurricular activities
- The more specific the question, the less overwhelming it feels
- Example: "What country did you study in Geography class this morning?"

**Learn names of people in their world:**
- Know classmates, friends, teachers, and other staff members
- Instead of: "What did you do today?"
- Try: "What was it like to have Matthew's dad come in for your science class today?"

### Secondary Benefit
Knowing your child's schedule and peers' names signals to your child that their world matters to you.

---

## Strategy 2: Practice Reflective Listening

### Core Principle
Reflective listening is a communication method where the listener seeks to understand the speaker by reflecting their words back to them.

### Why This Works
Children need assurance that their parent is actually listening. When children feel truly heard and followed, they become more inclined to share.

### Reflective Listening Phrases
- "I'm hearing that..."
- "It sounds like..."
- "So, you're saying that..."

### Anti-Pattern
Asking questions while distracted (on phone, unpacking bags, doing chores) makes it difficult for children to know if the parent is truly paying attention.

---

## Strategy 3: Ask Open-Ended Questions

### Core Principle
The type of question you ask can either close conversations before they begin or open them up.

### Question Type Comparison

**Close-ended questions:**
- Can be answered with yes or no
- Have limited responses
- Prevent conversations from emerging

**Open-ended questions:**
- Have unlimited answers
- Usually start with: "Why?" "How?" or "What?"
- Encourage children to elaborate

### Examples

| Close-ended | Open-ended |
|-------------|------------|
| "Did you have fun at school today?" | "Who did you play with at recess today?" |
| "Did you finish the art assignment at school?" | "What's one thing you accomplished today?" |

## Strategy 4: Don't Bombard Them With Questions

### Core Principles
- **Sometimes less is more**: One specific question is more effective than multiple overwhelming questions
- **Sometimes downtime is needed**: Children may need quiet time to process their day and recharge before discussing it

### Why This Works
Space to decompress often promotes more conversation in the long term.

### Implementation

**If your child needs processing time, use welcoming statements instead of questions:**
- "It's so good to see you! I can't wait to hear about your day!"
- "I'm happy you made it home! We can talk about your day when you're ready."

### Practical Tool
**Question Jar technique**: Create a jar full of questions, label it the "Question Jar," and invite your child to pick out a question. This playfully eases into conversation and gives the child agency.

## Strategy 5: Show Genuine Interest

### Core Principle
Children are much less inclined to talk when parents are visibly uninterested.

### Three Key Components

1. **Avoid distractions**
   - Put your phone down
   - Move to a quiet setting
   - These actions show your child you really want to hear them

2. **Be mindful of eye contact and body language**
   - Make eye contact when possible
   - This communicates undivided attention

3. **Avoid interrupting**
   - Wait until your child has completed their sentence before sharing something important
   - If unavoidable interruptions occur, acknowledge this explicitly and state when you will return to the conversation

### Script for Handling Interruptions
"Could we please pause? I really want to hear what you're saying. I'm going to submit our order, and then you can finish what you were saying."

---

## Strategy 6: Talk While Engaged in Another Activity Together

### Core Principle
Having an external distraction takes pressure off both parent and child, allowing conversation to flow more naturally.

### Why This Works
Direct conversation can feel forced and awkward for some children. An activity provides a comfortable context that reduces the intensity of direct questioning.

### Suggested Activities
- Shooting hoops in the backyard
- Coloring together
- Washing dishes
- Playing board games
- Playing with family pets

### Key Insight
Conversation emerges more organically when attention is partially directed elsewhere, removing the interrogation-like quality of face-to-face questioning.

---

## Strategy 7: Create Rituals and Expectations

### Core Principle
Structure and predictability help children who struggle to articulate their experiences.

### "Rose and Thorn" Framework

**How it works:**
- Each family member takes turns sharing:
  - **Rose**: The highlight of their day
  - **Thorn**: The trickiest part of their day

### Why This Works

1. **Modeling**: When parents share first, children get an example of how to put experiences into words
2. **Predictability**: Making this a routine (e.g., at dinner table) helps children know what to expect
3. **Preparation**: Children can predict what will be asked and prepare themselves in advance
4. **Confidence**: The predictable structure gives children confidence when answering

---

## Strategy 8: Acknowledge and Validate Their Feelings

### Core Principle
Sharing personal feelings requires safety and trust. This is especially true for children.

### What Causes Children to Shut Down
When feelings are misunderstood or minimized, children learn that expressing themselves is not safe.

### Phrases That Create Shame and Shut Down Communication
- "Let it go."
- "That's no big deal."
- "Stop worrying."
- "You're okay!"
- "Don't worry about it!"

### Why These Phrases Fail
They unintentionally make children feel ashamed for expressing themselves. Telling a parent about feelings no longer feels safe.

### Alternative: Acknowledge and Validate

**Instead of dismissing, try:**
"I hear you. You're feeling really anxious. That makes so much sense!"

### Outcome
Acknowledging feelings is a powerful way to connect and lay groundwork for more conversations.

---

## Implementation Guidance

### Realistic Expectations
- Not every strategy will work for every child
- Success requires consistency, time, and effort
- Parents should experiment to find which approaches work for their specific child

### Types of Adjustments That Create Impact
- How you listen
- The questions you ask
- The timing of your questions

### Key Message
Simple shifts can significantly impact conversation quality. The goal is establishing connections through multiple small changes rather than one dramatic intervention.

---

## Summary of Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. Asking broad, vague questions ("How was your day?")
2. Being distracted while asking questions (phone, chores)
3. Asking close-ended yes/no questions
4. Bombarding with multiple questions at once
5. Asking questions immediately upon arrival without allowing decompression time
6. Visibly showing disinterest through body language
7. Interrupting children mid-sentence
8. Making conversations feel forced through direct interrogation
9. Minimizing or dismissing children's feelings
10. Using dismissive phrases that create shame

## Summary of Effective Practices

1. Break questions into specific, manageable parts
2. Use knowledge of schedule and names to craft specific questions
3. Reflect children's words back to them
4. Ask open-ended questions starting with Why/How/What
5. Ask fewer, more targeted questions
6. Allow downtime before initiating conversation
7. Use welcoming statements instead of immediate questions
8. Create a Question Jar for playful engagement
9. Remove distractions and make eye contact
10. Acknowledge interruptions explicitly and return to conversation
11. Have conversations during shared activities
12. Establish predictable rituals like Rose and Thorn
13. Model sharing by going first
14. Validate and acknowledge feelings explicitly
