# The Ultimate Guide to Toddler and Preschooler Sleep Struggles

**Source:** Nurtured First (Shannon Wassenaar, Registered Psychotherapist)
**Original URL:** https://nurturedfirst.com/toddler/sleep-struggle-guide
**Age Range:** Toddlers, Preschoolers, Big Kids

---

## Core Framework: The 5 S's of Sleep Struggles

The article introduces a diagnostic framework for understanding why bedtime is difficult. The five categories are:

1. **Scared** (nighttime fears)
2. **Sleep Pressure** (too much or too little tiredness)
3. **Separation** (anxiety about being apart from caregivers)
4. **Sensory** (unmet sensory needs)
5. **Situational** (life changes and transitions)

**Foundational Principle:** Understanding the underlying reason for bedtime struggles is the prerequisite to solving them. Children are not being manipulative or "bad" when they resist sleep; there is always an underlying cause driving the behavior.

---

## Reason 1: Fear (Scared)

### Developmental Context

Nighttime fears typically emerge between **ages 2 and 3**. This timing correlates with a cognitive developmental milestone: the emergence of **imagination**. While this milestone is positive, it has a side effect of transforming benign environmental features (shadows, darkness) into perceived threats (monsters, danger).

**Key Insight:** The fear is compounded because children simultaneously experience separation from their primary safety source (caregivers) at bedtime. Imagination plus separation creates amplified fear.

### Three Root Causes of Nighttime Fear

#### 1. Growing Imagination

The child's newly developed imaginative capacity turns:
- Shadows into monsters
- Darkness into danger

This is a sign of healthy cognitive development, but it creates real distress.

#### 2. Stressful Life Changes

Major transitions increase nighttime fears. Examples include:
- New sibling arriving
- Moving to a new home
- Change to sleep space (new room, new bed)

**Why new sleep spaces cause fear:** The old room felt predictable, familiar, and safe. The new room feels strange, unsettling, and potentially dangerous. Predictability equals safety for children.

**Why new siblings cause fear:** The child's entire world feels destabilized.

#### 3. Exposure to Scary Media or Information

Children may see or hear disturbing content through:
- Television programs
- News broadcasts (overheard)
- Events at school
- Conversations on the school bus
- Advertisements on tablets/devices

**Critical Insight:** Bedtime may be the first opportunity all day for the child to process disturbing content. They have been distracted during the day, but once they are quiet and alone, their mind processes what they absorbed. This processing manifests as stalling, protesting, resistance, or tears.

### The Role of Temperament in Fear Response

**Slow-to-warm children:** More easily impacted by media and stories. They feel and process deeply, making them more susceptible to lingering effects from unsettling content.

**Sensitive children:** Feel emotions intensely. When bedtime arrives, their emotions, thoughts, and imagination "run wild."

### Recommended Response to Fear

**Get curious about the fear.** When a parent investigates the fear with genuine interest, it communicates two things to the child:
1. The parent believes the fear is real
2. The parent cares about the child's emotional experience

This validation is therapeutic.

## Reason 2: Sleep Pressure Imbalance

### The Science of Sleep Pressure

**Sleep pressure** is the body's biological drive for sleep. It accumulates during periods of wakefulness.

**Wake window:** The period of awake time between sleep periods. The longer the wake window, the more sleep pressure builds.

### The Overtiredness Trap (Second Wind Mechanism)

When a child misses their optimal sleep window:
1. The body releases hormones (including **adrenaline**)
2. This creates a "second wind"
3. The adrenaline makes it physiologically difficult for the child to settle
4. The child appears hyper, silly, or wired, not tired

**Causal Chain:** Missed sleep window leads to adrenaline release leads to hyperactivity leads to difficulty settling leads to even more missed sleep.

### Signs of Undertiredness (Insufficient Sleep Pressure)

- Stalling tactics: requesting extra stories, asking for water, asking for snacks, multiple bathroom requests
- Active resistance and strong desire to stay awake
- Failure to fall asleep within 20 minutes of completing a solid bedtime routine

### Signs of Overtiredness (Excessive Sleep Pressure)

- Difficulty calming down at bedtime
- Acting silly, hyper, or giggling uncontrollably
- Easy frustration and irritability
- Outbursts of crying or screaming

### Variables Affecting Optimal Sleep Pressure

Three factors determine how much sleep pressure a child needs:
1. **Age**
2. **Development**
3. **Temperament**

### Solution

Shift bedtime earlier or later based on observed signs. When sleep is offered within age-appropriate wake windows, results improve significantly.

## Reason 3: Separation Anxiety

### The Biological Basis

**Core principle:** Children are biologically wired for closeness and connection with caregivers. A child feels safest when their caregiver is physically present.

**What bedtime represents to the child:** A 10 to 12 hour separation from their trusted caregiver. The child will be alone during this entire period.

### Behavioral Manifestations

When children sense separation is coming, they become frantic. Behaviors include:
- Clinging
- Whining
- Coming out of bed repeatedly
- Crying
- Tantrums
- Stalling

**Purpose of these behaviors:** The child is doing anything possible to avoid separation.

### Neuroscience of Separation Anxiety

**One-track mind phenomenon:** Toddlers and preschoolers can only feel one emotion at a time. When they feel anxious about separation, that anxiety becomes all-consuming. They cannot simultaneously feel tired or calm.

### Temperament and Separation

**Highly sensitive or deeply feeling children:**
- Experience separation more intensely than mild-tempered children
- Become anxious, clingy, or upset when bedtime is rushed
- Detect parental frustration or urgency, which amplifies their anxiety

**Strong-willed children:**
- May experience separation anxiety as a sense of injustice
- If they feel unseen, unheard, or unconsidered, they push back
- Their resistance is an expression of anxiety, not defiance

### Two Presentations of Separation Anxiety

1. **Clinging desperately** (obvious anxiety presentation)
2. **Angrily pushing away** (masked anxiety presentation)

Both behaviors communicate the same underlying message: separation feels very hard.

### Critical Reframe

**What parents often think:** My child is trying to make bedtime difficult.
**Reality:** Bedtime IS genuinely difficult for the child.

### Important Clarification on Solutions

Understanding separation anxiety does **not** mean the only solutions are:
- Co-sleeping
- Staying with the child until they fall asleep every night

Instead, the goal is to **create ways for the child to feel close to the parent even when the parent is not physically present**.

## Reason 4: Sensory Needs

### Core Concept

Every child has unique sensory needs. To stay calm, children may need to seek certain sensory inputs or avoid others. Understanding the child's sensory profile allows parents to adjust the bedtime routine and sleep environment.

### Sensory-Seeking Children

**Profile:** Typically very active. Requires significant sensory input during the day (especially proprioceptive and vestibular input).

**Problem:** If they have not received enough sensory input during the day, they cannot settle at night. Their body still craves movement.

**Solution:** Incorporate movement into the bedtime routine or in the period before the routine begins.

**Examples of appropriate pre-bedtime movement:**
- Jumping on a trampoline
- Rough and tumble play
- Twirling
- Cartwheels
- Somersaults

### Sensory-Avoiding Children

**Profile:** Particular about their sleep environment and routine. Easily overstimulated by:
- Loud noises
- Rough textures
- Strong smells
- Bright lights

**Problem:** If the bedtime routine is rushed or inconsistent, protesting and tears increase. Sensory overload prevents settling.

**Solution:**
- Allow more time to wind down in the evenings
- Keep the bedtime routine calm and consistent
- Minimize sensory stimulation

### Mixed Sensory Profiles

**Important nuance:** Children can be both sensory-seeking AND sensory-avoiding simultaneously. This is common.

**Example:** A child may need (or enjoy) rough and tumble play before bed to meet their movement needs, yet bright lights and loud noises would be overstimulating. The parent must provide high proprioceptive/vestibular input while minimizing visual and auditory stimulation.

## Reason 5: Situational Factors (Transitions and Life Changes)

### Core Principle

Children thrive in predictable environments. Predictability is especially important for sleep. Disruptions to predictability destabilize the child's sense of safety.

### Diagnostic Questions

When sleep problems arise, consider:
- Has my child moved to a new home, room, or bed?
- Have there been any recent additions to the family?
- Has my child started a new school or daycare?
- Has my child lost a friend, pet, or family member?
- Could my child have seen something disturbing on TV or YouTube?

### The Impact of Situational Changes

These changes "turn a child's world upside down." Sleep disruption is an expected consequence of destabilization.

### Solutions for Situational Struggles

**Primary strategy:** Maintain as much consistency as possible during transitions. Consistency is described as "a huge component of sleep success."

**Supporting strategies:**
- Create bedtime rituals (predictable sequences)
- Add comfort items to the sleep environment

## Perspective Shift: The Meta-Framework

### The Compassion Reframe

**Traditional parental interpretation:** The child is being bad, difficult, or manipulative.

**Recommended interpretation:** The child is struggling with one or more of the 5 S's. Their behavior is communication, not manipulation.

**Result of reframe:** Parent moves from frustration to compassion. This emotional shift in the parent benefits both the parent's wellbeing and the effectiveness of interventions.

### Getting Curious

The phrase "get curious" appears repeatedly. It represents a fundamental stance:
- Investigate rather than judge
- Seek to understand rather than control
- Treat behavior as information

When parents "tune in" to behavior, they can see that the child is not causing difficulty; the child is experiencing difficulty.

## Key Takeaways (Author's Summary)

1. Understanding the reasons behind bedtime struggles is the prerequisite to better sleep
2. Both too much and too little daytime sleep create nighttime difficulties
3. Growing imagination and stressful life changes cause fear at bedtime
4. Separation is a major driver of bedtime struggles; creating connection helps alleviate worry
5. Sensory needs (needing more activity OR less stimulation) affect settling ability
6. Consistency and comforting routines support children through transitions
7. Taking the child's perspective generates compassion and improves outcomes

## Practical Applications Summary

| Root Cause | Signs | Intervention |
|------------|-------|--------------|
| Fear | Tears, stalling, resistance after exposure to scary content or during transitions | Get curious, validate the fear, consider media consumption |
| Undertiredness | Stalling tactics, active resistance, not asleep within 20 min | Push bedtime later |
| Overtiredness | Silly/hyper behavior, irritability, emotional outbursts | Push bedtime earlier |
| Separation Anxiety | Clinging, coming out of bed, tantrums, or angry pushing away | Create connection rituals; do not rush; manage parental frustration |
| Sensory-Seeking | Unable to settle after low-activity day | Add movement before/during bedtime routine |
| Sensory-Avoiding | Protests when routine is rushed or environment is stimulating | Calm, consistent routine; reduce sensory input; allow wind-down time |
| Situational | Sleep problems correlating with life changes | Maximize consistency; add rituals and comfort objects |

## Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)

- **Do not dismiss fears as irrational** because they are real to the child
- **Do not interpret behavior as manipulation** because this creates frustration rather than solutions
- **Do not rush bedtime with sensitive children** because they detect and absorb parental urgency
- **Do not assume overtired children look tired** because they may present as hyper or silly
- **Do not keep undertired children in bed** because this creates prolonged stalling cycles
- **Do not ignore sensory needs** because unmet needs prevent physiological settling
- **Do not expect normal sleep during major transitions** because disruption is developmentally expected
