# Secrets vs. Surprises: Protecting Children Through a No-Secret-Keeping Policy

**Source:** Nurtured First
**Author:** Shannon Wassenaar (Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** February 23, 2024
**Target Ages:** Toddler, Preschooler, Big Kid

---

## Core Thesis

Teaching children to keep secrets is inherently problematic because children lack the cognitive ability to distinguish between safe and unsafe secrets. Establishing a "no secrets" family rule protects children from abuse and manipulation while still allowing for positive surprises.

---

## Why Secret-Keeping Is Dangerous

### The Fundamental Problem

**Secrets are an abuser's best friend.** When children are conditioned to keep secrets and fear truth-telling, they cannot get help in unsafe situations.

Even when adults intend secrets to be fun and harmless, the practice is problematic because children cannot logically differentiate between safe and unsafe secrets.

### Definition of an Unsafe Secret

An **unsafe secret** is one that, if kept hidden, could allow abuse, inappropriate behavior, or danger to continue. These secrets make a child feel:
- Scared
- Confused
- Uncomfortable

### Psychological Harm from Keeping Harmful Secrets

Children who keep harmful secrets may experience:
- **Guilt** for not telling
- **Burden** from carrying the secret
- **Responsibility** for outcomes of withholding truth
- **Helplessness** to stop abusive behaviors from continuing or escalating
- **Withdrawal, anxiety, and/or depression**

### How Abusers Exploit Secret-Keeping

Abusers commonly tell children they must keep a secret or face consequences such as:
- Getting in trouble
- Being disliked
- Physical harm (threats like "snitches get stitches")
- Being labeled a liar or bad child

The illustrative scenario shows how a babysitter's boyfriend manipulated a child by:
1. Making the child feel nervous and uncomfortable
2. Laughing off the child's discomfort
3. Threatening consequences for telling ("snitches get stitches")
4. Claiming no one likes children who "tattle tale"
5. Warning they would tell parents the child was a liar
6. Threatening she would get in trouble for being "a bad girl"

These manipulation tactics kept the child silent because she:
- Did not want physical harm
- Wanted to be liked
- Did not want to get in trouble

## The Secret vs. Surprise Framework

### Characteristics of Surprises

- Meant to be fun and exciting
- Make us and others feel good
- Are always shared eventually
- Include others in a positive way

**Example:** A surprise birthday party where the person eventually finds out, and everyone feels joy.

### Characteristics of Secrets

- Meant to be kept and never shared
- Exclude others
- Do not make us or others feel good
- Can make us feel nervous or sad
- Are never intended to be revealed

**Example:** A babysitter telling a child not to tell parents about watching inappropriate content that caused nightmares.

## Five Teaching Strategies

### 1. Use Examples from the Child's Own Life

Straightforward, simple explanations using familiar experiences help children grasp concepts and illustrate differences more clearly.

**For surprises:** Reference actual surprise events the child has experienced (birthday parties, gift-giving).

**For secrets:** Reference times when secret-keeping caused problems the child experienced or can understand.

### 2. Emphasize That Truth-Telling Will Not Get Them in Trouble

**Why this matters:** Children often keep quiet because they fear punishment for telling the truth.

**Key message to communicate:**
- Coming forward with secrets will never result in punishment
- This applies even if the child believes they did something wrong
- The child is safe to share anything

**Caregiver responsibility:** Parents must monitor their own reactions and responses when children tell the truth. Children need to feel genuinely safe coming forward.

### 3. Use Age-Appropriate Explanations

Adjust approach based on:
- Child's age
- Developmental stage
- Personality and temperament

**Important consideration:** Children with a more sensitive temperament need high censorship when discussing harmful elements of secret-keeping. What works for a 6-year-old will likely overwhelm a 3-year-old.

#### Toddlers and Preschoolers
Keep it simple and straightforward:
- "Secrets make us feel sad; surprises make us feel happy!"
- Use concrete examples like Christmas presents

#### School-Aged Children
Offer more specific information including:
- Anecdotes and scenarios
- Examples of trusted adults they can talk to
- Scenarios involving peer pressure (classmate stealing and demanding silence)
- Named list of specific trusted adults in their life

#### Older Children with Logical Thinking
Introduce complexity such as the **secret vs. privacy distinction:**
- Secrets can exclude others and be hurtful
- Privacy is keeping personal information (name, address) safe from untrusted people
- Privacy protects; secrets conceal

### 4. Practice and Role-Play Scenarios

**Why play is effective:** Play is one of the most effective ways to teach tricky concepts. It builds confidence because children can practice different scenarios, make mistakes, and ask questions in a safe environment.

**Role-play approaches:**
- Have the child practice what to say if someone asks them to keep a worrying secret
- Parent plays the child, child plays the manipulator, demonstrating appropriate responses
- Practice scenarios involving fear, confusion, or discomfort

### 5. Encourage, Affirm, and Model Truth-Telling

#### Affirmation
When a child tells the truth, explicitly affirm their actions:
- Let them know they did the right thing
- Express pride in their honesty
- This reinforces truth-telling behavior

#### Modeling
Caregivers must demonstrate the behaviors they want to see. Let children observe you telling the truth even when it is difficult.

**Example:** Admitting to eating the child's Valentine's chocolates without asking, explaining that keeping it hidden did not feel good.

## Creating a "No Secrets" House Rule

### Purpose

Create an environment that:
- Discourages any form of secret-keeping
- Models desired behaviors
- Cultivates open, trusting relationships
- Makes children comfortable coming to parents

### Recommended House Rules

1. **This is a secret-free family.**
2. **No one gets in trouble for telling the truth.** (Note: Consequences for actions may still exist, but this differs from punishments, shaming lectures, or threats.)
3. **We always tell a trusted adult when someone says we have to keep something a secret.**
4. **We never trust or believe someone who says we will get hurt for telling the truth.**

### Implementation Guidelines

#### Do Not Force Truth-Telling

Instead of demanding truth, be patient and create a space where truth-telling feels safe and easy. Excessive pressure can be interpreted as unsafe, making children less inclined to share.

**Approach:** "It seems like you have something on your mind but don't want to talk about it right now. That's okay, but know I'm always here whenever you're ready."

#### Offer Regular Check-Ins

Make it routine to touch base with your child using open-ended questions:
- "Did anything feel tricky when you were at the birthday party today?"
- This creates opportunities to share without interrogation

---

## Trusted Adults

Children should be empowered to speak up to a trusted adult immediately when something feels wrong. Examples of safe people include:
- Parents
- Teachers
- School counselors
- Doctors
- A friend's parent

For school-aged children, provide a specific named list of trusted adults in their life.

---

## Distinction: Consequences vs. Punishments

The house rule that no one gets in trouble for telling the truth does not mean actions have no consequences. The distinction:
- **Consequences:** Natural or logical outcomes of actions
- **Punishments, shaming lectures, or threats:** Not appropriate responses to truth-telling

This distinction allows parents to address problematic behavior while maintaining the child's trust that honesty is always safe.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **Demanding truth under pressure:** Creates an unsafe feeling, reduces willingness to share
2. **Punishing children for honest disclosures:** Destroys trust and future truth-telling
3. **Teaching any form of secret-keeping:** Children cannot distinguish safe from unsafe secrets
4. **Reactive responses when children tell difficult truths:** Caregivers must regulate their own reactions
5. **Age-inappropriate explanations:** Too much detail can overwhelm younger children; too little leaves older children unprepared
6. **One-time conversations:** Regular check-ins and ongoing dialogue are necessary

---

## Key Causal Chains

1. **Secret-keeping conditioning** leads to **silence when abused** leads to **abuse continuing or escalating**
2. **Fear of punishment** leads to **secret-keeping** leads to **child vulnerability**
3. **Affirmation of truth-telling** leads to **reinforced truth-telling behavior** leads to **child safety**
4. **Modeling truth-telling** leads to **normalized family honesty** leads to **child comfort in sharing**
5. **Role-play practice** leads to **confidence and prepared responses** leads to **child protection in real scenarios**

## Long-Term Outcome

The illustrative story concludes with the child (Marcy), now a parent herself, creating a "secret-free" culture in her own home. She wanted to give her children the tools she lacked as a child and ensure they felt safe telling the truth. This demonstrates the intergenerational impact of implementing these practices.
