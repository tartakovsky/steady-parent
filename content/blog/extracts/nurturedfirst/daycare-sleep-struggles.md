# Daycare Sleep Struggles: Strategies for Starting School

**Source:** Nurtured First (Shannon Wassenaar, Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** February 13, 2024
**Core Topic:** Understanding and addressing sleep disruptions that occur when toddlers and young children start daycare or preschool

---

## Foundational Concepts

### Sleep Struggles vs. Sleep Regression: Diagnostic Framework

**Sleep Struggle** is a temporary or situational difficulty with sleep that does not involve regression of previously mastered skills.

**Sleep Regression** is defined by two criteria:
1. The child is struggling with a skill they have **previously mastered**
2. The struggle is **persistent**, occurring consistently over a period of time

There is no specific duration threshold required for classification as a regression. The key differentiator is whether the child had mastered the skill before and whether the problem is consistent (not sporadic).

**Illustrative Example of Sleep Struggle:**
Tommy has always required caregiver support to fall asleep (being fed or rocked to sleep). He has never mastered independent sleep. When daycare cannot replicate his sleep regime, he gets no daytime sleep. This is a struggle because he never had the skill of independent sleep to lose.

**Illustrative Example of Sleep Regression:**
Betty has fallen asleep independently since six months old. After starting daycare, she protests naps and refuses to stay in her crib consistently for over two weeks. This is a regression because she previously mastered independent sleep and is now consistently unable to do it.

**Important Nuance:** If Betty refused naps sporadically (not consistently) throughout two weeks, it would NOT classify as a regression.

## Four Root Causes of Daycare-Related Sleep Disruption

### 1. Overstimulation
Daycares are sensory-rich environments with many new sights, sounds, smells, people, and routines. This flood of novel stimulation keeps children awake and alert when they would normally be napping or settling for sleep.

### 2. Routine Disruption
Children thrive on consistency. Daycare introduces different nap times, playground times, meal times, and activities that disrupt circadian rhythms. Daycares must coordinate schedules for multiple children, making it impossible to match each child's individual rhythms. This mismatch directly causes sleep struggles.

### 3. Separation Anxiety
Being separated from parents and caregivers for extended periods causes stress and anxiety. Separation anxiety is developmentally normal but interferes with the child's ability to relax and sleep away from trusted adults. This separation stress affects both naps and nighttime sleep.

### 4. Developmental Leaps
Major developmental milestones (walking, talking, holding a spoon) often coincide with daycare transitions. When children are working hard to master new skills, their brains are in overdrive, leading to overstimulation that makes quality sleep more difficult.

### Timing as Diagnostic Indicator
**If sleep struggles begin within the first few weeks of starting daycare:** The daycare transition is the likely cause.

**If sleep struggles begin several weeks after starting daycare:** The cause is likely unrelated to the transition (e.g., sleep pressure issues, nighttime fears, separation anxiety from other sources).

## Five Common Daycare-Related Sleep Problems

These can be new struggles or amplification of pre-existing issues.

### 1. Longer Naps
Daycare nap schedules may differ from home, causing children to nap longer than needed. This creates **under-tiredness**, making it hard to fall asleep at regular bedtime or causing night wakings.

### 2. Nap Resistance
The exciting stimulation and novel experiences cause some children to resist napping at daycare entirely. Missing daytime naps leads to **overtiredness** and night sleep disruptions.

### 3. Poor Quality or Short Naps
Noise from other children and an unfamiliar sleep environment lead to shorter or more interrupted naps. Insufficient daytime sleep causes **overtiredness**, which negatively impacts nighttime sleep.

### 4. Bedtime Struggles
Children may fight bedtime or stall going to sleep as a way to **reconnect after separation**. Bedtime protests are a common mechanism for children to extend time with parents after being apart all day.

### 5. Night Wakings
Caused by:
- Inconsistent nap schedules between home and daycare
- Overtiredness from short daytime naps
- Under-tiredness from excessively long naps
- Early bedtimes resulting from long naps

## Adjustment Timeline

**Expected range:** 2 weeks to 2 months

### Temperament as Key Variable

**Mild temperament children:**
- Adjust to change more easily
- Settle into new routines faster

**Highly sensitive children:**
- Take significantly longer to adjust
- Often need more support to fall asleep even before daycare
- Require extended patience during transitions

### Example Application of Temperament Framework
Tommy (sensitive temperament) has always needed more support to fall asleep. He will take longer to adjust because of both his temperament and his lack of pre-existing independent sleep skills.

Betty (milder temperament) could fall asleep independently before daycare. She will adjust more quickly due to both her temperament and her established skills.

### Critical Mindset Shift
**Anti-pattern:** Focusing on "fixing" sleep problems
**Recommended approach:** Understanding, empathizing with, and supporting sleep problems

This shift in focus from correction to support produces more positive outcomes during the transition.

## Four Support Strategies for Parents

### 1. Prepare for Changes in Advance
- Talk about upcoming changes in simple, positive language
- Visit the new classroom before the first day
- Meet the teachers before school starts
- Drive by the daycare building for visual familiarity
- Read books about daycare/preschool
- Role-play daycare scenarios
- Use activities designed to familiarize children with what to expect

**Mechanism:** These steps reduce anticipatory anxiety.

### 2. Maintain Consistency with Bedtime Routine
- Keep the normal soothing bedtime routine unchanged
- Make bedtime a "consistent oasis" during upheaval
- **Critical warning:** Do NOT use this period to make other major changes (e.g., transitioning to a toddler bed). Additional changes compound disruption.

### 3. Be Responsive During Periods of Upheaval
- Recognize that the transition is genuinely difficult for the child
- Prioritize empathy and understanding over correction
- Respond to feelings
- Provide physical comfort (holding close)
- Offer reassurance throughout the adjustment period

### 4. Communicate with Childcare Providers
- Have open discussions about specific sleep struggles and habits
- Work collaboratively to find solutions
- Request updates about daytime sleep and behavior
- Use this information to make helpful adjustments at home

## Case Study 1: Jack (Mild Temperament, Good Sleeper)

**Background:**
- 2-year-old boy
- Previously cared for by grandmother for one year
- History of sleeping well: long naps, peaceful nights

**Presenting Problems After Daycare Start:**
- Resisting nap time at daycare
- Taking longer to fall asleep at bedtime
- Frequent middle-of-night wakings

**Parent Response Evolution:**
1. Initial reaction: frustration (surprised because he was always a good sleeper)
2. Shifted to curiosity about causes
3. Recognized link to routine and environment changes

**Interventions:**

At daycare (coordinated with providers):
- Maintained specific sleep routine elements consistent with home
- Providers sang the same song parents used before bed
- Offered the same lovey used at home

At home:
- Maintained regular bedtime routine: bath, book, song, cuddles
- During night wakings: brief soothing without turning on lights or engaging in play
- Focused on connection and consistency

**Outcome:** Within approximately two weeks, Jack began taking longer naps at daycare and was less restless at bedtime.

**Key Success Factor:** Empathetic support and consistency across environments.

## Case Study 2: Amy (Spirited Temperament, Schedule Mismatch)

**Background:**
- 3-year-old, spirited and energetic
- Home with mother since birth
- Never separated from mother for more than a couple hours
- Starting preschool requiring staying until 3 p.m.
- Had dropped afternoon nap one year prior

**Presenting Problems After Preschool Start:**
- Fighting parents at bedtime (taking almost 2 hours to fall asleep)
- Waking earlier than before

**Root Cause Discovery:**
Parents contacted preschool teachers and learned Amy was taking a 45-minute nap during scheduled rest time after lunch. Since she had dropped naps a year ago, this added nap was disrupting her sleep pressure.

**Interventions:**

Sleep schedule adjustments:
- Pushed bedtime back 30 minutes later
- Asked teachers to cut nap short by 15 minutes
- Goal: ensure sufficient tiredness at bedtime

Emotional support:
- Prioritized more connection time before bed
- Addressed underlying separation anxiety

**Outcome Timeline:**
- After about one month: began adjusting (falling asleep more quickly, waking later)
- After two months: completely adjusted to preschool schedule

**Key Success Factors:**
- Investigating with curiosity (contacting teachers)
- Identifying the specific cause (unexpected nap)
- Making targeted schedule adjustments
- Providing extra emotional support
- Patience and consistency

## When to Seek Professional Help

### Three Warning Signs

1. **Disrupted sleep persists after three months in daycare**
   The adjustment period should not exceed this timeframe for most children.

2. **Drastic changes in sleep patterns or total sleep time**
   Significant departures from baseline sleep behavior warrant professional evaluation.

3. **Difficulty functioning due to inadequate sleep**
   When sleep deprivation affects daily functioning, intervention is needed.

### Guiding Principle
Parents know their child best. Trust instincts if the child seems to need more help returning to healthy sleep habits.

---

## Core Principles Summary

1. **Disrupted sleep during daycare transition is normal and expected**
2. **Adjustment takes time** (2 weeks to 2 months depending on temperament)
3. **Focus on support rather than correction**
4. **Maintain consistency at home** while the child adapts to inconsistency elsewhere
5. **Communicate with daycare** to align approaches where possible
6. **Be patient and responsive** to the child's emotional needs
7. **Celebrate small wins** during the transition
8. **Seek help** if problems persist beyond three months or significantly impact functioning

```
Daycare Transition (Major Life Change)
    |
    +-- Causes Sleep Disruption via:
    |       |-- Overstimulation (sensory overload)
    |       |-- Routine disruption (circadian mismatch)
    |       |-- Separation anxiety (attachment stress)
    |       |-- Developmental leaps (cognitive overload)
    |
    +-- Manifests as:
    |       |-- Sleep struggles (no skill loss)
    |       |-- Sleep regression (skill loss + consistency)
    |
    +-- Common Symptoms:
    |       |-- Longer naps --> under-tiredness --> bedtime issues
    |       |-- Nap resistance --> overtiredness --> night disruption
    |       |-- Poor/short naps --> overtiredness --> night disruption
    |       |-- Bedtime struggles (reconnection seeking)
    |       |-- Night wakings (schedule inconsistency)
    |
    +-- Resolution requires:
            |-- Advance preparation (reduce anxiety)
            |-- Consistency at home (stable bedtime routine)
            |-- Responsiveness (emotional support)
            |-- Provider communication (environmental alignment)
            |-- Time (2 weeks to 2 months based on temperament)
```

## Warnings and Anti-Patterns

1. **Do not introduce additional major changes during the transition** (e.g., toddler bed transition, moving rooms, new sibling arrival if avoidable)

2. **Do not focus on "fixing" sleep problems** instead of understanding and supporting them

3. **Do not expect all children to adjust on the same timeline** because temperament significantly affects adjustment speed

4. **Do not ignore the role of daytime sleep** in nighttime sleep quality (too much or too little napping directly causes night problems)

5. **Do not assume sleep problems that start weeks after daycare began are daycare-related** because the timing pattern suggests other causes

6. **Do not dismiss sporadic sleep issues as regression** because regression requires consistent, persistent problems with previously mastered skills
