# Stop Your Child's Hitting With 4 Empathy-Building Steps

**Source:** Nurtured First (Jess VanderWier, MA, RP)
**Published:** January 20, 2025
**Applicable Ages:** Toddler, Preschooler, Big Kid

---

## Core Thesis

Forced apologies after hitting incidents are counterproductive to emotional development. Instead of demanding immediate "sorry," parents should use four empathy-building strategies that address the root causes of behavior and develop lifelong emotional intelligence. The goal extends beyond stopping hitting; it is to equip children with emotional tools for navigating conflicts and relationships throughout life.

---

## Why Forced Apologies Fail

### "Sorry" Becomes an Empty Word

When children are compelled to apologize on command, they say the word to avoid trouble rather than because they feel genuine remorse or understand why their action was wrong. This pattern creates **insincere apologies that carry into adulthood**. The child learns that "sorry" is a social escape mechanism rather than an expression of authentic feeling.

### Missed Learning Opportunity

Rushing to apology **skips the critical step** of helping the child develop empathy skills and understanding. Without processing what happened and why, the apology functions as a quick fix rather than a meaningful lesson. The child gains no new capacity for future situations.

### Prioritizing Adult Comfort

Parents often push for immediate apologies **for their own sake** rather than the child's benefit. The apology helps the parent feel the situation has been addressed, allowing them to move past their own embarrassment or discomfort in front of other parents or observers.

### Neglecting Emotional Processing

Children, like adults, **need time to process emotions after conflict**. Forced immediate apology does not allow for emotional regulation. The child cannot genuinely feel remorse while still in a dysregulated state.

## The 4 Empathy-Building Strategies

### Strategy 1: Set Clear Boundaries

**Purpose:** Establish the foundation for addressing hitting and all challenging behaviors.

**Three Components:**

1. **Clearly state the boundary** using simple, direct language the child can understand
2. **Explain why you set the boundary** so the child understands how it keeps them and others safe
3. **Offer a "creative yes"** by providing alternative ways to deal with emotions

**Example Phrases:**
- "Hands are not for hitting. Hitting hurts others."
- "I can't let you near the baby if you hit her. We need to keep everyone safe."
- "When you're angry, you can use your words, take deep breaths, or ask for help."

**Critical Implementation Note:** **Consistency is key.** All caregivers must be on the same page and reinforce boundaries consistently. Over time, the child will internalize these rules and understand that hitting is not an acceptable way to express feelings or solve problems.

---

### Strategy 2: Notice Emotions Without Shame

**Purpose:** Help the child recognize and understand their own and others' emotions, developing emotional intelligence and empathy.

**Three Steps:**

1. **Point out the emotional impact of their actions** using clear, descriptive language to explain what you observe
2. **Encourage empathy by drawing attention to others' feelings** and guide the child to notice and interpret emotions of the person they hurt
3. **Avoid shame or judgment in your observations** because the goal is understanding emotions, not making the child feel bad about themselves

**Example Phrases:**
- "Do you see how your sister is crying? Hitting hurts."
- "I'm noticing Tommy looks really sad right now. I see him crying."
- "When you hit, it made your friend feel scared and upset. How do you think that feels?"

**Long-Term Effect:** Regular practice helps the child develop emotional awareness and empathy. They become more attuned to others' feelings and better able to consider emotional consequences of their actions.

---

### Strategy 3: Focus on Repair

**Purpose:** Guide the child toward repairing damaged relationships instead of forcing empty apologies.

**Three Benefits:**

1. **Teaches problem-solving skills** because the child learns how to actively make amends, not just say words
2. **Encourages taking responsibility** by having the child acknowledge their role through thinking about how to repair the situation
3. **Fosters genuine empathy and understanding** because the child must consider what the other person needs, promoting perspective-taking

**Example Phrases:**
- "What do you think Tommy needs to hear right now?"
- "I see two children having a hard time. Hmm... what could help?"
- "Your friend looks sad. What could you do to help them feel better?"

**Implementation Approach:** Brainstorm ideas with the child about how to make amends. Options include offering a favorite toy, giving a hug (if the other child is comfortable with physical contact), or doing something kind for the person they hurt.

**Important Distinction:** The goal is **not to force** the child to make amends but to **guide them toward understanding** the importance of repairing relationships and taking responsibility for actions.

---

### Strategy 4: Give Time to Find Genuine Remorse

**Purpose:** Recognize that genuine remorse and desire to apologize cannot be rushed.

**Three Elements:**

1. **Acknowledge the child might not be ready to apologize immediately.** It is acceptable if the child needs time to process what happened and how they feel. (Recommended resource: "The Sorry Plane" children's book illustrates this concept.)
2. **Encourage reflection on their actions** by helping the child think about what happened, why it happened, and how it affected others.
3. **Support them in expressing genuine remorse when ready** by helping them express feelings sincerely once they reach that point.

**Example Phrases:**
- "You aren't ready to say sorry yet. When your sorry is ready, you can apologize."
- "When you find your sorries, you can talk to your brother."
- "Let's take some time to think about what happened. We can talk about it when you're ready."

**What This Teaches:** Apologies are meaningful expressions of remorse, not just words to escape trouble. This approach respects the child's emotional process and helps them learn to manage feelings in a healthy way.

## Real-Life Implementation Tips

### Stay Calm

Take a deep breath before responding. Your calm demeanor helps your child regulate their own emotions. The parent's emotional state directly influences the child's capacity to regulate.

### Remove From the Situation

If necessary, gently remove your child from the immediate situation to a quieter space where you can talk without external pressure or audience.

### Acknowledge Both Children's Feelings

Show empathy for both the child who was hit and your own child. Example: "I see that Tommy is hurt, and I can see that you're upset too." Neither child's emotions are dismissed.

### Use Simple Language

Especially for younger children, explain what happened and why it is not okay using clear, simple language. Complexity undermines comprehension.

### Be Consistent

Apply these strategies consistently, even when inconvenient. **Consistency helps children internalize lessons.** Inconsistent application confuses and delays learning.

### Model the Behavior You Want to See

When you make a mistake, model how to genuinely apologize and make amends. Children learn more from observed behavior than from instructions.

### Notice the Good

When you see your child handling conflicts well or showing empathy, **narrate it out loud.** Example: "I noticed you apologized to your brother. You felt sorry for taking this toy." Positive reinforcement strengthens desired behaviors.

### Permission to Pause

It is acceptable to take a moment to collect yourself and respond thoughtfully rather than reactively. Example phrase: "I see what happened. Let's take a moment to calm down, and then we'll talk about it." This gives both parent and child time to process.

## Long-Term Benefits of This Approach

### Improved Emotional Intelligence

The child learns to recognize and manage their own emotions and those of others. This skill is crucial for success in relationships, school, and eventually the workplace.

### Better Conflict Resolution Skills

By learning to consider others' feelings and repair relationships, the child develops valuable conflict resolution skills that serve them throughout life.

### Stronger Relationships

Children who can empathize and make genuine amends form stronger, more meaningful relationships with peers and family members.

### Increased Self-Awareness

These strategies help children become more aware of their own emotions and behaviors, leading to better self-regulation over time.

### Reduced Aggression

As children develop empathy and learn alternative ways to express feelings, instances of hitting and other aggressive behaviors tend to decrease.

### Enhanced Communication Skills

Encouraging the child to express feelings and consider others' perspectives develops crucial communication skills.

### Greater Resilience

Understanding emotions and how to navigate conflicts helps children become more resilient when facing challenges.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why not make my child apologize immediately after hitting?

Forced apologies lack sincerity and do not teach true empathy. Focus instead on helping the child understand the impact of their actions and guiding them toward genuine remorse.

### How can I help my child develop empathy?

Nurture empathy by modeling empathetic behavior, discussing emotions openly, encouraging perspective-taking, and using the four strategies: setting clear boundaries, noticing emotions without shame, focusing on relationship repair, and giving time for genuine remorse.

### What if my child refuses to apologize even after time?

Continue to model empathy and guide them toward understanding the impact of their actions. **Focus on repairing the relationship rather than forcing words.** The words matter less than the relational healing.

### How do I handle hitting in public situations?

Stay calm, remove your child from the situation if necessary, and apply the strategies consistently. Taking a moment to collect yourself before responding is acceptable. **Handling the situation effectively is more important than appeasing onlookers.** Social pressure should not compromise proper response.

### At what age can children understand empathy?

Children start developing empathy from a very young age, with signs appearing **as early as 2 years old**. Empathy continues to develop throughout childhood and adolescence. It is never too early to start nurturing empathetic behavior.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **Demanding immediate apologies** when the child is still emotionally dysregulated
2. **Shaming the child** when pointing out the impact of their actions
3. **Prioritizing your own embarrassment** over the child's learning opportunity
4. **Inconsistent boundary enforcement** across caregivers or situations
5. **Forcing amends** rather than guiding toward voluntary repair
6. **Skipping the reflection phase** and jumping straight to resolution
7. **Using complex language** that younger children cannot process
8. **Ignoring your own emotional state** rather than modeling self-regulation
9. **Forgetting to acknowledge both children's feelings** in conflict situations
10. **Failing to notice and reinforce** positive conflict resolution behaviors
