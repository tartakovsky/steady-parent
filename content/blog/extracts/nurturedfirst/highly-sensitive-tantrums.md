# Understanding Tantrums for Highly Sensitive Kids

**Source:** Nurtured First (Shannon Wassenaar, Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** July 27, 2023
**Reviewed by:** Nurtured First's team of child development experts

---

## Core Premise

**Highly sensitive children** experience the world at a deeper sensory and emotional level, which makes them more susceptible to intense tantrums. These children are not "bad" or manipulative. They require curiosity, compassion, and strategies tailored to their sensitivity and strong will rather than punishment.

---

## Profile of the Highly Sensitive Child

### Sensory Characteristics
- **Heightened sensitivity to textures:** food textures, bedsheets, clothing fit
- **Sound sensitivity:** may become overwhelmed by household noise, may prefer TV volume lower (or sometimes extremely loud)
- **Environmental overwhelm:** more easily overstimulated by sensory input (lights, tastes, touch)

### Emotional and Social Characteristics
- **Strong sense of justice:** will stand up for what they believe is right
- **Deep empathy and compassion:** may absorb others' emotions when someone in the home is upset
- **High observational acuity:** notices when something feels "off," behavior changes when sensing caregiver distress
- **Deeper appreciation for aesthetics:** music, art, and food resonate more intensely
- **Incredible ability to understand** the world around them

### Behavioral Consequence
When highly sensitive children do not feel understood, or when sensory input becomes overwhelming, they need a way to release the feeling of "too muchness" from their body. This manifests as tantrums.

---

## How Tantrums Differ for Highly Sensitive Children

### Emotional Responsiveness

**Highly Sensitive Children:**
- Become overwhelmed more quickly and more deeply by stimuli (loud noises, bright lights, routine changes)
- Triggers lead to more intense emotional responses compared to non-sensitive peers
- Highly attuned to caregiver emotional states during tantrums
- Experience **dual distress:** (1) overwhelmed nervous system, and (2) awareness that their tantrum is negatively affecting loved ones
- Cannot easily "shrug it off" when tantrums are received negatively by others

**Mild-Temperament Children (contrast):**
- Less intense emotional response to tantrum triggers
- Calm down quickly with less external support
- Can typically dismiss negative reactions from others

### Emotional Regulation

**Highly Sensitive Children:**
- Struggle significantly with emotional regulation
- Tantrums are longer and more intense
- Find it challenging to calm down once upset because emotions are felt so intensely
- Require substantial support to regulate during early years
- Need more recovery time to process emotions after a tantrum
- **Developmental timeline:** ability to self-regulate may not emerge until ages 7 to 9

**Mild-Temperament Children (contrast):**
- Easier time regulating emotions during tantrums
- Greater success using coping skills
- "Bounce back" more quickly
- **Developmental timeline:** noticeable self-regulation improvements around age 5

### Bodily Sensations

**Highly Sensitive Children:**
- Experience stronger physical reactions during tantrums: rapid heartbeat, shallow breathing, tense muscles
- Intensity of physical sensations makes it harder to "bounce back" physically
- Bodies require more downtime to recover from the physical toll

---

## Caregiver Influence on Tantrums

**Critical insight:** Highly sensitive children are more susceptible to tantrums when trusted adults struggle to regulate their own emotions.

- If the caregiver is having an "off day," the child is more likely to struggle with their emotions
- The child's high observational ability means they detect and respond to caregiver emotional states
- Caregiver dysregulation amplifies child dysregulation

## Three Intervention Strategies for Highly Sensitive Children

### 1. Nurture the Relationship

**Guiding principle:** Above all else, the sensitive child needs to feel seen, heard, and loved for who they are.

**Verbal affirmations to use:**
- "I love you just the way you are!"
- "There's nothing you could do that could take away my love for you!"
- "Being your mom/dad is one of the greatest gifts!"

**Practical connection strategies:**
- **One-on-one time:** Spend dedicated time doing activities they love (dancing, crafting, sports). This makes a significant difference.
- **Repair after conflict:** Highly sensitive children have difficulty moving forward in their day after an argument or anger that has not been repaired. Always return and apologize if you lost your cool.

**Why repair matters:** The sensitive child's emotional memory and attunement means unresolved conflict lingers and compounds distress.

### 2. Acknowledge the Depth of Their Feelings

**Anti-pattern to avoid:** Dismissing big feelings because they seem disproportionate or incomprehensible to the adult.

**Example of dismissive response (avoid this):** When the child cries about a sibling's whistling, telling them to "just move on."

**Correct approach:** Acknowledge that their feelings are real to them, even if the trigger seems minor.

**Scripted responses:**
- "The whistle feels really loud. I hear you. You don't like that sound. Hmm, I wonder what would help?"
- "You really don't like these PJs! They are the worst! I hear you! I wonder why they are the worst?"

**Technique elements:**
- Name the experience from the child's perspective
- Validate explicitly ("I hear you")
- Invite collaborative problem-solving or exploration ("I wonder...")

### 3. Set Clear and Reliable Boundaries

**Underlying need:** Sensitive children need to know that the caregiver can handle their emotions.

**Harmful message to avoid:** Communicating that they are "too much" or their emotions are "too big."

**Paradox of the sensitive child:** They may appear to know what they need and make demands or resist instruction. In reality, they need firm, loving leadership.

**Why boundaries are loving:** Setting boundaries is one of the most loving tools for sensitive children of any age (toddler through preteen).

**Example 1: Toddler tantrum when storytime ends**

Scripted responses:
- "You can be sad that storytime is over, but I can't let you throw your books. If the books break, we can't read them anymore."
- "Hmmm, it sounds like this is really hard for you. I'm going to keep you and our property safe."

**Structure:** Validate the emotion + State the boundary + Explain the rationale (consequence or safety)

**Example 2: Seven-year-old emotional outburst over movie rating restriction**

Scripted responses:
- "I know you really wanted to watch that movie. It's okay to be upset. I can handle your tears."
- "It's my job to keep you safe, and that means we can't watch certain movies. I know this feels unfair because your friends told you they watched it. If you need to have some tears, that's okay."

**Structure:** Acknowledge desire + Validate emotion + Affirm caregiver capacity to handle feelings + Explain boundary rationale + Give permission to express emotion

**Key phrase:** "I can handle your tears" directly counters the "too much" message and reassures the child.

## Self-Care for Parents of Highly Sensitive Children

**Rationale:** Parenting a highly sensitive child is tiring. Daily exposure to big feelings makes it difficult to stay calm. Caregiver self-regulation directly impacts the child's regulation.

### Practice 1: Mindful Shower

Use the shower as a reset opportunity. Engage senses deliberately:
- Notice the way water feels on your back
- Notice the sound of water hitting the ground
- Notice the smell of the shampoo
- Notice the calming feeling the shower brings
- Notice the sense of worries being washed away
- Notice thoughts that arise, then return attention to sensory experience

### Practice 2: Daily Gratitude

**Long-term benefit:** Changes thought patterns over time.

**Application:** Especially useful in overwhelming parenting moments.

**Method:** Maintain a journal or list as a mindfulness practice for stress management.

**Example gratitudes:**
- "I'm grateful that the sun is shining."
- "I'm thankful for this cup of coffee."
- "I'm thankful that my son has his own voice."
- "I'm grateful for the way my daughter turns to me when she's sad."

**Note:** The last two examples reframe challenging traits (strong will, emotional expression) as positives.

## Anti-Patterns and Warnings

1. **Do not label sensitive children as "bad":** Others often perceive them as the kids who get in the most trouble.
2. **Do not use punishment:** These children do not need punishment; they need understanding and tailored support.
3. **Do not dismiss emotions:** Minimizing feelings damages trust and prevents the child from feeling understood.
4. **Do not communicate "too much":** The message that emotions are too big is one of the most hurtful things a sensitive child can receive.
5. **Do not skip repair:** Unrepaired conflict prevents the sensitive child from moving forward emotionally.
6. **Do not neglect self-care:** Caregiver dysregulation directly increases child tantrum frequency and intensity.

---

## Reframing the Highly Sensitive Child

**Shift in perspective required:**
- From: "manipulative," "bad," "too much"
- To: "beautiful humans deserving of love and respect"

**Approach tantrums with:** kindness and empathy rather than frustration or punishment.

**Working with (not against):** The child's sensitivity and strong will are features to work with, not problems to eliminate.

---

## Key Takeaways (Summary)

1. Highly sensitive children feel emotions and sensations more deeply, leading to more intense and longer tantrums.
2. Nurture the relationship through one-on-one time and activities the child enjoys.
3. Encourage autonomy by allowing the child to make decisions (e.g., choosing next steps in routines).
4. Validate emotions and help the child feel understood by explicitly acknowledging feelings and experiences.
5. Set clear, reliable boundaries to provide stability and reassurance while permitting emotional expression.
6. Practice self-care (mindfulness, gratitude) to maintain caregiver regulation and better support the child.
7. View the sensitive child as a unique human deserving love and respect to approach tantrums with kindness and empathy.
