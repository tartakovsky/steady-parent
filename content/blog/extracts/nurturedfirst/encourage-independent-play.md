# Encouraging Independent Play: Creating Invitations to Play

**Source:** Nurtured First (Jess VanderWier, MA, RP)
**Published:** February 13, 2023
**Applicable Ages:** Toddler, Preschooler, Big Kid

---

## Core Concept: Play as Developmental Foundation

Play is **essential** for developing critical skills including:
- Creativity
- Leadership
- Problem-solving

**Key Insight:** The most valuable type of play for children is play that **lacks a clear start or finish** (open-ended play). This unstructured quality allows children to direct their own exploration and learning.

---

## What is an Invitation to Play?

**Definition:** An **invitation to play** is the deliberate arrangement of the environment to "invite" a child to approach an area and independently explore, investigate, touch, and manipulate objects.

**Purpose:** Teaching children how to engage in **independent play**

**Developmental Starting Point:** Can begin with very young toddlers

**Core Mechanism:** Set up objects, show the child the area, then allow them to manipulate objects **without instruction from the adult**. The absence of adult direction is fundamental to the approach.

### Example Setups
- Blocks in a playroom
- Playdough and pipe cleaners on a table
- Painting with water
- Any open-ended materials

## Six Strategies for Encouraging Independent Play

### 1. Start with Small Invitations Together

**The Principle:** Independent play is built through a **gradual weaning process**, not an immediate expectation. Adults begin as active participants, then slowly remove themselves.

**Why This Works:** Children who want adult participation are not showing a problem; they are at the starting point of the process. Resistance to immediate independence is expected and normal.

**Practical Example (Drawing a House):**

| Stage | Adult Role | Child Role |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| Initial | Drawing the picture | Watching, perhaps requesting adult draw |
| Step 1 | Drawing, asking "What kind of door should I draw?" | Verbally contributing ideas |
| Step 2 | Handing child the marker, asking them to draw windows | Drawing one element |
| Step 3 | Observing | Drawing the whole house independently |

**Causal Chain:** Adult models creativity → Adult invites verbal input → Adult transfers physical execution incrementally → Child internalizes process → Child executes independently

### 2. Create a "Yes Space"

**Definition:** An enclosed physical space where a child can explore without accessing anything dangerous or forbidden

**Primary Benefit:** Allows adults to step back (literally have a hot coffee) while the child explores without constant supervision or correction

**Target Demographic:** Particularly important for "tiny tots" who can easily access inappropriate items

**Underlying Logic:** When every object in the space is safe and allowed, the child receives no interruptions, and the adult is not forced into constant monitoring. This uninterrupted exploration builds the foundation for independent play.

### 3. Follow Your Child's Interests

**Principle:** Invitations to play become **significantly easier** when the child is already interested in the objects presented

**Implementation:** Observe what types of play your child naturally gravitates toward, then select open-ended toys aligned with those interests

**Examples of Interest-Based Setups:**
- Cars with a mat (for vehicle-interested children)
- Play kitchen with food items (for domestic play interests)
- Figurines from a favorite movie (leveraging existing media interests)

**Conceptual Link:** Interest acts as intrinsic motivation. When motivation is already present, the invitation requires less external encouragement to succeed.

### 4. Trust the Process

**The Child's Internal Question:** Children look to parents to answer: "Can I do this?" and "Will I be okay playing on my own?"

**The Message to Communicate:** "Yes, I trust that you can do this!"

**Graduated Withdrawal Protocol:**
1. Play together for 5-10 minutes
2. Remove yourself from play for a few minutes
3. Return to play
4. Over time, extend the duration of removal before returning
5. Continue extending until child demonstrates sustained independent play

**Why This Works:** Children need to see that the adult believes in their capability. The return after each withdrawal communicates safety (the adult is still available) while the withdrawal itself communicates trust (the child is capable).

### 5. Stay Out of Their Way

**Critical Moment:** When you notice your child has **independently started playing**, do not join in

**What to Avoid:** Rushing in with your own thoughts and ideas

**Why Intervention is Harmful:** Adult participation, even well-intentioned, can easily **take over the play**. The child's imagination and creativity get displaced by adult direction.

**The Goal:** Allow the child to get lost in their own imagination and enjoy their own creativity without external shaping

**Anti-Pattern:** Seeing child playing alone → Feeling compelled to participate → Joining the play → Inadvertently directing the play → Child loses ownership of creative process

### 6. Understand Developmental Norms

**Normal Attention Span:** Children will typically switch activities every **5-15 minutes**. This is developmentally appropriate, not a failure.

**Trajectory with Practice:**
- Initial: 5-15 minute engagement periods
- With repeated opportunities: Gradually longer play duration
- As adult withdraws: Extended independent engagement

**Parental Expectation Calibration:** Short attention spans should not be interpreted as disinterest or inability. They reflect normal developmental capacity. Extended focus develops over time with repeated exposure to independent play opportunities.

## Key Conceptual Relationships

**Independence develops through supported dependence:** The path to independent play runs through co-play, not around it. Attempting to skip co-play and demand immediate independence will likely fail.

**Environment design precedes behavioral change:** Creating the right physical setup (yes space, interesting materials) makes independent play possible. Without appropriate environmental scaffolding, behavioral expectations are unrealistic.

**Trust is communicated through action, not words:** Telling a child "you can do this" matters less than showing them through graduated withdrawal and non-intervention during independent play episodes.

**Adult restraint is active parenting:** Staying out of play when a child is engaged is not passive neglect but intentional, skilled parenting. The urge to join must be recognized and resisted.

---

## Warnings and Anti-Patterns

1. **Expecting immediate independent play:** Children need the graduated process; skipping steps creates frustration for both parent and child

2. **Interrupting self-directed play:** Once a child is independently engaged, adult "help" disrupts the developmental benefit

3. **Misinterpreting short attention spans as failure:** 5-15 minute activity switches are normal and expected

4. **Providing instruction during invitation setup:** The core principle is manipulation without adult direction; instruction undermines the purpose

5. **Creating spaces with forbidden items:** If the child must constantly hear "no" or be redirected, independent exploration cannot develop

6. **Ignoring child's interests:** Generic setups that don't align with the child's natural interests require more adult effort to sustain engagement
