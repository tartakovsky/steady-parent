# Staying Calm During a Child's Tantrum: Comprehensive Extract

**Author:** Shannon Wassenaar, Registered Psychotherapist
**Date:** July 27, 2023
**Reviewed by:** Nurtured First's team of child development experts

## Core Problem Statement

Parents often lack tools to stay calm during their child's tantrums. When parents have no strategies for managing their own emotional reactions, yelling becomes the default release mechanism for pent-up emotions. This creates a cycle where the parent feels helpless to change their response pattern.

---

## The Intergenerational Transmission of Emotional Responses

### The Mechanism of Triggering

When a child's tantrum triggers strong emotional reactions in a parent, the source is often the parent's own childhood experiences. A parent's instinctive response to their child's emotions mirrors how their own emotions were handled in childhood.

### Example Case Study

A mother found her toddler's tantrums overwhelming, making her angry, irritable, and snappy. Upon reflection, she discovered:
- Her parents had shut down, ignored, or punished her when she expressed emotions as a child
- Her son's tantrums were triggering those old childhood feelings
- Her instinct to yell was the ONLY tool she had because it was the only model she received

### The Insight That Enables Change

**Awareness of the trigger pattern empowers change.** Once the mother understood that her son's tantrums were activating old childhood wounds, she recognized she had choices:
- She did not have to yell
- She did not have to ignore the tantrums
- She could parent in a way that created connection rather than rupture

## The Emotional Landscape of Parents During Tantrums

### Range of Emotions Parents Experience

**Helpless:** Unable to make a change because the source of the child's distress is unclear and the overstimulation feels unmanageable

**Frustrated:** Tantrums occur at inconvenient times, compounding stress

**Angry:** The child is not cooperating or calming down as expected

**Ashamed:** Awareness that one's reactions and responses to the outburst do not align with one's values

**Afraid:** Fear of judgment from family, friends, or strangers regarding one's reactions

**Embarrassed:** Particularly acute when tantrums occur in public settings

**Overwhelmed:** Sensory overload from screaming, whining, and crying sounds

**Anxious:** A state of being on edge, anticipating the next tantrum

### Why Acknowledgment Matters

**Acknowledging emotions is essential for maintaining emotional balance during tantrums.** If a child's tantrums cause significant emotional distress, the parent must take time to reflect on what specifically they are feeling. This self-awareness becomes the foundation for changing response patterns.

## Three Strategies for Maintaining Emotional Balance

### Strategy 1: The 3-4-5 Breathing Technique

**Purpose:** Physiological regulation when emotions begin to take over

**Execution:**
- Breathe in through the nose for 3 seconds
- Hold the breath for 4 seconds
- Breathe out through the mouth for 5 seconds
- Repeat as needed

**Timing:** Initiate when emotions start to escalate, not after losing control

### Strategy 2: "Yell-Proof Shield" Visualization

**Purpose:** Mental protection against the triggering effect of a child's screams

**Target use case:** When the child's screaming specifically serves as an emotional trigger

**Execution:**
- Envision holding up a shield when the child is in an emotional outburst
- Imagine all cries and yells bouncing off the shield
- The visualization creates psychological distance from the triggering sounds

### Strategy 3: Grounding Statements

**Purpose:** Anchor the parent when flooded with anxiety during tantrums

**Example grounding statements:**
- "I am safe."
- "My child is safe."
- "I am [state your age] years old." (This grounds you in present reality, not childhood feelings)
- "Everyone has anxious thoughts. This doesn't mean they are true."
- "I can cope with this."
- "I'm grateful for [specific thing]."

**Implementation Tip:** Write these affirmations down and post them in visible locations throughout the home:
- Refrigerator
- Bathroom mirror
- Car dashboard
- Any location accessible during a tantrum or when about to lose control

## Building a Support System

### Why Support Systems Matter

Building a support system is a practical way for parents to set themselves up for success. The specific configuration will vary by family.

### Option 1: Including Trusted Relatives in Childcare

**Benefits:**
- Provides opportunity for breaks to recharge
- Enables the parent to show up as the parent they want to be

**Potential barrier:** Asking family for help can feel challenging when:
- The family member's discipline style is unknown
- Uncertainty exists about whether they will handle tantrums consistently with the parent's approach

**If family support is not viable:** Consider local parenting groups as an alternative support system

### Option 2: Joining a Local Parenting Program

**Where to find resources:**
- Local library
- Hospital
- Community center

**Barrier:** Physically leaving the house to attend a parenting group

**Lower-barrier alternative:** Start with an online parenting group, such as a local Facebook group for parents. This provides a gentler, more realistic transition into a parenting community.

### Reframe Around Self-Care

**Taking care of your needs IS taking care of your little ones.** Seeking help or support is not a failure but an essential component of effective parenting.

## Setting Realistic Goals and Embracing Imperfection

### The Impossibility Standard

**It is not realistic to expect yourself to stay calm at all times.** Holding yourself to a perfection standard sets you up for shame and failure. Grace toward yourself is necessary as you learn and grow alongside your child.

---

## The Do-Over: Repairing After Rupture

### Why Repair Matters

If you lose your cool, you are not alone. Success at staying calm will not be 100%. **Repairing the relationship after rupture is one of the most important things a parent can do.**

### What Repair Sounds Like

**Example repair statements:**

"I shouldn't have yelled at you like that. That was not ok. I'm sorry."

"Can we try a do-over? I don't like how I talked to you just now."

"My feelings are my responsibility, not yours. It's not ok for me to yell."

"Ahhhh, that's not what I wanted to say. I'm sorry. Can we rewind and start over?"

### The Permission to Stop Mid-Discipline

It is acceptable to stop yourself in the middle of a discipline interaction and ask internally: "Am I responding to my child in a way that aligns with my values?" If not, initiate repair immediately.

### What Children Learn From Do-Overs

When children see parents:
- Realize they made a mistake
- Own up to that mistake
- Try again

They learn the power of the do-over through modeling. Parents are learning alongside their children and will not always get it right. Children internalize this lesson too.

### Humility as a Teaching Tool

There is beauty in the humility that comes from asking your child for a do-over. This models emotional accountability and growth mindset.

## Key Conceptual Framework

### Responsibility Boundaries

**Your child is not responsible for fixing or taking away your emotions.** You are not responsible for fixing or taking away your child's emotions. Each person owns their emotional experience.

### The Purpose of Calming Strategies

Practicing calming strategies is a practical way to maintain emotional balance so you can respond to tantrums in a way that feels good for both parent and child.

### The Nature of Parenting

Parenting is about constantly connecting, nurturing, and growing. It is not about perfection.

---

## Summary of Key Takeaways

1. **Acknowledge your own emotions:** Embrace the many emotions you may feel during tantrums. Self-awareness guides positive responses.

2. **Understand your triggers:** Identify and explore personal triggers related to tantrums. This enables changing reactions and strengthening the bond with your child.

3. **Use strategies to maintain emotional balance:** Deploy the 3-4-5 breathing technique, visualizations, and grounding statements to remain calm.

4. **Build a support system:** Seek support from trusted family, friends, or parenting communities to manage stress and share nurturing responsibilities.

5. **Harness the power of a do-over:** Apologize when needed. Trying again teaches children that parents are not perfect and that repair is possible.

6. **Repairing after rupture is crucial:** After conflict, make amends. Use these experiences to model positive behavior.

7. **Parenting is not about perfection:** Show self-compassion throughout the journey.

---

## Anti-Patterns and Warnings

**Anti-pattern:** Yelling as the sole tool for managing tantrums
**Result:** Temporary behavior cessation but relationship rupture and modeling of dysregulation

**Anti-pattern:** Ignoring tantrums entirely
**Result:** Fails to address the child's underlying needs and misses connection opportunity

**Anti-pattern:** Expecting 100% calm responses
**Result:** Shame spiral when the unrealistic standard is not met

**Anti-pattern:** Isolating without support
**Result:** Burnout and diminished capacity to respond as desired

**Warning:** If tantrums trigger strong reactions, investigate your own childhood experiences with emotional expression. The trigger source often lies there.

**Warning:** Family support may backfire if discipline styles are misaligned. Assess before delegating childcare.
