# Why Kids Hit and What Parents Can Do About It

**Source:** Good Inside (Dr. Becky Kennedy, Clinical Psychologist)

---

## Core Framework: Hitting as Skill Deficit, Not Character Flaw

The central reframe is that childhood aggression represents a **skill deficit** rather than disobedience or bad character. Children are born with all of the feelings and none of the skills to manage those feelings. This perspective shifts parental response from punishment toward teaching.

The parent's role is explicitly defined as teaching emotion regulation skills so that big feelings do not emerge as behaviors like hitting. When parents view hitting through this lens, they can respond with instruction rather than frustration or shame.

---

## Why Children Hit: The Developmental and Emotional Explanation

### The Feeling-Skill Gap

Intense emotions such as frustration, jealousy, disappointment, or excitement can manifest as physical aggression because children lack the internal regulation abilities to process these feelings in other ways. The underlying issue is never disobedience; it is the absence of learned emotional regulation capacity.

### Toddler-Specific Context

Hitting in toddlers is **developmentally appropriate** as they are actively learning emotional expression. Physical behaviors indicate that a **fight-or-flight response** has been triggered. The child's body feels overwhelmed and self-protective reactions activate automatically. Critically, children are not actually in danger during these moments; they simply experience emotions at an intensity they cannot yet process effectively.

This understanding normalizes the behavior without excusing it. Almost every child goes through phases of hitting, kicking, and biting. These behaviors are both developmentally typical and simultaneously frustrating and painful for parents to experience.

---

## Why Punishment and Consequences Backfire

### The Missed Teaching Opportunity

Using punishment strategies actually misses critical teaching opportunities. When parents apply punishments or consequences for hitting, they fail to teach children how to manage the underlying feeling that caused the behavior.

### Two Harmful Outcomes of Punishment

**Emotional Suppression:** Children may learn to suppress emotions entirely rather than regulate them. This suppression can lead to future anxiety because feelings that are pushed down do not disappear; they find other outlets.

**Behavioral Escalation:** Alternatively, punishment can cause children to escalate to more dangerous behaviors like lying or sneaking out. The child learns to hide behavior rather than change it.

### The Proper Parental Role

The job is to teach kids the skills they need to thrive in life, instead of punishing them for not knowing yet. This phrasing emphasizes that children are not refusing to behave well; they simply have not yet acquired the necessary skills.

---

## The Three-Step Intervention Protocol

### Step 1: Keep Yourself Calm

Parental composure directly helps dysregulated children feel less overwhelmed. When a parent becomes activated, the child's dysregulation intensifies. The parent must ground themselves first because you cannot regulate a dysregulated child while you yourself are dysregulated.

**Cognitive Reframe for Parents:** "I have a good kid who is having a hard time, not a bad kid giving me a hard time." This self-talk shifts the parent from adversarial stance to supportive stance.

### Step 2: Keep Your Child Safe

Set clear boundaries without punishment. The verbal statement is: "I won't let you hit." This is spoken while physically preventing contact through hand-holding or placing barriers between children.

The boundary is firm but not punitive. The parent acts to stop the behavior without adding shame, lectures, or consequences.

### Step 3: Validate Emotions

Acknowledge the underlying feelings that drove the behavior. Model healthy regulation by providing alternative outlets.

**Example Script:** "You must feel really angry to hit me like that. If you have mad energy in your body, you can hit a soft toy or a pillow."

This response accomplishes multiple goals simultaneously:
- It names the emotion (angry)
- It validates the intensity (really angry, enough to hit)
- It normalizes having "mad energy" in the body
- It provides a concrete alternative behavior (hitting soft objects)

---

## Key Principles Summary

**Hitting represents dysregulation, not disobedience.** The behavior is a symptom of overwhelming emotion, not willful defiance.

**Teach the missing emotional regulation skills rather than punishing their absence.** Skill-building is the path forward; punishment creates new problems.

**Ground yourself first.** Parental regulation precedes child regulation. A dysregulated parent cannot help a dysregulated child calm down.

---

## Teaching Points from Examples

The phrase "mad energy in your body" provides child-accessible language for describing the physical sensation of anger. It locates the feeling in the body rather than making it about character, and it frames the energy as something that needs an outlet rather than something that is wrong to feel.

Offering "hit a soft toy or a pillow" gives a specific, immediate, physically similar alternative. The child's urge to hit is redirected rather than suppressed, meeting the developmental need for physical release while preventing harm.

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Viewing hitting as disobedience or bad character
- Applying punishments or consequences that skip emotional teaching
- Attempting to regulate a child while the parent is dysregulated
- Suppressing emotions rather than teaching regulation
- Lecturing or shaming during the moment of dysregulation
