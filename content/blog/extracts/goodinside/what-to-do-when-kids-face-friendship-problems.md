# What to Do When Kids Face Friendship Problems

Source: Dr. Becky Kennedy, Clinical Psychologist (Good Inside)

## Core Philosophy: Support, Don't Solve

When children come to parents with tricky friendship dynamics, **they are rarely asking for answers**. They are asking for connection. The fundamental insight is that children's feelings need support, not solutions.

### The Feelings Bench Metaphor

Picture your child sitting on a "feelings bench" of peer rejection. Two possible responses exist:

**Yanking them off the bench** (offering solutions) leaves kids feeling more confused, ashamed, and dysregulated. This approach backfires because it communicates that the feeling should not exist or cannot be tolerated.

**Sitting down next to them** (offering support) through listening, validating, and reminding them they are not alone gives children the confidence and space to eventually get off the bench on their own. Connection provides the foundation for self-regulation.

### Why This Shift Matters

Childhood friendships are where kids build the **emotional intelligence and social skills** that will carry them into adulthood. When parents resist the urge to fix and focus on connection instead, they teach their child: "Yes, this feels hard AND you can cope with hard things."

The goal is not to narrow children's experiences so they only have "good" experiences. The goal is to help them develop **coping skills for the entire range of experiences and feelings**. This is what builds lasting, meaningful emotional intelligence and resilience.

## The Parent's Actual Role

**It is not our job to eliminate our kids' social challenges or solve every struggle** because those struggles are how they learn. Childhood friendships play a major role in the development of emotional intelligence in children. Through friendships, kids learn to express their needs, navigate conflict, and build healthy relationships.

### Common Parental Instincts to Resist

When a child comes home upset about friendship issues, parents often feel an urge to:
- Text the teacher
- Call another parent
- Reassure with statements like "Sweetie, I'm sure they'll play with you tomorrow!"

These instincts come from a good place but can shortcut the learning process children need.

## Five Common Friendship Issues

### 1. Feeling Left Out

Children might say: "No one ever plays with me!"

**Context matters:** This statement sometimes represents a single tough day at recess. Other times, it can happen after a big change like moving cities or starting at a new school.

**Why it hurts so much:** Feeling left out by peers is one of the most common social challenges and one of the most hurtful because **humans inherently seek belonging**. The pain is real and significant.

**The best antidote:** Connection with the parent. Not solutions, not reassurance that minimizes, but genuine presence and validation.

### 2. Rejecting Others

Children might say: "I don't want Miguel over!" or "I'm not playing with her anymore."

**Critical reframe:** This does not mean you have a mean kid. It is okay for children to like some kids better than others. Parents should want their children to grow up into adults who can build meaningful relationships, and rejection is part of how kids learn to recognize why they connect with certain people over others.

**The balanced response:** Validate your child's feelings while setting boundaries around how and when they express those feelings. The preference itself is acceptable; the expression of that preference has room for guidance.

### 3. Clinginess

**Manifestations:** Some kids follow one friend everywhere, latch onto peers who are not always kind, or struggle to join new groups.

**What drives it:** Clinginess can come from wanting to feel safe, included, or confident through connection with another child. It is a common phase, especially when kids are still learning self-trust.

**The signal and response:** Clinginess is a sign to focus on building your child's confidence. The underlying need is legitimate; the strategy of meeting it through another child is what needs development.

### 4. Being Bullied

**Spectrum of bullying:** Bullying can range from subtle exclusion (being told they cannot join a game) to overt name-calling or cruelty.

**The hardest part:** For kids, the most painful aspect is often the **aloneness of their experience**. The feeling that no one is on their side can hurt even more than the mean words or actions themselves.

**Why connection is the first step:** Feeling connected and seen by the parent is powerful precisely because it addresses the aloneness that makes bullying so painful. It does not solve the external problem but changes the child's internal experience of it.

**Warning signs of bullying:**
- Child refuses school
- Withdrawing from friends
- Saying others don't like them

**Response pathway:** Believing your child, helping them feel safe, and potentially partnering with their school for additional support.

### 5. Your Child Is the One Bullying

**The essential reframe:** Remember that they are a **good kid having a hard time**, not a bad kid doing bad things. This distinction matters for how parents approach the conversation and how the child experiences themselves.

**Why bullying happens:** All kids experiment with power and belonging. Sometimes this shows up as bullying behaviors: calling people names, excluding others, or lashing out during group play.

**What to look for underneath:** These moments are opportunities to understand what is happening beneath the behavior. Possibilities include:
- Insecurity
- Jealousy
- Testing out power dynamics

**The approach:** Curiosity rather than condemnation. Asking what feelings might be underneath the behavior models emotional intelligence and connection.

These moments **do not define your child**. They are learning opportunities.

## Three Go-To Strategies for Managing Childhood Friendships

### 1. Reflect on Your Own Childhood

When your child shares a story about a tough friend experience, it often **triggers the parent's own insecurities**: moments when you felt lonely, embarrassed, or insecure as a kid.

**The practice:** Take a breath and notice: "What is my stuff? And what is my child's stuff?"

**Why this matters:** By separating the two, you give yourself space to respond thoughtfully rather than reactively. Your childhood wounds can hijack your response to your child's present situation if you do not create this separation.

### 2. Remember That Friendships Are Complicated

**The reality of childhood friendships:** They are not linear. Kids can be best friends one day and not speak the next. They can adore a playmate in kindergarten and drift apart by third grade.

**What to expect:** Throughout their lives, children will have a wide range of experiences and feelings in their friendships.

**The goal is not** to narrow their experiences and feelings so they only have "good" experiences.

**The actual goal:** Help them develop coping skills for the entire range. This is what builds lasting, meaningful emotional intelligence and resilience.

### 3. Support, Don't Solve

Your child needs your **presence, not your answers** to their problems.

**Go-to validating phrases:**
- "I'm so glad we're talking about this."
- "I believe you."
- "Tell me more."

These phrases accomplish multiple things: they signal safety, they communicate that the parent is engaged, and they invite the child to continue processing rather than shutting down.

## Building Long-Term Healthy Friendships

Helping kids with social skills is about **more than arranging playdates**. It involves teaching:
- Flexibility
- Empathy
- Confidence in who they are

**The focus:** Supporting your child through listening, validation, and boundaries helps them develop resilience and emotional intelligence they will carry through to adulthood.

**The perspective shift:** Managing childhood friendships is not about avoiding every conflict. It is about giving kids the tools to navigate tricky friendship dynamics now and in the future.

## Normalizing the "No Friends" Statement

When a child says "I don't have any friends," it might reflect:
- One hard day at recess
- A recent transition like starting at a new school

Going through this experience does not mean your child will always struggle socially. Managing childhood friendships takes time. With parental support, kids learn resilience through these ups and downs.

## Key Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**Solution-giving instead of presence:** Jumping to fix the problem communicates that the feeling is intolerable or wrong.

**Minimizing reassurance:** Statements like "I'm sure they'll play with you tomorrow" dismiss the child's current emotional reality.

**Labeling children based on behavior:** Calling a child who bullies a "bad kid" or "mean kid" conflates behavior with identity and misses the opportunity to explore underlying causes.

**Reacting from your own triggers:** When parental childhood wounds drive the response, the child's actual situation gets lost.

**Trying to eliminate all negative friendship experiences:** The goal is coping skills, not problem-free relationships.

## Core Teaching Points

1. Connection is the antidote to the pain of social rejection because it addresses the aloneness that makes rejection hurt.

2. Children learn relationship skills through the full range of friendship experiences, including difficult ones.

3. Parents who can sit with discomfort alongside their children teach those children that discomfort is survivable.

4. The behavior a child shows in friendships (rejection, clinginess, bullying) is a signal pointing to an underlying emotional need.

5. Separating parental emotional history from the child's present situation allows for more attuned responses.

6. Validation does not mean agreement or approval of all behaviors; boundaries around expression can coexist with validation of feelings.

7. Resilience develops through navigating challenges, not through having challenges removed.
