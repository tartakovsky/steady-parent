# The Learning Space: Building Resilience Through Tolerating Struggle

Source: Dr. Becky Kennedy, Clinical Psychologist, Good Inside

---

## Core Thesis

A significant gap exists between "not knowing" and "knowing" something. Normalizing this in-between space is the key to helping children build resilience. Parents who jump in to fix frustration unintentionally rob children of the opportunity to build critical life skills.

---

## Understanding Resilience

### What Resilience Is

**Resilience is acknowledging distress and trusting that you can move through the distress.** It does not mean bouncing back immediately or putting on a brave face. Resilience is built, not born. Like a muscle, it strengthens with practice.

### What Resilience Looks Like in Real Time

Resilience being built does not look "resilient." It is messy, loud, and uncomfortable. Parents must expect and tolerate these qualities rather than interpret them as signs something is wrong.

---

## The Learning Space Framework

### Definition

The **Learning Space** is the gap between "not knowing" and "knowing." It is the messy middle, full of ups and downs, where growth actually occurs.

### Why Children Struggle and Give Up

When children break down or give up while trying something new, the root cause is often a false expectation. They believe "not knowing" and "knowing" sit right next to each other. When children expect to master new subjects, skills, or hobbies instantly, any stumble or setback feels like failure. This false expectation produces self-defeating statements like "I'm dumb!" or "I'll never figure this out!"

### The Parent's Job Defined Correctly

The parent's job is **not** to make the Learning Space smaller. The parent's job is **not** to move children from "not knowing" to "knowing" as quickly as possible. The parent's job **is** to help children build tolerance for the Learning Space. The longer children can tolerate this space, the more they will be able to learn and grow.

### The Key Distinction: Support vs. Solutions

When children struggle, they need parental support, not parental solutions. Solving frustration for children removes the very experience that builds their capacity.

## Practical Strategies for Parents

### Strategy 1: Draw the Learning Space

Children grasp big ideas more easily through concrete visuals. The same is true for adults.

**How to execute:**
1. Draw two dots on opposite sides of a piece of paper
2. Label one dot "not knowing" and the other dot "knowing"
3. Draw a big squiggly line between them
4. Explain: "Do you know everyone has a space between 'not knowing' and 'knowing'? It's called the Learning Space. And that's where we all go when we're learning something new."

The squiggly line communicates that the path is not straight or predictable. The visual provides a reference point children can return to during future struggles.

### Strategy 2: Validate Feelings in the Learning Space

Validation builds tolerance for frustration over time. When a child is struggling, use specific language that reframes their experience.

**Script options:**

**Reframe frustration as normal progress, not failure:**
"It feels frustrating because you're working really hard, and you're not at the end yet. This doesn't mean you're failing; it means you're in the Learning Space."

**Name the specific skill and introduce temporal perspective:**
"This spelling word/math problem/dance move is part of your Learning Space. One day, it'll move to your Knowing Space. Today, you're still working on it, and that's okay."

**Offer presence without pressure to exit:**
"Ah, you're in the Learning Space. It's hard here, I get it. You don't have to rush out of it. I'll be right here with you."

The phrase "You don't have to rush out of it" is particularly important. It communicates that remaining in the struggle is acceptable and that the child is not supposed to escape discomfort immediately.

### Strategy 3: Model Being in the Learning Space Yourself

**Why modeling matters:**
Children watch parents succeed at countless small tasks: reading books, reaching high shelves, tying shoes. From the child's perspective, adults appear to get everything right immediately. This makes it hard for children to embrace mistakes and take on challenges because the adults around them seem exempt from struggle.

**How to model:**
Normalize frustration by sharing your own Learning Space moments in front of children. Examples include struggling to open a jar of tomato sauce or practicing a new yoga pose where the child can observe.

**Self-talk script to use out loud:**
"I am between 'not knowing' and 'knowing'... and I can live here for a little bit. I can do this. I don't need to reach 'knowing' right away. I can feel good about myself even when I'm in the Learning Space."

This self-talk demonstrates several key principles: tolerating the in-between, self-encouragement without rushing, and maintaining self-worth independent of mastery.

## Reframe for Parents in Difficult Moments

When a child is deep in the struggle of learning, parents should remember this reframe:

The child is **not behind**. The child is **not broken**. The child is **not doing anything wrong**. The child is in the Learning Space and is doing exactly what they are supposed to be doing.

This reframe protects parents from anxiety that could lead to premature intervention.

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Jumping in to fix the situation
Restacking the blocks or solving the equation when a child struggles removes the growth opportunity. The impulse is understandable because watching children struggle is hard. However, solving frustration for children is counterproductive.

### Trying to make the Learning Space smaller
Any attempt to compress or bypass the struggle undermines resilience building.

### Moving children to "knowing" as fast as possible
Speed is not the goal. Tolerance is the goal.

### Expecting resilience to look calm or composed
Parents who expect resilience building to appear graceful will misinterpret the messy, loud, uncomfortable process as evidence of a problem.

## Examples That Carry Teaching Points

**Block tower falling:** A child crumbling into tears when blocks keep falling is not a sign of inability or character flaw. It is a Learning Space moment requiring validation, not intervention.

**Homework frustration:** A child slamming homework down and shouting "I can't do it!" is expressing the discomfort of the Learning Space. The appropriate response is validation and presence rather than solving the problem for them.

**Jar of tomato sauce:** A parent struggling to open a jar can model Learning Space tolerance by narrating the experience out loud rather than hiding the struggle or immediately asking someone else to do it.

**New yoga pose:** Practicing something unfamiliar where the child can observe demonstrates that adults also inhabit the Learning Space regularly.

## Conceptual Hierarchy

1. **Foundational understanding:** Resilience is built through practice, not inherited or automatic
2. **Core mechanism:** The Learning Space is where growth occurs
3. **Parental role:** Build tolerance, not provide solutions
4. **Practical implementation:** Visual tools, validation scripts, personal modeling
5. **Mindset protection:** Reframe struggling children as doing exactly what they should be doing
