# The Benefits of Boredom for Kids: Building Resilience, Creativity, and Independence

Source: Good Inside (Dr. Becky Kennedy, Clinical Psychologist)

## Core Reframe: Boredom as Opportunity, Not Problem

The central thesis holds that boredom is not a parenting failure requiring immediate correction. When a child declares "I'm bored!", parents often experience this as a crisis demanding intervention, whether turning on a screen, googling activities, or arranging emergency playdates. This impulse stems from a misunderstanding of what boredom represents.

**The big idea:** Boredom is not a problem for parents to fix. It is an opportunity for kids to build new skills.

## Understanding What Boredom Actually Signals

When a child says "I'm bored!", they are recognizing that they are not being entertained in that moment. They are missing the fast, frequent dopamine hits that characterize modern childhood, especially in a digital-first world. This recognition triggers discomfort.

**Critical insight:** When parents jump in to fill this gap and provide that dopamine hit, they actually remove the child's ability to tolerate the frustration of boredom and eventually work through it themselves.

The foundational principle states that children can only learn to tolerate feelings that their parents tolerate in them. When parents make space for boredom and demonstrate through their response that it is not a "bad" feeling, children will internalize this and make space for it as well.

## The Three Core Benefits of Boredom

Boredom functions as the gateway to resilience, creativity, and independence through three specific developmental pathways.

### Benefit 1: Learning to Sit with Discomfort

Boredom feels frustrating, and this frustration is developmentally valuable. Learning to tolerate the "blah" feeling of unplanned, open time constitutes a crucial developmental milestone.

**The mechanism:** Sitting with boredom rather than escaping it builds frustration tolerance. The child learns to internalize the belief: "This feels tricky and I'm a kid who can sit with tricky feelings."

### Benefit 2: Tapping into Imagination

Boredom sparks creativity, storytelling, and problem-solving. When left to their own devices for sufficient time, children turn inward. They invent imaginary creatures or build cities out of sofa cushions.

**The distinction between free play and entertainment:** Open-ended play, which is play without clear instructions, goals, winners, or losers, is where flexible thinking and confidence take root. This differs fundamentally from structured entertainment that delivers predetermined experiences.

### Benefit 3: Developing Self-Regulation

One of the most powerful benefits is that children learn they do not need an adult, a snack, or a screen to "make it better." Instead, they learn to sit with and navigate tricky feelings on their own.

**Long-term significance:** Boredom helps children regulate their own emotions. This constitutes a lifelong skill that will serve them far beyond summer.

## The Cultural Pressure Behind "Perfect Summer" Parenting

Parents live in a world that communicates children need constant stimulation: summer camps, crafts, sports, pinterest-worthy parties. These images are sold to parents as the standard. The result is that summer hardly feels like a break.

**The multiple roles parents carry:** During summer, parents typically juggle makeshift camp director, sibling referee, personal chef, and chauffeur. The pressure to entertain children every minute and "make the most" of every day leads quickly to burnout. Ironically, this burnout makes meaningful connection with children harder.

**The cascade effect:** When stretched thin, it becomes hard to respond with patience, hard to stay grounded, hard to be fully present. Reducing summer parenting pressure is not merely nice-to-have. It is essential in order to access the version of yourself you want to be for your child.

**The paradox of memorable moments:** The best memories are rarely manufactured. They emerge from conversations in the car, snuggles before bedtime, and giggles over melting popsicles.

## The Boredom Block Strategy

A "boredom block" is a built-in period where nothing is planned, no one is entertained, and everyone simply exists. This may sound counterintuitive, especially for children who greet every unstructured moment with complaints of boredom. However, that discomfort is precisely where growth begins.

There is no magic formula for the boredom block because every family is different and parents know their children best. The following implementation guidelines help families discover what works for them.

### Implementation Step 1: Set Expectations

Tell the child the plan in advance using language like: "We're going to try something new. It's called a boredom block where we don't plan anything. I know, I know, this might sound... well, boring! And that's okay. It will feel tricky and you're a kid who can figure out tricky things!"

**Why this matters:** When boredom arrives, the child is not surprised by it. Additionally, the parent is not caught off guard by whining because it was anticipated.

### Implementation Step 2: Start Small

Consider what feels approachable for the child based on their current schedule. A boredom block can be as short as 15 minutes or as long as two hours. The starting point should match the child's current tolerance level.

### Implementation Step 3: Validate the Feeling While Holding the Boundary

When the child whines "But I don't know what to do!", validate their frustration without changing the plan.

**Example script:** "Ooh, I believe you. It's frustrating to not have a plan. Remember, this is our boredom block. It's okay to feel bored right now. You're a creative kid and I know something will come to you..."

**The dual action:** Validating the feeling communicates that the emotion is real and acceptable. Holding the boundary communicates that the discomfort is tolerable and temporary.

**Practical recommendation:** Put "boredom" on the calendar. The act of scheduling it legitimizes it and makes it predictable for everyone.

## Responding to "I'm Bored!" in the Moment

The approach involves normalizing boredom as a normal, if sometimes frustrating, experience. Parents can offer support rather than a solution.

**Effective response script:** "Boredom is tricky, and I know you're a kid who can figure out tricky things."

**Proactive preparation:** Parents can prepare children for boredom ahead of time by talking about it as a normal part of the day, especially in summer.

## Why Summer Creates Ideal Conditions for Boredom

During summer, children have less structure and more space in their schedules compared to the continuous obligations of the school year. Without back-to-back activities, children experience natural pauses in outside stimulation. These pauses create the conditions to lean into the benefits of boredom.

## Adapting for Different Children: Deeply Feeling Kids and Neurodivergent Kids

Two truths coexist: all children benefit from boredom, and different children need different kinds of support to access these benefits.

**The spectrum of experience:** Some children experience boredom as an uncomfortable pause. Other children experience it as an actual trigger. The trigger response is especially common for Deeply Feeling Kids (DFKs) or neurodivergent kids, who crave more predictability and structure.

**The correct response is not avoidance:** This does not mean parents should avoid letting these children experience boredom. Instead, the approach involves supporting them through boredom differently.

### The Boredom Toolkit Strategy for DFKs

Deeply Feeling Kids might need extra support preparing for boredom. The strategy involves creating a "boredom toolkit" together in advance. The child gets to pick two or three go-to ideas for when they feel bored.

**Why this works:** Having a toolkit helps the child feel more in control when boredom inevitably shows up. Once they have an idea of what to expect, tolerating the discomfort of unstructured time becomes a little bit easier.

## Three Summary Takeaways

1. Boredom is not a problem for parents to solve. It is an opportunity for kids to build critical skills.

2. Being bored functions as the gateway to creativity, independence, and resilience.

3. Parents can ease the mental load of summer parenting by scheduling boredom blocks for their children. The reassurance holds: you are doing enough, you are enough.
