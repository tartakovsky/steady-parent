# How to Help Your Child Sleep Through the Night

Source: Good Inside (Dr. Becky Kennedy, Clinical Psychologist)
Published: June 18, 2025

## Core Paradigm Shift: From Training Behaviors to Teaching Skills

The fundamental premise rejects traditional sleep training. Sleeping independently does not come from "training" kids' behaviors. It comes from teaching kids new skills. This distinction matters because the skill-building approach produces both immediate sleep improvements and long-term developmental benefits including emotional regulation, resilience, and confidence.

Traditional sleep training methods (crying it out, sticker charts, locking bedroom doors, gradual parental withdrawal) treat sleep as a behavioral problem to be corrected. The Good Inside approach treats sleep as a skill deficit to be addressed through teaching.

## The Root Cause: Sleep Struggles Are Separation Struggles

**Understanding before intervening** is a core principle. Parents must understand the underlying cause before attempting any solution.

The biological reality: Children are evolutionarily wired to stay attached to caregivers for safety. At night, parents ask children to separate for 10 to 12 hours, in the dark, alone. This creates a fundamental conflict between a child's survival instincts and parental expectations.

**The opposing needs at night**: Parents crave separation and alone time. Children want closeness. Recognizing this tension allows a mindset shift from "What's wrong with my kid?" to "My kid is having a hard time, not giving me a hard time."

The reframe: When a toddler fights bedtime or a child climbs into the parental bed at 2 AM, it is not manipulation or defiance. The child is attempting to re-establish safety by achieving physical proximity to the caregiver. The behavior is attachment-seeking, not rule-breaking.

## Four Common Sleep Challenges

### 1. Bedtime Battles

**Manifestation**: Child protests at bedtime, begging for "one more book" or "three more hugs."

**What is actually happening**: The child is trying to delay separating from the parent.

**Intervention**: Maintain consistent bedtime routines. Consistency helps children prepare psychologically for separation and builds tolerance skills for the discomfort of being away from the parent.

### 2. Sleep Regression

**Definition**: A child who was previously sleeping well begins waking frequently or resisting bedtime.

**Common triggers**: Major developmental leaps (learning to walk, learning to talk), routine changes (dropping a nap, starting daycare).

**Reframe**: Regression is a signal, not a setback. It indicates the child needs extra support and closeness during a period of adaptation to change.

**Important reassurance**: Regression does not mean something is wrong with the child, and it will not last forever.

### 3. Nighttime Fears

**Why fear intensifies at night**: The environment is dark, quiet, and the child is alone. These conditions naturally increase anxiety for toddlers and young children.

**The common mistake that backfires**: Well-intentioned reassurances like "It's okay, there's no monster under the bed!" can escalate anxiety rather than reduce it. This approach leaves children feeling alone and confused because it solves the fear for them rather than with them.

**Better approach**: Instead of solving kids' worries for them, help kids build the skills to cope with their worries. This creates long-term resilience rather than temporary relief.

### 4. Middle-of-the-Night Wake-Ups

**What happens biologically**: When children wake at 2 AM, they are dysregulated and alone. Their attachment system activates with the questions: "Where is my parent? Am I safe?"

**Why they seek the parent**: Their bodies are wired to be close to caregivers for survival. Coming to find the parent restores connection. This is not rule-breaking behavior.

**The parent's role**: A calm, sturdy presence helps the child re-regulate.

**The developmental goal**: Help the child internalize a sense of safety so they can learn to self-soothe and regulate themselves independently.

## Primary Strategy: Daytime Practice of Separation Skills

**The timing problem**: At night, children are tired and experiencing intense separation anxiety. These are not ideal conditions for learning new skills.

**The solution**: Practice separation skills during the day so children feel ready to use them at night.

## The Bedtime Mantra Technique

**How mantras work**: Mantras help calm anxiety by giving a child a soothing, rhythmic phrase to focus on.

**Introduction method**: Turn it into a story about your own childhood. Example script: "When I was little, my dad taught me a mantra. It helped me feel safe and connected to him right before bed. Maybe it's something we could try, too."

**Example mantra**: "[Child's name] is safe, Mommy is near, my bed is cozy."

The structure (safety statement + connection statement + comfort statement) addresses the core separation anxiety by affirming safety, maintaining perceived connection to the parent, and grounding the child in their physical environment.

## Key Differentiators from Traditional Sleep Training

**Traditional methods**:
- Cry it out
- Sticker charts
- Locking bedroom doors
- Gradually withdrawing parental presence
- Focus on behavioral modification

**Skill-building approach**:
- Rooted in safety and connection, not fear
- Teaches coping skills for separation
- Builds sense of safety during daytime hours
- Prepares child for nighttime separation through practice
- Addresses the root cause (separation anxiety) rather than the symptom (not sleeping)

## Three Consolidated Takeaways

1. Sleeping independently comes from teaching skills, not training behaviors.

2. Sleep struggles are separation struggles. The child is not being manipulative or misbehaving. The child is expressing a developmentally appropriate need for connection.

3. Building up a child's skills and sense of safety during the day prepares them to separate from parents at night.

## Boundaries and Consistency

**The balance**: Offer comfort without negotiating boundaries. Stay consistent with the bedtime routine. The structure itself provides security while the parent maintains their role as the calm, sturdy presence.

**What not to do**: Abandon routines during regression or fear phases. The child needs extra support, structure, and closeness simultaneously.

## Developmental Appropriateness

The article emphasizes that children's separation difficulties manifest differently across developmental stages. The behaviors (bedtime battles, regression, fears, night waking) are all variations of the same underlying separation struggle, expressed according to the child's developmental capacity.

This framing removes judgment from both parent and child. The parent is not failing by having a child who struggles with sleep. The child is not being difficult or manipulative. Both are navigating a fundamental tension between human attachment needs and the practical requirements of independent sleep.
