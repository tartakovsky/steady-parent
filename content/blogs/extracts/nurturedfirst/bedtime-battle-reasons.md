# Why Your Child Isn't Sleeping: 5 Common Reasons For Bedtime Battles

**Source:** Nurtured First (Shannon Wassenaar, Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** January 26, 2024
**Audience:** Parents of toddlers and preschoolers experiencing bedtime resistance

---

## Core Framework

Bedtime battles in toddlers and preschoolers are never random or manipulative. There is always an underlying reason driving the resistance. The author identifies **five root causes** that explain why children struggle to fall asleep. Understanding which cause applies is the essential first step toward resolution.

---

## Baseline Expectations for Sleep Onset

**Normal sleep onset time:** 30 to 45 minutes for toddlers to fall asleep (varies by child and circumstances)

**Threshold for concern:** If a child consistently takes longer than 45 minutes to fall asleep, the duration is considered "too long" and warrants investigation into underlying causes

---

## Reason 1: Separation Anxiety

### Definition

Separation anxiety occurs when a child becomes fearful of being away from parents or caregivers. This fear activates the child's stress response system, keeping their mind and body on high alert.

### Why It Prevents Sleep

The anxious state blocks the normal physiological response to sleep cues. Even environmental signals like darkness fail to trigger sleepiness because the child's nervous system remains in a vigilant, protective mode. The child cannot relax sufficiently to transition into sleep.

### Behavioral Signs

- Crying, screaming, or panicking when parent attempts to leave the room
- Physical clinging to parents and resisting being put down
- **Stalling techniques** such as repeated requests for drinks or bathroom visits
- Begging parent to stay in room or lie down with them
- Only able to fall asleep in parent's arms or through co-sleeping

### Causal Chain

Fear of separation triggers stress response -> Nervous system remains on high alert -> Child cannot relax -> Sleep cues fail to work -> Extended time to fall asleep or inability to fall asleep without parent present

## Reason 2: Nighttime Fears

### Developmental Basis

Around ages 2-3, children develop the cognitive ability to create new ideas, images, and concepts in their minds. This emerging imagination enables them to conjure fears about things that are not physically present (monsters, ghosts, creatures in closets).

### Why Evening Intensifies Fears

The **slowness of evening** creates space for worries to surface. During the day, children can distract themselves with activities. At night, the reduced stimulation allows suppressed fears and concerns to emerge.

### Common Triggers

- Exposure to unsettling movies or disturbing content (including overheard conversations, content from other children at school/bus)
- Major life changes that create underlying stress (moving homes, new baby, switching schools)

### Typical Nighttime Fears

- Fear of the dark
- Fear of being alone
- Fear of monsters, ghosts, or bugs
- Fear of bad dreams

### Behavioral Signs

- Crying or screaming when lights are turned off
- Calling out for caregiver during the night
- Requesting night lights or door left open
- Difficulty falling asleep or frequent night waking
- Asking parents to check under bed or in closet
- Talking about scary creatures or bad dreams

### Resolution Direction

Provide comfort through consistent routines, night lights, and reassurance. Create an environment that helps the child feel safe and secure.

## Reason 3: Sleep Pressure

### Definition

**Sleep pressure** is the biological drive to sleep that accumulates the longer a person remains awake. It naturally increases throughout the day and peaks at night.

### The Balance Problem

Getting bedtime right requires finding the **sweet spot** where the child is tired enough to fall asleep relatively quickly but not so exhausted that they have a meltdown. Both extremes cause problems.

### Undertiredness (Sleep Pressure Too Low)

**Cause:** Child has not been awake long enough for sufficient sleep pressure to build (common when naps are too long, too late, or child's sleep needs have decreased)

**Signs of undertiredness:**
- Taking 1 to 2 hours to fall asleep
- Playing or talking in bed instead of sleeping
- Waking up early, refreshed and ready to start the day
- Refusing or skipping naps
- Resisting bedtime and wanting to stay up later

**Mechanism:** Without sufficient sleep pressure, the body simply does not feel sleepy enough to transition into sleep, regardless of external cues or routines.

### Overtiredness (Sleep Pressure Too High)

**Cause:** Child has been awake too long and sleep pressure has built up excessively, often triggering a stress response

**Signs of overtiredness:**
- Crying, screaming, or tantrums at bedtime
- Tossing and turning for extended periods before falling asleep
- Frequent night waking
- Waking up grumpy and irritable
- Requires sleep crutches (rocking, nursing, other interventions) to fall asleep

**Mechanism:** When overtired, the body releases stress hormones that make it paradoxically harder for the child to relax and shut off their mind and body. The exhaustion itself becomes a barrier to sleep.

### Practical Implication

Parents should track and adjust wake windows, nap timing, and bedtime to optimize sleep pressure for their individual child.

## Reason 4: Sensory Needs

### Framework

Every child has a unique sensory profile that affects how their nervous system responds to input like sounds, textures, tastes, and movement. This profile determines what the child needs to feel calm and ready for sleep.

### Two Sensory Profiles

**Sensory-seeking children:**
- Crave sensory input
- Can handle (and may need) extra sounds, textures, movement
- **Bedtime challenge:** May be unable to settle because they need more sensory input to satisfy their nervous system
- **Behavioral signs:** Jumping on bed, making loud noises, repeatedly getting up after being tucked in

**Sensory-avoiding children:**
- Easily overwhelmed by sensory input
- **Bedtime challenge:** May struggle due to sensitivity to sounds, lights, textures of pajamas or sheets, or other environmental cues
- **Critical insight:** Some sensory triggers are so subtle that parents do not realize their child could be affected by them. For a sensory-avoiding child, even minor inputs can feel overwhelming and prevent relaxation.

### Intervention Approaches

- **For sensory-seeking children:** Provide extra "heavy work" sensory input before bed (activities that provide deep pressure or resistance)
- **For sensory-avoiding children:** Limit stimuli and create a calming, controlled environment (consider lighting, sounds, fabric textures, room temperature)

### Key Insight

Parents must understand their individual child's sensory profile to make effective adjustments. A one-size-fits-all approach will not work because sensory needs vary dramatically between children.

## Reason 5: Life Situations and Transitions

### Core Principle

Big life changes and transitions disrupt a toddler's sense of stability and can directly cause sleep problems. When sleep struggles emerge, parents should investigate recent or ongoing changes in the child's life.

### Major Life Changes That Disrupt Sleep

- New baby in the family
- Starting daycare or preschool
- Significant loss in the family (death, pet loss)
- Moving to a new home
- Family conflict such as divorce or separation

### Indirect Effects from Parent Life Changes

Changes in a parent's life that do not directly involve the child can still affect the child's sleep through **ripple effects**:
- Parent illness
- Parent traveling for work
- Parent returning to work after being home

**Mechanism:** These situations change the parent's available time, energy, and emotional state. Children are highly attuned to their parents and can sense when something has shifted, even if they cannot articulate it.

### Considerations for Highly Sensitive Children

What appears small or simple to a parent may be overwhelming to the child. Examples of changes that can significantly disrupt a sensitive child's sleep:
- Transitioning to sharing a room with a sibling
- Moving to a "toddler bed" from a crib
- Moving to a different bedroom within the same home

### Blind Spot Warning

Parents who are experiencing significant life changes themselves may be so consumed by their own stress that they fail to notice how their situation is affecting their child. This prevents them from identifying a key piece of the puzzle when trying to solve sleep struggles.

---

## Overarching Principles

### Anti-Pattern: Assuming Manipulation

Children do not resist sleep to be "bad" or manipulative. There is always an underlying reason. Attributing bedtime battles to defiance or manipulation prevents parents from identifying and addressing the actual cause.

### Primary Intervention Strategy

Adopt an **attitude of curiosity** rather than frustration. The most important step in solving sleep struggles is understanding why the child is having difficulty. Once the underlying cause is identified, targeted tools can be applied.

### Interaction of Causes

While presented as five distinct reasons, multiple causes may operate simultaneously. A child experiencing a life transition may also develop separation anxiety or nighttime fears as secondary effects. Parents should consider all five categories when investigating.

### Temporary Nature

The article emphasizes that these struggles are often "temporary sleep setbacks." With patience and consistency, families can work through these periods. The framing suggests that bedtime battles are a normal part of development rather than permanent problems.

---

## Summary of the Five Causes

| Cause | Core Issue | Key Sign |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Separation Anxiety** | Fear of being away from parent | Clinging, stalling, only sleeps in parent's presence |
| **Nighttime Fears** | Imagination-driven fears | Requests to check closet/under bed, fear of darkness |
| **Sleep Pressure** | Timing mismatch (undertired or overtired) | Takes 1-2 hours to fall asleep OR tantrums at bedtime |
| **Sensory Needs** | Nervous system requires specific input (more or less) | Jumping/noise-making OR sensitivity to textures/sounds |
| **Life Situations** | Disruption from transitions or changes | Sleep problems correlate with recent life events |
