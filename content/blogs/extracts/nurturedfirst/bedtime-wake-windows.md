# Wake Windows and Bedtime Sleep Timing for Toddlers and Preschoolers

**Source:** Nurtured First (Shannon Wassenaar, Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** January 26, 2024
**Age Groups:** Toddler (1-3), Preschooler (3-5), Big Kid (6-12)

---

## Core Concept: Individual Sleep Latency Variation

Children, like adults, have **different natural sleep onset patterns**. Some children fall asleep immediately upon lying down, while others require a transition period involving tossing, turning, babbling, singing, fidgeting, or playing before their bodies are ready for sleep. This variation is normal and reflects individual temperament differences.

---

## Average Time to Fall Asleep by Age

**Sleep latency** (time from "lights out" to actual sleep onset) varies by developmental stage:

| Age Group | Age Range | Average Time to Fall Asleep |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------------|
| Toddlers | 1-3 years | 30-45 minutes |
| Preschoolers | 3-5 years | 20-30 minutes |

**Critical Definition:** "Lights out" marks the start of sleep latency measurement. This period includes any parental presence activities such as lying beside the child, holding their hand, or singing songs.

**Warning Sign:** If a child consistently takes significantly longer than these averages, changes to the sleep approach are needed. Example given: a preschooler taking almost an hour to fall asleep after a new sibling arrived indicated the schedule was not working for the child or family.

---

## Wake Windows: Complete Reference Guide

**Definition:** Wake windows are the periods of time children can stay awake between naps or before bedtime. Following age-appropriate wake windows ensures the child has accumulated sufficient **sleep pressure** (tiredness) to fall asleep and remain asleep.

### Wake Window Chart by Age

| Age (Years) | Approximate Sleep Needed Per Day | Suggested Wake Window |
|-------------|----------------------------------|----------------------|
| 1-2 | 12-14 hours | 4-6 hours |
| 3 (with nap) | 11-13 hours | 6-7 hours |
| 3 (no nap) | 11-13 hours | 11-13 hours |
| 4-5 | 10-13 hours | 11-14 hours (no nap) |
| 6-12 | 9-12 hours | 12-15 hours (no nap) |

**Important Caveat:** These are guidelines, not rigid rules. Every child is unique and may fall outside these ranges.

## Biological Sleep Mechanisms

### Circadian Rhythm Function

As the day progresses, eyes naturally become heavier. When the sun sets, darkness sends signals to the brain that prepare the body for sleep. This biological rhythm exists in children as well as adults.

**Parental Role:** Work WITH these natural rhythms rather than against them. Understanding sleep science helps parents make sense of sleep struggles and identify where changes are needed.

---

## Undertired vs. Overtired: Diagnostic Framework

When a child takes too long to fall asleep, determining whether they are undertired or overtired guides the appropriate intervention.

### Signs of Being Undertired (Wake Window Too Short)

- Acting silly, playful, or hyperactive during the bedtime routine
- Protesting, stalling, or procrastinating at bedtime
- Restlessness and fidgeting in bed

### Signs of Being Overtired (Wake Window Too Long)

- Increased whining and fussiness
- Clumsy movements
- Uncontrollable giggles (counterintuitive but indicates exhaustion)

### The "Sweet Spot" Concept

The goal is to identify each child's individual **sweet spot**: the window of time when they have accumulated the right amount of tiredness to fall asleep within an appropriate timeframe. This requires observation of the child's specific bedtime behaviors over time.

---

## Five Factors Affecting Time to Fall Asleep

1. **Sleep pressure** (accumulated tiredness based on wake time)
2. **Sleep environment** (room conditions, comfort factors)
3. **Pre-bedtime activities** (what happens in the hour before bed)
4. **Life situations** (major changes, stressors, transitions)
5. **Sensory needs** (individual sensory processing differences)

Getting curious about these factors provides more information to work with, increases compassion for the child, and builds patience during bedtime challenges.

## Bedtime Routine Framework

### Research Foundation

Studies demonstrate that consistent bedtime routines in early childhood lead to better sleep habits long-term. Establishing healthy sleep routines early makes sleep transitions easier as children grow.

### Timing Parameters

- **Routine duration:** 30-45 minutes before the target "lights out" time
- **"Lights out" timing:** Should occur 20-30 minutes before the child is expected to actually fall asleep
- **Consistency:** Follow a similar plan each night to help the brain and body prepare for and anticipate sleep

### Planning Calculation Method

1. Note how many hours the child slept the previous night
2. Consider their age-appropriate wake window
3. Work backward from wake time to determine appropriate bedtime
4. Build in routine time BEFORE the child reaches overtired state

## Sample Schedules with Detailed Breakdowns

### Sample 1: 2-Year-Old with One Nap

**Calculation Logic:**
- Wake time: 7:30 a.m.
- Wake window before nap: 4-6 hours (nap at 12:30 p.m.)
- Nap ends: 2:00 p.m.
- Wake window after nap: 4-6 hours
- Total sleep need: 12-14 hours per 24 hours
- Target bedtime: 7:30 p.m.

**Schedule:**
- 7:30 a.m.: Wake up
- 12:30 p.m. - 2:00 p.m.: Nap (1.5 hours)
- 6:15 p.m. - 7:00 p.m.: Bedtime routine
- 7:00 p.m.: Lights out
- 7:30 p.m.: Falls asleep

**Routine Components:**
- Clean up toys
- Diaper change and pajamas
- Brush teeth
- Read two books
- Sing one lullaby and kiss all the stuffies

**Analysis:**
- Pre-nap awake time: 5 hours
- Post-nap awake time until sleep: 5.5 hours
- Total sleep: 13.5 hours (nap + night)

**Key Principle:** The routine starts well before overtiredness would set in, preventing longer, more challenging bedtimes.

### Sample 2: Toddler Without Nap

**Target:** Bedtime roughly 11-13 hours after morning wake time (adjusted for sleep cues and temperament).

**Sensory-Based Modifications:**

**For Highly Sensitive Children (need less sensory input):**
- Earlier bedtime
- Slower, calmer routine
- Transition to quiet play before routine

**For Active/Sensory-Seeking Children (need more sensory input):**
- May need closer to 13 hours awake before sleep
- Include rough and tumble play in routine
- Bodies need movement before they can settle

**Schedule (Active Child Example):**
- 7:00 a.m.: Wake up
- 5:45 p.m. - 6:00 p.m.: Rough and tumble play
- 6:00 p.m. - 6:30 p.m.: Bedtime routine (pajamas)
- 6:30 p.m.: Lights out
- 7:00 p.m.: Falls asleep

**Analysis:**
- Total awake time: 12 hours
- Total sleep: 12 hours

**Transition Period Note:** A toddler who has just dropped their nap may need an earlier bedtime (6:00-6:30 p.m.) as they adjust to being awake all day.

### Sample 3: Preschooler (3-5 years) Without Nap

**Target:** 11-14 hours of awake time before falling asleep.

**Schedule:**
- 6:30 a.m.: Wake up
- 6:15 p.m. - 6:30 p.m.: Yoga stretches
- 6:30 p.m. - 7:00 p.m.: Bedtime routine
- 7:00 p.m.: Lights out
- 7:20 p.m.: Falls asleep

**Routine Components:**
- Brush teeth and go potty
- Read a chapter of a book
- Sing two songs while snuggling in bed

**Analysis:**
- Total awake time: ~13 hours
- Total sleep: 11 hours

## Pre-Routine Wind-Down Phase

### Purpose

An hour or so before bed, begin transitioning the child toward sleep readiness.

### Wind-Down Activities by Temperament

**Highly Sensitive Children:**
- Transition to quiet play

**Active/Sensory-Seeking Children:**
- Roughhousing or rough and tumble play

### Universal Wind-Down Options

- Bath
- Stretching
- Yoga

After wind-down time, begin the more structured bedtime routine steps.

---

## Flexibility and Adaptation Principles

### Dynamic Adjustment

Even with a perfect plan in place, be prepared to adapt the routine when:
- Running out of time
- Sensing the child needs more time before bed that day
- Sensing the child needs less time before bed that day

### Timing Philosophy

A consistent routine does not require timing every single step. The purpose of having a plan is to help parent and child stay on track, ensuring the child gets into bed around the time their body is optimally tired for sleep.

## Why Sleep Matters: Developmental Impact

### Cognitive Effects

Sleep is essential for:
- Growth
- Learning
- Development
- Information retention
- Coping with stress
- Learning new skills
- Impulse control

### Consequence of Inadequate Sleep

Research shows sleep deprivation negatively impacts:
- Behavior
- Mood
- Focus
- Overall health

Children's rapidly developing brains make adequate sleep even more critical than for adults.

## Key Takeaways Summary

1. **Sleep latency expectations:** Toddlers need 30-45 minutes; preschoolers need 20-30 minutes. These averages set realistic expectations.

2. **Wake windows matter:** Age-appropriate wake windows prevent both overtiredness and undertiredness at bedtime.

3. **Recognize tiredness signals:** Overtiredness shows as fussiness; undertiredness shows as restlessness. Recognition enables routine adjustment.

4. **Consistency is crucial:** A calming, consistent bedtime routine signals to the child that it's time to wind down, enabling faster or smoother bedtime.

5. **Individualization required:** Bedtime routines and schedules must be tailored based on age, nap status, sensory needs, and individual temperament.

6. **Sleep drives development:** Adequate sleep is essential for growth, learning, and emotional regulation. Address sleep struggles proactively.
