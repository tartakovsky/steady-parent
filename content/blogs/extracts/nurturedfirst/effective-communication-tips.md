# 10 Effective Tips For Better Communication With Your Child

Source: Nurtured First (Jess VanderWier, MA, RP)
Applicable ages: Toddler, Preschooler, Big Kid

## Core Philosophy

Healthy communication with children requires intentional practice across all relationships. The parent serves as both a model and a safe harbor. Communication habits formed in the parent-child relationship shape how the child will communicate throughout life. It is never too late to begin building healthy communication, whether the child is an infant or an adult.

## 1. Use "I Feel" Statements

### The Framework

Replace blaming statements with the structured formula: **"I feel [emotion] when you [behavior]. Could you please [requested change]?"**

### Why This Works

- **Models healthy communication**: Teaches children how to express themselves constructively
- **Helps children label emotions**: Expands their emotional vocabulary
- **Reduces defensiveness**: Avoids triggering the child's protective reactions because you are not blaming them
- **Opens collaboration**: Positions you as a partner seeking solutions rather than an adversary assigning fault

### Examples

**Scenario 1: Child not listening**
- Anti-pattern: "You never listen to me!"
- Recommended: "I feel like you are not listening to me when you do not look at me as I talk. Could you please give me your attention next time I talk to you?"

**Scenario 2: Child lying**
- Anti-pattern: "You are such a liar!"
- Recommended: "I feel hurt when you are not truthful with me. Could you please be honest next time?"

### Key Insight

This "simple change in language can do wonders" for parent-child communication because it shifts from accusation to invitation.

## 2. Define Expectations BEFORE Behavior Happens

### When to Apply

- When there is a specific behavior the child struggles with
- When certain circumstances make problematic behavior more likely (predictable triggers)

### How to Implement

Communicate exactly what you expect from your child before the triggering situation occurs.

### Why This Works

- The child knows what to expect, reducing anxiety and confusion
- Prevents the need for reactive, in-the-moment responses
- Avoids ineffective tactics like **bribing** or **yelling** that parents resort to when caught off-guard

### Anti-patterns to Avoid

- Waiting until the behavior occurs to address it
- Using bribes as a last-minute intervention
- Resorting to yelling when unprepared

## 3. Use a Calm Voice

### Acknowledgment of Difficulty

Maintaining calm is genuinely hard. The author explicitly validates that keeping composure is not always easy, but emphasizes it remains important.

### The Parent's Role

Parents must be the child's "place of calm especially during their emotional storms." This framing positions the parent as an anchor, not a participant in the chaos.

### Research Findings on Yelling

Yelling is an **ineffective form of communication** for multiple reasons:

1. **Children hear tone, not content**: When yelled at, children perceive the parent's anger through vocal tone but cannot report afterward why they got in trouble
2. **Creates fear without understanding**: The child may become fearful and temporarily stop the behavior, but does not learn why the behavior was wrong or what the parent expects
3. **No lasting behavioral change**: Because the child does not understand the lesson, the behavior is likely to continue in the future
4. **May reinforce unwanted behavior**: If the child's behavior is attention-seeking, yelling provides attention and thus reinforces the exact behavior you want to stop

### What to Do Instead

Calmly **follow through** with consequences that you had previously outlined (connects to Tip 2).

## 4. Show You're Listening With Nonverbal Communication

### Purpose

Children need to know that what they are saying matters to you. Demonstrating active listening encourages children to continue talking and sharing.

### Specific Nonverbal Techniques

- **Make eye contact**: Shows focused attention
- **Posture your body towards them**: Physical orientation signals engagement
- **Nod**: Indicates you are following along
- **Make appropriate facial expressions**: Match your reactions to what they are saying

### Outcome

When children feel listened to, they are encouraged to keep communicating.

## 5. Show You're Listening With Verbal Communication

### Core Technique

**Repeat back** what the child is saying to demonstrate understanding and engagement.

### Reflection Phrases to Use

**For clarification:**
- "I want to make sure I am hearing you correctly, did you say _____?"
- "Am I correct in saying that you are feeling ____?"

**For validation:**
- "It's really hard when Daddy leaves for work, you feel sad."
- "You feel frustrated right now, you don't want to share."
- "You are frustrated right now, you don't want to brush your teeth."
- "It makes sense that you would feel _______."

**For acknowledgment:**
- "I hear you saying _________."
- "It sounds like you are telling me _______."

### Key Pattern

These phrases combine **naming the emotion** with **acknowledging the situation**, helping children feel both heard and understood.

## 6. Model the Desired Communication Style

### The Principle

Children learn from what they see their parents doing. Your communication with spouse, friends, and family members becomes the child's template.

### Anti-patterns That Children Will Learn From

- Yelling
- Swearing
- Withdrawing (silent treatment, avoidance)

### Requirement

Make sure your child sees you practicing healthy communication with those around you. The child will naturally adopt what they observe.

### Implication

Your communication in all relationships, not just with the child, shapes their development.

## 7. Talk About Things Your Child Enjoys

### Purpose

Building healthy communication habits by finding topics that excite and motivate the child to engage in conversation.

### What This Communicates to the Child

- You care about things they care about
- You want to talk to them
- Their interests matter to you

### How to Apply

Identify what topics genuinely excite your child and use these as conversation starters. Apply the listening techniques from Tips 4 and 5 during these conversations.

## 8. Listen to Your Child's Opinion

### The Challenge

Healthy communication requires listening to opinions you disagree with without responding in problematic ways.

### Behaviors to Avoid When You Disagree

- **Being argumentative**: Fighting their point rather than understanding it
- **Being condescending**: Talking down to them or dismissing their view
- **Interrupting**: Cutting them off before they finish their thought

### What to Do Instead

Allow your child to complete their full thought before responding with your own opinion.

### Why This Matters

When children feel their opinion is valued even when there is disagreement, they are much more likely to come to you again in the future. This builds long-term trust and openness.

### Key Outcome

The child learns: "Even if my parent disagrees, they still hear me out."

## 9. Don't Argue About Who Is Right

### Why Arguments About Rightness Fail

- It is not always relevant who is objectively correct
- The drive to be right is "mostly self-serving" because humans naturally want to feel correct
- Right/wrong framing creates winners and losers rather than partners

### Alternative Framework

**Basic validation:** "Even though I understand that you feel ______, I feel ______."

**Collaboration-focused:** "Even though I understand that you feel ______, I feel ______, can we agree on ______?"

### Why This Works

- **Validates their feelings**: They feel heard even when you disagree
- **Builds willingness to compromise**: When children experience parents working with them rather than against them, they learn to reciprocate
- **Sets pattern for future interactions**: Children who experience collaborative conflict resolution will be willing to work with you in future disagreements

## 10. Be Intentional About Your Own Communication Habits

### Core Insight

Healthy communication becomes natural in the parent-child relationship when it is a habit in all aspects of your life. The inverse is also true: poor communication habits elsewhere will seep into parenting.

### Encouragement

It is never too late to start building healthy communication. This applies whether:
- Your child is brand new (infant)
- Your child is an adult

### The Two Fundamental Practices

1. **Intentional listening**: Actively working to hear and understand
2. **Respecting each other's opinion**: Valuing their perspective even in disagreement

### Universal Application

These practices benefit children at any age, making communication improvement always worthwhile regardless of the child's developmental stage.

## Summary of Key Principles

### Proactive vs. Reactive

- Set expectations before behavior occurs (proactive)
- Have consequences outlined in advance (proactive)
- Avoid bribing, yelling, or other in-the-moment reactions (avoid reactive)

### Modeling

- Children learn communication patterns from observing parents
- All relationships you have teach your child how to communicate
- Your behavior with spouse, friends, and family matters

### Validation Over Winning

- Avoid blame language
- Validate feelings even when you disagree
- Seek collaboration rather than proving who is right

### Active Listening

- Combine nonverbal cues (eye contact, body posture, nodding, facial expressions)
- Use verbal reflection (repeat back, name emotions, acknowledge situations)
- Listen fully before responding

### Emotional Regulation

- Parent must be the calm anchor during the child's emotional storms
- Yelling is counterproductive because children hear tone without content
- Yelling may actually reinforce attention-seeking behaviors
