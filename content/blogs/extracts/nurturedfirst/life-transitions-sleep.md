# Sleep Challenges During Life Transitions: Complete Knowledge Extract

**Source:** Nurtured First (nurturedfirst.com/toddler/life-transitions-sleep)
**Author:** Jess VanderWier, MA, RP (Registered Psychotherapist, Master's in Counselling Psychology)
**Date:** July 12, 2023
**Audience:** Parents of toddlers, preschoolers, and big kids

## Core Premise

Good sleepers can suddenly experience sleep disruptions when significant life changes occur. This phenomenon parallels adult experiences: adults also struggle to sleep when facing big changes due to excitement or fear. Children are not immune to this pattern; they experience the same underlying mechanisms.

## The Safety-Sleep Connection

**Central mechanism:** Day-to-day routine creates a sense of safety for children. When routine changes abruptly, children lose this sense of safety, which directly manifests as sleep struggles.

**Behavioral manifestations of lost safety:**
- Clinging to parents longer than usual
- Larger, more intense meltdowns
- Coming out of bed repeatedly to seek parents
- All behaviors serve the purpose of re-establishing felt safety

## Category 1: Changes in Family Dynamics

### Why Family Changes Disrupt Sleep

Family dynamics form the foundational structure of a child's felt safety. When this structure shifts, children must recalibrate their sense of security, which takes time and support.

### Specific Disrupting Events

- Addition of a new sibling
- New teacher, nanny, or babysitter
- New daycare or preschool
- A parent traveling for work
- A parent or sibling hospitalized
- Loss of a family member (death)
- Separation or divorce

### Case Study: Daycare Transition

**Observed symptoms:**
- Early morning waking
- Difficulty settling at night

**Parent's parallel experience:** The author experienced the same anxiety about returning to work, being apart from her child, and feeling overwhelmed by routine changes. This recognition that both parent and child were experiencing parallel emotional responses led to:
- Less frustration
- More compassion
- Understanding that both parties needed time and patience to adjust

**Key insight:** When parents experience transition anxiety, children likely experience it too. This parallel recognition can shift the parental response from frustration to compassion.

## Category 2: Sleeping in New Environments

### The Adult Parallel

Adults often struggle to sleep the first night in a new bed while on vacation. It takes time to settle the mind and body. Children experience this same phenomenon.

### Types of Environmental Changes

- Moving to a new bed
- Moving to a new room
- Moving to a new house
- Adding a sibling to share the room

### Why New Environments Feel Unsettling

Familiar comforts of home are replaced with:
- Strange smells
- Foreign noises
- Unknown surroundings

### Case Study: Toddler in New Sleep Space

**Lost elements of routine:**
- Cannot run to favorite chair for bedtime story
- Cannot say "night night" to beloved cars
- Spacious, cozy crib replaced with firm, cold, compact playpen

**Environmental disruptions:**
- Lack of blackout curtains causes distraction
- Instead of lying down, child stands and scans room with curiosity
- Change in scenery captures attention

### Recommended Mitigation Strategies

When child sleeps in new environment, emulate the bedtime routine by bringing:
- Favorite bedtime stories
- Stuffed animals
- Clean linens that smell like home
- White noise machine
- Blackout curtains

**Principle:** Recreate as many sensory and procedural elements of home as possible to maintain continuity.

## Category 3: Physical Discomfort

### Common Physical Causes of Sleep Disruption

- Teething
- Illness
- Digestive problems (diarrhea, constipation)
- Pain from various sources
- Asthma
- Allergies

### The Communication Challenge

Young children often cannot verbally communicate physical discomfort. Parents must become observational detectives.

### Signs of Physical Discomfort in Pre-Verbal or Limited-Verbal Children

**Behavioral changes:**
- Increased irritability
- Increased fussiness
- Unusually quiet demeanor (absence of typical behavior)

**Eating pattern changes:**
- Refusal to eat or drink
- Difficulty swallowing

**Bowel movement changes:**
- Diarrhea
- Constipation

**Body language cues:**
- Rubbing or touching specific body part causing discomfort
- Arching back
- Pulling legs up toward chest
- Holding body in unusual positions

**Physical symptoms:**
- Fever
- Rashes
- Vomiting
- Coughing
- Sneezing
- Runny nose
- Unusual body temperature (too warm or too cool to touch)

### Trust Parental Instinct

Parents and caregivers possess an innate sense of their child's well-being. Even without specific observable signs or symptoms, if a strong feeling exists that something is wrong, this instinct should be trusted and medical advice should be sought.

## Promoting Better Sleep During Transitions

### The Core Principle

When children feel safe, they are able to sleep. Therefore, the primary intervention during any life transition is to help children feel safe.

### Practical Applications

1. **Maintain routine elements where possible:** Even amid change, preserve bedtime rituals, familiar objects, and predictable sequences.

2. **Bring comfort items to new environments:** Stories, stuffed animals, linens with home scent, white noise, blackout curtains.

3. **Recognize parallel emotions:** When parents feel anxious about transitions, children likely do too. Use this recognition to cultivate compassion rather than frustration.

4. **Give time:** Both parents and children need time and patience to adjust to transitions. Rushed expectations create additional pressure.

5. **Watch for physical causes:** Rule out or address physical discomfort before assuming the cause is purely emotional/situational.

6. **Trust instincts:** Parental intuition about child well-being is valuable. When something feels wrong, seek medical guidance.

## Key Takeaways (Author's Summary)

1. Significant family life changes (new sibling, new job, daycare start) often create sleep disturbances in children.

2. Routine symbolizes safety for children. Drastic routine changes trigger overwhelm, which disrupts sleep.

3. Physical discomfort (illness, teething, allergies) prevents peaceful sleep.

4. New sleeping environments (new bed, bedroom, house) disturb sleep.

5. To help children adjust to new sleep environments, emulate familiar elements: similar bedtime routine, favorite bedtime items.

6. Parental instincts about child well-being are typically accurate. When something feels wrong, seek medical advice.

## Conceptual Framework Summary

```
Life Transition → Routine Disruption → Loss of Felt Safety → Sleep Struggles
                                                              ↓
                                        Manifests as: clinging, meltdowns, seeking parent at night
```

**Intervention point:** Restore felt safety through routine maintenance, familiar objects, parental compassion, time allowance, and addressing physical needs.
