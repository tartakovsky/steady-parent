# Why Obedience Should Not Be the Parenting Goal: Fostering Curiosity and Assertiveness

**Source:** Nurtured First
**Author:** Jess VanderWier, MA, RP (Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** January 31, 2022
**Audience:** Parents of toddlers, preschoolers, and big kids

---

## Core Thesis

**Obedience and instant compliance should not be the goal of parenting.** The goal should be raising children who are reflective, assertive, confident, and willing to ask questions. Children who question authority are better protected and better prepared for the real world.

---

## Key Conceptual Distinction: Compliance vs. Listening

**Compliance:** The child does what you say.

**Listening:** The child hears what you say.

When parents say "my kids don't listen," they typically mean "my kids don't comply." These are fundamentally different outcomes. It is not a child's job to comply with every parental request.

---

## The Paradigm Shift

### Old Perspective (Traditional View)

- Compliance is the goal of parenthood
- The mantra: "You get what you get, and you don't get upset"
- A child's obedience is treated as a report card on parenting quality
- Good parent = obedient child; bad parent = disobedient child

### New Perspective (Recommended View)

- When children question adults, they learn to find their voice
- Questioning helps children understand the "why" behind expectations
- **A child's behavior is not your report card**
- Obedience is not the goal; raising reflective and assertive children is
- This approach encourages children to get curious and ask questions about demands placed on them
- Expecting instant compliance does not set children up for real-world success

---

## Why Raising Question-Asking Children Matters

Children who are raised to ask questions develop:

- Their own confidence
- Protection against abuse
- Understanding of why rules and expectations exist
- An assertive voice

### The Safety Argument

Parents' demands come from unconditional love and safety. However, there are people in the world who may demand things from children that are neither loving nor safe. By raising children who are confident and question demands placed on them, parents set children up for future success when they encounter people who might cause them harm.

**Key insight:** A child trained to instant obedience is vulnerable to bad actors. A child trained to question and understand the "why" has built-in protection.

---

## The Importance of Explaining "Why"

### Illustrative Example: The Advil Incident

The author's 4.5-year-old daughter found children's Advil set aside for her teething baby sister. The daughter was about to consume it.

**Sequence of events:**
1. Parent says "Stop! I can't let you have that."
2. Child puts it down but returns to it minutes later
3. Parent says "I thought I said no..."
4. Child asks: "But Mom, why is it okay for my sister to have it but not me?"
5. Parent responds with "Because I said so!"
6. Confusion spreads across the child's face

**The correction:** The parent took a moment to compose herself, got on the child's level, and explained why the Advil was not okay for her.

### The Underlying Reasoning

- The parent wants the child to ask questions
- The parent wants the child to question authority
- While this parent is a good authority figure, someday there may be a bad boss or teacher
- The child needs to know her questions matter
- The child needs to know there is a reason behind demands and requests

### Observed Outcome

The longer the family practices allowing curiosity, the more the child is likely to listen, because she knows there is a reason behind requests.

## Handling the Objection: "But Sometimes They Just Need to Listen!"

This objection arises every time compliance is discussed. There are real moments when you need to get your child out the door to school or transition from playing to dinner.

**Three practical strategies for these moments:**

### Strategy 1: Join Your Child's World

**Time investment:** As little as 30 seconds

**Mechanism:** Joining your child's world before placing a demand helps you gain their attention and interest, making it much more likely they will genuinely listen.

**How to implement:**
1. If your child is playing, get down on their level
2. Play with them briefly
3. While playing, introduce the transition

**Example script (magnet tiles scenario):**
> "This is so much fun! I love how you used the magnet tiles to make a garage for your cars. Let's put the garage somewhere safe, so we can keep playing with them again when we get home from daycare this afternoon. Should it be put on the kitchen table or the coffee table?"

**Example script (tablet/racing game scenario):**
> "It's almost time for school. You can finish this race, and then we're going to put the tablet away. I see you chose the red car for this round; what kind of car is that?"

### Strategy 2: Offer Choices

**When to use:** When your child has a hard time listening and does not want to do what you ask.

**Benefit:** Avoids power struggles.

**Key requirement:** Offer **structured choices** where both options will work for you.

**Example scripts:**
> "It's almost time to get ready for school. Do you want me to set a timer for 2 or 3 more minutes?"

> "We have to clean your room before you can go to Sadie's house. Would you like to sort the clothes in the closet or dust your shelves?"

**Mechanism:** Structured choices give children a sense of power and autonomy over the situation. This increases the likelihood of compliance with your requests.

### Strategy 3: Give Advance Notice

**When to use:** For transitions or activities that are particularly challenging for your child.

**Purpose:** Children know what to expect.

**Principle:** Kids of all ages thrive when they know what to expect.

**Example script (morning routine):**
> "Hey buddy, here's the plan: we're going to eat breakfast, then get dressed. Then you can play with your Pokemon cards again. When it's almost time to go, I will set a 5-minute timer to let you know when it's time to put the cards away and get your coat on."

**Example script (afternoon plan):**
> "When we get home, we are going to spend 15 minutes tidying the basement, and then you can play outside with Sam. Do you have any questions about the plan?"

**Implementation detail:** Spend time during a calm moment (like breakfast) to go over the plan, especially for known difficult transitions.

## Emotional Context for Parents

### Why Parents Struggle

- When you have asked your child to do something "one million times" it can be extremely triggering
- Parents feel flustered at this point
- Parents are often on a schedule and need to get going
- Parents get annoyed because children do not notice or value what parents value
- This frustration leads to yelling or punishment because parents do not know how to get compliance

### Why This Pattern Exists

Obedience has been seen as the goal of child-rearing for many years. These values and views are deeply ingrained in society and in how we view children. The reaction to seek obedience through punishment or yelling makes sense given this cultural context, but it is not the optimal approach.

---

## Summary of Recommendations

1. **Reframe your goal:** Shift from seeking compliance to raising assertive, curious children
2. **Distinguish listening from compliance:** Recognize these are different outcomes
3. **Explain the "why":** Take time to help children understand reasons behind demands
4. **Encourage questions:** Actively welcome children questioning demands
5. **Join their world:** Spend 30 seconds connecting before making demands
6. **Offer structured choices:** Both options must work for you
7. **Give advance notice:** Especially for challenging transitions
8. **Remember the long game:** Children who question authority are protected from future bad actors

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Using "because I said so" as a regular response
- Expecting instant compliance
- Viewing your child's behavior as your report card
- Yelling or punishing when children do not comply
- Placing demands without first gaining the child's attention
- Surprising children with transitions they struggle with
- Treating every request as equally urgent
