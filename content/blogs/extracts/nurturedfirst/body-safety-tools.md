# Essential Body Safety Tools to Teach Your Child

**Source:** Nurtured First (Shannon Wassenaar, Registered Psychotherapist)
**Published:** January 4, 2024
**Age Groups:** Toddler, Preschooler, Big Kid

## Core Problem Statement

Teaching children about body safety feels daunting and uncomfortable for most parents. Parents often perceive the topic as "too mature" and struggle to know how to begin these conversations.

### Consequences of Not Teaching Body Safety

Adults who grew up without parents discussing body parts, consent language, or answering questions about bodies:
- Could not turn to their parents with questions or concerns about their bodies
- Struggled in silence
- Turned to peers for information
- Searched for answers on the internet, often encountering misinformation

### Foundational Principle

If you want your child to come to you with their questions about body safety and consent, **they need to learn this information from you first**.

## Tool 1: Secrets and Surprises

### Why This Matters

**Secrets are an abuser's best friend.** Abusers use secrets to control victims and ensure nobody discovers the abuse. This applies to:
- Sexual abuse
- Emotional abuse
- Physical abuse
- Neglect

This is precisely why abuse can happen right under our noses.

### Abuser Manipulation Tactics (Examples)

Perpetrators say things like:
- "If you share this, you'll get in trouble."
- "This is your fault. You can't tell anyone."
- "Let's keep this our little secret..."

### Teaching the Difference Between Secrets and Surprises

The challenge: These two concepts are similar, making it difficult for children to distinguish between them.

**Key Distinguishing Characteristics:**

| Secrets | Surprises |
|---------|-----------|
| Make us feel nervous, worried, sad, or scared | Make us feel good, happy, and excited |
| Meant to exclude others | Something we will share with others |
| No end date ("never tell anybody") | Will come out eventually (e.g., birthday surprise) |

**Example Script:**
"When we get you a special present for your birthday, and we don't tell you what it is, that is a surprise! It is something you are excited about and that we can share with you."

### The "No Secrets Rule"

**Purpose:** Helps children feel comfortable coming to parents with anything, even if someone tells them not to or threatens them with consequences.

**Core Principle:** No one should ever ask children to keep secrets, not even people they know and trust.

**Implementation Beyond the Home:**
Share this rule with family and friends who spend time with your children. Example: If grandparents take children for ice cream, they should not say "Don't tell mommy about this. It will be our little secret."

**Rationale:** Even innocent secrets teach children that it is acceptable for trusted people to ask them to keep secrets. The rule is absolute: no secrets in the family, no matter what anyone tells them.

**Scripts for Explaining the No Secrets Rule:**

1. "If someone tells you something bad will happen if you tell a secret, you still tell me the secret. Don't believe them."

2. "If someone tells you that you will get in trouble for telling a secret, you tell me right away. You will NEVER get in trouble for it."

3. "If someone tells you I (or someone else) will be hurt because you told the secret, DON'T believe them, and tell me right away."

## Tool 2: Anatomically Correct Names

### Parental Resistance (Common Pattern)

Initial reactions often include:
- "Why would she need to know that?"
- "Isn't that word a little too mature for her?"
- "Is she going to start saying that word in public?"

**Reality Check:** Many adults do not know anatomically correct names for their body parts and feel uncomfortable using them. However, this is a very important element of body safety.

### Three Reasons to Teach Anatomically Correct Names

**1. Protective Factor Against Abuse**

Studies show that perpetrators are less likely to abuse a child who has the language to describe what is happening to them.

**2. Enables Children to Describe Abuse or Concerns**

Anatomically correct names help children describe what is happening with their bodies. This applies to:
- Minor concerns (e.g., a rash)
- Serious concerns (e.g., describing abuse)

**3. Ensures Perpetrators Receive Proper Sentencing**

If abuse occurs, correct language helps children describe what happened, which supports appropriate legal sentencing of perpetrators.

### Reframing Adult Discomfort

**Key Insight:** The shame adults feel is their own internalized view of these words being "dirty."

**For Children:** Using correct anatomical terms is just as natural as labeling an elbow or a leg. It is not a big deal to children if parents start using this language early.

## Tool 3: Safe People

### Defining Safe vs. Unsafe People

Children must know what safe grown-ups will **never** say or do.

**A Safe Grown-Up Would Never Say:**
- "This is our little secret."
- "You'll get in trouble if you tell anyone."
- "You have to let me touch you."
- "You have to look at these pictures."
- "Don't tell anyone what we did today."

**A Safe Grown-Up Will Never:**
- Call you mean names
- Try to embarrass you on purpose
- Hurt you with their words or body
- Ignore you when you say "Stop!"
- Ask you to keep a secret

### Ensuring Children Will Report

After teaching what safe grown-ups will never say or do, reinforce these messages:
- They should always tell a parent if any of those things happen
- They will **never** get in trouble for sharing

**Outcome:** Children are equipped with knowledge and confidence to recognize an unsafe situation if it occurs.

### The Safety Team Concept

**Definition:** A group of trusted adults that the caregiver has personally chosen; people the caregiver knows and trusts intimately.

**Purpose:** Ensures children always have somebody to talk to, especially if they are too nervous to tell a parent.

**Typical Safety Team Members:**
- Parents
- Grandparent
- Aunt/Uncle
- School staff member

## Tool 4: Responding to Unwanted Touch

### Two Components

1. **What to say** when they do not want to be touched
2. **What to do** when receiving unwanted touch

### What Children Can Say

**Prerequisite:** Children cannot assert boundaries and protect themselves without:
- The language to do so
- Permission to keep their bodies safe

**Phrases to Teach:**
- "No."
- "Stop."
- "I don't want to be touched."

**For Younger Children:**
Simply teach them to say "no" or shake their head to signal they do not want to be touched.

**Role-Play Using Toys (Examples):**

Example 1: "I asked your doll if she wanted a hug, and she shook her head to tell me 'no!'"

Example 2: "The elephant stuffy is asking the cheetah stuffy, but the cheetah stuffy doesn't want a hug. What can the cheetah say to the elephant?"

### What Children Can Do

**Situations Where Physical Response Is Needed:**
- Child says no but touch continues
- Child is not asked for consent but receives touch anyway

**Physical Responses to Teach:**
- Hold up your hands
- Step aside or turn away
- Walk or run away

**Critical Addition:** After any of these responses, children must tell a safe person. This is why teaching about safe people and establishing a safety team is essential.

### Practice Methods

**1. Role-Play with Toys:**
"The dump truck is bumping into the tractor, but the tractor doesn't want to be touched, so he's going to move away from the dump truck."

**2. Narrating Real-Life Situations:**
"I see your brother is hugging you, even though you said no. You can step aside or walk away!"

## Why These Tools Matter

### Reality of Child Supervision

Children will not be in parental care 100% of the time. They will:
- Be in the care of others
- Be approached by new people
- Possibly find themselves in tricky situations

### Critical Statistic

**The most common perpetrators of child abuse are people the child knows.**

### Goal

Equip children with confidence to approach a parent or safety team member in any situation where they feel:
- Uncomfortable
- Nervous
- Scared

### Important Clarification

The purpose is not to instill fear in parents, but to educate so parents can protect their children.

### Universality of the Tools

These tools apply to situations with:
- Adults the child knows
- Adults the child does not know
- Peers

---

## Summary of Key Takeaways

1. **Secrets vs. Surprises:** Secrets create discomfort or nervousness; surprises are positive and temporary. Establish a "No Secrets Rule" in the family.

2. **Anatomically Correct Names:** Enable accurate reporting of inappropriate touch and support overall body understanding.

3. **Safe and Unsafe People:** Educate children on behaviors safe people will never exhibit, creating understanding of what constitutes inappropriate behavior.

4. **Safety Team:** Trusted adults who can be approached if children feel uncomfortable or unsafe, ensuring a support system beyond parents.

5. **Responses to Unwanted Touch:** Teach children to firmly express discomfort and assert personal boundaries as protection against potential abuse.

6. **Role-Play:** Helps children better understand and implement body safety lessons.

---

## Implementation Notes

**Parental Discomfort:** If feeling nervous or uncomfortable about these conversations, use engaging tools and activities (coloring, storytelling) to facilitate learning.

**Consistency Across Caregivers:** Share the "No Secrets Rule" and other principles with anyone who spends time with your children (grandparents, relatives, babysitters, etc.).

**Age-Appropriate Adaptation:** For younger children, use toys and simple language. For older children, more direct conversation and scenarios can be introduced.
