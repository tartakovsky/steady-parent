# Connection-Seeking Behaviour: Reframing Attention-Seeking in Children

**Source:** Nurtured First
**Author:** Jess VanderWier, MA, RP (Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** November 6, 2019
**Applicable Ages:** Toddler, Preschooler, Big Kid

---

## Core Premise

**Attention is a basic human need.** All humans, both adults and children, crave feeling seen, heard, and appreciated by others. Dismissing or ignoring attention-seeking behavior fails to address this fundamental need.

---

## The Reframe: Attention-Seeking as Connection-Seeking

The central conceptual shift is to view attention-seeking behavior as **connection-seeking behavior**. When children seek attention, they are seeking to be seen and heard. This reframe changes the intervention approach entirely.

**Definition of attention-seeking behavior:** Behavior that results in the child gaining access to attention that was not available prior to the behavior.

---

## Two Categories of Attention-Seeking Behavior

### Healthy Forms
- Asking if someone has time to talk
- Asking a friend to play
- Waving to a parent from across the room

### Unhealthy Forms (commonly seen in children)
- Hitting
- Biting
- Yelling
- Interrupting

**Important caveat:** These challenging behaviors are not always attention-seeking. Some cases require professional support. However, they are commonly used as a way to receive attention.

---

## Why Traditional Discipline Fails

Traditional methods of addressing challenging attention-seeking behavior **do not work** because they fail to address the underlying unmet need for connection. These methods include:

- Timeouts
- Verbal reprimands
- Spanking
- Other punishment-based approaches

### The Reinforcement Problem

Traditional discipline methods often **reinforce** the challenging behavior rather than eliminating it. The causal chain:

1. Child has an unmet need for attention/connection
2. Child engages in challenging behavior
3. Parent responds with yelling, spanking, or punishment
4. Child receives attention (even though it is negative)
5. Child learns that challenging behavior successfully meets their attention need
6. Challenging behavior increases or persists

**Key insight:** Negative attention is still attention. The child's need for connection can be met even through punishment, which inadvertently rewards the very behavior the parent is trying to stop.

---

## Three Strategies to Foster Connection

### Strategy 1: Plan Time With Kids ("Enriched Attention")

**Research finding:** Children who have access to "enriched attention" engage in much less challenging attention-seeking behavior.

**What enriched attention looks like:**
- Does NOT require hours of floor play or elaborate activities
- Does NOT require big outings or drawn-out activities
- Simply means being present and letting children know you notice them

**Practical examples of enriched attention:**
- Watching a show together
- Reading stories
- Coloring together
- Cooking dinner together
- Checking in with them
- Occasionally asking them a question while they play independently

**Core principle:** Being engaged with children simply means being there and communicating that you see them.

**Acknowledgment of parental reality:** Parents are often tired and have low energy by the end of the day. The bar for "enriched attention" is achievable even under these constraints.

---

### Strategy 2: Catch the Behavior You Want to See

**The challenge:** When children are engaging in challenging attention-seeking behavior, it becomes difficult to notice the times they ask for attention/connection in positive ways. Parental overwhelm and frustration create a filter that blocks recognition of positive behavior.

**The principle:** If you want to see positive attention-seeking behavior, you must give attention when that behavior occurs.

**Why this works:** Behavior that successfully produces a desired outcome (attention) will be repeated. By providing attention for positive bids for connection, you reinforce the positive behavior pattern.

**Implicit warning:** Failing to notice and respond to positive attention-seeking behavior leaves the child with only one working strategy: the challenging behavior that does get noticed.

---

### Strategy 3: Teach New Ways to Ask for Connection

**Underlying truth:** Seeking connection is a universal human need. However, knowing HOW to ask for connection appropriately is learned through observing others.

**Primary method:** Modeling

**How to model:** Find opportunities to ask for your child's attention in the same way you would like them to ask you.

**Why modeling works:** Children learn new ways of seeking connection by watching the world around them. When parents demonstrate appropriate connection-seeking behavior, they provide a template the child can adopt.

---

## Conceptual Hierarchy

1. **Foundation:** Attention/connection is a basic human need (not a luxury or indulgence)
2. **Reframe:** Attention-seeking = Connection-seeking
3. **Why behavior occurs:** Unmet need for connection drives challenging behavior
4. **Why punishment fails:** It meets the need (reinforcing behavior) without teaching alternatives
5. **Solution:** Proactively meet the connection need AND teach healthy ways to seek it

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Dismissing attention-seeking behavior as manipulation or unnecessary
- Using punishment-based responses (timeouts, verbal reprimands, spanking) for connection-seeking behavior
- Only noticing children when they engage in challenging behavior
- Assuming "enriched attention" requires extensive time or elaborate activities
- Failing to model the connection-seeking behaviors you want children to use

---

## Key Warnings

1. **Not all challenging behavior is attention-seeking.** Some cases require professional support. Do not assume every instance of hitting, biting, yelling, or interrupting is purely connection-seeking.

2. **Punishment reinforces challenging behavior** when the underlying need is for attention. Even negative attention satisfies the child's need, teaching them that challenging behavior works.

3. **Parental overwhelm creates blind spots.** When exhausted and frustrated, parents may fail to notice positive bids for connection, inadvertently forcing children to escalate.
