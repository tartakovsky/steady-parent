# How to Teach Anger Management to Children: Breaking the Cycle of Spanking

**Source:** Nurtured First
**Author:** Jess VanderWier, MA, RP (Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** December 9, 2024
**Reviewed by:** Nurtured First's team of child development experts

## Core Thesis

Teaching anger management skills is more powerful and long-lasting than spanking. Spanking fails to provide tools for long-term emotional regulation and creates a destructive cycle of anger and aggression. Breaking this cycle requires replacing punishment with skill-building.

## Why Spanking Fails

### The Psychological Harm of Spanking

**Negative Self-Perception:** Spanking unintentionally reinforces a harmful internal message. Instead of teaching children "your behavior is bad," children internalize "I am bad." This belief becomes a destructive cycle of anger and aggression because children lack healthy emotional outlets.

**No Skill Transfer:** Spanking may stop immediate behavior but fails to provide tools for long-term emotional regulation. A "true lesson learned" requires teaching coping skills so children know how to handle anger in healthy ways.

### Research Evidence

A comprehensive study published in the **Journal of Family Psychology** found spanking was associated with:
- Increased aggression
- Mental health problems
- Cognitive difficulties in children

## Understanding Childhood Anger

### Developmental Context

**Anger is normal.** Children experience intense emotions but often lack the vocabulary and skills to express them appropriately. Children's brains are still developing their ability to regulate emotions. Parents must help build regulatory pathways through consistent, empathetic guidance.

### Five Root Causes of Childhood Anger

1. **Frustration:** When children cannot accomplish a task or get what they want
2. **Powerlessness:** Feeling powerless over their environment or circumstances
3. **Overstimulation:** Too much noise, activity, or sensory input
4. **Unmet needs:** Hunger, tiredness, or a need for attention
5. **Learned behavior:** Mimicking angry responses they have observed in others

**Key Insight:** By identifying these triggers, parents can address the root causes of anger rather than just reacting to the outward behavior.

## Six Alternative Strategies to Spanking

### 1. Model Healthy Anger Management

**Principle:** Children learn by example. Parent behavior sets the tone for how children handle their own emotions.

**Techniques to demonstrate:**
- Take deep breaths and count to ten when frustrated
- Use "I feel" statements to express emotions calmly
- Take time to cool down when needed
- Problem-solve out loud, showing the child how to work through anger constructively

**Illustrative Quote:** A father who was spanked as a child reflected: "Maybe I wouldn't have got into so many fights back then (or now) if I had some tools to deal with my anger."

### 2. Teach Emotional Vocabulary

**Why it matters:** Many children act out in anger simply because they lack the words to express how they are feeling. Helping children identify and name emotions is a crucial step in managing them.

**Techniques:**
- Use emotion charts with faces depicting different feelings
- Read stories about emotions together
- Play emotion charades to help children recognize and express different feelings
- Regularly check in with the child about their emotional state

### 3. Teach Coping Skills

**Purpose:** Equip children with strategies they can use when anger strikes to regain control of their emotions.

**Four Core Coping Techniques:**
- **Deep breathing exercises:** Teach the child to take slow, deep breaths when they feel angry
- **Progressive muscle relaxation:** Guide the child through tensing and relaxing different muscle groups
- **Visualization:** Help the child imagine a calm, happy place when upset
- **Physical activity:** Encourage running, jumping, or dancing to release pent-up energy

### 4. Create a Calm-Down Corner

**Concept:** Designate a special area in the home where the child can go to calm down when feeling angry or overwhelmed.

**Stock the space with:**
- Soft pillows or cushions
- Stress balls or fidget toys
- Coloring books and crayons
- A feelings chart

**Critical Implementation Detail:** Spend time in this space with the child when they are calm and regulated. Show them how they can use it when they are feeling angry and overwhelmed. Do not introduce it only during moments of distress.

### 5. Notice the Good (Positive Reinforcement)

**Principle:** When you notice the child using coping tools to manage their anger, let them know you see this.

**Example Script:** "I noticed how you took deep breaths when your brother took your toy. That was a great way to stay calm!"

### 6. Teach Problem-Solving Skills

**Purpose:** Help the child develop the ability to resolve conflicts and find solutions to problems that make them angry. This process empowers children to take control of their emotions and situations.

**Five-Step Problem-Solving Process:**
1. Identify the problem
2. Brainstorm possible solutions
3. Choose the best solution
4. Try it out
5. Evaluate how it worked

## Breaking the Generational Cycle

### Parental Self-Work

Changing parenting approach is challenging, especially for those raised with spanking as the primary disciplinary tool. Many parents fear being seen as "soft" or losing control.

**Reframe:** Allowing feelings and teaching skills is not "soft." It provides children with tools so they do not end up angry and yelling as adults.

### Six Steps for Parents to Break the Cycle

1. Reflect on your own childhood experiences of discipline and anger
2. Identify your triggers and work on managing your own emotions
3. Educate yourself about child development and positive parenting techniques
4. Practice new strategies consistently, even when it feels challenging
5. Seek support from other parents, support groups, or a family therapist
6. Be patient with yourself and your child as you both learn and grow

**Encouragement:** It is never too late to change your parenting approach. Every interaction is an opportunity to build a stronger, more positive relationship with the child.

## Real-Life Application: Complete Scenario Script

### Context
A child spent all day building a Lego tower. A sibling accidentally knocked it over. The child began yelling and swearing.

### The Constructive Response

**Step 1: Acknowledge feelings while setting limits**
Mom: "I see you're really upset that your Lego tower fell down. It's okay to feel angry, but it's not okay to yell and swear."

**Step 2: Co-regulate with breathing**
Mom: "Let's take some deep breaths together to calm down."

**Step 3: Invite appropriate expression**
Mom: "Can you tell me how you're feeling without using those words?"

**Step 4: Validate the child's perspective**
Child explains their frustration about their hard work being destroyed.
Mom: "I understand you're frustrated. You worked hard on that tower, and it wasn't okay for your sister to knock it down."

**Step 5: Facilitate expression to the sibling**
Mom: "What would you like to say to your sister?" (Give opportunity to express feelings)

**Step 6: Offer choices and involve child in problem-solving**
Mom: "Now we have a couple of options. We can rebuild it together, or we can take a break and try something new. What do you think?"

### Why This Approach Works

This response:
- Acknowledges the child's feelings
- Teaches appropriate expression
- Involves the child in problem-solving
- Maintains connection while addressing behavior

## Summary of Key Principles

**Short-term vs. Long-term:** Spanking seems like a quick solution to anger outbursts but does not address the root of the problem or provide children with the tools they need to manage their emotions effectively.

**Investment in Future:** Teaching anger management skills helps children in the short term AND sets them up for emotional success in the future.

**Patience Required:** Learning new skills takes time, but the results are worth it. Both parent and child are learning and growing together.

**Broader Impact:** Committing to teaching anger management skills rather than resorting to spanking is an investment in the child's emotional intelligence and future success. It leads to stronger, healthier relationships and happier families.
