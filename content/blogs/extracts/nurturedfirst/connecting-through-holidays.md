# Easy Ways To Show Your Child Love This Holiday Season

**Source:** Nurtured First
**Author:** Paige Shiels (Registered Early Childhood Educator, B.A. Youth and Children's Studies)
**Date:** October 21, 2021
**Reviewed by:** Nurtured First's team of child development experts

## Core Thesis

What children need most during the holiday season (and throughout the year) cannot be wrapped and placed under a tree. The fundamental need is for children to know they are loved and cared for. Connection and presence outweigh material gifts in creating lasting positive memories.

## Emotional Context for Parents

The holidays bring **mixed feelings** for many families, creating two common parental starting points:

1. **Positive childhood holiday memories**: Parents want to recreate the magic and traditions they experienced
2. **Negative childhood holiday memories**: Parents want to break cycles and create new, better traditions for their children

Regardless of which category a parent falls into, the approach remains the same: focus on demonstrating love and care rather than material provisions.

## The Concept of Tiny Moments

### Definition and Evolution

**Tiny moments** are small, intentional acts of connection that communicate love. As children grow from infancy, the form changes but the need remains constant.

**Developmental progression:**
- **Baby stage**: Hundreds of tiny kisses every day
- **Older children**: Different expressions that still fulfill the same connection need

### Common Mistake

Parents often forget that older children still need these moments of connection. The expressions change, but the underlying need does not diminish.

### Forms Tiny Moments Can Take

- Whispering "I love you"
- Love notes in their lunch
- Making their favorite food for dinner
- A smile from across the room
- A wink
- A thumbs up from across the room

### Dual Benefit of Tiny Moments

1. **For the child**: Shows them how much they are loved
2. **For the parent**: Creates a feeling of connection with the child

Both parties receive value, making tiny moments a reciprocal gift.

## Practical Holiday Connection Activities

### Shared Work Activities

**Wrap Gifts Together**
- Involve children in the wrapping process
- Enables natural conversation while working side by side
- Children feel valued by being part of holiday preparation

**Work on a Puzzle or Craft Together**
- Choose something to do side by side
- Allows for conversation and focused time
- Creates something tangible together

**Help Them with a Holiday Project**
- If they want to make homemade gifts or decorate their room, offer step-by-step assistance
- Makes them feel supported and cared for
- Child-led with parent support

### Physical Affection Activities

**Give Warm Hugs Throughout the Day**
- Particularly during holiday moments: decorating, meal prep, gift wrapping
- Even brief moments of holding close are reassuring
- Integrate affection into activity transitions

**Wrap Them in a Cozy Blanket**
- Frame it playfully as wrapping them like a "holiday present"
- Let them sit on your lap for warmth and connection
- Creates feelings of safety and being loved

**Go Ice Skating or Sledding Together**
- Physical activities that naturally involve hand-holding or being close for support
- Combines fun, active outing with connection
- Physical proximity occurs organically through the activity

### Verbal Affirmation Activities

**Thank Them for Being Themselves**
- Express during holiday events
- Sample phrases: "I'm so glad you're here with me" or "You make this season brighter"
- Affirms their inherent value, not their accomplishments or behavior
- Small statements have significant impact

**Create a Holiday Countdown with Daily Affirmations**
- Advent-style countdown format
- Each day includes a note with a compliment or words of encouragement
- Builds anticipation while providing daily positive messaging about their identity
- Children look forward to reading positive things about themselves

### Surprise and Thoughtfulness Activities

**Bring Home Their Favorite Snack or Treat**
- Small, thoughtful gesture
- Demonstrates you were thinking of them when apart
- Does not need to be elaborate

**Arrange a Bedtime Surprise with Holiday Decor**
- While child is away or busy, add festive touches to their room
- Examples: twinkling lights, small holiday wreath, cozy new blanket
- Makes bedtime feel special
- Element of surprise creates delight

**Give Thoughtful, Personalized Gifts**
- Choose gifts reflecting their unique interests, hobbies, or favorites
- Examples: art supplies, books on topics they love, specific desired toys
- The thought behind the gift is what matters most
- Requires knowing and paying attention to the child's individual preferences

### Support and Assistance Activities

**Give Extra Help with Homework or Holiday Projects**
- Offer assistance with school projects, homework, or holiday-related assignments
- Communicates that you are there to help them succeed
- Creates feeling of appreciation

### Tradition-Building Activities

**Create a Holiday Tradition Together**
- Choose an activity specifically for the two of you, done every year
- Examples: making hot cocoa together, setting up decorations, watching a favorite holiday movie
- Simplicity is acceptable and often preferable
- Establishes predictable, meaningful connection points

**Start a Holiday Journal Together**
- Child writes down favorite memories or things they enjoyed
- Parent adds notes of encouragement or love
- Spend time together discussing what they write about
- Children appreciate heart-to-heart conversations
- Creates lasting record of the relationship

### Redeemable Connection Offers

**Make a "Coupon" for a Special Activity Together**
- Child can redeem for something they enjoy
- Examples: "Hot Cocoa Night," "One Extra Story at Bedtime," "30-Minute Game with You"
- Puts control in child's hands for when to activate connection
- Makes time together a tangible, valued "gift"

## Key Takeaways

1. **Moments over things**: Connection experiences are what children remember most about holidays, not material gifts
2. **Simplicity is sufficient**: Activities can be small and simple while still being meaningful
3. **Consistency matters**: Tiny moments accumulate over time to build strong relationships
4. **Personalization shows love**: Knowing and responding to a child's unique interests demonstrates deep attention and care
5. **Both parent and child benefit**: Connection is reciprocal, not just parent giving to child
6. **Age-appropriate adaptation**: The form of connection changes as children grow, but the need remains constant
7. **Integration into existing activities**: Many connection opportunities arise naturally within holiday tasks like decorating, cooking, and wrapping

## Conceptual Framework Summary

**Input**: Intentional, small acts of love and presence (tiny moments)
**Mechanism**: Child feels seen, valued, and loved; parent feels connected
**Output**: Magical memories children will look back on and cherish throughout their lives

The emphasis is on **preparing parents** to shower children with extra love and connection rather than preparing elaborate material celebrations.
