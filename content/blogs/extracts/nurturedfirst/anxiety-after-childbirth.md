# Postpartum Anxiety: Coping Strategies and Recovery

Source: Nurtured First (nurturedfirst.com)
Author: Jess VanderWier, MA, RP (Registered Psychotherapist specializing in child/family emotional well-being)
Guest Writer: Amanda Dover (stay-at-home mom blogger)
Date: May 7, 2018

## Overview

This article combines professional expertise with lived experience to address **postpartum anxiety (PPA)**, a maternal mental health condition that is less discussed than postpartum depression but equally debilitating. The author experienced severe PPA after her second child and shares both the experience and recovery strategies.

## The Nature of Postpartum Anxiety

### How PPA Manifests

**Cognitive symptoms:**
- Mind plagued with "what-ifs" and worst-case scenarios
- Intrusive thoughts about death (own death or death of loved ones)
- Mental imagery of being absent from children's milestones
- Imagining children growing up motherless
- Visualizing catastrophic loss of family members
- Feeling one's days are "numbered"
- Getting "stuck inside your head"

**Physical symptoms:**
- Heart racing
- Chest tightening
- Sleep deprivation (anxiety "robbing" needed sleep)
- Panic attacks (can mimic heart attack symptoms)
- Paralysis from fear

**Behavioral symptoms:**
- Going through motions like a "zombie"
- Losing sight of everything else
- Quiet, internal suffering while family sleeps unaware

### Pattern of Escalation

The author distinguishes between severity levels:
- **"Better nights":** Intrusive thoughts focused on own death, imagining family continuing without her
- **"Worst nights":** Intrusive thoughts about harm to children or loved ones, creating unbearable imagined grief

**Critical escalation pattern:** Anxiety that begins during nighttime hours eventually "creeps into day-to-day life" and "follows around all day," indicating progression when left untreated.

## Prevalence and Recognition

**Statistical context:** Research suggests 15% or more of women experience postpartum anxiety.

**Recognition gap:** While postpartum depression is widely known, postpartum anxiety lacks equivalent public awareness, contributing to delayed recognition and treatment.

## Barriers to Seeking Help

### Shame and Invalidation

**Internal self-dismissal logic:**
- "I'm a stay-at-home mom, what do I really have to worry about?"
- "I'm living my dream of staying home with my girls, I have no right to feel anxious"
- Feeling "silly" for having uncontrollable feelings

**Key insight:** The presence of desired life circumstances (fulfilling role, loving family) does not preclude or invalidate anxiety. Anxiety is not a logical response to circumstances; it operates independently of external "reasons."

### Isolation and Silence

**Assumption of being misunderstood:** Belief that "no one would understand" leads to silence.

**Consequences of bottling up:** Keeping feelings inside "only exacerbated the problem" by providing "no release."

## The Breaking Point

**Trigger for seeking help:** A panic attack so severe it was mistaken for a heart attack, requiring emergency room visit. Hospital diagnosis of panic attack served as the turning point for recognizing the problem was "bigger than me."

**Lesson:** Sometimes physical crisis forces acknowledgment of mental health crisis when internal suffering is dismissed.

## Treatment Approach

### Medical Treatment

**Medication (Zoloft/sertraline):**
- Prescribed after "meltdown" in GP's office
- Initial hesitation due to stigma around mental health medication
- Recognition that situation was "beyond my control" overcame hesitation

**Breastfeeding concern and resolution:**
- Common maternal worry: safety of medication while breastfeeding
- Resolution: Consulting multiple doctors and pharmacists who unanimously reassured safety
- Professional consensus enabled comfortable decision-making

**Outcome of medication:** "I became myself again"

### Holistic Approach

The author emphasizes medication was paired with researched coping strategies, indicating a **combination approach** (medication plus behavioral/cognitive strategies) rather than medication alone.

## Seven Coping Strategies

### 1. Release Shame

**Core principle:** There is "nothing to be ashamed of."

**Reframe:** Anxiety can be "every bit as crippling as depression." The lack of visible cause does not make the suffering less valid.

**Anti-pattern:** Feeling you have "no right or reason" to be anxious because of favorable external circumstances.

### 2. Talk About It

**Anti-pattern:** Keeping feelings bottled up internally.

**Recommended actions:**
- Open up to spouse about fears
- Communicate on difficult days (e.g., "I'm having a bad day and getting stuck inside my head")
- Find friends who can serve as "safe sounding boards"
- Seek people who can relate and won't shame you

**Benefit:** Talking provides emotional "release" and normalizes experience.

### 3. Maintain Individual Identity

**Risk:** Getting "lost in identities as moms."

**Recommended action:** Find time to be yourself "sans kids," even briefly.

**Examples:**
- Quick solo trip to Target
- A couple of hours alone

**Mechanism:** Even small amounts of time alone can be "refreshing" and supports mental health.

### 4. Accept Medication When Needed

**Barriers to acceptance:**
- Stigma around mental health medication
- Specific concerns (e.g., breastfeeding safety)

**Resolution path:**
- Consult professionals (doctors, pharmacists)
- Gather reassurance from multiple sources
- Recognize when situation is beyond personal control alone

**Reframe:** Taking medication serves children by enabling "a fully present Mom who can laugh and live with them."

### 5. Practice Meditation

**Suggested resources:**
- YouTube videos
- Headspace app

**Reported effects:**
- Winding down
- Heart rate slowing
- Mind and body becoming relaxed and calm

**Optimal timing:** Especially beneficial before bedtime (addresses nighttime anxiety pattern).

**Note:** The author admits limited prior knowledge, suggesting meditation is accessible even to beginners.

### 6. Practice Patience with Recovery

**Key insight:** "Healing and recovery takes time."

**Expectation setting:** It will not get better overnight or right away.

**Promise:** Once the right approach is found (therapy, medication, or combination), feeling "normal again" is achievable.

**Recommended stance:**
- Be patient
- Go easy on yourself
- Trust the process of breaking through

### 7. Practice Gratitude

**Described as:** "Huge" for the author; "crucial" to dealing with postpartum anxiety.

**Mechanism:** Conscious gratitude practice "tremendously" improves outlook.

**Implementation options:**
- Gratitude journal (author attempted but fell behind)
- Stopping in the moment to reflect
- Incorporating into nightly prayers
- Simply slowing down to notice small moments

**Examples of gratitude-worthy moments:**
- Older baby falling asleep on mother (rare occurrence)
- Watching children play and giggle together

**Framing:** These moments serve as "calm in the storm."

**Practical note:** Formal journaling is not required; integration into daily routine is acceptable and effective.

## Underlying Philosophy

### Children's Needs Drive Self-Care

**Central premise:** "Your sweet children deserve nothing but the best, and that means you need to be at your best."

**Implication:** Maternal self-care and mental health treatment are not selfish but serve children's wellbeing.

### Multiplicity of Valid Approaches

**Flexibility emphasized:** "Below are just a few of the many strategies that exist." The author encourages "researching your options," indicating personalization is expected.

## Message to Struggling Mothers

**Reassurance:** "You are not alone."

**Promise:** "Even though it doesn't feel like it now, you will make it through this difficult time."

**Call to action:** First seek treatment, then research coping options.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Postpartum anxiety is common** (15%+ prevalence) but under-discussed compared to postpartum depression
2. **External circumstances do not prevent or invalidate anxiety**; having a "good life" does not mean you cannot struggle
3. **Silence and isolation worsen the condition**; talking provides necessary release
4. **Professional help is essential** when anxiety escalates beyond personal control
5. **Medication can be safe during breastfeeding** with proper medical consultation
6. **Combination approaches work** (medication plus coping strategies)
7. **Recovery is a process**, not an overnight transformation
8. **Self-care enables better parenting**, reframing treatment as serving children's needs
9. **Gratitude practice provides immediate relief** and can be informal
10. **Panic attacks can mimic cardiac events**, underscoring the physical reality of anxiety
