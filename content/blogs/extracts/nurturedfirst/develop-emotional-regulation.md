# Understanding Child Tantrums: A Framework for Developing Emotional Regulation

**Source:** Nurtured First
**Author:** Jess VanderWier, MA, RP (Registered Psychotherapist, Master's in Counselling Psychology)
**Original URL:** https://nurturedfirst.com/toddler/develop-emotional-regulation
**Target Age Group:** Toddlers and Preschoolers

---

## Core Premise

Children are not born with the ability to regulate emotions. **Emotional regulation is a learned skill** that develops over time through repeated practice and modeling. The reason adults rarely become upset about cup colors while toddlers frequently do is because of developmental differences, not willful defiance.

---

## The Fundamental Reframe

**Key insight:** "My child isn't trying to give me a hard time; they are having a hard time."

When children throw tantrums over seemingly trivial issues (wrong cup color, milk being "too milky," eggs not "goopy" enough, bubble bath "too wet"), they are not being deliberately difficult. They are **dysregulated**, meaning their emotional state has overwhelmed their capacity to respond rationally.

---

## The Parent's Natural (But Counterproductive) Response Pattern

When faced with a tantrum, parents typically experience a two-stage reaction:

1. **First instinct:** Attempt to use logic and reasoning with the child
2. **Second instinct:** When logic fails, the parent's body wants to "join the chaos" by yelling to make the child stop

Both responses are ineffective because they fail to address the underlying neurological reality of what is happening in the child's brain.

---

## The 4 Essential Truths During Tantrums

### Truth 1: Logic and Reasoning Will Not Be Understood

During tantrums, children cannot process logical arguments regardless of how clearly or calmly they are explained. **Words become meaningless** to the dysregulated child. Attempting to reason adds to the chaos and often causes the parent to become dysregulated as well.

**Causal chain:** Parent tries logic → child cannot process → parent becomes frustrated → parent escalates → both become dysregulated

**Success strategy:** Recognizing this truth in advance allows parents to avoid the futile logic trap and preserve their own emotional equilibrium.

### Truth 2: The Child's Brain Is Still Developing

The **prefrontal cortex** is the brain region responsible for:
- Emotional regulation
- Thinking before acting
- Empathy
- Self-reflection
- Logical thinking

In toddlers and preschoolers, this brain region is **not yet developed**. During moments of dysregulation, **primitive fight-or-flight reactions** take control, manifesting as:
- Crying
- Tantrums
- Yelling
- Kicking

**Critical implication:** Children cannot think logically when dysregulated because the brain structures required for logical thinking are not yet mature and are offline during emotional arousal.

### Truth 3: It's Not About the Surface Issue

When a child claims the milk is "too milky," they are likely **just as confused as the parent** about what is actually wrong. The stated reason is not the real issue.

**What is actually happening:** The child is experiencing overwhelming feelings they do not understand and cannot articulate. They lack the coping mechanisms to process these big emotions.

**Anti-pattern:** Engaging with the literal content of the complaint (debating cup color, milk consistency) misses the point entirely and prolongs the dysregulation.

### Truth 4: Co-Regulation Is the Teaching Mechanism

**Co-regulation defined:** The process by which children learn emotional self-management by experiencing and internalizing how their parents and trusted caregivers respond to them during emotional distress.

**Developmental continuity:** This is the same mechanism that operated in infancy. When babies cried, they needed:
- Soothing voice
- Rocking
- Back rubbing
- Physical comfort

Toddlers and preschoolers still need this same co-regulatory support to **learn how to manage high states of emotional arousal**.

---

## The Co-Regulation Mechanism in Detail

### The Learning Cycle

**Step 1:** Child experiences emotional upset (e.g., crying about the "too milky" milk)

**Step 2:** Parent provides calming intervention:
- Speaking in a soothing voice
- Validating feelings
- Remaining calm themselves

**Step 3:** This cycle, repeated many times, builds:
- Trust and safety
- The foundation for self-soothing

**Step 4:** Over time, the child learns to expect a soothing response rather than chaos following big emotions

**Step 5:** Through internalization, the child learns to provide this soothing response to themselves

### Research Support

The research literature confirms that repeated cycles of emotional upset followed by parental calming intervention provide the basis for both attachment security (trust and safety) and self-regulation skills (self-soothing).

---

## The Recommended Response Protocol

### What to Do During a Tantrum

1. **Be the calm in their storm:** The most powerful intervention is remaining calm yourself
2. **Narrate with confidence:** Use a confident, calm tone to describe what is happening
   - Example: "It's tough to drink from the blue cup; you want the pink cup!"
3. **Wait with them:** Stay present as they work through the emotional wave
4. **Model deep breathing:** Allow them to "borrow your calm" through deep breaths
5. **Simply be present:** Trust the process as they move through the emotion

### The Long-Term Outcome

Through consistent application of this approach, children gradually internalize the ability to calm themselves in the same way the parent has modeled.

---

## Conceptual Framework: Teaching Emotions Like Any Other Skill

### The Analogy

Parents naturally provide time, patience, and instruction for:
- Learning to walk (modeling, practice, patience with falls)
- Learning to talk (modeling, practice, patience with errors)
- Riding a bike
- Tying shoes
- Using a fork
- Clearing dishes

**The logical extension:** Emotional coping skills deserve the same patient, instructional approach.

### The Cultural Anti-Pattern

Society teaches parents to:
- Dismiss children's emotions
- Punish the behaviors that result from dysregulation

**Why this fails:** It treats the symptom (behavior) while ignoring the skill deficit (inability to regulate emotions). This approach makes "no sense" given what we know about child development.

---

## Key Reminders

1. **Age is not competence:** Just because a child is no longer a baby does not mean they know how to regulate emotions
2. **Skill requires practice:** Emotional regulation is learned over time with repeated practice, not through instruction alone
3. **Self-care matters:** Parents experiencing frustration need to attend to their own well-being to maintain the capacity for co-regulation

---

## Anti-Patterns Summary

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|--------------|--------------|
| Using logic during tantrums | Child's prefrontal cortex is offline; words are meaningless |
| Yelling or joining the chaos | Models dysregulation rather than regulation; escalates situation |
| Engaging with stated complaint | The surface issue is not the real problem |
| Dismissing emotions | Fails to teach coping skills; damages trust |
| Punishing tantrum behaviors | Addresses symptom, not underlying skill deficit |
| Expecting immediate results | Regulation is a developmental process requiring many repetitions |

---

## Success Patterns Summary

| Success Pattern | Mechanism |
|-----------------|-----------|
| Remaining calm | Models regulation; provides template for internalization |
| Narrating feelings | Validates experience; builds emotional vocabulary |
| Confident, calm tone | Communicates safety; avoids adding to chaos |
| Waiting with child | Allows natural emotional processing; communicates patience |
| Deep breathing together | Provides physiological regulation tool; enables "borrowing calm" |
| Physical presence | Maintains connection during distress; builds trust |
| Repeated practice | Creates neural pathways for self-regulation over time |

---

## The Central Paradox

The moments when parents most want to use logic, escalate, or end the tantrum quickly are precisely the moments when these approaches are least effective. The counterintuitive truth is that **slowing down, staying calm, and simply being present** accelerates the long-term development of emotional regulation far more than any quick fix.
