# Teaching Good Manners Without Forcing

**Source:** Nurtured First
**Author:** Jess VanderWier, MA, RP
**Date:** July 5, 2021
**Applicable Ages:** Toddler, Preschooler, Big Kid

---

## Core Philosophy

### The Central Question Reframe

**Common parental question:** "How can I make my child say please and thank you?"

**Reframed question:** "How can I raise respectful kids?"

The shift from forcing specific words to cultivating genuine respect represents the fundamental paradigm change this approach requires.

### Why Forcing Manners Fails

**Outcome of forced manners:** When children are compelled to say polite words, they:
- Do not understand the actual meaning of the words
- Use them out of habit rather than genuine gratitude
- Learn to perform the behavior to please adults or get what they want
- Miss the deeper connection between words and authentic respect

**The underlying value:** Parents who want to teach manners typically have a deeper value underneath: they want to raise respectful children. The method of forcing words undermines this actual goal.

### The Core Principle

**"Treating kids with respect will help you raise respectful kids."**

This statement appears twice in the source, emphasizing its foundational importance. Respect is transmitted through modeling, not instruction.

## Framework: 3 Ways to Raise Respectful Kids

### Strategy 1: Modeling Manners

**Mechanism:** Children are constantly watching and listening. They learn language, including polite words, through observation rather than direct instruction.

**Language acquisition parallel:** When children first learn to talk, parents do not explicitly teach every word's meaning. Children watch, listen, and derive meaning organically. Polite words like "please" and "thank you" follow the same acquisition process.

**Key insight:** Modeling does not require special effort. If these words are present in everyday adult conversation, children will absorb them naturally.

**Practical Examples of Modeling:**

1. **Direct requests to others:** "Hey Jill, could you please pass me the pillow?"

2. **Expressing genuine appreciation:** "I really love this card you made for my birthday, thank you!"

3. **Extended modeling with explanation:** "It was really nice of Grandma to drop off Mac and Cheese for dinner. I am going to write her a thank you card to let her know how much we loved it."

**Why the third example is especially effective:** It connects the words "thank you" to their deeper meaning. The child sees the causal chain: receiving something appreciated leads to expressing gratitude in written form. This demonstrates what gratitude means in action.

**Reassurance:** Modeling is not "a big scary thing that we need to work really hard on." It happens naturally when adults use respectful language in daily life.

### Strategy 2: Narrate the Process

**When to use:** When modeling alone does not seem sufficient and the child is not picking up polite language despite consistent exposure.

**What to avoid:** Directly telling children to use specific words (e.g., "Jill, you need to say thank you to Max")

**Why direct instruction fails:** Children learn to use polite words because they know it will please adults and help them get what they want. They do not learn what the words actually mean.

**The narration technique:** Instead of commanding the child to speak, the parent speaks the polite words on the child's behalf while explaining the context.

**Example Transformation 1:**

| Instead of | Try |
|------------|-----|
| "Jill, you need to say thank you to Max." | "Thank you, Max, for sharing your snack with Jill; I know she really loves when you share with her." |

**What narration accomplishes:**
- Models the correct words in context
- Explains what the words mean
- Helps children make connections about meaning and timing
- Removes pressure from the child while still providing the learning opportunity

**Example Transformation 2:**

**Scenario:** Child is reaching for their cup and shouting "Water!"

| Instead of | Try |
|------------|-----|
| "What's the magic word?" | "It looks like you are asking me to please pass you your water." |

**Key difference:** The "magic word" prompt teaches children that these words are transactional keys to getting what they want. The narration approach teaches that politeness is about how we communicate requests.

### Strategy 3: Trust the Process

**The parental challenge:** It may feel difficult to imagine children ever using polite words without being forced.

**Required mindset:** Trust that:
- The process works over time
- Children will learn from consistent modeling and narration
- Development of genuine respect takes longer but produces better outcomes

**Long-term benefit:** By not forcing children to "be polite," parents set them up for success. Children learn to:
- Appreciate what these words actually mean
- Understand the power and significance of polite language
- Use manners from genuine understanding rather than rote compliance

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### "What's the magic word?"

**Problem:** This phrase teaches children that "please" is a transactional key that unlocks desired items. It does not teach the meaning or purpose of politeness.

### Telling children what to say

**Examples:** "Say thank you to Max" or "You need to say please"

**Problem:** Creates compliance without comprehension. Children learn to perform rather than understand.

### Forcing or compelling manners

**Problem:** Produces habitual use without genuine gratitude. The child speaks the words but does not feel or understand them.

---

## Conceptual Hierarchy

1. **Ultimate Goal:** Raising respectful, compassionate children who genuinely want to respect others

2. **Intermediate Goal:** Children who understand the meaning and power of polite language

3. **Method:** Modeling respect in how adults treat children and others

4. **Supporting Techniques:** Narration of polite language in context; trust in developmental process

5. **What to avoid:** Forcing, commanding, or using transactional prompts like "magic word"

---

## Causal Chains

**Modeling leads to learning:**
Adults use polite language consistently in conversation → Children observe and listen → Children absorb meaning through context → Children eventually use words themselves with genuine understanding

**Narration builds connections:**
Parent narrates polite words in real situations → Child hears words in meaningful context → Child understands when and why to use them → Child develops authentic politeness

**Forcing produces hollow compliance:**
Parent commands child to say polite words → Child complies to please adult or get desired item → Child learns words are transactional tools → Child does not understand genuine respect or gratitude

---

## Key Warnings

1. **Patience required:** This approach takes time. Children will not immediately begin using polite language. The payoff is long-term and substantive.

2. **Resist the urge to force:** Even when children do not seem to be "getting it," returning to commanding or forcing will undermine the deeper goal.

3. **The question matters:** How parents frame the goal (forcing specific words vs. cultivating respect) determines the methods chosen and the outcomes achieved.

---

## Summary of Practical Applications

**Daily practice:**
- Use "please" and "thank you" naturally in conversations with all family members
- Include children in observing acts of gratitude (like writing thank you cards)
- Narrate polite language when children make requests or receive things

**In the moment:**
- When a child demands something, narrate: "It looks like you are asking me to please..."
- When someone shares with or gives something to your child, narrate: "Thank you [person] for [action]; [child's name] really appreciates it"

**Mindset:**
- Trust that modeling works even when results are not immediately visible
- Remember that the goal is genuine respect, not verbal performance
- Treat children with the same respect you hope they will show others
