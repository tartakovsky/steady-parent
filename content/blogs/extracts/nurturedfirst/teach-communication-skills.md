# Teaching Communication Skills: Three Strategies to Stop Yelling

**Source:** Nurtured First
**Author:** Jess VanderWier, MA, RP (Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** November 15, 2017
**Applicable Ages:** Toddler, Preschooler, Big Kid

## Core Premise

Communication patterns are deeply ingrained because they are learned from parents, family, and environmental influences throughout life. This long learning history makes changing communication patterns difficult. The strategies presented offer simple, actionable ways to alter these patterns and improve relationships with children.

## Strategy 1: Encourage Children to Express Their Own Opinions

### The Problem with Suppressing Child Opinions

**Disagreement as a friction point:** Differing opinions between caregivers and children frequently lead to arguments and power struggles.

**Long-term consequence of suppression:** Over time, children who feel judged or rebuked for disagreeing will stop feeling comfortable communicating their opinions. This shuts down open communication.

### Self-Reflection Framework

Before responding to a child's differing opinion, ask yourself these perspective-taking questions:
- "Would I want to be my child?"
- "Would I want to be treated the same way?"
- "Would I feel comfortable if someone talked to me this way?"

### Key Principle: Listening Does Not Equal Agreement

**Critical distinction:** Allowing a child to express their opinion does not require agreeing with that opinion. These are separate actions.

### Implementation Guidelines

**Active listening requirement:** Allow the child to fully finish their thought without interruption.

**Response template when you disagree:**
"I hear you are feeling ______ [summarize what child said], I feel ______ [calmly explain your feeling], can we please ______ [work toward a solution you both agree with]."

### Causal Chain: Modeling Effects

**Positive modeling outcome:** By responding calmly to differing opinions, you teach the child how to respond to others in their life who may think differently. You demonstrate that people do not need to agree on everything but can always listen respectfully.

**Reciprocity principle:** The more you listen to your child, the more they will listen to you.

**Anti-pattern and its consequence:** The more you interrupt and yell at children during disagreements, the more they will interrupt and yell at you. Children mirror communication patterns.

## Strategy 2: Follow Through with Expectations

### The Preventive Approach

**Timing principle:** Children should know what is expected of them and what the consequences will be BEFORE they are in a situation where they normally misbehave. Expectations must be communicated proactively, not reactively.

**Rationale:** By setting expectations before the behavior occurs, the child knows exactly what you are looking for and what they can expect if they do not meet the expectation.

### Four-Step Communication Framework

1. **Clearly explain the behavior you don't want to see** - Define the undesirable behavior specifically
2. **Explain the behavior you want to see** - Define the desired alternative behavior
3. **Explain the consequences for both behaviors** - State outcomes for compliance and non-compliance
4. **Follow through with consequences** - Execute what you stated

### Why Follow-Through Matters

**Behavior reduction mechanism:** Communicating your plan reduces challenging behavior. Following through with the plan reduces the caregiver's temptation to yell and nag.

**Child security:** When children know what is expected, they feel a sense of safety and security.

**Credibility building:** As you consistently follow through with what you say, your child learns that you mean what you are saying. This creates a foundation where the child will start to listen because your words have consistent meaning.

### Anti-Pattern: Inconsistent Follow-Through

**Warning:** If you state expectations but do not follow through, children learn that your words do not carry consistent meaning. This undermines future communication.

**During resistance:** Even when your child is yelling and making a scene, follow through. Consistency during difficult moments is when follow-through matters most.

## Strategy 3: Use "I Feel" Statements for Strong Emotions

### Modeling Healthy Emotional Expression

**Core principle:** Children are always watching and are "little sponges soaking up everything that we do." Every time you express strong emotions (anger, frustration, sadness) in a calm and clear manner, you teach your child how to effectively talk about difficult things.

**Outcome of consistent modeling:** Your child will be much more likely to listen to you when you communicate this way consistently.

### "I Feel" Statement Template

**Structure:** "I feel ______, when you ______, could you please ______."

### Practical Examples

**Frustration example:** "I feel frustrated when you ignore me, could you please look at me when I am talking to you?"

**Concern example:** "I feel concerned when you forget to tell me you are going out after school, could you please let me know where you are going next time?"

**Anger example:** "I feel angry when you leave your clothes all over the floor, could you please put your clothes away next time?"

**Sadness example with self-regulation:** "I feel sad when you say hurtful things to me, I need to go for a walk to help me calm down."

### Why This Works

**Alternative to yelling:** Using an "I feel" statement instead of yelling models healthy communication.

**Teaching function:** You are teaching your child how to talk calmly and respectfully to you and others.

## Summary of Three Strategies

1. **Encourage children to express their own opinions** - Listen fully, respond calmly, model respectful disagreement
2. **Follow through with what you say** - Set expectations before situations, state consequences, execute consistently
3. **Use "I feel" statements when experiencing strong emotions** - Model calm emotional expression, name the feeling, name the trigger, request change

## Underlying Principles

**Communication is learned behavior:** Patterns come from environment and can be changed with intentional practice.

**Children mirror adults:** Both positive and negative communication patterns are absorbed and replicated.

**Consistency builds trust:** Following through creates predictability, which creates safety and compliance.

**Emotional modeling shapes development:** How parents handle strong emotions teaches children their own emotional regulation and expression skills.

**Listening creates listening:** Reciprocal communication patterns emerge from how parents initiate communication.
