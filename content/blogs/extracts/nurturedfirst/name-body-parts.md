# Anatomically Correct Body Part Names: Why It's Important

**Source:** Nurtured First (Shannon Wassenaar, Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** February 23, 2024
**Reviewed by:** Nurtured First's team of child development experts

## Core Thesis

Teaching children the anatomically correct names for all body parts, including genitals (penis, vulva, scrotum, testicles) and private areas (bum, nipples, chest), is a foundational protective strategy. This practice empowers children, reduces vulnerability to abuse, and establishes healthy communication patterns that persist through adolescence and adulthood.

---

## Three Core Benefits of Using Anatomically Correct Terms

### 1. Enhances Body Image, Boosts Self-Esteem, and Reduces Shame

**Mechanism of action:**
Using proper anatomical names removes the sense of shame or taboo around body parts. When parents use accurate terms like "penis" and "vulva" instead of cute nicknames or silly made-up words, children receive the message that their entire body is normal and natural.

**Outcomes:**
- Children develop comfort and confidence in their physical selves
- Private parts are normalized as just another part of the human body, no different than an arm or leg
- Prevents body parts from seeming strange, embarrassing, bad, or dirty
- Reduces bodily shame
- Promotes healthy body image

**Downstream effects on sexuality:**
Giving children proper language to understand their bodies paves the way for open, judgment-free communication about sexual health. Parents can discuss puberty, reproduction, and sex without stigma. This fosters healthy sexuality grounded in mutual care, consent, and respect.

**Warning (anti-pattern):**
When children feel ashamed or insecure about their body parts, it can prevent them from speaking up when their body parts are the target of jokes, bullying, or abuse. If private parts are referred to as "naughty," "dirty," or "bad" words, it stops children from feeling confident and safe to share their experiences with caregivers.

---

### 2. Prevents Abuse

**Research basis:** The American Association of Pediatrics supports this approach, showing children who know proper terms for genitals and anus are less vulnerable to abuse.

**Two key protective mechanisms:**

**A. Increases child's confidence and knowledge**
- Naming private parts removes shame and secrecy around those body areas
- Abusers often rely on a child's innocence, lack of knowledge, and discomfort about private parts to take advantage of them
- Abusers specifically target children who appear innocent, shy, and unlikely to report abuse
- A child who openly uses anatomically correct terms is less appealing as a potential victim
- The openness communicates confidence, knowledge, and lack of shame
- This reduces their desirability and likelihood of being targeted

**B. Gives language to report inappropriate behavior**
- Children gain vocabulary to articulate if someone does something inappropriate, such as receiving unwanted touch
- A child who knows to call their genitals a "penis" or "vulva" can communicate clearly if someone tries to touch them there
- This allows them to tell a parent or trusted adult if abuse occurs

**Summary principle:** Removing discomfort and secrecy is a primary way to protect kids. Correct anatomy terms are an important piece of that protection.

---

### 3. Improves Communication

**First benefit:** Allows kids to ask questions and express concerns about their bodies more clearly. When children know proper terms, they can articulate questions or issues without confusion, embarrassment, or shame. This paves the way for open, judgment-free dialogue.

**Practical example:** A child who knows the terms "vulva" and "penis" could tell their parents when and where they are experiencing pain, irritation, or discomfort.

**Second benefit:** Using accurate anatomy terms normalizes these conversations within the family. This is especially helpful for setting the stage for clear and open communication during puberty. With experience coming to parents with medical questions or concerns, kids feel more comfortable asking questions about their private parts or bringing concerns to parents during puberty.

**Key insight:** Approaching these topics without judgment supports and enhances communication between caregiver and child.

---

## When to Start Teaching

**Optimal timing:** Start naming genitals from a young age, as early as infancy.

**Rationale:** The key is to normalize body part conversations from a young age. This builds healthy communication that will be helpful for future talks about sex, puberty, and more.

## How to Teach: Natural Incorporation Methods

### Method 1: During Diaper Changes or Potty Time

**Example scripts:**
- "I have to wipe your bum and your penis before I put on a clean diaper."
- "When you're wiping your vulva, you need to wipe front to back to keep it clean."

### Method 2: At Bath Time

**Example scripts:**
- "Mommy is a safe person. I have to help you wash your penis until you're able!"
- "It's time to scrub a dub! Let me see you wash your belly button, nipples, chest, armpits..."

### Method 3: While Getting Dressed

**Example scripts:**
- "We have to put on a bathing suit before we can go to the beach because it's a public place. Your bathing suit covers all of your private parts: your chest, nipples, vulva, and bum."
- "The part you're touching is called a scrotum. Inside the scrotum are your testicles; you might have felt those, too. These are private parts, which is why we cover them before we go to play outside."

## Playful Teaching Methods

Play is a child's language and an incredibly effective way to teach body safety.

**Creative activities:**
- Create a "body parts" matching game
- Trace the outline of a child's body and label the body parts together
- Play "body parts" Pictionary
- Read books that name and label body parts

**Principle:** Just like you would ask a child to label their hands, fingers, feet, and toes, invite your child to label their private parts.

---

## Guidance for Parents

### Lead by Example
Use proper terms instead of silly names or made-up words for private parts.

### Normalizing Discomfort
If using anatomically correct terms feels uncomfortable at first, that is normal. Practice saying the words alone to get more comfortable.

### Consistency Principle
Treat private parts the same way you treat all other body parts in conversation and teaching.

## Illustrative Case Study: Linneah

**Context:** Linneah is a 3.5-year-old in junior kindergarten, sensitive, shy, and often picked on by older kids on her bus.

**Incident:** An eighth grader stole her hat. When she threatened to tell the bus driver, the bully whispered: "I'll tell the bus driver that you touched my penis, and you'll get in even bigger trouble!"

**Child's internal experience:**
- The interaction was scary and confusing
- She did not know what the bully was referring to
- She could tell by the tone that it was a "bad word"
- She wondered if she should tell her parents but worried she would get in trouble for saying that word
- She feared the bully would find out and pick on her even more
- Result: She stayed quiet

**Lesson:** This story demonstrates how correctly naming body parts can help empower a child, whether being bullied by peers or targeted by abusive adults. Had Linneah been taught anatomically correct terms in a shame-free context, she would have had the confidence to report the incident without fear of punishment for using the word.

## Body Safety Framework Components

A comprehensive body safety approach teaches children to:
- Learn the correct names for body parts
- Understand public and private spaces and body parts
- Say "NO" when needed
- Identify their personal team of safe people
- Understand the difference between secrets and surprises

---

## Summary of Causal Chains

**Shame-free naming** leads to **body confidence** leads to **willingness to communicate** leads to **ability to report inappropriate behavior** leads to **reduced vulnerability to abuse**

**Shame-free naming** leads to **open family dialogue** leads to **normalized puberty conversations** leads to **healthy sexual development**

**Using nicknames or avoidance** leads to **internalized shame** leads to **reluctance to discuss body** leads to **inability to report abuse** leads to **increased vulnerability**

## Key Terms to Teach

**Male anatomy:** penis, scrotum, testicles, bum, nipples, chest
**Female anatomy:** vulva, bum, nipples, chest
**General:** all standard body parts (belly button, armpits, etc.)

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Using cute nicknames for genitals
- Using silly made-up words for private parts
- Referring to private parts as "naughty," "dirty," or "bad" words
- Creating shame or secrecy around body parts
- Avoiding conversations about body parts
- Treating private parts differently (with embarrassment or avoidance) than other body parts
