# Ditch Sharing and Teach Turn-Taking: Complete Knowledge Extract

**Source:** Nurtured First (nurturedfirst.com/toddler/teach-turn-taking)
**Author:** Paige Shiels (Registered Early Childhood Educator, BA in Youth and Children's Studies)
**Published:** July 29, 2021
**Target Ages:** Toddler, Preschooler, Big Kid

---

## Core Concept: The Sharing to Turn-Taking Paradigm Shift

The fundamental recommendation is to **replace the language of "sharing" with "turn-taking"** when children struggle with sharing. This is not merely semantic but represents a fundamentally different approach to toy conflicts.

### Why Children Struggle with Sharing

**Developmental reality:** Little kids are not developmentally expected to share generously. Sharing is one of the most challenging tasks for children.

**Adult perspective analogy:** Adults have personal items (like phones) they would not want to share with friends or siblings. This mirrors how children feel about their favorite toys, and often extends to all toys.

---

## Sharing vs. Turn-Taking: Key Distinctions

### Traditional Sharing

- **Control locus:** Limits set by an outside force (parent/caregiver)
- **Time constraint:** Often imposes arbitrary time limits on toy possession
- **Dependency:** Requires parents to be present to mediate conflicts
- **Child experience:** Child may feel angry because forced to give up toy before finished playing

### Turn-Taking

- **Control locus:** Child-directed
- **Time constraint:** Child decides when they are done with the toy
- **Independence:** Equips kids to resolve battles on their own
- **Child experience:** Child feels good about voluntarily giving the toy to another

**Important caveat:** Neither approach is inherently right or wrong as a goal. Both sharing and turn-taking are complex skills requiring time, practice, and modeling to learn.

---

## The Turn-Taking Protocol: Step-by-Step Mechanics

### Basic Sequence

1. **Initiation:** Child A is playing with a toy. Child B wants the toy.
2. **Request:** Child B approaches Child A and asks for a turn.
   - If Child B is non-verbal, a caregiver facilitates this request alongside them.
3. **Response options for Child A (the "toy holder"):**
   - Option 1: Give up the toy immediately (their choice, not forced)
   - Option 2: Say "You can have the toy when I'm done" and continue playing
4. **Transfer:** When Child A is ready to do something else, they hand the toy to Child B.
5. **Edge case:** If Child A keeps the toy until bedtime, Child B gets first pick of the toy the next morning.

## Five Reasons Turn-Taking Works Better Than Forced Sharing

### 1. Teaches Patience

Patience is a complex skill children need to learn. Turn-taking provides structured practice in waiting for their turn rather than demanding immediate gratification.

### 2. Takes Away the Toy's Power

**Problem with timers:** When parents force sharing using timers, telling children they must give the toy up in X minutes:
- Many children do not yet understand the concept of time
- When the timer goes off, they are still upset because they were not finished playing
- The toy remains a source of conflict and power struggle

**Turn-taking solution:** Giving children the ability to choose when they are done removes the toy's power. Result: fewer challenging behaviors around giving toys up over time.

### 3. Allows the Child to Feel Good

**Emotional outcome difference:**
- Forced sharing: Child feels angry about premature relinquishment
- Turn-taking: Child feels good about voluntarily giving the toy

**Example positive reinforcement script:**
"Lou, you wanted to play with your cars, but you noticed Anne wanted to play with the cars, too! You gave her a turn with one of your cars and played together. You both looked like you were having so much fun. That was cool to watch!"

### 4. Children Learn You Don't Always Get What You Want

**Life lesson integration:** Essential for children to understand delayed gratification and that immediate desires are not always satisfied.

**Caregiver response framework:** Acknowledge feelings while maintaining the boundary.
- Validate the upset about not getting an immediate turn
- Communicate that the other child is still playing
- Explain they must wait until the other child is finished

### 5. Encourages Independence

**Traditional sharing outcome:** Children become dependent on parents to mediate and provide answers.

**Turn-taking outcome:** With modeling and encouragement, children begin to handle conflicts independently and become well-equipped to resolve issues with peers on their own.

## How to Teach Turn-Taking: Implementation Methods

### Method 1: Practice, Model, and Encourage

**Core principle:** Focus time and energy on behaviors you want to see more of.

**Three components:**
1. **Practice** with children directly
2. **Notice the good out loud** (verbal positive reinforcement)
3. **Model** the behavior yourself

**Example scripts for positive reinforcement:**

When child gives a turn voluntarily:
"Ah, Jamie! I noticed you giving Si a turn with your doll! Look at his face; he's so happy you gave him a turn!"

When child asks and waits appropriately:
"Hey, buddy! I loved how you asked your sister for a turn with the train and patiently waited until she was finished and gave it to you!"

### Method 2: In-the-Moment Coaching

**Context:** Use when multiple children are actively fighting over things.

**Real-World Example (4-year-old and 1-year-old):**

**Situation:** Emmy (4) wants a ball that Jules (1, non-verbal) is holding.

**Emmy's behavior:** Complaining to parent, actively trying to take the ball.

**Parent coaching response:**
1. Get on child's level physically
2. Acknowledge desire: "I see you want the ball, Emmy."
3. Provide the protocol: "You can ask Jules for a turn, and when she is done playing with it, you can have it."

**Outcome:** Emmy, still angry, asks Jules for a turn.

**Parent follow-up validation:**
"I noticed you asked her for a turn even though you were really angry. That was tough. I see that! Hopefully, you can find another toy, and soon enough, she will share it."

**Natural resolution:** Fifteen seconds later, Jules moved on to another activity. Emmy got the ball.

## Long-Term Benefits and Trajectory

### Routine Establishment

Consistent application creates a predictable routine when children fight over toys. Children learn that the parent will ask "Have you asked for a turn?" every time.

### Reciprocity Development

Children recognize that giving siblings turns means siblings will likely do the same for them in future situations.

### Parental Exit Strategy

**Key outcome:** You are "working your way out of a coaching job." With consistent practice, you will eventually hear your child independently saying "Hey, [sibling], can I have a turn?" without parental prompting.

## Anti-Patterns and Implicit Warnings

### The Timer Trap

Using timers to force toy handover:
- Does not account for child's lack of time concept understanding
- Guarantees upset when timer sounds (child was not finished)
- Maintains toy as power object
- Keeps parent in perpetual mediator role

### The Forced Handover

Snapping and commanding "Give your brother the toy NOW!" out of parental frustration:
- Leads to one child crying, one screaming
- Often preceded by push, hit, grab behaviors
- Creates negative emotional associations with sharing

### Expecting Developmental Impossibilities

Expecting little kids to share generously goes against developmental reality. Reframe expectations to match what children can actually do at their stage.

## Key Terms and Concepts

- **Toy holder:** The child currently in possession of the contested toy
- **Waiting child:** The child who wants a turn with the toy
- **Child-directed:** The child maintains control over timing and decisions
- **Outside force:** Parent or caregiver imposing rules externally
- **Coaching:** Guiding children through the turn-taking protocol in real-time during conflicts
- **Modeling:** Demonstrating the desired behavior for children to observe and emulate
- **Notice the good out loud:** Verbally acknowledging positive behaviors as they occur

---

## Conceptual Hierarchy

1. **Foundational understanding:** Sharing is developmentally hard for children
2. **Paradigm shift:** Replace "sharing" language with "turn-taking" framework
3. **Mechanism change:** Move from external control to child-directed control
4. **Implementation:** Practice, model, encourage, and coach
5. **Outcome:** Independent conflict resolution capability
