# Body Safety: Teaching Children the Difference Between Public and Private

**Source:** Nurtured First (Shannon Wassenaar, Registered Psychotherapist)
**Date:** February 23, 2024
**Reviewed by:** Nurtured First's team of child development experts

---

## Core Thesis

Childhood curiosity about bodies and private parts is natural, expected, and developmentally normal. Parents should respond to this curiosity without shame, fostering healthy exploration while teaching clear boundaries about appropriate contexts for certain behaviors. The goal is to nurture bodily awareness and autonomy, which serve as protective factors against abuse.

---

## The Problem: Shame-Based Responses

### The "Jill" Case Study (Anti-Pattern)

The article opens with a composite narrative illustrating generational harm from shame-based parenting:

**Childhood experiences that caused lasting damage:**
- Parents displayed visible discomfort with body questions, causing child to stop asking
- Hand-slapping when child touched private parts during bath, combined with labeling child "bad girl"
- Mouth washed with soap for saying "vagina" at home (learned on school bus)
- Punishment for peeking at relative changing into bathing suit, labeled "naughty"

**Long-term consequences:**
- Adult struggles with shame about bodies and sexuality
- Internal conflict when own child shows normal curiosity
- Paralysis between two competing internal parts: one recognizing child's innocence, another fearing raising a "bad girl"
- Difficulty parenting through body-related moments

**Key insight:** The parent's unresolved shame creates a cycle where they cannot respond appropriately to their own child's developmentally normal behavior.

## Foundational Concept: Developmental Normalcy of Body Curiosity

### Timeline of Curiosity Development

**Age 2 (Toddlers):**
- Curiosity can begin this early
- Toddlers touch their genitals as they become more aware of their bodies

**Preschool Age:**
- Increased awareness of bodies and gender differences
- Heightened curiosity about different people's bodies
- Active exploration phase

### Normal Behaviors to Expect in Early Childhood

The following behaviors are explicitly labeled as common and normal:

1. **Touching/rubbing their genitals**
2. **Attempting to touch genitals of peers, siblings, or parents**
3. **Peeking at others' private parts**
4. **Asking questions about private body parts and bodily functions**
5. **Playing "doctor" or "house" to explore curiosity about other children's private parts**

### Why Allowing Exploration Matters

**Causal chain:**
Touch → Body awareness → Understanding of body's appearance, sensations, needs → Bodily autonomy → Protection against abuse

**Critical distinction:** Children are NOT exploring for sexual purposes. They are gaining understanding of what their body feels like, looks like, and needs.

**Bodily autonomy defined:** The feeling of "inchargeness" over one's body

**Protective mechanism:** A child who:
- Is aware of their body
- Knows correct anatomical names
- Believes they are in charge of their body

...becomes more confident and articulate when determining what physical touch they consent to.

### Red Flags Requiring Professional Consultation

Seek support from a pediatrician or mental health professional when curiosity/exploration is:
- **Excessive**
- **Persistent** (beyond typical developmental phase)
- **Causing harm to themselves or others**

## Framework: Private vs. Public (Spaces, Behaviors, Body Parts)

### Private Spaces
- Areas where people can expect privacy
- Examples: home, bathroom, bedroom
- Function: Where it IS safe to touch and explore private parts

### Public Spaces
- Common areas in a home where people gather (living room, kitchen)
- Areas anyone can access (park, store, sidewalk)
- Function: Where it is NOT safe to expose or touch private parts

### Private Parts
- Specifically refers to: **mouth, genitals, and buttocks**
- Key rule: Private parts (excluding mouth) are kept covered and not shown to others without consent

### Public Parts
- Body parts that don't need to be covered in public
- Examples: hands, arms, legs, feet
- Key rules:
  - Not to be used to hurt others (hitting, pushing, kicking)
  - Still require consent before being touched

### Sample Scripts for Teaching Private vs. Public

**Script 1 (Bathing Suit Rule):**
"Our private parts are the parts of our body that are covered by our bathing suit. Those stay private, just for you. We keep them covered in public spaces, like the grocery store, school and the living room."

**Script 2 (Naming Safe Exploration Spaces):**
"The bathroom and your bedroom are private spaces where you can safely explore your private parts."

## Four Strategies for Fostering Healthy Exploration

### 1. Use Anatomically Correct Names for Private Parts

**Outcome:** Promotes development of a healthy, shame-free relationship with their body

### 2. Set Guidelines Around When and Where Exploration Can Happen

**Framework:**
- Allow privacy to explore when alone in a private space
- Provide gentle redirection if touching private parts in public spaces

### 3. Model Consent and Boundaries With Your Own Body

**Examples:**
- Explain that you want privacy when using the bathroom
- Explain that you want privacy when changing

**Mechanism:** Children learn by watching; modeling shows them how to respect physical boundaries

### 4. Avoid Judgmental Tones or Shame When Addressing Behavior

**Redirection script (public touching):**
"Hey, sweety. This is a public space. If you want to touch your private parts, you can go to the bathroom or your bedroom."

**Follow-up for emotional processing:**
"How did it feel for you when mommy asked you not to touch your private parts at the dinner table?"

## Ongoing Communication Strategy

### Key Principle
Teaching private vs. public is NOT a one-time conversation. It is an ongoing process that evolves with the child's development.

### Age-Specific Communication Progression

**Infancy:**
- Simply name body parts using anatomically correct names during caregiving
- Example: "Mommy is going to wipe your penis and your bum before I put on a clean diaper."

**Preschool:**
- Give child autonomy tasks related to their body
- Example: Have preschooler wash their own privates during bath time to foster body autonomy

**Teachable Moments (Any Age):**
When curiosity naturally arises, use it as a learning opportunity.

### Teachable Moment Example: Sibling Curiosity

**Situation:** Daughter looking at baby brother's private parts during diaper change (first time seeing boy's anatomy)

**Response framework:**

**Step 1: Validate curiosity + ask open-ended questions**
"I noticed you were looking at your baby brother's private parts when I was changing his diapers. Did you have any questions?"

**Step 2: Answer honestly with age-appropriate detail + teach privacy concept**
"Your baby brother might touch his penis because he's curious about his body, just like when he touches his toes and his feet. It helps him learn about his body. When he gets older, mommy will teach him to do this in private spaces, like the bathroom or his bedroom."

### Building Trust Through Personal Stories

Sharing age-appropriate personal experiences establishes trust on tricky topics.

**Example script:**
"When I was your age, my sister and I would turn around when we were changing in our shared bedroom. This made our space private for each other."

### Reframing: Teaching Opportunity, Not Punishment Occasion

When child touches private parts, treat as teaching opportunity rather than reason to scold, punish, or embarrass.

**Script for itching scenario:**
"I noticed you were touching your vulva under your bathing suit. I know sometimes you get itchy. When that happens, you can itch over your bathing suit or move to a private space, like the bathroom. If you touch your private parts with your hands, you can scrub them with soap and water, just like we would after using the potty!"

**Script for comfort adjustment:**
"I can see that you're touching your private parts because they're not in a comfortable position. Why don't we pause the game so you can go to the bathroom and adjust them so you're more comfortable? After you wash your hands, you can come back, and we can keep picking up where we left off."

### Long-term Trajectory

As child ages, build on early talks with:
- More specifics on privacy
- Bodily autonomy
- Puberty
- Relationships

**Core principle:** Maintain judgment-free space for child to share experiences, perspectives, and questions. Speaking about bodies openly without shame signals that parent is a trusted, safe source of information.

## Troubleshooting: Persistent Public Touching

### When Teaching Isn't Working

If child persistently touches private parts in public despite teaching, investigate underlying causes.

### Parental Mindset Shift

**Anti-pattern:** Assuming child is being "naughty" or "bad"
**Reframe:** Child's behavior is communication of a need

### Three Questions to Ask

1. **Do they need medical attention?** (Example: itching a rash)
2. **Do they need tools for emotional regulation?** (Example: self-soothing)
3. **Do they need more connection time with you?** (Example: seeking big reaction from caregiver)

**Key insight:** Getting to the root cause of behavior informs appropriate response.

## Summary: Principles for Positive Body Relationship

### What to Do
- Start age-appropriate conversations early
- Answer questions honestly
- Respond without shame
- Model respect for privacy and consent

### Ultimate Goal
Children grow up without shame or misinformation about their private parts.

### Call to Action for Parents

**Self-reflection:** Examine own experiences with how bodies/private parts were discussed (or not discussed) in childhood

**Cycle-breaking:** Aim to break cycles of silence or negativity surrounding private parts

**Comfort acknowledgment:** Conversations may feel uncomfortable initially

**Trust-building:** Creating atmosphere of trust and openness allows child to come to parent as issues arise
