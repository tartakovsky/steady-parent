# Why Spanking and Taking Things Away Are Ineffective Discipline Methods

## Source Information
- **Author**: Jess VanderWier, MA, RP (Registered Psychotherapist)
- **Source**: Nurtured First
- **Date**: February 13, 2023
- **Target Ages**: Toddler, Preschooler, Big Kid
- **Review Status**: Reviewed by Nurtured First's team of child development experts

## Core Thesis

Traditional discipline methods such as spanking and taking things away are ineffective for creating genuine behavior change in children. These methods fail to teach new skills, damage the parent-child relationship, and may increase problematic behaviors and emotional struggles over time.

---

## Part 1: Why Spanking Is Ineffective

### Key Research Foundation
The primary evidence comes from Elizabeth Gershoff's 2013 study "Spanking and Child Development," which reviewed the literature on spanking's effectiveness for behavior change.

### Reason 1: Fails to Teach Replacement Skills

**The Problem**: Spanking does not teach children why their behavior was wrong. It provides no replacement skill or alternative way to behave.

**What Spanking Actually Teaches**: Children learn how they should behave only when the threat of punishment exists (e.g., when they are around mom and dad).

**Critical Consequence**: Once the threat is removed, the child has no internal reason to behave appropriately. The behavior change is externally motivated and temporary, not internalized.

### Reason 2: May Inadvertently Reinforce Behavior

**The Underlying Dynamic**: Sometimes a child's behavior serves the purpose of seeking attention or connection with the parent.

**The Paradox**: If the child is engaging in behavior to get attention, the time spent spanking them may actually help them get this need met, even though the interaction is negative.

**Result**: Spanking can reinforce the challenging behavior, making it more likely to happen again in the future. The parent's response (even negative attention) becomes a reward.

### Reason 3: Children Remember Fear, Not Lessons

**Research Finding**: Studies on children who were spanked showed that children typically did not remember the reason they were spanked.

**What Children Actually Retained**:
- Messages of fear
- Anger towards their caregiver
- Sadness towards their caregiver

**Implication**: The "teaching moment" is entirely lost. Instead of learning from the experience, children associate the parent with negative emotions.

### Reason 4: Modeling Contradiction ("Do As I Say, Not As I Do")

**Core Principle**: Research is very clear that children learn from what is modeled to them.

**The Contradiction**: When parents spank, they may be modeling the very same behaviors they are trying to get the child to stop doing.

**Even "Calm" Spanking Is Problematic**: Even when spanking is done in a calm way, it still models the use of pain from a trusted person in order to create change. This communicates that inflicting physical pain is an acceptable way to get what you want.

**Research Verdict**: The "do as I say, not as I do" model of parenting is clearly shown in research as ineffective for creating behavior change.

### Reason 5: Long-Term Negative Outcomes

**Research-Documented Risks for Spanked Children**:
- Increased risk for mental health struggles
- Negative view of the parent-child relationship
- Increased challenging behavior during adolescence

### Summary of Why Spanking Fails

Spanking is ineffective because it:
1. Does not teach new skills
2. Creates a culture of fear in children
3. Models inappropriate behavior
4. Could lead to increased risk for emotional struggles later in life

## Part 2: Why Taking Things Away Is Ineffective

### Definition of the Practice
**Taking things away** involves removing privileges, toys, or activities as a consequence for behavior.

### Reason 1: Does Not Teach New Skills

**The Core Problem**: Like time-outs and spanking, taking things away does not teach the child new skills or get to the root of the child's behavior.

**Why It Feels Effective**: In the moment, it may feel effective because a child may be highly motivated to have a certain item or activity back. This motivation can be mistaken for learning.

### Reason 2: Young Children Do Not Understand Cause and Effect

**Developmental Reality**: Little children most often do not understand the "cause and effect" relationship between their behavior and the consequence.

**What Young Children Actually Understand**:
- They are angry at you for taking away a valued toy or activity
- You are the "bad guy" for taking away the toy or activity
- They want that toy back

### Reason 3: Misdirected Focus

**The Problem**: Instead of focusing on learning from their behavior or coming up with new ways to get their needs met, children focus on their feelings towards the loss of the beloved item.

**The Emotional Hijack**: The child's emotional energy goes toward grieving the loss rather than toward reflection or skill-building.

### Summary of Why Taking Things Away Fails

Taking things away:
1. Does not address the root cause of behavior
2. Does not teach replacement skills
3. Is not developmentally appropriate for young children
4. Damages the parent-child connection (parent becomes the "bad guy")
5. Redirects the child's attention away from learning

## Conceptual Framework: What Effective Discipline Requires

Based on why these methods fail, effective discipline must:

1. **Teach replacement skills**: Show children alternative ways to behave
2. **Address the root cause**: Understand why the behavior is happening (e.g., attention-seeking may indicate a need for connection)
3. **Create internal motivation**: Help children want to behave appropriately even when no one is watching
4. **Model desired behavior**: Demonstrate through action what you want children to learn
5. **Preserve the relationship**: Maintain connection rather than creating fear or positioning parent as adversary
6. **Be developmentally appropriate**: Match the child's cognitive capacity to understand cause and effect

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Anti-Pattern 1: Relying on Threat-Based Compliance
Using fear or threat of punishment creates only superficial compliance that disappears when the threat is removed.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Providing Negative Attention for Attention-Seeking Behavior
Any attention (even negative) can reinforce behavior if the child's underlying need is for connection.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Expecting Young Children to Connect Actions to Consequences
Toddlers and preschoolers lack the cognitive development to reliably understand abstract cause-and-effect relationships.

### Anti-Pattern 4: Modeling Behaviors You Want to Eliminate
Using physical force, yelling, or other behaviors you want your child to stop will undermine your teaching.

### Anti-Pattern 5: Prioritizing Immediate Compliance Over Long-Term Development
Methods that "work" in the moment may create worse outcomes over time.

## Practical Implications

### For Parents Currently Using These Methods
The article frames the information as empowering: "When you know more, you can do things differently."

### What to Do Instead
While the article does not provide detailed alternatives (those are covered in the referenced Parenting Little Kids course), the framework suggests parents should:

1. Investigate what need the child's behavior is trying to meet
2. Teach specific replacement skills
3. Model the behaviors they want to see
4. Maintain connection even during challenging moments
5. Focus on long-term skill development rather than immediate compliance

---

## Critical Takeaways

1. **Decades of research** support the conclusion that spanking and taking things away are ineffective
2. **Children remember emotions, not lessons** when punished physically
3. **Attention-seeking behavior** may actually be reinforced by punishment if the root need is connection
4. **Modeling matters more than words**: "Do as I say, not as I do" does not work
5. **Developmental capacity** must be considered; young children cannot connect distant consequences to actions
6. **Relationship damage** from these methods can persist into adolescence
7. **Effective discipline teaches**, rather than simply punishes
