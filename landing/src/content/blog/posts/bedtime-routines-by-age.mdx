## TLDR

- **Match the routine to your child's sleep pressure**, not a one-size-fits-all checklist. A toddler who napped late needs a different approach than one who skipped it entirely.
- **Start the routine earlier than you think.** For an 8pm bedtime, you probably need to begin at 6:30. Yes, really.
- **Physical play 40 minutes before bed** actually helps (paradoxically). Rough-and-tumble play discharges stress hormones and builds connection.
- **Give your child time to process their day** at the beginning of the routine, before they're overtired and can't talk about it.
- **Your departure script matters more than the routine itself.** A predictable goodbye closes the loop and tells their brain it's safe to let go.

## Why your current routine probably isn't working

You followed the advice. Bath, book, bed. Same time every night. And yet here you are, 47 minutes into negotiations about whether the closet door is open at the correct angle. The routine is consistent. The child is not sleeping.

**The problem usually isn't the routine itself. It's the timing.** Your child is either overtired (stress hormones have kicked in, creating a wired, giddy, paradoxically energetic monster) or undertired (they genuinely aren't sleepy, and no amount of lavender lotion will fix that). A perfectly designed routine running at the wrong time is just a fancy way to waste an hour.

Here's how to tell which one you're dealing with. Overtired kids get clingy, silly, irritable, or catch a second wind. Undertired kids stall, ask for one more story six times, and take over 30 minutes to fall asleep after lights out. The fix is adjusting wake windows, not adding more routine steps.

But here's the thing. Timing is only half the equation. The other half is what actually happens inside that routine, and that changes dramatically by age.

## Sleep pressure by age (the numbers that actually matter)

Every parent wants the magic bedtime. But bedtime isn't a fixed number. It's a math problem.

**Sleep pressure is the biological drive to sleep, and it builds during awake time.** The longer your child has been awake, the more adenosine accumulates in their brain, and the sleepier they get. Too little pressure and they'll bounce off the walls. Too much and stress hormones flood in, making sleep harder, not easier. Your job is to hit the sweet spot.

Here are the wake windows that matter:

- **Ages 1-2:** 4-6 hours of awake time before bed
- **Age 3 (still napping):** 6-7 hours after last nap
- **Age 3 (dropped the nap):** 11-13 hours total awake time
- **Ages 4-5:** 11-14 hours of awake time
- **Ages 6-12:** 12-15 hours of awake time

Work backwards from wake-up time. If your 8-year-old wakes at 6:30am and needs to be asleep by 8pm, that's 13.5 hours of awake time. Totally reasonable. But if they're napping after school (yes, some still do), you've just blown the math.

What most parents don't realize is that the signs of bad timing look nothing like "sleepy." A two-year-old who shortened their nap but still takes 45 minutes to fall asleep at night? Undertired. A nine-year-old who's suddenly emotional at bedtime and can't settle? That might not be a sleep problem at all. Sometimes they're processing something bigger (missing a parent, school stress, friendship drama), and the routine needs space for that conversation.

## The toddler routine (ages 1-3): roughhousing is not the enemy

You've been told to create a "calm environment" before bed. Dim lights. Soft music. Whisper voices. And your toddler responds by launching themselves off the couch like a tiny professional wrestler.

**Counterintuitive truth: rough-and-tumble play about 40 minutes before bed actually helps toddlers sleep.** Blanket swings, wheelbarrow walks across the hallway, building pillow towers and crashing into them. This kind of play provides deep sensory input that regulates their nervous system, burns off stress hormones accumulated during the day, and (crucially) fills their connection tank with the parent doing bedtime.

{/* IMAGE: A parent swinging a laughing toddler in a blanket like a hammock in a living room, with a pillow tower toppled nearby and a clock on the wall showing 7:00 alt="Parent swinging toddler in blanket hammock with toppled pillow tower nearby" */}

Here's what the toddler routine looks like in practice:

1. **40 minutes before bed:** Rough-and-tumble play (10-15 minutes). Not for sensory-avoiders. If your kid hates being swung, skip it. Pillow crashes or wheelbarrow walks might work instead.
2. **30 minutes before bed:** Bath or wash-up. This is the transition signal.
3. **20 minutes before bed:** Pajamas, teeth, one or two books.
4. **10 minutes before bed:** Lights dim. Song or quiet talk. Say goodnight to body parts (toes, knees, tummy, fingers). Kids love this, and it's a body-awareness exercise disguised as a game.
5. **Departure:** Your script matters. "I love you. I'll check on you. Sleep time now." Same words, every night, delivered like a boring robot.

If your toddler only wants one parent at bedtime and melts down when the other one shows up, that's a [parent-preference pattern](/blog/sleep/parent-preference-bedtime/) worth addressing separately. It's common, it's fixable, and it doesn't mean the non-preferred parent is doing something wrong.

The key with toddlers is that **99% of under-fives need parental support to fall asleep.** That's not a failure. That's biology. Your job isn't to make them independent sleepers overnight. It's to make the support predictable.

## The preschooler routine (ages 3-5): discharge the anxiety first

Your preschooler has a rich inner life that they cannot articulate, a full day of social navigation they barely survived, and a brain that processes all of it approximately three seconds after you say "lights out."

**Give your child time to process their day at the beginning of the routine, not at the end.** Once they're overtired, they can't think clearly, and everything becomes a crisis. At the start of the routine, when they still have some cognitive bandwidth, ask what happened today. Let them talk. You'll be amazed what comes out when you just shut up and listen for five minutes.

After that emotional download, the physical routine kicks in:

- **Monster management:** If your kid is afraid of what's under the bed, don't dismiss it. Grab a broom and sweep the monsters out, or make monster spray (water bottle, label, done). You're not validating an irrational fear. You're giving them a tool to manage it.
- **The body-part goodnight:** Say goodnight to each body part from toes up. "Goodnight toes, you ran so fast today. Goodnight knees, you jumped off that rock." It's progressive relaxation without the boring adult version.
- **The check-in promise:** "I'll come check on you in five minutes." Then actually do it. The first few nights they'll be awake when you check. Within a week, they won't be.
- **The boring return:** When they get out of bed (they will), walk them back without engagement. No conversation, no negotiation, no eye contact, no emotion. Be the most boring thing in the house. They'll stop getting up when it stops being interesting.

One tool that works surprisingly well: the **Get Out of Bed Free card.** Give them one card per night. They can use it to come out once for any reason (water, hug, question about dinosaurs). After that, the card is spent. It gives them control, which is the thing they're actually fighting for.

And about [screen time before bed](/blog/screens/end-screen-time/). You already know what I'm going to say. The research is clear, and no, "educational" content doesn't get a pass. Screens before bed extend sleep onset by suppressing melatonin. Build screen shutdown into the routine itself, not as a negotiation but as a fact.

## The school-age routine (ages 6-12): start earlier than you think

You assume your 8-year-old can handle a quick routine. Brush teeth, get in bed, lights out. Twenty minutes, tops. And then it's 9:30pm and they're still reading under the covers with a flashlight because you started the routine at 8:45.

**For an 8pm sleep time, start the routine at 6:30.** That's not a typo. Here's why: cortisol (the stress hormone) stays in your child's system for 24 hours or more. A stressful school day doesn't evaporate at dinner. Your routine needs enough decompression time to counteract that.

A school-age routine that works:

- **6:30pm:** Bath or shower. This is the physiological signal. Body temperature rises in warm water, then drops afterward, triggering sleepiness.
- **7:00pm:** Bedroom. Pajamas. Reading together or independently. This is connection time and cognitive wind-down.
- **7:30pm:** Lights out. Progressive relaxation (feet, calves, thighs, stomach, chest, arms, face). Or put on quiet instrumental music as a sleep association.
- **The 1-3 hour rule:** Get at least one to three hours of outdoor activity earlier in the day. Physical exhaustion is the single best sleep aid that exists for school-age kids. No supplement comes close.

Here's the part that makes parents uncomfortable: **lying next to your child while they fall asleep is temporary, not a failure.** If your kid needs you there, be there. You're not creating a dependency. You're providing safety during a developmental window that closes on its own. Nobody goes to college needing their parent to lie next to them.

If you're dealing with a child who takes hours to fall asleep despite a solid routine, that's a different problem. [Check if the issue is timing, anxiety, or something else entirely.](/blog/sleep/takes-hours-to-sleep/)

## How to handle transition meltdowns between routine steps

You announced it's time for pajamas and now someone is sobbing on the bathroom floor because they weren't done with their bath. Classic.

**Transitions are the friction points where routines break down.** The routine itself might be perfect, but if every step change triggers a [meltdown over transitions](/blog/tantrums/transition-meltdowns/), you'll abandon the whole thing within a week. The fix is warnings, choices, and zero negotiation.

- **Two-minute warning:** "Two more minutes of bath, then pajamas." Every time. Even when they ignore you (they heard you, they're just calculating if a tantrum is worth it).
- **Binary choices:** "Do you want to walk to your room or be carried like a sack of potatoes?" Both options end in the bedroom. The child picks the how, not the whether.
- **Acknowledge without caving:** "You're upset because bath was fun. I get it. Pajama time." Validate the feeling, hold the boundary. Repeat as needed while remaining aggressively boring.

The goal isn't a tantrum-free routine. Some nights will be rough. The goal is a routine that survives the tantrums because the steps are non-negotiable, predictable, and boring enough that fighting them eventually costs more energy than complying.

## FAQ

**How long should a bedtime routine take?**
About 20 to 40 minutes of active routine, depending on age. Toddlers trend shorter, school-age kids need more wind-down. If your routine regularly exceeds 45 minutes, something is off. Either you're starting too late (overtired child fights everything) or the routine has too many negotiable steps that invite stalling.

**Is it bad to lie with my child until they fall asleep?**
No. For younger children especially, parental presence provides the safety signal their nervous system needs to let go. It's not a habit you'll be stuck with forever. Most children naturally outgrow the need between ages 5 and 8. If it's working for your family, it's fine.

**My child needs the exact same routine or they melt down. Is that normal?**
Completely. Young children use predictability as a regulation tool. The sameness isn't rigidity, it's their brain building a reliable map of what comes next so they can relax. Lean into it. The need for exact repetition usually fades as their prefrontal cortex matures.

**What if my consistent routine suddenly stops working?**
Routines break for three reasons: the child's sleep pressure changed (nap transition, schedule shift), something emotional is happening (new sibling, school stress, missing a parent), or the routine aged out and needs updating. Check timing first, emotional factors second, structure third.

**Should I use a reward chart for staying in bed?**
Reward charts can help for a specific, short-term behavior change, but they don't address the underlying reason your child is getting out of bed. The Get Out of Bed Free card works better because it gives your child agency without requiring you to track stickers at 9pm when your own brain has checked out.
